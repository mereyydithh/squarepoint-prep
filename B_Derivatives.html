B — DERIVATIVES: MECHANICS, PRICING & STRATEGIES

Futures, options, swaps — what they are, how they're priced, how they're traded, and where they blow up

Depth: 🔴 DEEP

PART 1: WHAT IS A DERIVATIVE?

One-liner: A derivative is a contract whose value is derived from an underlying asset — it lets you take exposure to (or hedge away) the price of something without owning it.

The three purposes:
1. Hedging — Remove risk you don't want. An airline buys oil futures to lock in fuel costs. A PM buys puts to protect a portfolio.
2. Speculation — Take directional or volatility bets with leverage. Buy calls instead of stock to amplify returns.
3. Price discovery — Futures markets aggregate information from thousands of participants and produce benchmark prices.

Analogy: A derivative is like a side bet on a football game. You don't own the team (underlying asset), but your bet (derivative) gains or loses value based on how the team performs. The bet is "derived" from the game's outcome.

Why derivatives matter more than cash instruments for risk management: Cash instruments have linear payoffs — buy a stock, it goes up 5%, you make 5%. Derivatives can have non-linear payoffs, embedded leverage, and multiple risk dimensions (Greeks). A single options position can generate losses that are multiples of the initial investment. That's why they demand more sophisticated risk monitoring.

PART 2: FUTURES

What Is a Futures Contract?

One-liner: A future is a binding agreement between two parties to buy or sell a specific asset at a specific price on a specific future date — both sides are OBLIGATED to perform.

Analogy: If you pre-order a car at a locked-in price for delivery in 6 months, that's a forward contract. A future is the same thing, but standardized by an exchange and with daily settlement of gains/losses.

Key Mechanics

Feature Detail
Standardization Exchange-traded: fixed contract size, fixed delivery dates, fixed quality specifications. You can't customize.
Margin You post initial margin (~5-15% of notional), not the full price. This creates LEVERAGE.
Daily mark-to-market Every day, the exchange calculates your P&L and settles it in cash. If you lose → cash debited from your margin account. If margin falls below maintenance level → margin call → post more or get liquidated.
Central clearing The clearinghouse (CME Clearing, ICE Clear) sits between buyer and seller. Both sides face the clearinghouse, not each other. This eliminates bilateral counterparty risk.
Delivery Physical (WTI → actual oil at Cushing) or Cash-settled (S&P E-mini → cash difference). KNOW WHICH ONE you're trading.

Margin and leverage — a worked example:
- You buy 1 E-mini S&P 500 futures contract. Notional = index level × $50 = 5,400 × $50 = $270,000.
- Initial margin ≈ $13,000 (~5% of notional). That's the cash you post.
- Leverage = $270,000 / $13,000 = ~21x.
- If SPX moves +2%: your gain = $270,000 × 2% = $5,400. That's a 42% return on your $13,000 margin.
- If SPX moves -5%: your loss = $270,000 × 5% = $13,500. You just lost MORE than your entire margin. You owe the exchange money beyond what you posted. Margin call.

This is why leverage kills. The upside looks great. The downside can exceed your entire investment. And the margin call comes at the worst possible time — when the market is moving against you the fastest.

Futures Pricing: Cost of Carry

$$F = S \times e^{(r - q) \times T}$$

Variable What it is Effect
$S$ Spot price Higher spot → higher futures
$r$ Risk-free rate (financing cost) Higher rates → higher futures (costs more to finance holding the asset)
$q$ Yield / convenience yield Higher dividends or convenience yield → lower futures (you're missing out by not holding spot)
$T$ Time to expiry More time → more carry cost

In plain English: The futures price = spot price + what it costs to hold the asset until delivery (financing, storage, insurance) minus any income you'd earn from holding it (dividends, convenience yield). If futures deviate from this, arbitrageurs trade the gap until it closes.

Convenience yield — a concept unique to commodities: The benefit of physically owning the commodity right now (you can use it, avoid production shutdowns, meet customer orders). When supply is tight, convenience yield is high → futures trade below spot → backwardation.

Contango vs Backwardation

State Futures vs Spot Why Roll impact Who benefits
Contango Futures > Spot Ample supply. Carry costs dominate. Market expects prices flat or higher. Negative roll yield (sell cheap near, buy expensive far) Short futures positions
Backwardation Futures < Spot Tight supply NOW. Convenience yield dominates. Scarcity premium. Positive roll yield (sell expensive near, buy cheap far) Long futures positions

Roll mechanics:
- Futures expire. To maintain exposure, you "roll" — close the expiring contract, open the next month.
- In contango: You sell the near-month at $75, buy the next month at $77. You lost $2/barrel on the roll. Over a year, this can add up to 10-20% return drag.
- In backwardation: You sell near-month at $77, buy next month at $75. You earned $2/barrel. Roll yield adds to your return.
- Real-world impact: The USO ETF (oil) lost approximately 40% of its value over several years purely from roll costs in contango — even as oil prices were relatively stable. This is the "stealth tax" of commodity investing.

Futures Risks

  • Leverage: 20x leverage means a 5% adverse move wipes out your entire margin. Losses can exceed the initial investment.
  • Margin calls: Arrive at the worst time. In fast markets, you may be liquidated at a loss before you can post additional margin.
  • Basis risk: Futures ≠ spot. The gap (basis = spot − futures) changes unpredictably. Hedging with the "wrong" contract (e.g., Brent hedging jet fuel) means basis risk can blow out.
  • Liquidity at expiry: Volume concentrates in front months. Back-month liquidity can evaporate, leaving you unable to exit.
  • Physical delivery: Holding a physically-settled contract to expiry without delivery capability = catastrophe (WTI, April 2020, -$37/bbl).

PART 3: OPTIONS

What Is an Option?

One-liner: An option gives you the RIGHT but not the obligation to buy or sell an asset at a specified price (strike) by a specified date (expiry) — the premium you pay is the price of that right.

Analogy: An option is like a cancellable reservation. You pay a small fee (premium) for the right to buy the house at an agreed price by December. If house prices rise, you exercise and buy cheap. If they fall, you walk away and lose only the reservation fee. The premium is the most you can lose.

  • Call option: Right to BUY at the strike price. You want the underlying to go UP.
  • Put option: Right to SELL at the strike price. You want the underlying to go DOWN.
  • Long an option: You paid premium. Your risk is limited to the premium. You benefit from movement.
  • Short an option: You received premium. Your risk can be unlimited (short call) or very large (short put). You benefit from NO movement.

Option Types by Exercise Style

Why Different Models for Different Types?

The key insight: Black-Scholes gives you a formula you can solve on a calculator — but it only works when you know WHEN exercise happens (European = at expiry, period). The moment you add optionality about WHEN to exercise (American) or path-dependency (Asian), you need numerical methods (trees, simulation) because there's no closed-form shortcut.

Black-Scholes (European): 5 inputs → 1 formula → 1 price. Fast, elegant, but assumes constant vol, normal returns, no early exercise.

Binomial tree (American): Build a tree of possible price paths, step by step from today to expiry. At each node, check: "Is it better to exercise now or hold?" Work backward from expiry to today. More computational but handles early exercise correctly.

Monte Carlo (Asian/path-dependent): Simulate thousands of random price paths. For each path, calculate the option payoff (based on the average price, the path taken, etc.). The option price = the average of all simulated payoffs, discounted back. Slow but handles ANY payoff structure.

Option Value: What You're Paying For

Option Price = Intrinsic Value + Time Value (Extrinsic Value)

Component What it is Example (Call with K=100, S=105, premium=$8)
Intrinsic value The value if exercised RIGHT NOW. Call: max(0, S−K). Put: max(0, K−S). $5 (stock is $5 above strike)
Time value The premium for the POSSIBILITY that the option becomes more valuable before expiry. Reflects uncertainty. $3 ($8 premium minus $5 intrinsic)

Time value is highest for ATM options (maximum uncertainty about whether they'll expire ITM or OTM) and decays as expiry approaches (theta).

Moneyness

State Call condition Put condition Delta (approx) Key characteristics
Deep ITM S >> K S << K ~±0.90-1.00 Moves almost 1:1 with underlying. Mostly intrinsic value. Small time premium.
ITM S > K S < K ~±0.60-0.90 Has intrinsic value plus some time value.
ATM S ≈ K S ≈ K ~±0.50 Maximum time value. Maximum gamma. Where most vol trading happens.
OTM S < K S > K ~±0.10-0.40 Zero intrinsic value — pure time value. Cheap but unlikely to pay off.
Deep OTM S << K S >> K ~±0.01-0.10 Very cheap "lottery tickets." Huge percentage gains if the underlying moves far enough. Tail protection.

What Makes an Option More Expensive or Cheaper?

This is crucial — you need to understand what you're paying for and what's a good or bad deal.

Factor Higher → option premium is... Why
Higher implied vol (σ) MORE expensive More uncertainty → higher chance of a big move → option is worth more
More time to expiry (T) MORE expensive More time for something to happen (time value)
Higher interest rates (r) Calls MORE expensive, Puts CHEAPER Higher rates increase the forward price (forward = spot × e^rT), benefiting calls
Higher dividends (q) Calls CHEAPER, Puts MORE expensive Dividends reduce the forward price (stock drops by dividend amount on ex-date)
ATM strike MORE expensive (most time value) Maximum uncertainty about ITM/OTM outcome
Deep OTM strike CHEAPER Very unlikely to pay off
American vs European (same terms) American ≥ European Early exercise right has value (or at minimum, no negative value)
Asian vs European (same terms) Asian CHEAPER Averaging reduces payoff volatility → less valuable option

The practical interview insight: If a PM says "I want to buy downside protection," the key variables are: how much vol am I paying (implied vol vs realized vol), how much time am I buying (theta), and how far out-of-the-money is the strike (delta / probability of exercise). Expensive protection means vol is high and you're paying a lot of theta rent. Cheap protection means vol is low — which is often when you should be buying it (before the market gets scared, not after).

Black-Scholes — What It Does, What It Assumes, Where It Breaks

The formula (for a European call):
$$C = S \cdot e^{-qT} \cdot N(d_1) - K \cdot e^{-rT} \cdot N(d_2)$$

Where:
$$d_1 = \frac{\ln(S/K) + (r - q + \sigma^2/2)T}{\sigma\sqrt{T}}, \quad d_2 = d_1 - \sigma\sqrt{T}$$

You don't need to derive this. But you need to understand:
- $N(d_1)$ and $N(d_2)$ are cumulative normal distribution values — probabilities under a bell curve
- $N(d_2)$ ≈ probability the option expires ITM (risk-neutral probability)
- The formula takes 6 inputs and produces 1 output (option price)
- All inputs are observable EXCEPT implied volatility — which is backed out from the market price

Implied volatility (IV): The market price tells you what vol traders are pricing in. If the BS formula gives $5 for a call at 20% vol, but the call trades at $7, the "implied vol" is higher than 20% — the market expects more volatility than the formula's 20% assumption. IV is the market's consensus estimate of future realized volatility.

Where Black-Scholes breaks:

Assumption Reality Consequence
Returns are log-normally distributed (no jumps) Returns have fat tails — crashes happen far more often than a bell curve predicts OTM puts are underpriced by BS → market adds skew (higher IV for OTM puts)
Volatility is constant Vol changes constantly and correlates with price (vol up when market down) The volatility surface exists — IV varies by strike and maturity
Continuous trading, no gaps Markets close overnight and on weekends. Stocks gap on earnings. Gap risk isn't captured by BS → actual hedging costs exceed model predictions
No transaction costs Every hedge costs bid-ask + commissions In practice, you can't delta-hedge continuously — you rebalance discretely
Risk-free rate is constant Rates change Minor for short-dated options, material for LEAPS
No early exercise American options can be exercised early Must use binomial models for American options

The vol surface (the consequence of BS being wrong):
- If BS were perfectly correct, all strikes and maturities would have the same IV
- In reality, OTM puts have higher IV than ATM options (the "skew") because the market fears crashes
- Different maturities have different IV levels (the "term structure")
- The full picture is a 3D surface: IV as a function of both strike and maturity → covered in depth in Volatility

Put-Call Parity

$$C - P = S \cdot e^{-qT} - K \cdot e^{-rT}$$

In plain English: A call minus a put (same strike and expiry) equals a forward position. If this relationship breaks beyond transaction costs, there's an arbitrage opportunity — buy the cheap side, sell the expensive side, and lock in risk-free profit. This is one of the few true "no arbitrage" relationships in finance.

Why it matters for risk: Put-call parity means if you know 3 of the 4 values (call, put, spot, strike), you can derive the 4th. It also means a long call + short put = synthetic long stock (same P&L, funded differently).

PART 5: SWAPS

What Is a Swap?

One-liner: A swap is an OTC agreement between two parties to exchange cash flows over time — typically one side pays a fixed amount and the other pays a floating amount, based on an underlying reference.

Analogy: A swap is like two neighbors agreeing to swap their electricity bills. One pays a flat $200/month (fixed), the other pays whatever the actual bill is (floating). If electricity prices rise, the fixed payer wins. If they fall, the floating payer wins. Neither neighbor buys electricity for the other — they just settle the difference.

Interest Rate Swap (IRS)

One-liner: An IRS is an agreement to exchange fixed-rate payments for floating-rate payments on a notional amount — the most traded derivative in the world.

Role Pays Receives Wins when
Fixed payer (pays fixed, receives floating) Fixed rate (e.g., 4.00%) SOFR (floating, resets periodically) Rates RISE (you locked in low fixed, receive higher floating)
Fixed receiver (pays floating, receives fixed) SOFR Fixed rate (e.g., 4.00%) Rates FALL (you locked in high fixed, pay lower floating)

Key features:
- Notional amount is never exchanged — it's just a reference for calculating payments. A $100M notional swap doesn't mean $100M changes hands — just the net interest differential.
- Tenor: Common: 2Y, 5Y, 10Y, 30Y. Longer tenor = more rate sensitivity (higher DV01).
- DV01 of a swap: A 5Y IRS with $100M notional has DV01 ≈ $50,000. Meaning: a 1bp move in the swap rate changes the swap's value by $50K.
- Swap rate = the fixed rate that makes the swap fair (PV of fixed leg = PV of floating leg at inception).
- Swap curve = swap rates plotted by tenor. It's the market's expectation of future short-term rates, incorporating credit and liquidity premiums.

Who uses IRS:
- Banks: Convert floating-rate assets to fixed income streams (or vice versa)
- Corporates: Issue floating-rate debt but want fixed → enter swap as fixed payer
- Hedge funds: Express rate views with leverage. A $100M swap requires much less capital than buying $100M in bonds.
- Pension funds: Match long-dated liabilities (pension payments) with long-dated fixed-rate swap receipts

Total Return Swap (TRS)

One-liner: A TRS gives you the total economic exposure (price change + dividends) to an asset WITHOUT owning it — in exchange for paying a financing rate.

Party Receives Pays
Total return receiver Price appreciation + dividends on reference asset SOFR + spread (financing cost)
Total return payer SOFR + spread Exposes themselves to price depreciation + pays dividends

Why TRS matters — the Archegos lesson:
- No ownership: You never buy the stock, so no 13F disclosure (for equity TRS). No voting rights. But you have full economic exposure.
- Leverage: Initial margin on a TRS might be 10-15%, giving you 7-10x leverage. Archegos used TRS to build $30B+ in notional equity exposure on ~$3B of capital.
- Opacity: Each prime broker only sees their own TRS positions with you. No single PB saw Archegos' total exposure until it was too late.
- Risk: Counterparty risk (PB can demand margin or terminate), leverage (amplifies both ways), concentration (TRS on a single name is a leveraged single-stock bet).
- Post-Archegos regulations: The SEC now requires TRS reporting on large positions. Prime brokers improved cross-margining visibility. But the fundamental risk of synthetic leverage remains.

Credit Default Swap (CDS)

One-liner: A CDS is insurance against a company defaulting — the buyer pays a regular premium and gets paid if the reference entity has a "credit event."

See Cash Instruments Part 5 for full CDS mechanics, spreads, and basis.

Swap What it is Who uses it
Cross-currency swap Exchange principal AND interest payments in two different currencies. Includes FX exchange at inception and maturity. Corporates with foreign-currency borrowing. Banks managing multi-currency books.
Basis swap Exchange two floating rates (e.g., 3M SOFR vs 6M SOFR, or SOFR vs Fed Funds rate) Banks managing funding spread; relative value traders exploiting basis gaps

PART 6: EXOTIC DERIVATIVES — AWARENESS LEVEL

You won't be asked to price these, but you should know what they are and why they're risky:

Exotic What it is Pricing approach Key risk
Barrier option Knock-in (activates at barrier) or knock-out (dies at barrier) Analytical (simple) or Monte Carlo Pin risk near barrier — delta jumps violently. A knock-out at $100 means at $99.90 you have an option and at $100.10 you don't.
Asian option Payoff based on AVERAGE price over the option's life Monte Carlo (path-dependent, no closed-form) Lower vol than vanilla → cheaper. Useful for hedging average costs (e.g., monthly fuel purchases).
Lookback option Payoff based on the maximum or minimum price achieved during the life Monte Carlo Very expensive — like being told "you can buy at the lowest price of the year." High path dependency.
Quanto option Payoff in one currency, underlying in another, with FX rate FIXED Modified BS with correlation adjustment Removes FX risk from foreign asset exposure. Requires modeling correlation between asset and FX.
Cliquet / Ratchet Series of forward-starting options — locks in periodic returns Monte Carlo or semi-analytical Popular in structured products. Complex vol surface dependency.
Swaption Option on a swap — right to enter an IRS at a future date at a set rate Modified BS (Black's model for swaptions) Vol-of-rates exposure. Critical for rates vol trading. Used by mortgage servicers to hedge prepayment risk.
Variance swap Pays realized variance minus strike variance × notional No option pricing needed — traded as pure swap Short variance = unlimited downside. In Feb 2018, XIV (short VIX ETN) lost 96% of its value overnight.
Convertible bond Bond with embedded call option on the issuer's equity BS + credit model + bond pricing Complex Greeks (delta, gamma, rho, credit spread sensitivity). "Cheap equity" if conversion value exceeds bond value. Arb strategy: long convert, short stock, harvest gamma.

PART 7: DERIVATIVE RISK — WHAT KILLS FUNDS

1. Leverage

Every derivative has implicit leverage. A $500K premium on SPX options can give you $20M notional exposure. If SPX moves 10%, your option P&L scales to the $20M notional, not the $500K premium. This is by design.

2. Non-Linear Payoffs (Gamma Risk)

Options don't move 1:1 with the underlying — gamma accelerates or decelerates P&L. A short gamma position can go from "manageable" to "catastrophic" in a single session because losses ACCELERATE as the market moves against you. The sell-off doesn't just cost you delta × move — it costs you delta × move PLUS gamma × move² / 2. That squared term is what kills.

3. Liquidity Illusion

In calm markets, OTC derivatives show tight bid-ask spreads. In stress, market makers widen quotes or stop quoting entirely. Your mark-to-market valuation may not reflect what you can actually sell for. This is especially true for:
- Long-dated options
- OTM options in stressed names
- Exotic structures with no active secondary market

4. Model Dependence

Exotic derivatives are priced by models. If the model's assumptions break (vol surface shifts in an unexpected way, correlations spike to 1, liquidity vanishes), your "perfectly hedged" position isn't hedged. This happened in:
- Feb 2018 (vol models didn't anticipate VIX > 50)
- March 2020 (correlation assumptions broke in credit derivatives)
- LME nickel 2022 (price moves exceeded model boundaries)

5. Margin Cascades

The deadly spiral:
1. Market moves against you → exchange increases initial margin requirements during the vol spike
2. You must post more cash → sell other positions to raise cash
3. Those forced sales push down prices → trigger margin calls at OTHER counterparties
4. They sell → prices drop further → more margin calls → spiral

This is how Nick Leeson (Barings), Amaranth (NG), and MF Global (European sovereign bonds) all died.

6. Counterparty Risk

OTC derivatives (swaps, forwards, exotic options) expose you to the counterparty's ability to pay. If your counterparty defaults while you have a large unrealized gain, you lose that gain. AIG in 2008 sold massive CDS protection and couldn't pay when defaults spiked — required a $182B government bailout. Post-2008 reforms (central clearing, margin requirements, CSA agreements) reduced but didn't eliminate this risk.

PART 8: INTERVIEW APPLICATION

If asked "Explain the difference between a future and an option":

"A future is an obligation — both sides MUST transact at expiry. An option gives the holder the RIGHT but not the obligation. This asymmetry is the fundamental difference, and it's why options cost a premium while futures are theoretically free to enter.

The risk profiles are completely different. Futures have linear payoffs — you gain or lose dollar-for-dollar with the underlying, amplified by leverage. Options have non-linear payoffs — if you're long, your max loss is the premium, but your exposure changes as the underlying moves because of gamma. For risk management, futures are simpler — just offset the delta. Options require managing multiple dimensions simultaneously: delta, gamma, vega, theta. A single Greek in isolation is misleading — it's the interaction of all of them together that determines the real risk."

If asked "How do you price different types of options?":

"It depends on the exercise style and payoff structure. European options — where exercise only happens at expiry — can be priced with Black-Scholes, which gives a closed-form analytical solution. American options — where you can exercise early — need a binomial tree, because at every point you have to check whether early exercise is optimal. Asian options — where the payoff depends on the average price over the life — require Monte Carlo simulation because the average is path-dependent and there's no analytical shortcut.

The practical implication: American options are always at least as expensive as European options because the early exercise right has value. Asian options are cheaper than European because averaging reduces the payoff volatility. So when a PM asks 'how should I hedge my quarterly fuel costs?' an Asian option structure makes more sense — and it's cheaper — than a chain of European options."

If asked "What makes options more or less expensive?":

"Five things drive option premiums. Implied vol is the dominant factor — higher vol means bigger expected moves means the option is worth more. Time to expiry matters because more time = more opportunity for the option to become valuable. Interest rates affect the forward price, making calls pricier and puts cheaper when rates rise. Dividends do the opposite — they reduce the forward, making calls cheaper. And moneyness: ATM options have maximum time value, while deep OTM options are cheap but have low probability of paying off.

For the RM, the key question is: 'Are we paying too much?' If implied vol is well above recent realized vol, we're overpaying for protection. If it's below, we're getting cheap insurance — which is often the best time to buy, even though it feels like we don't need it."
