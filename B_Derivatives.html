B — DERIVATIVES: MECHANICS, PRICING & STRATEGIES

Futures, options, swaps — how they work, how they're priced, how they're used

Depth: 🟡 WELL

PART 1: WHY DERIVATIVES EXIST

Three reasons:
1. Hedging — Transfer risk you don't want. Airline buys oil futures to lock in fuel costs.
2. Speculation — Take risk with leverage. Buy call options instead of stock to amplify returns.
3. Price discovery — Futures markets aggregate information and set benchmark prices.

Every derivative is a contract whose value is derived from an underlying asset. The underlying can be anything: stocks, bonds, rates, currencies, commodities, volatility, even weather.

PART 2: FUTURES

What It Is

A contract to buy or sell an asset at a specified price on a specified future date. Both sides are OBLIGATED.

Key Mechanics

  • Standardized: Exchange-traded, fixed contract size, fixed expiry dates.
  • Margin: You don't pay the full price — you post initial margin (~5-15% of notional). This creates LEVERAGE.
  • Daily mark-to-market: Gains/losses settled daily via a margin account. If your margin falls below maintenance level → margin call → deposit more or get liquidated.
  • Physical vs cash settlement: Some futures deliver the underlying (WTI crude → actual oil). Others settle in cash (S&P 500 E-mini → cash difference).

Pricing: Cost of Carry

$$F = S \times e^{(r - q) \times T}$$

Where:
- $F$ = futures price
- $S$ = spot price
- $r$ = risk-free rate (financing cost)
- $q$ = dividend yield / convenience yield / storage cost
- $T$ = time to expiry

In plain English: Futures price = spot + cost to hold the asset until delivery (financing, storage, insurance) minus any income from holding (dividends, convenience yield).

Contango vs Backwardation

State Futures vs Spot Why Impact on roll
Contango Futures > Spot Storage + financing costs dominate. Supply is plentiful. Negative roll yield (rolling is expensive)
Backwardation Futures < Spot Scarcity premium for immediate delivery. Supply is tight. Positive roll yield (rolling makes money)

Roll Mechanics

Futures expire. To maintain a position, you must "roll" — sell the expiring contract, buy the next one.
- In contango: you sell low (near month), buy high (next month) → lose money.
- In backwardation: you sell high, buy low → make money.
- Roll cost can dominate returns. The USO ETF (oil) lost ~40% of its value over years just from roll costs in a contango oil market.

Futures Risk

  • Leverage: A 5% margin means 20x leverage. A 5% move in the underlying = 100% of your margin.
  • Margin calls: Position goes against you → post more margin or get liquidated at the worst time.
  • Basis risk: Futures don't perfectly track the spot price (basis = spot - futures). Basis can move independently.
  • Physical delivery: If you hold to expiry on a physically-settled contract and can't take delivery → disaster (April 2020 WTI).

PART 3: OPTIONS

What It Is

The RIGHT (not obligation) to buy or sell an asset at a specified price (strike) before/on a specified date (expiry).
- Call option: Right to BUY at the strike.
- Put option: Right to SELL at the strike.
- American: Can exercise any time before expiry.
- European: Can only exercise at expiry.

Option Value Components

Option Price = Intrinsic Value + Time Value

  • Intrinsic value: How much the option is worth if exercised now.
  • Call: max(0, S - K)
  • Put: max(0, K - S)
  • Time value: The extra premium for the POSSIBILITY that the option becomes more valuable before expiry. Always positive (before expiry). Decays as you approach expiry (theta).

Moneyness

State Call Put Characteristics
In-the-money (ITM) S > K S < K Has intrinsic value. Higher delta.
At-the-money (ATM) S ≈ K S ≈ K Delta ≈ 0.50. Max time value. Max gamma.
Out-of-the-money (OTM) S < K S > K No intrinsic value. Pure time value. Lower delta.

Black-Scholes: What It Does and What It Assumes

The model gives a theoretical price for European options.

Inputs: Spot price (S), Strike (K), Time to expiry (T), Risk-free rate (r), Dividend yield (q), Volatility (σ).

Five outputs: Option price, and the five Greeks (delta, gamma, theta, vega, rho).

Key assumptions (and how they break):

Assumption Reality
Returns are normally distributed Returns have fat tails — extreme moves happen more often than predicted
Volatility is constant Vol changes constantly — that's why the vol surface exists
No transaction costs or taxes Both exist and matter for hedging
Continuous trading possible Markets close, gap risk exists
No early exercise American options can be exercised early (dividends, deep ITM puts)

Put-Call Parity: $C - P = S \times e^{-qT} - K \times e^{-rT}$

This is an arbitrage relationship. If it breaks beyond transaction costs → arbitrage opportunity.

Option Strategies

Single-Leg

Strategy Construction View Max gain Max loss
Long call Buy call Bullish Unlimited Premium paid
Long put Buy put Bearish Strike - premium Premium paid
Short call Sell call Neutral/bearish Premium received Unlimited
Short put Sell put Neutral/bullish Premium received Strike - premium

Multi-Leg Spreads

Strategy Construction View What it does
Bull call spread Buy low-strike call + sell high-strike call Moderately bullish Caps upside but reduces cost
Bear put spread Buy high-strike put + sell low-strike put Moderately bearish Caps downside protection but cheaper
Straddle Buy call + put at same strike Expecting big move, direction unknown Profits from large moves in either direction
Strangle Buy OTM call + OTM put Cheaper than straddle, needs bigger move Similar to straddle but wider breakevens
Collar Long stock + buy put + sell call Protect downside, give up upside Zero-cost or near-zero hedge
Butterfly Buy 1 low, sell 2 middle, buy 1 high Expecting stock stays near middle strike Cheap bet on low volatility
Risk reversal Sell OTM put + buy OTM call (or vice versa) Directional with no net premium Measures market skew/sentiment
Iron condor Sell OTM put + buy further OTM put + sell OTM call + buy further OTM call Range-bound Profit if stock stays in a range
Calendar spread Sell near-dated + buy far-dated (same strike) Theta play Profit from near-dated decaying faster

When to Use What

Situation Strategy
"I want downside protection but hate paying premium" Collar (buy put, sell call) or put spread
"I think this stock will move big on earnings but I don't know which way" Straddle or strangle
"I want to express a bullish view with defined risk" Bull call spread
"I think vol is too high and the stock will stay in a range" Iron condor or short straddle
"I want to hedge my portfolio without selling anything" Buy index puts or put spreads

PART 4: SWAPS

Interest Rate Swap (IRS)

  • Exchange fixed rate payments for floating rate payments (or vice versa) over a period.
  • Fixed payer: Pays fixed, receives floating. Benefits if rates RISE.
  • Fixed receiver: Pays floating, receives fixed. Benefits if rates FALL.
  • Notional: Not exchanged — just used to calculate payment amounts.
  • DV01 of a swap: How much the swap's value changes per 1bp move in rates. An IRS with $100M notional and 5Y maturity has DV01 ≈ $50K.
  • Who uses them: Banks manage interest rate exposure, corporates convert floating debt to fixed, hedge funds express rate views.

Total Return Swap (TRS)

  • One party receives the total return (price change + dividends) on a reference asset, the other receives a financing rate (SOFR + spread).
  • Why it matters: TRS gives you synthetic exposure WITHOUT owning the asset. No 13F reporting. No share borrowing needed for shorting.
  • Archegos used TRS to build $30B+ in notional equity exposure that no single prime broker could see in full. → Case Studies
  • Risk: Counterparty risk (PB can call margin), leverage (small equity, large notional), opacity (no public reporting).

Credit Default Swap (CDS)

  • Insurance against default. Buyer pays spread, seller pays par minus recovery if default occurs.
  • Covered in Cash Instruments Part 5 (credit section).

Commodity Swap

  • Exchange floating commodity price for a fixed price. Airlines use these to fix fuel costs.
  • Physical vs financially settled.

PART 5: EXOTIC DERIVATIVES (AWARENESS)

You won't get deep questions on these but should know they exist:

Exotic What it is Risk concern
Barrier options Knock-in/knock-out at a specific price level. Changes payoff structure. Pin risk near barrier, sharp delta changes
Asian options Payoff based on AVERAGE price over a period, not just final price. Lower vol than vanilla → cheaper. You priced these at BNP.
Variance swap Pays realized variance minus strike variance. Pure vol bet. Short variance = unlimited downside. Volmageddon 2018.
Swaption Option on a swap. Right to enter an IRS at a future date. Vol-of-rates exposure. Key for rates vol trading.
Convertible bond Bond that can convert to equity. Embedded call option. Complex Greeks, hard to hedge, arb strategies exist.

PART 6: DERIVATIVE RISK — WHAT KILLS FUNDS

1. Leverage

Every derivative has implicit leverage. A $2M options position can have $20M notional exposure. If the underlying moves, your losses scale to the notional, not the premium.

2. Non-Linear Payoffs

Options don't move 1-for-1 with the underlying. Gamma accelerates or decelerates P&L. A short gamma position can go from "manageable" to "catastrophic" in a single session.

3. Liquidity Illusion

OTC derivatives can have wide bid-ask spreads in stress. Mark-to-market valuation may not reflect what you can actually sell for.

4. Model Dependence

Exotic derivatives are priced by models. If the model is wrong (February 2018, March 2020), your "hedged" position isn't hedged.

5. Margin Cascades

Commodity futures margin example:
- Oil vol spike → exchange increases initial margin → fund must post more cash → sell other positions to raise cash → those positions drop → more margin calls elsewhere → spiral.

PART 7: INTERVIEW APPLICATION

If asked "Explain the difference between a future and an option":

"A future is an obligation — both sides must transact at expiry. An option gives the holder the RIGHT but not the obligation. This asymmetry is the key difference and it's why options cost a premium while futures are theoretically free to enter.

The risk profiles are completely different. A futures position has linear P&L — you gain or lose dollar-for-dollar with the underlying (amplified by leverage). An option position has non-linear P&L — your maximum loss is the premium paid (if you're long), but your exposure changes as the underlying moves (that's gamma).

For risk management, futures are simpler to hedge (just offset the delta) but options require managing multiple dimensions of risk simultaneously — delta, gamma, vega, theta. That's why options books are more complex to risk-manage."
