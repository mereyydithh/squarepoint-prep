S — PRACTICAL RISK JUDGMENT

The difference between knowing risk and DOING risk

Depth: 🔴 DEEP

WHY THIS MATTERS

A bad risk manager knows every formula and says "no" to everything. A good risk manager enables PMs to take informed risk and adds value they can't get on their own. This domain is about the JUDGMENT layer on top of technical knowledge — the part that makes you useful on day 1.

PART 1: READING A RISK REPORT IN 5 MINUTES

What a Position Blotter Looks Like

When you sit down at your desk, you'll see something like this:

Name Direction Notional ($M) % NAV Sector Delta Gamma Vega P&L Today ($K) P&L MTD ($K)
AAPL Long 45 4.5% Tech 0.85 0.02 - -320 -850
XOM Long 80 8.0% Energy 1.00 - - +950 +2,100
SPX Jun 6500P Short 30 3.0% Index +0.35 -0.04 -180 -410 -600
TLT Long 25 2.5% Rates 1.00 - - -150 -400
BRL/USD Short 20 2.0% EM FX - - - +80 -200

What to Look For (in order of priority)

1. Concentration — what's the biggest position?
XOM at 8% of NAV is a large single-name. Is this within limits? What's the thesis? If oil reverses, this alone can cause a significant drawdown.

2. Direction — is the book net long, short, or neutral?
This book is clearly net long risk assets. Long equities, short puts (adding more long exposure via gamma), long bonds (small offset). What's the overall beta to the S&P?

3. Greeks aggregation — what's the portfolio-level exposure?
The short SPX puts are short gamma and short vega. If the market drops, delta on those puts increases (more negative), so the PM gets LONGER as the market falls. That's the dangerous profile.

4. Correlation — are "different" positions actually the same bet?
Long XOM (oil up is good) + short BRL/USD (oil up hurts Brazil if... wait, oil up typically helps BRL because Brazil is an exporter). Is this pair actually doubling up on the same bet or offsetting? You need to check.

5. P&L trajectory — is anything bleeding?
AAPL down $850K MTD. Is the thesis still intact? At what point does this trigger a drawdown conversation?

6. Hedge effectiveness — does the book have protection that works?
Long TLT is supposed to hedge equities — but in a stagflation environment, bonds and equities can sell off together. The TLT "hedge" might ADD to losses, not reduce them. This is a critical insight to flag.

The 5-Minute Scan Checklist

  • [ ] Any position > 5% NAV? → Concentration flag
  • [ ] What's the net delta / net beta? → Directional exposure
  • [ ] Any short gamma or short vega? → Acceleration risk
  • [ ] Are "hedges" actually hedging in the CURRENT regime? → Correlation check
  • [ ] Any position down > 10% from entry? → Thesis review trigger
  • [ ] Margin utilization? → Funding risk
  • [ ] Any positions in illiquid names? → Liquidity risk
  • [ ] What's the biggest single-day P&L scenario? → Stress test

PART 2: PRIORITIZING RISKS

The Urgency Matrix

Low probability High probability
High impact Tail risk — monitor with stress tests, have contingency plans. Don't ignore because "it's unlikely." TOP PRIORITY. Act now. Reduce, hedge, or get explicit IC approval.
Low impact Accept and move on. Don't waste time on this. Nuisance — manage with limits and routine checks.

What Gets Flagged and When

Situation Timing Action
Position breaches hard limit Immediate Escalate to IC. Reduction required. No debate.
Position approaching soft limit (80%+) Within the hour Talk to PM: "You're at 80% of your vol limit. Where do you want to be by close?"
Correlation spike across book Same-day analysis Run correlation decomposition. Present to PM: "Your diversification is breaking down — here's where."
Concentration in factor you didn't intend Next morning meeting "Your book looks like 5 positions but it's really 1 bet on [factor]. Is that intentional?"
PM wants to add to a losing position Before the trade "Walk me through the thesis again. What's changed since entry?"
New geopolitical event overnight Pre-market, before PM arrives Run stress scenarios, have exposure summary ready. PM walks in, you hand them one page.
Margin call Immediate Coordinate with treasury/ops. Identify which positions to trim for minimum portfolio impact.
Liquidity deterioration in held names Same day Estimate time-to-exit at various impact levels. Flag to PM if exit would take > 1 day for any material position.

Concrete Examples

Bad: "Your VaR is $12M against a $10M limit. You need to reduce."
Good: "Your VaR is $12M. The excess is coming from the short gamma on the SPX puts. If you roll the puts from 6500 to 6400 strike, VaR drops to $9.5M and the premium is similar. Alternatively, you could add a long put spread at 6200/6100 to cap your tail risk — that brings VaR to $10.2M at lower cost. What do you prefer?"

Bad: "Oil is up 8%. Here's the P&L."
Good: "Oil is up 8%. Your net P&L is +$900K, mostly from XOM. But your short puts are now deeper short gamma — if SPX drops 3% from here, the puts will add $15M to your long delta, which doubles your effective equity exposure at the worst time. I'd recommend adjusting the put strike or adding a long put to flatten the gamma near current SPX levels."

Bad: "The portfolio's realized vol is above target."
Good: "The portfolio's realized vol is running 30% above target. I decomposed it — 60% is from market vol increasing (we can't control that) and 40% is from concentration in XOM which is 3x more volatile than the rest of the book right now. If you want to bring vol back to target, trimming XOM by 25% gets you there. If you want to keep the position, we need IC sign-off on the elevated vol."

The Key Principle

Your job is not to minimize risk. Your job is to maximize INFORMED risk-taking. Every dollar of risk should be:
1. Intentional (the PM knows it's there)
2. Sized appropriately (within budget)
3. Understood at the portfolio level (not just the position level)
4. Compensated (the expected return justifies the risk)

PART 4: THE DAILY WORKFLOW

Pre-Market (7:00 - 9:00 AM)

  1. Overnight review: What moved? Check Asia close, Europe open, oil, rates, FX. Any gaps?
  2. Position check: Has anything moved enough overnight to change our risk profile materially?
  3. News scan: Geopolitical developments, data releases overnight, any company-specific news affecting positions.
  4. Stress test update: Re-run key scenarios with updated market levels. Refresh the numbers before the PM arrives.
  5. Prepare the morning snapshot: One page. Net delta, top 3 risk concentrations, overnight P&L, key events today.

Market Hours (9:30 AM - 4:00 PM)

  1. Real-time monitoring: Watch P&L, Greeks, limit utilization.
  2. Respond to PM requests: "Can I add $20M to this position?" → Run the numbers → "Here's the impact on portfolio VaR, concentration, and margin."
  3. Flag anomalies: Unusual moves, correlation breaks, liquidity changes.
  4. Scheduled data releases: CPI day, FOMC day, NFP day → pre-position the analysis, know what the consensus is, have scenarios for beat/miss.

Post-Close (4:00 - 6:00 PM)

  1. End-of-day risk report: Final P&L, P&L attribution (delta/gamma/vega/theta/residual), limit status.
  2. Review tomorrow's catalysts: Earnings, data releases, options expiry, geopolitical.
  3. Update scenarios: If market conditions changed today, re-run stress tests.
  4. Weekly review: Once a week, deeper dive — factor decomposition, correlation evolution, performance attribution (alpha vs beta).

PART 5: COMMUNICATION

How to Talk to a Trader

  • Lead with P&L impact. Not "your gamma is -0.04" but "if SPX drops 2%, your short gamma adds $12M to your long exposure and your P&L swing is -$3.5M."
  • Know their language. Delta, vega, P&L, levels, catalysts. Not "standard deviation of portfolio returns" but "your book moves $2M per 1% SPX move."
  • Time it right. Don't interrupt during a trade. Pre-market and post-close are your windows for deeper conversations. During market hours, keep it to alerts and quick answers.
  • Be definitive. Not "maybe we should think about reducing" but "I'd recommend reducing XOM by $15M. Here's why. Your call."
  • Pick your battles. Not every risk is worth flagging. If you cry wolf daily, you get tuned out. Flag the things that MATTER.

How to Push Back Without Being Annoying

  1. Start with their thesis, not your objection. "I understand the trade is [X] because you believe [Y]. I agree with the thesis — my concern is the sizing relative to the portfolio."
  2. Bring data, not opinions. "Under the March 2020 scenario, this position alone generates a $15M loss. Combined with the rest of the book, total drawdown is $22M which breaches your 5% limit."
  3. Offer alternatives. "Instead of doubling the position, what if we add a call spread to increase upside exposure with defined risk? You get 70% of the upside for 40% of the additional risk."
  4. Know when to escalate. If the PM disagrees and you believe there's genuine risk, escalate to the IC. Don't fight the trader — make the case to the committee with data.

How to Talk to Constantine (or any senior)

  • Be concise. He has 7+ junior RMs and 4+ seniors reporting to him. You get 5 minutes.
  • Structure it: Situation → Risk → Recommendation → Trade-offs. One paragraph, not a lecture.
  • Have the numbers ready. "PM X is at 4.2% drawdown against a 5% limit, concentrated in energy, short gamma via index puts. I recommend reducing the puts and trimming the energy position by 20%. PM disagrees — requesting IC review."
  • Show judgment, not just compliance. "Technically within limits but the combination of concentration + short gamma + stagflation tail risk makes me uncomfortable. I want visibility from you before we continue."

PART 6: RED FLAGS TO WATCH FOR

Position-Level Red Flags

Red flag What it means What to do
Position sizing > 5% NAV in a single name Concentration risk — one event can cause serious damage Review thesis, check if PM is aware of risk, flag to IC if excessive
Adding to a losing position with no new catalyst Loss aversion / averaging down on hope Ask PM to re-articulate thesis. If it's refitted, escalate.
Thesis has changed 3 times but position hasn't Post-hoc rationalization Document the shifting thesis. This is a behavioral red flag.
PM says "it has to come back" No catalyst, just hope Escalate. This is the most dangerous sentence in trading.
Stop-loss moved lower repeatedly Can't take the loss Same as above — discipline is breaking down
Correlation between position and hedge is deteriorating Hedge is becoming less effective Re-evaluate hedge. May need to adjust or replace.

Portfolio-Level Red Flags

Red flag What it means What to do
All positions profitable in the same way Hidden factor concentration Run factor decomposition. The book is one bet.
Vol running 30%+ above target for > 1 week Persistent risk overweight Decompose: market vol vs position-specific. Recommend action.
More than 3 positions past their original time horizon Thesis drift across the book Review each. Dead positions tie up risk budget.
Margin utilization > 80% No buffer for a margin call Flag. One bad day forces liquidation at worst possible time.
P&L attribution shows growing "unexplained" residual Model is missing something Investigate. Could be cross-gamma, correlation, or data error.
