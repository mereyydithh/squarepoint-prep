Y — PROBE DEFENSE GUIDE

Every claim they'll probe, every follow-up they'll ask, every trap they'll set

Depth: 🔴 DEEP

HOW TO USE THIS FILE

Interviewers at Squarepoint don't ask surface-level questions. They PROBE. Every claim you make in your walkthrough will get a follow-up — sometimes two or three levels deep. This file prepares you for exactly that.

The probe chain: You say something → they ask "tell me more" → you go one level deeper → they ask "and then what?" → you give the technical detail that proves you actually did it.

Rule of three depths:
1. Surface — the claim itself (your walkthrough)
2. Mechanism — HOW it worked (the follow-up)
3. Edge case — what went WRONG or what was HARD (the killer probe)

If you can answer at depth 3, they believe you. If you stall at depth 2, they doubt you.

CLAIM: "BNP Paris — OTC commodities desk, priced Asian/European options"

Probe Answer
"Explain an Asian option" "Average-price option — payoff based on the arithmetic average of the underlying over the option's life, not the spot at expiry. Corporates love them for hedging because they smooth out price spikes. Harder to price than vanilla: no closed-form under GBM because the average of log-normals isn't log-normal. We used Turnbull-Wakeman approximation and Monte Carlo."
"How does Turnbull-Wakeman work?" "Moment-matching approximation. The key insight: while the arithmetic average of log-normals isn't log-normal, you can fit a log-normal distribution that matches the first two moments of the true distribution. Compute the mean and variance of the arithmetic average analytically under GBM, then find the log-normal parameters (μ, σ) with the same mean and variance. Plug those into the standard Black-Scholes formula. Fast and reasonably accurate for typical corporate hedging structures. For exotic payoffs or when accuracy matters more than speed, you use Monte Carlo with antithetic variates for variance reduction."
"What's the difference between Asian fixed strike and Asian floating strike?" "Fixed strike (average price): payoff = max(A − K, 0) where A is the average spot. This is what most corporates use — they want to lock in an average purchase price over a year. Floating strike (average strike): payoff = max(S_T − A, 0) where A is the average and S_T is the final spot. This pays if the final spot exceeds the average — useful for capturing momentum while partially hedging. Risk implication: floating strike options have different gamma and delta profiles because both the reference level (A) and the terminal value (S_T) are uncertain."
"What commodities specifically?" "Full spectrum: crude oil (Brent, WTI), refined products (jet fuel, gasoil, naphtha), natural gas (TTF, Henry Hub), power (baseload, peak), emissions (EUAs, CERs), base metals (copper, aluminum), ags (wheat, corn). Each has unique pricing conventions, seasonality, and liquidity profiles."
"What's the difference between pricing for corporates vs. trading?" "Corporate pricing adds credit risk (CVA), wider bid-ask for illiquidity, and bespoke structures. An airline wanting jet fuel protection needs an Asian call with monthly averaging, specific grade, specific delivery point. That's 3 layers of basis risk on top of vanilla. When I quoted these, I had to decompose into: flat price risk + time spread + crack spread + location basis + credit charge."
"How did you handle a mispricing?" "Once I noticed my jet fuel option quote was 8% cheaper than Bloomberg OVML. Traced it to a stale vol surface — our internal vol hadn't updated for a supply disruption in the Strait of Hormuz that spiked short-dated vols. I flagged it before the quote went out, updated the surface, re-ran. The corrected quote was in line with the market. Lesson: always cross-check with at least two sources."
"Tell me about the Suez Canal Crisis pricing" "The Ever Given grounding in March 2021 jammed the Suez Canal for six days — ~12% of global trade transits Suez — and the aftershocks reshaped how corporates thought about supply-chain hedging for the next two years. By the time I was on the desk in 2023, clients were actively requesting freight-contingent structures they'd never asked for before. The risk: a European refiner hedged on Brent CIF Rotterdam could still get caught by a delivery delay or freight spike that the Brent hedge didn't cover. We priced bespoke structures: Asian options on freight indices (Baltic Clean Tanker Index, BCTI) layered with crude oil swaps to create integrated supply-chain hedges. The challenge was pricing correlation between freight and crude — historically low correlation (~0.2), but it spiked during supply-chain disruptions because both are driven by the same physical bottleneck. With Red Sea/Hormuz tensions still elevated in mid-2023, the structures were in real demand."
"What are freight indices and how do you price them?" "Baltic Exchange publishes indices for different vessel types: BDI (Dry bulk — iron ore, coal, grain), BDTI (Dirty Tanker — crude), BCTI (Clean Tanker — refined products). They're assessment-based — a panel of brokers submits daily route-by-route freight rate quotes, and the exchange computes a weighted average. To price freight derivatives (FFAs — Forward Freight Agreements, and options on FFAs): the underlyings are these indices. Pricing uses the same framework as commodity options except: (1) freight has extreme seasonality (Q4 grain season, pre-Chinese New Year demand), (2) mean reversion is strong because supply (ships) responds to high rates with a 2-3 year lag, (3) liquidity is thin — wide bid-ask, so you embed a liquidity premium. The challenge during my time on the desk: elevated geopolitical risk meant our vol surface, calibrated to calmer periods, understated the potential for sudden freight spikes. We added scenario weighting — incorporating different supply-disruption durations — to produce more realistic premiums for clients seeking physical delivery hedges."

TF-IDF alone isn't enough — domain-specific weighting matters more than statistical frequency."

"Two actionable ones: (1) A European steel client covered by the metals desk was spending €20M/year on EUA allowances but hadn't been approached by the emissions trading team. Connected the desks — led to a structured collar on EUA exposure. (2) Three airline clients with active jet fuel hedging were entering voluntary carbon offset markets but buying spot with no hedging structure. Identified them for the commodities sales team to pitch Asian-style averaging on carbon offsets."
"What were the limitations?" "Three. First: keyword scoring is crude — it can't distinguish genuine climate transition from greenwashing. Sentiment analysis or NER with context windows would be more accurate. Second: annual reports are backward-looking — by the time you read the 2022 report, the company may have already found a broker. Third: I only processed English-language reports. French and German corporates often publish in local languages first — a production version would need multilingual parsing."

Feed it the annual report, ask it to extract every reference to carbon exposure, physical risk, transition risk, and hedging activity. Then classify by commercial opportunity type. But in 2023, the keyword approach was pragmatic, fast to build, and delivered actionable results within 2 weeks."

CLAIM: "Live stress-testing model — position-level granularity on risk drivers"

Probe Answer
"Walk me through how it worked" "Input: position data from the booking system + market data (curves, vol surfaces). The model applied shocks to each risk factor independently and combined: (1) flat price move (±10%, ±20%, ±30%), (2) curve shape (flattening/steepening), (3) vol shift (±25%, ±50%), (4) basis moves (location, grade). For each scenario, it recalculated position-level P&L using first-order sensitivities (delta, vega, DV01) plus second-order (gamma, vanna) for options. Output: a matrix of scenarios × positions with aggregate and drill-down views."
"Why position-level granularity?" "Because aggregate numbers hide risk. A portfolio can show zero net delta but be long crude and short nat gas — those are completely different markets. The Constellation-Calpine merger showed this: aggregate exposure looked manageable, but position-level showed concentrated basis risk between power regions. Without granularity, you'd miss it."
"What technology?" "Python. Pandas for data manipulation, numpy for vectorized calculations, Bloomberg API for market data. Excel output for traders (their preferred interface). Automated via Windows Task Scheduler — ran every 30 minutes during trading hours."
"What was the biggest stress test that actually flagged a real risk?" "Liberation Day — April 2, 2025. Trump announced sweeping reciprocal tariffs: 10% baseline on all imports, 34% on China, 20% on the EU. Markets went into freefall — S&P dropped ~5% on April 3 alone and kept falling, bond yields whipsawed as recession fears collided with inflation concerns, and commodity basis spreads blew out. My stress model flagged something the aggregate numbers missed: clients with cross-border commodity hedges — aluminum, steel, copper — had basis risk between US-delivered and international-reference prices that the tariffs suddenly made real. A 25% tariff on aluminum means the US price diverges from LME, so a client hedged on LME but delivering in the US is now short a basis that didn't exist a week ago. Position-level view showed several clients with concentrated exposure to this new tariff-driven basis. We flagged it the morning of April 3, traders reached out to affected clients to restructure hedges before the April 9 escalation. Avoided ~$3M in mark-to-market losses across the book."
"How did you validate the model?" "Three approaches: (1) back-test against known historical scenarios — replay COVID March 2020 market moves and compare model output to actual P&L (within 5%). (2) Cross-check against Bloomberg MARS risk system for a subset of positions. (3) Sensitivity analysis — perturb one input at a time and verify the output moved in the expected direction and magnitude."

CLAIM: "$2.6B default fire drill — 9 counterparties — <0.2% P&L drag"

Probe Answer
"Why $2.6B specifically?" "It was our top 3 clients by notional — combined exposure. We chose them because a simultaneous default of our largest exposures would be the worst-case scenario. The portfolio was long natural gas, short power and carbon — correlated positions that would be hardest to unwind because both sides need to be liquidated in illiquid markets simultaneously."
"Walk me through the liquidation sequence" "Phase 1 — Inventory: mapped every position, every counterparty, every margin agreement. Built a complete position waterfall. Phase 2 — Liquidity assessment: ranked positions by market liquidity × position size. Most liquid first (front-month gas futures → orderly unwind), least liquid last (illiquid power basis swaps → might need to novate). Phase 3 — Counterparty coordination: contacted 9 banks and trading houses. Negotiated indicative bids for the illiquid positions. Tested CCP margin calls for the cleared portion. Phase 4 — Execution simulation: ran the full unwind over simulated 5 business days. Tracked slippage at each step. Result: 0.2% total P&L drag, which was well within our tolerance."
"What were the 2 operational gaps?" "First: one counterparty's ISDA had an ambiguous close-out netting clause for cross-commodity positions. In a real default, we might have disputed whether nat gas and power positions could net. Legal drafted a clarification. Second: margin call timing — one CCP would have required initial margin top-up within 1 hour of the default declaration, but our treasury process assumed 4 hours. We pre-positioned cash to cover the gap."
"How is this different from a real default?" "Three differences: (1) no market impact — in a real default, the market knows and prices adjust against you as you liquidate. Our simulation used mid-market prices, so real drag would be higher. (2) No legal uncertainty — real defaults involve lawyers, disputes, stays on close-out netting. (3) No cascade risk — a real default of $2.6B clients might trigger margin calls on OTHER counterparties. We modeled the direct impact, not the systemic contagion."
"What would you do differently?" "I'd add market impact modeling — estimate how our liquidation flow would move prices and include that in the P&L drag estimate. Probably doubles the drag from 0.2% to 0.4-0.5%. I'd also run the drill with staggered timing — what if the default happens during Asian hours when we can't reach US counterparties? Worst-case timing matters."

CLAIM: "SOFR/UST options skew scanner — for traders"

Probe Answer
"What exactly did the scanner produce?" "A daily report showing: (1) by strike and maturity — open interest, day-over-day OI change, implied vol, delta, gamma. (2) Net dealer gamma positioning — aggregated by strike bucket. (3) Skew metrics — 25-delta risk reversal, butterfly, and their day-over-day changes. (4) Heatmap of gamma concentration across the strike/maturity surface. Traders used it to identify: where is dealer hedging activity concentrated? Where is the 'gamma pin' — the strike where dealer gamma flips from long to short?"
"What is the gamma pin?" "The strike where aggregate dealer gamma is zero. Above the pin, dealers are net long gamma (hedging suppresses vol — they sell into rallies, buy dips, dampening moves). Below the pin, dealers are net short gamma (hedging amplifies vol — they're forced to sell into declines and buy into rallies, reinforcing moves). The danger zone is when the underlying crosses the pin from long-gamma into short-gamma territory — vol regime shifts abruptly. The scanner tracked where this level was daily. Traders used it to time entries around FOMC meetings — avoided selling vol when the underlying was near or below the pin."
"Why SOFR specifically?" "Post-LIBOR transition, SOFR options were growing rapidly but market structure was still maturing. Pricing conventions were evolving, OI was shifting from Eurodollar legacy to SOFR. Traders needed visibility into this transition — where was liquidity building, where were positioning imbalances. The scanner was an edge because most desks were still manually tracking this."
"How did you compute net dealer gamma?" "Assumptions: (1) assume dealers are generally short options (they sell to clients who buy protection). (2) Use CFTC Commitments of Traders to get dealer vs. non-dealer split. (3) For each option, compute gamma × OI × contract multiplier. (4) Aggregate by strike bucket. This is an approximation — you can't know exact dealer positioning — but directionally useful. I validated against periods where gamma effects were visible in price action (e.g., options expiry pinning)."

CLAIM: "Python + SQL back-test engine on AWS — 1M trades/day, 50% faster"

Probe Answer
"Walk me through the architecture" "Three layers. Data layer: PostgreSQL on AWS RDS storing trade history across 4 asset classes — IR, commodities, FX, equities. Ingestion from the booking system via nightly ETL pipeline. Processing layer: Python engine (pandas + numpy) on EC2 instances — reads trade populations, replays them against historical market data, computes P&L, Greeks, and risk metrics at each time step. Output layer: results written back to RDS and surfaced in dashboards for the risk team and for VaR backtesting reports."
"What were you back-testing?" "Risk model accuracy. Core question: when our VaR model predicted a $5M loss, how often did actual losses exceed that? We replayed 1M trades/day against 2 years of historical data — computing daily hypothetical P&L vs. model-predicted P&L. This feeds the VaR backtesting framework (Kupiec and Christoffersen tests). Also used it for stress-test validation: replay COVID March 2020 and compare model output to realized P&L to make sure our stress scenarios were calibrated correctly."
"How did you cut processing time 50%?" "Three optimizations. First: vectorized the P&L calculations — the legacy version looped trade-by-trade in Python. I restructured to compute entire trade populations as numpy arrays. That gave ~30% improvement. Second: partitioned SQL queries by asset class so each ran in parallel on AWS — instead of one monolithic query, four concurrent queries each pulling ~250K trades. Third: cached static data (contract specs, holiday calendars, curve bootstrapping parameters) instead of recomputing every run. The second and third combined gave the remaining ~20%."
"Why AWS instead of on-prem?" "Scalability. On-prem had fixed compute — during quarter-end when we needed more scenarios, jobs queued for hours. AWS let us spin up additional EC2 instances for peak loads and scale back down. Also: multiple analysts needed to run backtests simultaneously. Compliance had strict requirements on data residency and encryption-at-rest, so we used VPC with no public endpoints and KMS encryption. All data stayed in the us-east-1 region."
"What were the limitations?" "Two. First: the engine used first-order sensitivities (delta, vega) for P&L approximation. For options with high gamma or vanna, approximation error grows for large market moves. Full-revaluation would be more accurate but 10x slower — we made the speed/accuracy tradeoff explicitly and documented it. Second: we assumed market data was clean. I built a simple staleness check (flag if a price is >2 days old) but a proper data quality framework with automatic fallback interpolation would have been better."
"What did you learn from building this?" "Two things. First: the biggest performance gains came from algorithm design (vectorization), not infrastructure (more compute). Engineers often reach for more hardware first — I learned to profile first and optimize the code. Second: building for production is fundamentally different from building a prototype. I had to add logging, error handling, automated alerts for failed runs, and documentation — none of which exist in a notebook. That discipline carried into every production system I've built since."

CLAIM: "Cross-asset quant — FRTB, SABR calibration, correlation monitoring"

Probe Answer + Bridge
"What is FRTB?" "Fundamental Review of the Trading Book — Basel III.1 framework replacing the current market risk capital rules. Two approaches: Internal Models Approach (IMA) and Standardised Approach (SA). Key changes: P&L attribution test (PLAT) determines if your desk can use IMA. Non-modellable risk factors (NMRFs) get punitive capital charges. Default risk charge replaces incremental risk charge. I worked on PLAT — ensuring the risk model P&L matches hypothetical P&L within thresholds. But honestly, the most impactful risk work I've done was at BNP NYC where it directly shaped trading decisions."
"What are the PLAT thresholds specifically?" "PLAT compares risk-theoretical P&L (from the risk model) against hypothetical P&L (full revaluation) daily. Two metrics: Spearman correlation and a ratio test. Green zone: pass both. Amber: fail one metric on no more than 20% of days over 12 months. Red: fail more — desk loses IMA status and must use the Standardised Approach, which typically means 2-4x higher capital requirements. The practical implication: if your vol surface RMSE is 45bps instead of 30bps, your Greeks are wrong, your risk-theoretical P&L drifts from hypothetical, and you risk slipping into amber or red. That's why the calibration improvement mattered beyond just technical accuracy."
"What are NMRFs?" "Non-modellable risk factors — risk factors that don't have enough observable market data to be included in the IMA model. FRTB requires at least 24 verifiable prices over a 12-month period (roughly 2 per month). If a risk factor fails this test — illiquid vol surfaces, exotic basis spreads, long-dated commodity curves — it's classified as NMRF and gets a stress scenario capital add-on rather than the model-based ES charge. NMRFs are often more punitive than the model charge because the stress scenario is floored at a conservative level. I identified several NMRFs in our exotic rates book and worked on improving data sourcing to reclassify them as modellable."
"Explain SABR" "Stochastic Alpha Beta Rho — a stochastic vol model for interest rate options. Four parameters: α (initial vol level), β (CEV backbone — fixed at 0.5 for rates, ~1.0 for equities), ρ (correlation between forward rate and vol — controls skew direction), ν (vol-of-vol — controls smile curvature/wings). Calibrated by minimizing squared error between SABR-implied vols and market swaption vols using Levenberg-Marquardt. β is fixed before calibration — it's a structural choice about how vol scales with the rate level. We achieved 15bps RMSE improvement over the legacy model. The calibration is interesting technically, but what I'm more passionate about is using models to inform risk decisions — which is what the Squarepoint role is about."
"What's the 15bps improvement mean?" "Legacy model had 45bps RMSE against market swaption vols. After SABR recalibration: 30bps. That matters for two reasons: (1) more accurate Greek calculations — delta and vega are derivatives of the vol surface, so a misfit propagates into hedging. (2) Better P&L predict — the PLAT test requires model P&L to match within tight bands, so surface quality directly impacts whether the desk keeps IMA status and avoids punitive SA capital charges."
"What are SABR's limitations?" "Three. First: calibrated per expiry independently — you fit one set of parameters to the 1-month smile, a different set to the 3-month smile. There's no single model fitting the full surface simultaneously, which means the term structure of vol can be inconsistent. Second: the Hagan approximation formula breaks down for deep OTM strikes — it can produce arbitrage violations (smile turns down in the wings → negative probability density). Third: the original model allows negative rates only in limited ways — post-2015 when EUR rates went negative, you needed Free Boundary SABR which reparameterizes the forward to handle sub-zero levels. I worked on Free Boundary SABR adaptations at Natixis for EUR swaptions."
"Why SABR over Heston for rates?" "Speed. SABR gives a closed-form analytical approximation for implied vol at any strike — you can reprice the entire swaption grid in milliseconds. Heston requires numerical integration (characteristic function + Fourier inversion) — more accurate dynamics but much slower. For a rates desk pricing hundreds of swaptions across a full strike-maturity grid in real time, SABR's analytical speed makes it the industry standard. Heston is better for equity exotics where you need better forward skew dynamics and speed is less critical."
"What do you mean by Gold-UST decoupling?" "Historically gold and US Treasury yields have -0.3 to -0.5 correlation — when yields fall, gold rises (both are safe havens but gold benefits from lower opportunity cost). In Q4 2025, this flipped positive — a 3-sigma event using a 10-year lookback. Root cause: central bank gold buying by China, Russia, Middle East (de-dollarization) creating structural demand independent of yields. Chow breakpoint test confirmed structural break at September 2025. We adjusted correlation assumptions in the risk model — without the fix, cross-hedge effectiveness was overstated by ~15%."
"What is the Sensitivity-Based Method (SBM)?" "SBM is the core capital charge under FRTB-SA. Three components: (1) Delta risk charge — sensitivity of each position to underlying risk factors (IR curves, FX rates, equity spot, commodity prices), aggregated with prescribed correlation matrices across buckets. (2) Vega risk charge — sensitivity to implied vol at each maturity point, same bucket-correlation aggregation. (3) Curvature risk charge — captures non-linear (gamma-like) risk by computing P&L under upward and downward shocks to each risk factor and taking the worst-case net. The three charges are computed independently and summed. No diversification benefit between delta, vega, and curvature — that's a conservatism the regulators built in deliberately."
"How did you recalibrate SBM charges?" "Three workstreams. First: sensitivity calculation accuracy — our existing Greeks pipeline was computing delta per trade but not mapping to the regulatory risk factor taxonomy. FRTB-SA requires sensitivities to SPECIFIC buckets (e.g., IR delta must be decomposed into tenors: 0.25Y, 0.5Y, 1Y, 2Y, 3Y, 5Y, 10Y, 15Y, 20Y, 30Y). I rebuilt the mapping from trade-level Greeks to regulatory tenor buckets. Second: correlation parameters — SA prescribes three correlation scenarios (low, medium, high) and you take the max charge. I validated that our implementation correctly applied all three scenarios and identified a bug where the medium scenario was double-counting cross-bucket correlations for commodities. Third: curvature charge — the legacy implementation used a flat ±1% shock for all risk factors, but FRTB-SA prescribes risk-class-specific shock sizes. I corrected the shock calibration. PART 6: TECHNICAL PROBES — QUANT & RISK

Greeks

Probe Answer
"What is gamma risk?" "Rate of change of delta. Long gamma: delta moves in your favor as underlying moves (buy low, sell high naturally). Short gamma: delta moves against you (you're forced to buy high, sell low to re-hedge). Most dangerous near expiry, ATM, because gamma spikes — small price moves cause large delta changes. A short gamma position near a pin strike on expiry day is one of the scariest things in options."
"Explain vanna" "Cross-Greek: sensitivity of delta to vol (or equivalently, sensitivity of vega to spot). Matters for risk reversals and skewed books. If you're long calls and short puts, vanna means your delta changes as vol changes. In a crash: vol spikes → delta shifts → you're suddenly more directional than you thought. Vanna is why 'delta-hedged' positions can still lose money in vol events."
"How would you hedge a book that's short gamma and long vega?" "This is a real position — short near-term options (giving gamma risk) and long far-dated options (giving vega). The risk: a quiet market hurts (theta on longs, no gamma benefit) but a sudden vol spike helps (long vega outperforms). I'd hedge the gamma with shorter-dated at-the-money options or delta-hedge more frequently — accepting the cost. I'd consider: is the vega worth the gamma risk? If the term structure is steep enough, the carry is positive. If not, I'd reduce the vega position and manage gamma separately."
"What is volga?" "Volga (also called vomma) is the second derivative of option price with respect to implied vol — or equivalently, the rate of change of vega as vol changes: ∂²V/∂σ². It measures convexity of the option price in vol space. High volga means your vega isn't stable — as vol moves, your vega exposure changes significantly. Deep OTM options have high volga because they're on the steep part of the vol smile. A long straddle is long volga (vol spike increases vega). Short gamma books that are also short OTM options can be surprise short volga — when vol spikes, their vega exposure increases in the wrong direction."
"What about theta — is it constant?" "No — theta is highly non-linear. ATM options lose theta at roughly √T pace: an option with 4 days to expiry loses ~2x more theta per day than one with 16 days (1/√16 vs 1/√4). Near expiry, theta accelerates sharply for ATM options — this is the 'theta bleed' traders watch on Fridays for Monday expiry. For deep OTM options, theta is actually small near expiry because the option has little time value left to decay. The worst theta position: short an ATM option approaching expiry in a quiet market — you're paying theta from your short with no gamma gains to offset it."

Derivatives Pricing & Stochastic Calculus

Probe Answer
"Derive Black-Scholes intuitively" "Three assumptions: (1) log-normal stock price — returns are normally distributed, prices can't go negative. (2) Continuous delta hedging — you can rebalance at any instant. (3) No arbitrage. The key insight: a perfectly delta-hedged portfolio (long option, short delta shares) is instantaneously riskless. A riskless portfolio must earn the risk-free rate. Write down the portfolio's P&L using Itô's lemma (which gives the option's change in value for a stochastic process), set it equal to the risk-free return, and you get the Black-Scholes PDE. The closed-form solution is the N(d1), N(d2) formula."
"What is Itô's lemma?" "The chain rule for stochastic processes. If X follows Brownian motion (dX = μdt + σdW), and f is a function of X and t, then df has an extra term compared to ordinary calculus: df = (∂f/∂t + μ∂f/∂X + ½σ²∂²f/∂X²)dt + σ∂f/∂X dW. The extra ½σ²∂²f/∂X² term is the Itô correction — it appears because Brownian motion has non-zero quadratic variation (dW² = dt). This term is exactly gamma in options pricing — it's why gamma matters. Without Itô's correction, Black-Scholes would be wrong."
"What does risk-neutral pricing mean?" "Instead of using real-world probabilities (where stocks earn risk premiums), we price derivatives under the risk-neutral measure — a mathematical probability space where every asset earns the risk-free rate. This lets us discount expected payoffs at r instead of dealing with risk premiums we can't observe. The real-world drift disappears from the pricing formula — this is why Black-Scholes doesn't need expected return as an input. It seems magical but it's just a change of measure: any derivative price consistent with no-arbitrage can be computed as E^Q[payoff × discount factor] where Q is this artificial risk-neutral measure."
"What's the difference between delta hedging and dynamic replication?" "Same thing, different framing. Delta hedging: you short Δ shares to offset the option's directional exposure. Dynamic replication: you build a portfolio of stock and bond that replicates the option's payoff at expiry. The replication portfolio at any point is (Δ shares) + (cash to make the values match). Rebalance continuously and you've replicated the option. Delta hedging IS the continuous rebalancing of this replication portfolio. The cost of replication is the option premium — which is why BS is both a pricing formula and a hedging instruction."

DV01 & Duration

Probe Answer
"What is DV01?" "Dollar Value of 1 basis point — the change in a bond's price for a 1bp move in yield. DV01 = Modified Duration × Price × 0.0001. Example: a 10Y bond with modified duration 8, price $100, face $1M has DV01 = 8 × $1M × 0.0001 = $800. So for every 1bp rates move, I gain or lose $800. DV01 is additive across positions — aggregate DV01 of a rates book tells you the total dollar sensitivity to parallel yield shifts."
"Duration vs modified duration vs convexity?" "Macaulay duration: weighted average time to receive cash flows (in years). Modified duration: Macaulay / (1 + y/m) — the price sensitivity to yield (% change per unit yield change). Convexity: the curvature correction. For a 100bp move, price change ≈ −ModDur × Δy + ½ × Convexity × (Δy)². Duration alone overestimates losses and underestimates gains for large moves. High convexity is good — the price falls less and rises more than duration predicts."
"What is negative convexity?" "Callable bonds and mortgage-backed securities exhibit negative convexity in certain yield environments. When yields fall, the issuer calls the bond (refinances) — you get par back just when you want to keep the high-coupon bond. So instead of price rising more than duration predicts (positive convexity), the price is capped by the call feature. MBS have the same problem: homeowners prepay when rates fall. You're effectively short a call option on the bond, and that short option position creates negative convexity. For an RM, this means VaR underestimates downside for MBS portfolios in falling rate environments."

VaR & Tail Risk

Probe Answer
"VaR says $5M at 99%. What does that actually mean?" "On 99 out of 100 trading days, we expect to lose LESS than $5M. It says nothing about the 1 day we exceed it — that could be $6M or $60M. That's VaR's fundamental limitation: it tells you the boundary of normal losses, not the shape of the tail."
"When does VaR fail?" "Four situations: (1) regime change — vol regime shifts make historical lookback irrelevant. (2) Fat tails — 99% VaR assumes the worst 1% is 'beyond the model' but the gap between VaR and actual tail can be enormous. (3) Correlation breakdown — diversification benefits assumed in parametric VaR evaporate when correlations spike to 1.0 in a crisis. (4) Illiquidity — VaR assumes you can exit positions at mark, but in a crisis you can't."
"What's better than VaR?" "Expected Shortfall (CVaR) — the average loss BEYOND VaR. It captures tail shape. ES of $8M at 99% means: in that worst 1%, the AVERAGE loss is $8M. If ES/VaR ratio is high (>1.5), the tail is fat. Complemented with stress tests for specific scenarios and reverse stress tests for 'what breaks us?'"
"How do you backtest VaR?" "Count exceptions: over 250 days at 99%, expect 2.5 breaches. Basel traffic light: 0-4 green, 5-9 amber, 10+ red. But also Kupiec test (are exceptions the right frequency?) and Christoffersen test (are exceptions clustered? — clustering means your model misses vol regimes). If I see 3 breaches in one week, that's worse than 6 spread across a year — even though the count is lower."

Correlation

Probe Answer
"How do you monitor correlation?" "Three timeframes: (1) long-term (3-5 year) structural correlation for model calibration. (2) Rolling 60-day for regime detection — if rolling deviates from long-term by more than 2 standard deviations, flag it. (3) Realized vs. implied — if implied correlation (from index options) diverges from realized, someone's mispriced. Also: Chow breakpoint tests for structural breaks and DCC-GARCH for time-varying correlation."
"Why do correlations spike in a crisis?" "Three mechanisms: (1) Contagion — losses force selling across asset classes (margin calls don't discriminate). (2) Information cascade — uncertainty makes everyone react to the same headlines. (3) Liquidity withdrawal — market makers pull back, so the same selling pressure moves everything. In 2008, equity-credit correlation went from 0.3 to 0.9 within weeks. Portfolio diversification based on normal-state correlations dramatically underestimated tail risk."

  4. "How would you implement VaR at Squarepoint?" → "Historical simulation as primary — 500-day lookback, daily reset. Monte Carlo with Cholesky as secondary for multi-asset correlation. Parametric as sanity check. Backtest weekly using Kupiec and Christoffersen."
