A — CASH INSTRUMENTS CHEAT SHEET

ONE-LINERS

Concept One-liner
Stock An ownership claim on a company's residual cash flows — you get what's left after everyone else has been paid.
Bond A loan that you can trade — the issuer borrows money, pays you regular interest, and returns principal at maturity.
FX rate The price of one currency in terms of another — EUR/USD = 1.08 means one euro costs $1.08.
Commodity A standardized physical good (oil, gold, wheat) traded on exchanges, mostly via futures rather than physical delivery.
Credit The risk that a borrower won't pay you back — traded via corporate bonds (cash) and credit default swaps (synthetic).
Duration How much your bond's price changes for a 1% move in rates — duration 7 means ~7% loss per 1% rate rise.
Convexity The curvature of the price-yield relationship — tells you how inaccurate your duration estimate will be for large moves.
Credit spread The extra yield a corporate bond pays above the risk-free Treasury rate — it's the price tag of default risk.
Carry trade Borrow in a low-rate currency, invest in a high-rate currency, pocket the difference — works until a violent reversal.
CDS An insurance contract — buyer pays a regular premium and gets paid if the reference company defaults.
Crowding Too many funds own the same stocks — works until everyone tries to exit through the same door at the same time.
Contango Front month cheaper than later months — storage costs make future delivery more expensive. Negative roll yield.
Backwardation Front month more expensive than later months — tight supply now creates a scarcity premium. Positive roll yield.
Yield curve Plots interest rates by maturity — tells you what the market believes about growth, inflation, and policy.

EQUITY PRICING

Master equation: Price = Earnings × Multiple

Valuation Method What it does Limitation
Relative (P/E, EV/EBITDA) Compare to peers/history "Cheap vs expensive" is relative, not absolute
DCF Discount future cash flows at WACC 60-80% of value comes from terminal value — tiny assumption changes → huge output swings
Sum-of-parts Value each segment separately Ignores synergies

Metric Formula Meaning Trap
P/E Price / EPS Years of earnings you're paying for Useless for negative earnings
Forward P/E Price / Next yr EPS Paying for future earnings Only as good as the estimate
EV/EBITDA (Mkt cap + Debt - Cash) / EBITDA Price of operating business regardless of leverage Ignores capex
FCF Yield FCF / Mkt cap Cash return on investment. Higher = cheaper Can be manipulated

Measure Use case

DURATION TYPES

Type What it measures
Macaulay Weighted-average time to receive cash flows (years)
Modified Price sensitivity: ΔP/P ≈ -D × Δy
Effective For bonds with embedded options (MBS, callables)
Key rate (KRD) Sensitivity to specific curve points (2yr, 5yr, 10yr, 30yr)

What increases duration: Longer maturity, lower coupon, lower yield, zero coupon (duration = maturity).

DV01 = Duration × Market Value × 0.0001 → dollar loss per 1bp rate rise.

BOOTSTRAPPING — BUILDING THE YIELD CURVE

Extract zero-coupon (spot) rates from observed market prices, one maturity at a time, each using previously solved rates.

Maturity Instrument Method
O/N – 6M T-bills, SOFR, repo Direct observation (zero-coupon → yield IS the spot rate)
6M – 2Y SOFR futures (SR3) Futures price → forward rate → compound to spot rate
2Y – 30Y Interest rate swaps Set PV(fixed leg) = 1, solve for unknown spot rate using known shorter rates

The bootstrap logic: For an n-year swap at par rate $c$:
- Discount each coupon by the spot rates you already know ($z_1, z_2, ..., z_{n-1}$)
- Solve for $z_n$ — the only unknown
- Repeat for each successively longer maturity

What you get from spot rates:
- Discount factors: $DF(T) = 1/(1+z_T)^T$ — price ANY fixed cash flow
- Forward rates: Market-implied rate between two future dates
- Par rates: Coupon at which a bond prices at 100 (= the swap curve)

Post-2008: Multi-curve bootstrapping — SOFR for discounting, separate curve for projection. Two curves, not one.

RM relevance: Every derivative is priced off this curve. Different interpolation methods → different forward rates → different derivative prices and hedge ratios.

CREDIT RATINGS & SPREADS

Rating Spread (bps) Default risk
AAA/AA 20-60 Negligible — spread is mostly liquidity premium
A 60-120 Very low
BBB 120-200 Cliff edge — downgrade to BB triggers forced selling
BB 200-400 Junk. First to blow out in stress.
B 400-700 Significant default probability
CCC+ 700+ Distressed. Trades on recovery value.

IG vs HY: IG = rate bet. HY = equity bet in a bond wrapper. Don't confuse them.

CDS MECHANICS

  • Buy protection: Pay spread quarterly. If default → receive (par - recovery).
  • Sell protection: Collect spread. Bear default risk. = Being long the credit.
  • Spread widening = more default risk = bearish.
  • CDS-bond basis = CDS spread minus cash bond spread. Large gap = liquidity stress or structural issue.

Example: 5yr CDS at 200bps on $10M notional → buyer pays $200K/yr. If default with 40% recovery → buyer receives $6M.
