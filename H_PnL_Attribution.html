H — P&L ATTRIBUTION

Decomposing daily P&L: where did the money come from (or go)?

Depth: 🟡 WELL

WHY THIS MATTERS

Every morning, the RM looks at yesterday's P&L. The number alone is useless. What matters is WHY.

  • PM is down $2M. Is it because their thesis was wrong (delta loss), or because vol spiked (vega), or because time passed (theta)?
  • These have completely different implications. A delta loss means the market moved against the view. A vega loss means the cost of optionality changed. A theta loss is expected — that's the price of holding options.

If you can't decompose P&L, you can't diagnose risk.

PART 1: THE FRAMEWORK

Daily P&L breaks down into components. For a portfolio with equities and options:

Total P&L = Delta P&L + Gamma P&L + Theta P&L + Vega P&L + Rho P&L + Cross Effects + Unexplained

Delta P&L

What it is: The P&L from the underlying moving. If you're long 100 shares of AAPL and it goes up $2, your delta P&L is +$200.

Formula: Δ P&L = Σ (Delta × ΔS × Multiplier)

For a portfolio: sum across all positions.

Why it matters: This is the "directional bet." If delta P&L dominates, the PM's performance is driven by market direction, not skill.

Gamma P&L

What it is: The P&L from convexity — the delta changing as the underlying moves. If you're long gamma (long options), big moves help you. If you're short gamma (short options), big moves hurt you.

Formula: Γ P&L = ½ × Gamma × (ΔS)²

Why it matters:
- Gamma P&L is always positive for long gamma (regardless of direction). Big up move? Win. Big down move? Also win.
- Short gamma: opposite. Small moves = ok (collect theta). Big moves = painful losses that accelerate.
- This is the "insurance" component — long gamma costs theta but protects in tail events.

Theta P&L

What it is: The P&L from time passage. Options lose value every day (time decay). If you're long options, theta is negative. Short options, theta is positive.

Formula: Θ P&L = Theta × Δt (usually 1 day)

Why it matters: Theta is the "rent" you pay for holding optionality. A PM who is consistently long options is paying theta every day. That cost needs to be offset by delta or gamma gains.

Vega P&L

What it is: The P&L from changes in implied volatility. If implied vol rises and you're long options, you make money (even if the underlying doesn't move).

Formula: V P&L = Vega × ΔIV

Why it matters:
- If a PM's P&L is dominated by vega, they're making a vol bet, not a directional bet.
- Important to distinguish: did the PM intend to trade vol? If yes, this is alpha. If no, this is unintended risk.

Rho P&L

What it is: P&L from interest rate changes. Usually small for equity options (short-dated), but material for long-dated options, convertible bonds, or any fixed income.

Cross Effects / Higher-Order Terms

  • Vanna: delta change due to vol change
  • Charm: delta change due to time passing
  • Volga: vega change due to vol change

These are usually small but can matter in high-vol regimes or for large gamma/vega positions.

Unexplained P&L

What it is: Whatever's left after you've accounted for all the Greeks. This should be small (ideally <5% of total P&L).

If unexplained is large:
- Data issue (bad prices, stale marks)
- Missing risk factor (exposure not captured by the model)
- Model calibration issue
- Someone is hiding something (Barings, London Whale)

Large unexplained P&L is a red flag. Always investigate.

PART 2: WORKED EXAMPLE

Setup

PM runs a long-short equity book with options overlay:
- Long 10,000 shares of XOM at $110 (delta = +10,000)
- Short 5,000 shares of CVX at $155 (delta = -5,000)
- Long 50 XOM June $115 calls (delta per contract = 0.45, gamma = 0.03, theta = -0.15, vega = 0.22)

What happened yesterday

  • XOM: $110 → $113 (+$3)
  • CVX: $155 → $157 (+$2)
  • XOM implied vol: 28% → 31% (+3 vol points)

P&L Decomposition

Component Calculation P&L
Delta — XOM shares +10,000 × $3 +$30,000
Delta — CVX shares -5,000 × $2 -$10,000
Delta — XOM calls 50 × 100 × 0.45 × $3 +$6,750
Gamma — XOM calls 50 × 100 × ½ × 0.03 × $3² +$675
Theta — XOM calls 50 × 100 × (-$0.15) -$750
Vega — XOM calls 50 × 100 × 0.22 × 3 +$3,300
Total +$29,975

What you tell the PM:

"You're up $30K. Roughly 90% is delta — XOM rallied and your net long paid off. You also picked up $3.3K from vol expanding. Theta cost you $750, which is normal for the long call position. Net delta risk went up because gamma added to your effective long. Your effective XOM delta is now ~+12,600 shares equivalent, up from ~+12,250 yesterday."

What you flag to yourself:

  • The PM is net long $5K shares of "oil" — XOM up, CVX up, but XOM outperformed. If this was meant to be a pair trade (long XOM, short CVX), the unhedged residual oil beta is growing.
  • Vol expanding on XOM: is there a catalyst? Earnings? Geopolitical (oil)?
  • Effective delta growing via gamma: if XOM continues rallying, the PM's long exposure gets bigger automatically. Is the PM aware?

PART 3: COMMON PATTERNS

Pattern 1: "The Accidental Vol Trader"

PM buys options "for hedging" but most of their P&L comes from vega, not delta. They're actually trading vol, not the underlying. Risk: they may not understand their true exposure.

RM action: Show the P&L attribution. "80% of your month's return came from vol expansion, not from the stock moving. Is that the trade you intended?"

Pattern 2: "Theta Bleed"

PM holds long options that slowly decay. Daily theta cost is -$50K. In 20 trading days, that's -$1M. If their delta bets aren't making $1M/month, the options are a drag.

RM action: "Your option book is costing $50K/day in theta. You need the underlying to move [this much] just to break even. Are you comfortable with that carry cost?"

Pattern 3: "Short Gamma P&L Pattern"

PM sold options — collecting premium (positive theta) but losing on big moves (negative gamma). Daily P&L: small positive, small positive, small positive... then a big negative.

RM action: The P&L looks great until it doesn't. Track the running P&L against max potential loss. "You've collected $800K in premium YTD but your worst-case loss on these short puts is $4M. Your Sharpe looks great because you haven't had a vol event — but when it comes, it'll take back more than you earned."

Pattern 4: "Unexplained is Too Big"

P&L attribution says delta/gamma/theta/vega explain 65% of the P&L. 35% is unexplained.

RM action: Investigate immediately. Check pricing (stale marks?), missing positions, wrong Greeks, OTC instruments with non-standard terms. If you can't explain it after digging → escalate.

PART 4: TALKING TO PMs ABOUT P&L

What PMs Want to Hear

  1. Was I right? (Did my thesis play out?)
  2. Where'd the money come from? (Delta? Gamma? Vol?)
  3. What's changed in my risk profile? (Am I bigger than yesterday?)
  4. What should I watch? (Upcoming catalyst, Greek exposure shift)

What PMs Don't Want

  • A 10-minute lecture on cross-Greeks
  • Being told they're wrong
  • Math they didn't ask for

The Template

"[PM name], you're [up/down] [$X] on the day. Main driver: [delta on NAME moving $Y / vol expansion / theta decay]. Your net delta is now [$Z equivalent], [up/down from yesterday]. One thing to flag: [specific risk — growing position, approaching limit, upcoming event]."

30 seconds. Done. If they want more detail, they'll ask.

PART 5: P&L ATTRIBUTION IN THE INTERVIEW

If asked "How would you explain a PM's daily P&L?":

"I'd start by decomposing it into delta, gamma, theta, vega, and any higher-order effects. Delta tells me if the directional bet worked. Gamma tells me about convexity — important for understanding how exposure changes with market moves. Theta shows the cost of holding options. Vega shows the impact of vol changes. Anything unexplained gets flagged.

The attribution isn't just accounting — it tells me what risk the PM is actually taking. If a PM thinks they're trading direction but 60% of their P&L is from vol, that's a conversation worth having. And if unexplained P&L is consistently large, that's a red flag that our risk model might be missing something."
