H — P&L ATTRIBUTION: FORENSIC ACCOUNTING FOR RISK

Decomposing daily P&L into Greeks, factors, alpha, timing, and the unexplained — the RM's most important diagnostic tool

Depth: 🔴 DEEP

WHY THIS IS YOUR MOST IMPORTANT DAILY TASK

At Squarepoint, every morning starts with the same question: "Why did we make or lose money yesterday?" The number is not what matters. The DECOMPOSITION is everything. P&L attribution is the bridge between risk measures (VaR, Greeks, factor exposures) and real-world money. It turns abstract risk into concrete dollars.

As a Junior Risk Manager, P&L attribution is how you:

  1. Diagnose risk — "The $3M gain was 80% vega. The PM thinks they're a stock picker — they're actually a vol trader."
  2. Validate models — "Our Greeks predicted −$1.5M. Actual was −$3.2M. The $1.7M unexplained means the model is missing a risk factor."
  3. Challenge PMs — "Your Sharpe is 2.0, but 70% of your return is theta collection on short gamma. You're earning premium with hidden tail risk."
  4. Detect fraud or error — "Persistent large unexplained P&L = stale marks, wrong Greeks, hidden positions, or something worse."

PART 1: THE FUNDAMENTAL DECOMPOSITION

One-liner: P&L attribution splits yesterday's dollar outcome into the building blocks that caused it — so you know whether the PM was RIGHT about their thesis, LUCKY from market moves, or EXPOSED to a risk they don't understand.

The Master Equation

For a portfolio containing options and linear instruments:

$$\text{Total P\&L} = \underbrace{\Delta \text{P\&L}}{\text{direction}} + \underbrace{\Gamma \text{P\&L}}{\text{convexity}} + \underbrace{\Theta \text{P\&L}}{\text{time decay}} + \underbrace{\mathcal{V} \text{P\&L}}{\text{vol change}} + \underbrace{\rho \text{P\&L}}{\text{rates}} + \underbrace{\text{Cross/Higher-order}}{\text{vanna, charm, volga}} + \underbrace{\text{Unexplained}}_{\text{residual}}$$

Each Component in Detail

Delta P&L — The Directional Bet

$$\Delta \text{P\&L} = \sum_i \delta_i \times \Delta S_i$$

Where $\delta_i$ = dollar delta of position i, $\Delta S_i$ = price change in underlying i.

What it tells you:
- This is the "was the PM right about direction?" component
- If delta P&L dominates, the PM is running a directional book — their performance is driven by market moves, not skill unless those directional calls are consistently correct
- For a market-neutral fund: delta P&L should be small relative to total. If it's consistently large → the fund isn't market-neutral

Components within delta:
| Source | Calculation | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| Stock/equity delta | Position × ΔPrice | Direct equity exposure |
| Options delta | Σ(contracts × multiplier × Δ_option × ΔS) | Directional option exposure |
| Futures delta | Contracts × multiplier × ΔFutures | Futures directional |
| Cross-asset delta | FX delta, commodity delta, etc. | Non-equity directional |

Gamma P&L — The Convexity Payoff

$$\Gamma \text{P\&L} = \frac{1}{2} \sum_i \gamma_i \times (\Delta S_i)^2$$

What it tells you:
- Gamma P&L is ALWAYS positive for long gamma positions (regardless of direction — up or down)
- Gamma P&L is ALWAYS negative for short gamma positions when moves are large
- This is the "insurance payoff" — long gamma pays when markets move big; short gamma pays in quiet markets but bleeds during big moves

Key insight for RM:

Move size Long gamma P&L Short gamma P&L Who wins
Small (ΔS < 0.5σ) Small positive Small negative (offset by theta) Short gamma (theta > gamma cost)
Medium (ΔS ≈ 1σ) Moderate positive Moderate negative Break-even zone
Large (ΔS > 2σ) Large positive (quadratic) Large negative (quadratic) Long gamma dominates

The math of why short gamma kills: Gamma P&L scales with $({\Delta S})^2$. Double the move → 4× the gamma P&L. A −3σ day produces 9× the gamma P&L of a −1σ day. Short gamma positions look fine in calm markets and EXPLODE in stress.

Theta P&L — The Cost of Time

$$\Theta \text{P\&L} = \sum_i \theta_i \times \Delta t$$

Where $\Delta t$ = 1 calendar day / 365 (or 1 trading day / 252, depending on convention).

What it tells you:
- Long options → negative theta (paying rent for optionality)
- Short options → positive theta (collecting rent, but exposed to gamma/vega)
- Theta is the MOST PREDICTABLE component of P&L — you know roughly what it will be before the day starts

Daily theta as a P&L constraint:
- If theta = −$80K/day, the PM needs to earn $80K/day from delta/gamma/vega just to break even
- Over 250 trading days, that's −$20M of theta drag
- If realized vol < implied vol → theta OVERPAYS for the gamma you receive → net loss on the options position
- If realized vol > implied vol → gamma gains exceed theta cost → net gain

Theta-Gamma breakeven: The daily move needed for gamma P&L to offset theta:

$$\Delta S_{\text{BE}} = \sqrt{\frac{2 |\theta|}{\gamma}}$$

If the underlying moves MORE than this daily, long gamma wins. If it moves LESS, short gamma wins.

Vega P&L — The Volatility Bet

$$\mathcal{V} \text{P\&L} = \sum_i \nu_i \times \Delta \text{IV}_i$$

Where $\nu_i$ = dollar vega, $\Delta \text{IV}_i$ = change in implied volatility.

What it tells you:
- If vega P&L dominates, the PM is a VOL TRADER whether they know it or not
- A PM who "buys options for hedging" but makes all their money from vol expansion is running a vol strategy — they should be evaluated on vol forecasting, not directional views
- Vega P&L can swamp delta P&L: a 5-vol-point move in IV on a $50M vega book = $250K, regardless of what the stock did

Bucketed vega P&L (important for vol surfaces):

Tenor bucket Vega ($K/vol pt) ΔIV Vega P&L
1M expiry +$30K +2 pts +$60K
3M expiry +$50K +3 pts +$150K
6M expiry −$20K +4 pts −$80K
1Y expiry +$10K +5 pts +$50K
Total vega P&L +$180K

The PM is long short-dated vol and short 6M vol — a calendar spread. The attribution reveals this structure.

Rho P&L — The Interest Rate Effect

$$\rho \text{P\&L} = \sum_i \rho_i \times \Delta r$$

Usually small for short-dated equity options. Material for:
- Long-dated options (LEAPS)
- Convertible bonds (embedded option + bond = rate-sensitive)
- Fixed income derivatives (pure rate expression)
- Dividend swaps and forwards (discount rate matters)

Cross Effects / Higher-Order Terms

Greek Formula When it matters
Vanna P&L $\frac{\partial \delta}{\partial \sigma} \times \Delta S \times \Delta \sigma$ Delta change due to vol change. Large in crash: spot drops + vol spikes = delta shifts.
Charm P&L $\frac{\partial \delta}{\partial t} \times \Delta t \times \Delta S$ Delta changes as time passes. Important near expiry — delta becomes more binary.
Volga P&L $\frac{1}{2} \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial \sigma^2} \times (\Delta \sigma)^2$ Vega convexity. Matters for large vol moves (10+ points).
Speed P&L $\frac{1}{6} \frac{\partial^3 V}{\partial S^3} \times (\Delta S)^3$ Gamma-of-gamma. Matters for very large spot moves.

When cross-effects matter:
- Normal days: cross-effects < 5% of total P&L. Ignorable.
- Crisis days (SPX −5%, VIX +15): cross-effects can be 20-30% of total P&L. Vanna dominates.
- 0DTE options: charm effects dominate as delta accelerates toward 0 or 1 through the day.

Greek P&L Ratio Diagnostics — The RM's Blood Panel

The individual Greek P&L components are useful. The RATIOS between them are diagnostic — they reveal the risk profile the PM is actually running.

Ratio / Metric How to compute What it tells you Healthy range Red flag
Vega % of total P&L |Vega P&L| / |Total P&L| Is the PM a directional trader or a vol trader? <30% for equity PM >50% → PM is trading vol, not stocks
Theta % of total P&L |Theta P&L| / |Total P&L| How much of the return is carry (renting options) vs active? Depends on strategy >70% consistently → short gamma collector
Gamma / Theta ratio Cumulative Gamma P&L / Cumulative |Theta P&L| Is the PM paying fair rent for gamma protection? >0.8 (realized vol ≥ implied) <0.5 → theta overpaying for gamma received
Cumulative Theta / Worst-case tail loss Σ Theta collected / Stress-day gamma+vega loss How many months of theta does one tail event wipe out? >0.3 (tail < 3× theta) <0.1 → earning pennies in front of a steamroller
Delta % of total P&L |Delta P&L| / |Total P&L| Is the PM a directional bettor? Varies by mandate >90% consistently → PM is just riding beta, no edge in convexity or vol
Factor P&L / Total P&L Σ factor-attributed P&L / Total P&L Is "alpha" really factor exposure? <50% → genuine alpha >80% → PM delivers factor, not skill
Unexplained / Total P&L |Unexplained| / |Total P&L| Is the model capturing reality? <5% >15% → model is broken, escalate
Cross-effects / Total P&L |Vanna + Charm + Volga| / |Total P&L| Are higher-order risks material? <5% on normal days >20% → large gamma/vega positions in a crisis; model needs full repricing

Worked example of ratio diagnosis:

PM's monthly P&L = +$2.4M. Decomposition: Delta = +$600K (25%), Gamma = +$200K (8%), Theta = +$1.8M (75%), Vega = −$200K.

Diagnosis: Theta is 75% of total P&L → this PM is a short gamma collector. They're earning $90K/day in theta. On a 3σ day with +10 vol points, gamma + vega loss could be $8M+. The PM has collected $1.8M to risk $8M — a 4.4:1 ratio against them. The Sharpe looks great (theta is smooth), but the P&L distribution is negatively skewed. Action: stress-test the book against a tail event, verify drawdown limits cover the $8M scenario, and flag to CRO.

PM's monthly P&L = +$1.5M. Decomposition: Delta = +$300K (20%), Gamma = −$100K, Vega = +$1.3M (87%), Theta = $0.

Diagnosis: Vega is 87% of P&L → this PM is an implied-vol trader. They're long vol and it's been expanding. If vol compresses, they give it all back AND potentially lose on delta too. Action: "Is the vol expansion trade your thesis, or a side-effect of your position structure?"

Unexplained P&L — The Red Flag

$$\text{Unexplained} = \text{Actual P\&L} - \text{Sum of attributed P\&L}$$

Unexplained Interpretation Action
< 2% of total Normal model noise No action
2-5% of total Worth investigating Check prices, marks, missing Greeks
5-15% of total Material model gap Formal investigation. Missing risk factor? Wrong pricing model?
> 15% of total Red flag Escalate immediately. Possible: hidden position, stale marks, model error, rogue trading

Common causes of unexplained P&L:
1. Stale marks — illiquid OTC positions not re-marked → sudden catch-up creates unexplained jump
2. Wrong Greeks — model calculates Greeks with wrong vol surface, wrong spot, or wrong dividend
3. Missing risk factor — correlation move, basis move, or liquidity premium not in the model
4. Discrete vs continuous — Greeks assume infinitesimal moves; actual moves are discrete → Taylor expansion residual
5. Gap risk — overnight moves not captured by Greeks computed at EOD
6. Dividend/corporate action — ex-div adjustments, spin-offs, special dividends not modeled

The P&L explain as model validation:
If daily unexplained P&L is consistently 10%+, your RISK MODEL IS WRONG. The VaR, stress tests, and Greeks reports generated by the same model are also wrong. Persistent unexplained P&L is the canary in the coal mine for model risk.

PART 2: FACTOR-BASED P&L ATTRIBUTION

One-liner: Greek-based attribution tells you WHICH INSTRUMENTS drove P&L. Factor-based attribution tells you WHICH BETS drove P&L — market direction, sector rotation, style tilt, or genuine stock selection.

The Framework

$$R_p = \underbrace{\alpha}{\text{skill (residual)}} + \underbrace{\beta{\text{MKT}} \times R_{\text{MKT}}}{\text{market}} + \underbrace{\beta{\text{SECT}} \times R_{\text{SECT}}}{\text{sector}} + \underbrace{\beta{\text{STYLE}} \times R_{\text{STYLE}}}{\text{style factors}} + \underbrace{\epsilon}{\text{idiosyncratic}}$$

Decomposition Layers

Layer 1: Asset Allocation Effect

How much P&L came from market/sector exposure (beta) vs stock selection (alpha)?

Brinson attribution (for long-only):

$$\text{Allocation effect} = \sum_j (w_{p,j} - w_{b,j}) \times (R_{b,j} - R_b)$$
$$\text{Selection effect} = \sum_j w_{b,j} \times (R_{p,j} - R_{b,j})$$
$$\text{Interaction effect} = \sum_j (w_{p,j} - w_{b,j}) \times (R_{p,j} - R_{b,j})$$

Component What it measures Example
Allocation Did the PM overweight the right sectors? Overweight tech in a tech rally → positive allocation
Selection Within each sector, did the PM pick the right stocks? Within tech, bought NVDA not INTC → positive selection
Interaction Combined effect of being in the right sector AND picking the right stock Overweight tech AND best stock in tech → interaction bonus

Layer 2: Factor P&L Attribution

For a multi-factor model (Barra, Axioma, or internal):

$$\text{Factor P\&L}k = \beta{p,k} \times f_k \times \text{Portfolio Notional}$$

Where $\beta_{p,k}$ = portfolio's loading on factor k, $f_k$ = factor return on the day.

Worked example — Daily factor P&L:

Factor Portfolio β Factor return Factor P&L ($100M fund)
Market +0.25 −1.2% −$300K
Momentum +0.40 +0.8% +$320K
Value −0.15 +0.3% −$45K
Size (small) +0.10 −0.5% −$50K
Rates (DV01=$20K/bp) N/A +3bp −$60K
VIX (vega=$50K/pt) N/A +1.5 pts −$75K
Total factor −$210K
Specific (stock selection) +$450K
TOTAL +$240K

Diagnosis: "You made $240K. But factor P&L was −$210K — the market went against you, and your momentum/size/rates/vol exposures all dragged. Your ACTUAL stock-picking P&L was +$450K — that's genuine alpha. Today, the factor headwinds masked your skill."

Why this matters at Squarepoint: A systematic-at-its-core fund KNOWS the factor exposures. The RM's job is to verify that the alpha component (specific return) is real and persistent, and that the factor exposures are intentional and within limits.

Layer 3: Timing vs Carrying Attribution

Separate P&L into decisions that require TIMING (active bets) versus returns from simply holding the position (carry/dividend/roll):

Component Source Example
Carry Coupon income, dividend yield, theta income, roll yield Bond pays 4.5% coupon → daily carry = $12.3K
Rolldown Move down the yield curve over time → price appreciation 5Y bond "becomes" 4.9Y bond → gains from curve steepness
Market move Changes in yields, spreads, prices Credit spreads widen 5bp → CS01 loss
Active trading Intraday/intraweek repositioning — buy/sell decisions PM traded around the position → $X gain from timing

For a rates desk specifically:

$$\text{P\&L} = \underbrace{\text{Carry}}{\text{coupon}} + \underbrace{\text{Rolldown}}{\text{curve}} + \underbrace{\text{Duration P\&L}}{\text{ΔYield × DV01}} + \underbrace{\text{Curve P\&L}}{\text{KRD moves}} + \underbrace{\text{Spread P\&L}}{\text{ΔCredit × CS01}} + \underbrace{\text{Convexity P\&L}}{\text{½ × Conv × ΔY²}} + \underbrace{\text{Residual}}_{\text{unexplained}}$$

PART 3: WORKED EXAMPLES — THREE SCENARIOS

Scenario 1: Equity + Options Book

Setup: PM runs long-short equity with options overlay.
- Long 10,000 shares XOM at $110 (δ = +10,000 shares, $1.1M dollar delta)
- Short 5,000 shares CVX at $155 (δ = −5,000 shares, −$775K dollar delta)
- Long 50 XOM Jun $115 calls (δ per contract = 0.45, γ = 0.03, θ = −$0.15/day, ν = $0.22/vol pt, each contract = 100 shares)

Yesterday's moves:
- XOM: $110 → $113 (+$3, +2.7%)
- CVX: $155 → $157 (+$2, +1.3%)
- XOM IV: 28% → 31% (+3 vol points)

Component Calculation P&L
Delta — XOM shares +10,000 × $3 +$30,000
Delta — CVX shares −5,000 × $2 −$10,000
Delta — XOM calls 50 × 100 × 0.45 × $3 +$6,750
Gamma — XOM calls 50 × 100 × ½ × 0.03 × $3² +$675
Theta — XOM calls 50 × 100 × (−$0.15) −$750
Vega — XOM calls 50 × 100 × $0.22 × 3 +$3,300
Rho Negligible (short-dated, no rate move) ~$0
Cross-effects Vanna/charm small (moderate move, mid-life option) ~+$50
TOTAL ATTRIBUTED +$30,025

What you tell the PM (30 seconds):
"You're up $30K. Main driver: delta — XOM rallied $3, your net long paid off. You also picked up $3.3K from vol expanding on XOM, which offset the $750 theta cost. Gamma added $675. One flag: your effective XOM delta grew via gamma — you're now long ~12,600 share-equivalents of XOM vs 12,250 yesterday. And the XOM-CVX pair spread widened — XOM outperformed CVX by $1 ratio — your 'pair trade' is becoming a directional oil bet."

What you flag internally:
- Net delta grew automatically (gamma). If XOM rallies another $3, delta grows further → exposure is self-reinforcing
- XOM IV jumped 3 pts → is there a catalyst? Earnings? Geopolitical? If vol is pricing an event, gamma exposure changes around the event
- The "pair trade" (long XOM / short CVX) isn't hedging well — XOM outperformed by 1.4% → basis risk

Scenario 2: Rates Desk

Setup: PM runs a curve steepener: long 2Y, short 10Y.
- Long $100M 2Y UST, DV01 = $18K/bp
- Short $40M 10Y UST, DV01 = $34K/bp
- Net DV01 = $18K − $34K = −$16K/bp (net short duration)

Yesterday's curve move:
- 2Y yield: 4.20% → 4.15% (−5bp)
- 10Y yield: 4.50% → 4.55% (+5bp)
- Curve steepened: 2s10s spread went from 30bp to 40bp (+10bp)

Component Calculation P&L
2Y position $18K/bp × (−5bp) = price UP +$90,000
10Y position −$34K/bp × (+5bp) = price DOWN, you're short → GAIN +$170,000
Carry Net coupon (2Y yield − 10Y yield × position sizes) / 252 +$2,500
Convexity Small — rates move not large enough +$500
TOTAL +$263,000

Factor decomposition:
| Factor | P&L |
|---|---|
| Parallel rates move (avg = 0bp) | ~$0 (parallel was ~0) |
| Curve steepening (+10bp 2s10s) | +$260K (the active bet) |
| Carry | +$2.5K |
| Convexity + residual | +$0.5K |

What you tell the PM: "You made $263K. Essentially all of it is from the curve steepening — 2s10s went from 30 to 40bp, your steepener paid off. Your parallel duration risk was roughly neutral, so you weren't exposed to the level moving — just the shape. Net DV01 is still −$16K/bp, so you'd lose $160K on a 10bp parallel selloff."

Scenario 3: Short Gamma — The Quiet Bleed and the Crisis

Setup: PM sells 1-month ATM straddles on SPX.
- Sold 200 SPX straddles (100 calls + 100 puts)
- Total theta collected: +$150K/day
- Total gamma exposure: −$500K per 1% SPX move (dollar gamma)
- Total vega: −$800K per vol point

Day 1-19: calm market (SPX moves ±0.3% daily, IV steady)

Day Delta P&L Gamma P&L Theta P&L Vega P&L Total
Each calm day ±$15K −$5K +$150K ~$0 ~+$130K/day
19-day total ~$0 (random walk) −$95K +$2,850K ~$0 ~+$2,470K

Day 20: SPX drops 4%, VIX jumps 12 points

Component Calculation P&L
Delta Approximately −$200K (delta was near-flat but gamma creates delta during the move) −$200K
Gamma −$500K × (4%)² / (1%)² = −$500K × 16 = −$8,000K
Theta +$150K (still collect one day's theta) +$150K
Vega −$800K × 12 pts −$9,600K
Cross Vanna + Volga (significant in crash + vol spike) −$1,200K
Day 20 total −$18,850K

Running total: +$2,470K − $18,850K = −$16,380K

One bad day wiped out 19 good days and then some. The Sharpe ratio for days 1-19 was stellar (~3.0+). Including day 20, cumulative P&L is deeply negative. This is the prototypical short-gamma/short-vol strategy that P&L attribution reveals.

RM lesson: When you see a PM with consistently positive theta, low delta P&L, and near-zero gamma P&L for weeks → they're short gamma/short vol. The P&L attribution is SCREAMING the risk profile. The blowup is a matter of WHEN, not IF. Monitor theta/gamma ratio, stress-test the position against a 3σ + vol-spike day, and ensure the drawdown limits can absorb the tail event.

PART 4: P&L EXPLAIN — THE MODEL VALIDATION TOOL

One-liner: P&L explain compares what your model PREDICTED the P&L should be (from Greeks and factor sensitivities) against what ACTUALLY happened. The gap is your model error — and persistent gaps mean your risk system is lying to you.

The Process

$$\text{Predicted P\&L} = \sum_k \text{Sensitivity}_k \times \Delta \text{Factor}_k$$

$$\text{P\&L Explain Ratio} = \frac{\text{Predicted P\&L}}{\text{Actual P\&L}}$$

Ratio Model quality
0.95-1.05 Excellent — model captures >95% of P&L drivers
0.80-0.95 Acceptable — small gaps, monitor
0.60-0.80 Poor — significant unexplained. Formal review needed.
<0.60 Failed — model is fundamentally wrong

Common Gaps and Their Causes

Gap pattern Likely cause Fix
Unexplained always POSITIVE Model overestimates losses → conservative Greeks Check vol surface calibration. Are Greeks computed with correct IV?
Unexplained always NEGATIVE Model underestimates losses → DANGEROUS Missing risk factor. Possibly: correlation move, basis shift, liquidity premium. Most concerning direction.
Unexplained spikes on high-vol days Gamma/convexity not captured correctly Taylor expansion truncation. Need higher-order terms or full repricing.
Unexplained on specific names Wrong Greeks for those positions Pricing model issue: wrong dividend assumptions, wrong borrow rate, exercise style mismatch.
Unexplained on OTC positions Mark uncertainty OTC marks are dealer quotes or models — may differ from actual liquidation value.

P&L Explain Across Asset Classes

Asset class Key sensitivities to explain Common unexplained sources
Equities Delta, beta, sector exposure Corporate actions (splits, dividends), short borrow costs
Equity options Delta, gamma, theta, vega, vanna, charm Vol surface move (skew/term structure rotation), discrete dividends
Rates / FI DV01, KRD, convexity, carry, rolldown Curve shape changes (butterfly, twist), repo rate changes
Credit CS01, JTD, recovery rate, carry Rating transitions, recovery assumption changes, CDS-bond basis
FX Spot delta, forward points, vol Central bank intervention, cross-currency basis
Commodities Spot delta, roll yield, basis, storage Physical delivery dynamics, weather, transportation constraints

PART 5: COMMON P&L PATTERNS THE RM MUST RECOGNIZE

One-liner: Certain P&L signatures are diagnostic — like a doctor reading an ECG, the RM reads daily P&L to diagnose the risk the PM is actually taking.

Pattern 1: "The Accidental Vol Trader"

Signature: PM buys options "for hedging" but 60%+ of monthly P&L is from vega, not delta.

Diagnosis: They're not hedging — they're running a vol book they don't recognize as a vol book. Their delta views may be wrong, but they're making money because vol is expanding.

Risk: When vol COMPRESSES, they'll lose on vega AND potentially on their wrong delta view simultaneously. Double hit.

RM action: "80% of your month's return came from vol expansion. Is that the trade you intended? If vol hadn't moved, you'd be down $X from delta alone."

Pattern 2: "The Theta Bleed"

Signature: Consistently negative daily attribution from theta: −$80K/day. Monthly cost: −$1.6M.

Diagnosis: PM holds long options. The carry cost is significant. They need delta/gamma/vega to earn $1.6M/month just to BREAK EVEN.

Key question: Is realized vol higher than implied vol? If yes → gamma gains offset theta → option position is well-priced. If no → PM is paying above fair value for the optionality.

RM action: "Your option book costs $80K/day in theta. SPX would need to move ±$X daily for your gamma to offset this. Over the last 20 days, actual daily moves averaged $Y. You're [above/below] breakeven."

Pattern 3: "The Short Gamma Collector"

Signature: Daily P&L: +$100K, +$110K, +$95K, +$105K... then suddenly −$2M. Small consistent gains followed by sharp losses.

Diagnosis: Classic short gamma/short vol profile. Theta income is steady and positive. Gamma P&L is slightly negative on normal days, catastrophically negative on big-move days.

The give-away metrics:
- Sharpe looks excellent (2.0+) before the blowup
- Theta P&L is consistently +$100K/day → PM is SHORT options
- Gamma P&L is −$5K/day on calm days → tolerable
- BUT tail gamma P&L on a 3σ day: −$3M+

RM action: Track the cumulative theta collected vs worst-case gamma/vega loss. "You've collected $1.5M in theta YTD. A 4% SPX drop with +15 vol points costs you $18M. Your risk/reward on this position is asymmetric — you're earning $1.5M to risk $18M."

Pattern 4: "Unexplained Is Growing"

Signature: Unexplained P&L is 2% of total in month 1, 5% in month 2, 12% in month 3.

Diagnosis: Progressive model deterioration. Either:
- Market regime changed and the model hasn't adapted (stale parameters)
- PM is adding positions/instruments the model doesn't cover
- Data feed degradation (stale prices, missing marks)

RM action: Escalate. Formally document the trend. Request quant/model review.

Pattern 5: "Factor Dress-Up"

Signature: PM's gross alpha is +$2M/month. Factor attribution shows: momentum factor contributed +$1.8M. Genuine alpha = +$200K.

Diagnosis: The PM is charging alpha fees for delivering momentum exposure. A momentum ETF would deliver roughly the same return at 1/10th the cost.

RM action: Not a risk issue per se — but the CIO/allocators should know. "Your returns decompose as 90% momentum factor, 10% idiosyncratic alpha. Your information ratio on the alpha component alone is 0.15."

Pattern 6: "The Basis Drift"

Signature: PM runs a hedged book (long stock, short index). Delta P&L is small. But basis P&L (stock vs index spread) is growing — sometimes positive, sometimes negative, but increasingly volatile.

Diagnosis: The hedge is drifting. The stock and the index are becoming less correlated, or the hedge ratio is stale.

RM action: "Your hedge effectiveness (R²) was 0.93 last month, now 0.78. The basis between your position and hedge has widened. You may need to adjust the hedge ratio or find a better instrument."

PART 6: TALKING TO PMs — THE 30-SECOND BRIEF

One-liner: The PM wants to know three things: Am I up or down? Why? What changed in my risk? Deliver all three in 30 seconds or less.

The Template

"[PM], you're [up/down] [$X] today. Main driver: [delta on NAME moving $Y / vol expansion-compression / theta / curve steepener-flattener]. Your [key exposure metric] is now [$Z], [up/from/unchanged] vs yesterday. One flag: [specific risk — growing position, approaching limit, upcoming catalyst, basis drift, correlation shift]."

What PMs Want

What they ask What they mean What to say
"Was I right?" Did my thesis work? Focus on delta P&L for their key position
"Where did the money come from?" attribution by Greek/factor "60% delta, 25% vega, 15% theta"
"Am I bigger?" Did risk grow? Report effective delta, gamma, VaR change
"What should I watch?" Upcoming risks to my position Catalyst, event, approaching limit, correlation shift
"Is my hedge working?" Basis risk check Report hedge ratio, correlation, basis P&L separately

What PMs Don't Want

  • A 10-minute lecture on second-order Greeks
  • Being told they're wrong (approach: ask questions, don't accuse)
  • Math they didn't ask for (save the derivations for the quant meeting)
  • Caveats without conclusions ("it could be this OR it could be that" → pick one and explain why)

How to Challenge Without Confronting

Instead of Say
"Your position is too risky" "Your effective delta has grown 30% via gamma. Was that intentional, or should we look at rebalancing?"
"You're bleeding theta" "The options book is costing $80K/day. What's the thesis for continuing to hold?"
"You're just running momentum" "Factor attribution shows 85% of your month's alpha is from momentum. What's your view on the sustainability of the momentum factor?"
"Your unexplained is too high" "We're seeing 12% unexplained on this book. I want to make sure we're capturing all the risk — can we walk through the positions?"

PART 7: SYSTEMATIC P&L ATTRIBUTION INFRASTRUCTURE

At a fund like Squarepoint, P&L attribution is NOT a human exercise done on a spreadsheet. It's an automated pipeline that runs every night and produces reports by morning.

The Pipeline

EOD MARKET DATA (prices, vols, curves)
          │
          ▼
POSITION DATA (from OMS/PMS)
          │
          ▼
RISK ENGINE (compute Greeks, factor exposures)
          │
          ▼
YESTERDAY'S Greeks × TODAY'S factor moves = PREDICTED P&L
          │
          ▼
COMPARE to ACTUAL P&L (from prime broker/custodian)
          │
          ▼
ATTRIBUTION REPORT (by Greek, by factor, by desk, by PM)
          │
          ▼
UNEXPLAINED P&L REPORT → flags if > threshold
          │
          ▼
RM DASHBOARD → morning meeting

Quality Checks in the Pipeline

Check What it catches Frequency
Price validation Stale prices, outlier marks, failed feeds Every EOD run
Position reconciliation PM system vs prime broker mismatch (trades not booked, wrong quantities) Daily, automated
Greek validation Greeks that are outliers vs historical (e.g., delta >1.0, negative gamma for a long call) Every EOD
Corporate action check Ex-div dates, splits, spin-offs that change position economics Daily, semi-automated
New position check Newly opened positions that may not have Greeks computed yet Daily

Attribution Hierarchy — Multiple Cuts

The same P&L gets sliced multiple ways:

Cut What it shows Who uses it
By position Which specific names/instruments made or lost money PM
By Greek Delta vs gamma vs theta vs vega PM + RM
By factor Market, sector, style, macro factor contributions RM + CIO
By desk Each desk's contribution to fund-level P&L CRO + CIO
By strategy Performance of each strategy within a desk PM + RM
Alpha vs factor Genuine skill vs systematic exposure CIO (for compensation, allocation)
Carry vs active Passive income vs active trading P&L RM (carry is predictable, active is not)

PART 8: P&L ATTRIBUTION AS A FRAUD DETECTION TOOL

One-liner: Every major rogue trading scandal has one thing in common — the P&L didn't match the risk. If the P&L attribution had been taken seriously, the fraud would have been caught earlier.

Historical Failures

Event What happened What P&L attribution would have shown
Barings (1995) Nick Leeson hid losses in error account 88888. Reported profits from "arbitrage" that required no risk. P&L showed profit but NO CORRESPONDING RISK EXPOSURES. Attribution would show: "You're making $X/day but your Greeks are zero — where is this coming from?"
SocGen (2008) Kerviel built massive long equity futures (~€50B). Hedged with fictitious offsetting trades. Delta P&L on real positions was enormous. The "hedge" P&L was fabricated. Position reconciliation between PM system and exchange would have caught the mismatch.
London Whale (2012) Bruno Iksil at JPM accumulated massive CDS positions. Marked them at mid-market (favorable) while actual liquidation prices were much worse. Reported P&L (at model marks) vs theoretical P&L (from Greeks × market moves) had growing divergence. The MODEL was being manipulated — marks were moved to minimize apparent losses.
Archegos (2021) Total return swaps to avoid 13F disclosure. Multiple PBs didn't know about each other's exposure. At the PB level, each PB would see that P&L attribution on single-name swaps was dominated by a few names. Concentration risk was visible within each PB's book — they just didn't aggregate across PBs.

Red Flags in P&L Attribution

Red flag What it might mean
Profit with no risk Fabricated trades or mismarked positions
Large unexplained P&L that always goes ONE direction (positive) Marks being smoothed in the PM's favor
Greeks show big positions but P&L is suspiciously flat Offsetting fictional trades or unauthorized loss-hiding
P&L is dominated by ONE illiquid position with no observable market price Mark manipulation on OTC/illiquid instruments
P&L explain ratio consistently < 0.70 on specific positions Those positions aren't what they appear to be

PART 9: ADVANCED TOPICS

P&L Attribution for Multi-Asset Portfolios

In a discretionary multi-asset fund (like Squarepoint's discretionary team), a single PM might have:
- Equity positions (delta P&L)
- Options overlay (gamma, theta, vega P&L)
- Rate hedges (DV01 P&L)
- FX exposure (spot delta + forward points P&L)
- Commodity trades (spot delta + roll P&L)

The attribution must capture the CROSS-ASSET INTERACTION:
- "SPX dropped 3% and your equity delta lost $1.5M. But your SPX put hedge gained $800K (delta + gamma + vega). Your rate hedge gained $200K (flight to quality). Your commodity short gained $100K (risk-off). Net: −$400K — the PM was RIGHT to have the hedge structure."

Without the integrated multi-asset attribution, each asset class would show a different story, and the PM's actual risk management (the hedge structure) would be invisible.

Intraday P&L Attribution

EOD attribution is the standard. But for significant intraday moves (SPX opens −2%, or a name gaps on news), intraday attribution can be critical:

  • What time did the loss occur?
  • Was the PM in the position before the move, or did they trade INTO the loss?
  • Did the PM's hedges respond in real-time?
  • Was the loss from market impact (trading into an illiquid market)?

Intraday attribution data sources:
- Trade timestamps from execution management system
- Intraday price snapshots (typically every minute or 5 minutes)
- Greeks at each snapshot

Attribution Aggregation Across Time

Daily attribution doesn't tell the whole story. Aggregate over:

Horizon What it reveals
Daily Immediate diagnosis. What just happened?
Weekly Short-term P&L trends. Is theta dominating? Is factor exposure drifting?
Monthly Strategy evaluation. Is the PM's stated thesis matching their P&L source?
Quarterly Performance review. Alpha vs factor. Sharpe on alpha component.
Annual Compensation review. Genuine value-add.

Cumulative factor P&L chart: Plot cumulative alpha, cumulative factor contributions, and cumulative total P&L over time. If cumulative alpha is flat while total P&L rises → the PM is delivering factor, not alpha.

PART 10: INTERVIEW APPLICATION

"How would you explain a PM's daily P&L?"

"I'd decompose it along two dimensions. First, Greek-based: delta tells me if the directional bet worked, gamma shows convexity payoff — critical for understanding how exposure changes with market moves — theta shows the time decay cost, and vega shows the impact of vol changes. Each Greek isolates a different source of risk. Second, factor-based: I'd attribute the return to market beta, sector, style factors like momentum or value, and macro factors like rates and vol. What's left after removing all factor exposure is genuine alpha — the PM's actual skill.

The attribution isn't just accounting — it's diagnostic. If a PM thinks they're trading direction but 60% of P&L is from vega, they're actually a vol trader. If a PM claims alpha but factor attribution shows 90% momentum exposure, they're selling factor at alpha prices. And if unexplained P&L is consistently large, it means our risk model is missing a factor or — worse — someone is manipulating marks.

At a multi-PM fund, the highest-value use is cross-desk attribution: are all four desks making money from the same factor? Because then the 'diversified' fund isn't diversified — it's four levered bets on one factor."

"What would you do if unexplained P&L keeps growing?"

"I'd treat persistent unexplained P&L as a model risk red flag with escalating severity. First, quantify: is it 2% of total P&L (noise) or 15% (broken model)? Is it consistently one direction (systematic bias) or random (missing risk factor)?

Then diagnose: check data quality — stale prices, missed corporate actions, position mismatches between PM system and prime broker. Check model calibration — are Greeks computed with the correct vol surface, correct dividend assumptions, correct repo rates? Check for missing risk factors — is there a correlation move, basis shift, or liquidity premium change the model doesn't capture?

If the gap persists after data and calibration checks, escalate formally. A model that can't explain its own P&L is a model whose VaR, stress tests, and Greeks outputs should not be trusted. The P&L explain is the canary in the coal mine — it tells you the entire risk framework is degraded before the tail event that proves it."

"How do you distinguish a PM's alpha from factor exposure?"

"Multi-factor regression: regress daily PM returns against market, size, value, momentum, quality, rates, and sector factors. Three outputs I focus on.

The R² tells me how much is explained by known factors — if it's 0.90, only 10% is idiosyncratic. The intercept (alpha) tells me whether there's systematic outperformance after removing factor exposure — I need a t-stat above 2.0 with at least 3-4 years of data for statistical significance. And the factor loadings tell me whether the PM's ACTUAL exposures match their STATED strategy — a PM claiming 'stock picking' with β_momentum of 0.7 is running a momentum strategy.

I'd also plot cumulative factor P&L vs cumulative alpha P&L over time. If the alpha line goes flat while total return rises, the PM's recent P&L is ALL factor — and that factor exposure can reverse."

"Short gamma P&L profile — how do you identify and manage it?"

"The P&L signature is diagnostic: small consistent daily gains from positive theta, near-zero gamma P&L on calm days, then a massive single-day loss when the market moves sharply.

I'd identify it through attribution: if theta P&L is consistently positive and represents >80% of daily P&L, the PM is short options — short gamma, short vega. The Sharpe ratio will look attractive, maybe 2.0+, but it's misleading because the return distribution is negatively skewed.

To manage it, I'd track the ratio of cumulative theta collected to worst-case tail loss. If the PM has collected $2M in theta YTD but a 4σ day with 15 vol-point spike costs $20M, the risk-reward is 10:1 against them. I'd run that scenario daily and ensure either the position is sized so the tail loss stays within drawdown limits, or the PM has tail hedges — long OTM puts or VIX calls — that cap the downside."
