RISK MANAGER'S COMPLETE REFERENCE

Every Risk Concept You Need to Know for a Discretionary Fund

PART 1: MARKET RISK

Market risk is the risk of loss from adverse movements in market prices. This is the core of your daily work.

1.1 THE GREEKS — First-Order and Second-Order Sensitivities

The Greeks measure how a portfolio's value changes when a single market variable moves. You need to know ALL of them.

Delta ($\Delta$)

Definition: The rate of change of portfolio value with respect to a change in the underlying price.

$$\Delta = \frac{\partial V}{\partial S}$$

  • What it tells you: If $\Delta = 0.6$, the portfolio gains/loses $0.60 for every $1 move in the underlying
  • Application:
  • Delta is the primary exposure measure. When a PM says "I'm long oil," what you care about is their Delta in oil
  • Dollar Delta = $\Delta \times S \times \text{position size}$ — the actual dollar P&L per 1% move
  • A 500-lot crude oil futures position ($\Delta = 1$ per future, each contract = 1,000 barrels) at $80/barrel = $40M dollar Delta
  • What to watch:
  • Delta can change as the underlying moves (that's Gamma)
  • A PM might say "I'm hedged" but that hedge only holds at current prices
  • Cross-asset Delta: a PM long gold might not realize they're also implicitly short USD (gold is priced in dollars)
  • Implementation: Pull positions daily, calculate dollar Delta by asset class, geography, sector. Aggregate across PMs. Compare to IC limits.

Gamma ($\Gamma$)

Definition: The rate of change of Delta with respect to the underlying price. The second derivative.

$$\Gamma = \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2} = \frac{\partial \Delta}{\partial S}$$

  • What it tells you: How fast your Delta changes. High Gamma means your exposure is unstable.
  • Application:
  • Long Gamma (bought options): Delta moves in your favor as the market moves. You make money from big moves in either direction. P&L profile is convex (good).
  • Short Gamma (sold options): Delta moves against you as the market moves. You lose more as the market moves. P&L profile is concave (dangerous).
  • Dollar Gamma = $0.5 \times \Gamma \times S^2 \times (\Delta S / S)^2 \times \text{position size}$
  • What to watch:
  • PMs who are short Gamma (sold options, sold vol) look like they're printing money... until the market moves sharply and they get destroyed
  • Gamma is highest for ATM options near expiry — "Gamma risk" spikes into expiration
  • Gamma squeeze: when short-Gamma dealers are forced to buy into a rising market, amplifying the move (your SOFR scanner experience is directly relevant here)
  • Implementation: Monitor net Gamma by underlier. Flag any PM with large short Gamma near expiry. Run "Gamma P&L" scenarios: what does a 2σ move do?

Vega ($\nu$)

Definition: The rate of change of portfolio value with respect to implied volatility.

$$\nu = \frac{\partial V}{\partial \sigma}$$

  • What it tells you: How much you make/lose per 1% (or 1 vol point) change in implied vol
  • Application:
  • PMs trade volatility directly (long straddles, selling vol) or have incidental vol exposure through options positions
  • Dollar Vega = sensitivity to a 1-point move in implied vol × position size
  • Vega risk is often overlooked — a PM might be "Delta neutral" but massively long Vega, meaning a vol crush destroys them
  • What to watch:
  • Vol is mean-reverting but can spike violently (VIX went from 15 to 80 in March 2020)
  • Term structure of Vega: exposure to near-term vol vs long-dated vol behave differently
  • Vega by strike: exposure to ATM vol vs wing vol (skew risk)
  • Correlation between Vega and Delta: when markets crash, vol spikes — if you're short Vega AND long Delta, both legs hurt you simultaneously
  • Implementation: Daily Vega report by underlier and tenor bucket. Watch for concentrated Vega positions. Stress test: what if vol doubles?

Theta ($\Theta$)

Definition: The rate of change of portfolio value with respect to time.

$$\Theta = \frac{\partial V}{\partial t}$$

  • What it tells you: How much value you gain or lose per day just from the passage of time (time decay)
  • Application:
  • Long options = negative Theta (you're paying for time). Short options = positive Theta (you're collecting time premium)
  • Theta is the "cost" of holding Gamma. PMs who are long Gamma pay for it through Theta bleed
  • Daily P&L from Theta = $\Theta \times \text{position size}$ — this should be part of your P&L decomposition
  • What to watch:
  • A PM collecting positive Theta through short options is essentially selling insurance — fine until the claim hits
  • Theta accelerates near expiry (Gamma-Theta trade-off is strongest ATM near expiry)
  • PMs sometimes confuse Theta income with alpha — they're not generating returns, they're getting paid to take tail risk
  • Implementation: Track daily Theta across all PM books. If Theta is a large percentage of total P&L, flag it — the PM may be running a hidden short vol strategy.

Rho ($\rho$)

Definition: The rate of change of portfolio value with respect to interest rates.

$$\rho = \frac{\partial V}{\partial r}$$

  • What it tells you: How sensitive the portfolio is to rate changes
  • Application:
  • Long bonds / long duration = positive Rho (you make money when rates fall)
  • Critical for rates-heavy discretionary books
  • Also affects options pricing (cost of carry) — usually a secondary effect unless the book is long-dated
  • DV01 (Dollar Value of 01): the P&L impact of a 1 basis point parallel shift in rates. More commonly used than Rho for rates trading

$$DV01 = \frac{\partial V}{\partial y} \times 0.0001$$

  • What to watch:
  • Rate sensitivity is hidden everywhere: credit positions, FX forwards/swaps, commodity futures (through discounting)
  • Key rate DV01: sensitivity to specific tenors (2Y, 5Y, 10Y, 30Y) — a parallel shift is unrealistic, curve can steepen/flatten/twist
  • Implementation: DV01 by tenor bucket for every PM. Aggregate fund-level DV01. Stress test: +50bp, +100bp, -50bp, curve steepener, curve flattener.

Vanna

Definition: The cross-Greek — sensitivity of Delta to implied vol, OR sensitivity of Vega to the underlying price.

$$\text{Vanna} = \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S \, \partial \sigma} = \frac{\partial \Delta}{\partial \sigma} = \frac{\partial \nu}{\partial S}$$

  • What it tells you: When vol changes, how does your Delta shift? When the market moves, how does your Vega shift?
  • Application:
  • Critical for skew trading. If you're long OTM puts, your Vega increases as the market falls (and vol rises with it)
  • Vanna risk explains why "Delta-hedged" positions can still lose: the Delta you hedged was calculated at one vol level. When vol changes, Delta changes, and your hedge is stale
  • What to watch:
  • In a crash: market falls → vol spikes → Vanna effect shifts Deltas → forces more selling → amplifies the crash
  • Dealers hedging their Vanna exposure can create positive feedback loops
  • Implementation: Monitor Vanna for options-heavy books. Flag positions where Vanna effect could break the Delta hedge.

Volga (Vomma)

Definition: The rate of change of Vega with respect to implied vol. The second derivative of value with respect to vol.

$$\text{Volga} = \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial \sigma^2} = \frac{\partial \nu}{\partial \sigma}$$

  • What it tells you: Is your Vega exposure convex or concave? If vol moves a lot, does your Vega position get better or worse?
  • Application:
  • Long OTM options have positive Volga — they benefit from vol-of-vol
  • Important for exotic options and structured products
  • Part of the SABR smile: the Volga term drives the wings of the vol surface
  • What to watch:
  • Less relevant for daily monitoring in a discretionary fund (more of a derivatives desk concern)
  • Matters if PMs are trading vol-of-vol directly (VIX options, variance swaps)
  • Implementation: Track for options-heavy books. Usually bundled into the Vega report rather than independently monitored.

1.2 VALUE AT RISK (VaR)

Definition

VaR answers: "What is the maximum loss over a given time horizon at a given confidence level?"

$$\text{VaR}_{\alpha} = -\inf{x : P(L \leq x) \geq \alpha}$$

In English: the 1-day 99% VaR of $5M means "there is a 99% probability that the portfolio won't lose more than $5M in one day."

Three Methods

1. Historical VaR

  • Take actual historical returns (e.g., last 500 trading days)
  • Apply those returns to today's portfolio
  • Sort the resulting P&L from worst to best
  • The 1st percentile (for 99% VaR) is your VaR

$$\text{VaR}{99\%} = -\text{Percentile}{1\%}(\text{historical P\&L distribution})$$

  • Pros: No distributional assumptions, captures fat tails, captures correlations naturally
  • Cons: Assumes the past predicts the future, slow to react to regime changes, limited by available history
  • Implementation: Daily calculation. Use at least 1-2 years of history. Consider weighting recent observations more heavily (EWMA-weighted historical VaR).

2. Parametric (Variance-Covariance) VaR

  • Assume returns are normally distributed
  • Estimate mean $\mu$ and covariance matrix $\Sigma$ from historical data
  • For a portfolio with weights $w$:

$$\text{VaR}{\alpha} = -\left(\mu_p - z{\alpha} \cdot \sigma_p\right)$$

Where:
- $\mu_p = w^T \mu$ (portfolio expected return)
- $\sigma_p = \sqrt{w^T \Sigma w}$ (portfolio volatility)
- $z_{\alpha}$ = 2.326 for 99% VaR, 1.645 for 95% VaR

  • Pros: Fast, analytical, easy to decompose by risk factor
  • Cons: Assumes normality (FAILS in fat-tailed markets), linear positions only (breaks for options)
  • Implementation: Good for quick estimates and risk decomposition. Never rely on it alone for options-heavy books.

3. Monte Carlo VaR

  • Simulate thousands of potential scenarios by sampling from estimated distributions
  • Can use any distribution (not just normal) — Student-t, skewed distributions, copulas
  • For each scenario, reprice the full portfolio (including nonlinear instruments like options)

  • Sort the resulting P&L distribution, pick the percentile

  • Pros: Handles nonlinearity (options), can incorporate any distribution, most flexible

  • Cons: Computationally expensive, dependent on chosen model/distribution, slow
  • Implementation: This is what you built at BNP NYC. Run overnight for next-day reporting. Use for the options-heavy books.

VaR Metrics to Monitor

Metric Formula What It Tells You
Individual VaR VaR per PM How much each PM can lose
Component VaR $\text{CVaR}_i = w_i \times \frac{\partial \text{VaR}}{\partial w_i}$ Each PM's marginal contribution to total fund VaR
Incremental VaR $\text{VaR}(\text{portfolio + new trade}) - \text{VaR}(\text{portfolio})$ Impact of adding a specific trade
Diversified VaR Total fund VaR < sum of individual VaRs The benefit of having uncorrelated PMs
Diversification benefit $1 - \frac{\text{Total VaR}}{\sum \text{Individual VaRs}}$ How much diversification saves you

VaR Limitations — Know These Cold

  1. VaR says nothing about losses beyond the threshold — the 99% VaR could be $5M, but the 99.5% loss could be $50M
  2. VaR assumes normal markets — in a crisis, correlations converge to 1, liquidity evaporates, and VaR models break
  3. VaR is backward-looking — it uses historical data that may not represent future risks
  4. VaR is NOT subadditive (for non-normal distributions) — meaning the sum of parts can be less than the whole, which is counterintuitive
  5. VaR can be gamed — a PM can construct a position that has low VaR but massive tail risk (e.g., selling deep OTM puts)

1.3 EXPECTED SHORTFALL (ES / CVaR)

Definition

Expected Shortfall answers: "IF we breach VaR, how bad is it on average?"

$$\text{ES}{\alpha} = -\frac{1}{1-\alpha} \int{\alpha}^{1} \text{VaR}_u \, du$$

In practice (discrete):
$$\text{ES}_{99\%} = -\text{Average of all losses beyond the 99th percentile}$$

  • If 99% VaR = $5M, but ES = $12M, that tells you the tail is heavy — when things go bad, they go REALLY bad
  • ES is subadditive — it doesn't have VaR's diversification problem
  • FRTB (Basel III.1) replaced VaR with ES as the primary regulatory risk measure for exactly this reason
  • What to watch: If ES is much larger than VaR (ratio > 2-3x), the portfolio has fat tail risk — usually from short options or concentrated positions

1.5 SENSITIVITY ANALYSIS & FACTOR DECOMPOSITION

Factor Models

Decompose portfolio risk into systematic factors:

$$R_p = \alpha + \beta_1 F_1 + \beta_2 F_2 + ... + \beta_n F_n + \epsilon$$

Common factor models:
- Fama-French: Market, Size (SMB), Value (HML), Profitability (RMW), Investment (CMA)
- Barra: Market, industry, style factors (momentum, vol, size, value, quality, leverage)
- Macro factors: GDP growth, inflation, rate level, rate slope, credit spread, oil price, VIX

Application:
- You can decompose a PM's return: how much came from the market factor (beta), how much from their specific picks (alpha)?
- If a PM's "alpha" turns out to be just disguised beta (they were long equities in a bull market), IC should know
- Also helps identify hidden factor exposures: "PM A says they're a stock picker, but 80% of their return variance is explained by the momentum factor"

Implementation:
- Run regression of each PM's daily returns against factor returns
- Report factor exposures (betas) and residual (alpha) to IC
- Flag when factor exposure drifts from the PM's stated strategy

Beta ($\beta$)

$$\beta_i = \frac{\text{Cov}(R_i, R_m)}{\text{Var}(R_m)}$$

  • Portfolio beta = weighted average of position betas
  • Beta = 1.5 means the portfolio moves 1.5x the market
  • What to watch: A "market neutral" PM should have beta near 0. If it drifts to 0.3, they're taking unintended market risk.
  • Dollar Beta = $\beta \times \text{NAV}$ — the equivalent market exposure

Correlation Risk

$$\rho_{X,Y} = \frac{\text{Cov}(X,Y)}{\sigma_X \cdot \sigma_Y}$$

  • Correlations are NOT stable — they change across regimes
  • In a crisis, correlations converge to 1 — diversification fails exactly when you need it most
  • What to watch:
  • Portfolio diversification benefit assumes current correlations. If correlations spike, your "diversified" portfolio is actually concentrated
  • Cross-PM correlation: if PM A and PM C are both implicitly long the same factor, you have concentration risk even if their instruments differ
  • Historical average correlation ≠ crisis correlation. Always stress test with elevated correlations

Implementation:
- Rolling correlation matrix (30-day, 60-day, 90-day windows)
- Correlation regime detection: use Chow test or rolling Z-score to flag when correlations break from historical norms
- This maps directly to the correlation monitor you have on your resume

1.6 LIQUIDITY RISK

Definition

The risk that you can't exit a position at a reasonable price when you need to.

Metrics

Bid-Ask Spread:
$$\text{Spread cost} = \frac{\text{Ask} - \text{Bid}}{\text{Mid}} \times \text{Position size}$$

Days to Liquidate:
$$\text{DTL} = \frac{\text{Position size}}{\text{Average daily volume} \times \text{Participation rate}}$$

  • Participation rate = what % of daily volume you're willing to be (typically 10-25%)
  • If DTL > 5 for any position, it's a liquidity concern

Liquidity-Adjusted VaR:
$$\text{LVaR} = \text{VaR} + \frac{1}{2} \times \text{Spread} \times \text{Position Value}$$

What to Watch

  • Illiquid positions in a crisis: bid-ask spreads blow out 5-10x, volume drops 80%, DTL goes from 2 to 20
  • Crowded trades: if everyone is in the same trade (e.g., short vol, long US tech), exit is jammed when everyone tries to leave simultaneously
  • Margin calls: when the portfolio drops, the prime broker demands more collateral. If you can't post it, they force-liquidate your positions at the worst possible time
  • Time zone liquidity: a European equity position can't be liquidated during Asian hours
  • Position concentration vs market: if the PM holds 5% of a bond's total outstanding, exiting is the market

Implementation

  • Daily liquidity report: DTL for every position. Flag anything > 5 days
  • Portfolio-level: "If we needed to liquidate 50% of the fund in 5 days, what's the cost?"
  • Monitor prime broker margin requirements and available credit lines

1.7 CONCENTRATION RISK

Metrics

Single-name concentration:
$$\text{Weight}_i = \frac{|\text{Position}_i|}{\text{Total Gross Exposure}}$$

  • Flag anything > 5% of gross. Alert at 10%.

HHI (Herfindahl-Hirschman Index):
$$\text{HHI} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i^2$$

  • HHI = 1 means one position is 100% of the portfolio
  • HHI < 0.1 means well diversified
  • Use HHI by: single name, sector, geography, asset class, PM

Sector/Geography concentration:
- No sector > 25% of gross (unless it's the strategy)
- No single country > 30% of gross

What to Watch

  • Cross-PM concentration: PM A is long oil, PM B is long energy stocks, PM C is long CAD (oil-correlated currency). Together, the fund is massively long energy — but no single PM sees it
  • Factor concentration: different instruments, same underlying bet. Long gold + long JPY + short equities = all "risk-off" positions
  • Maturity concentration: all positions expiring the same month = massive roll risk
  • Counterparty concentration: all OTC trades facing Goldman? What if Goldman has a credit event?

PART 2: MACRO RISK

Macro risk is the risk from large-scale economic and geopolitical events that move all markets.

2.1 INTEREST RATE RISK

Key Metrics

  • DV01 by tenor bucket: 0-2Y, 2-5Y, 5-10Y, 10-30Y
  • Duration: weighted average time to cash flows, measures rate sensitivity

$$\text{Modified Duration} = \frac{\text{Macaulay Duration}}{1 + y/n}$$

$$\Delta P \approx -D_{\text{mod}} \times \Delta y \times P$$

  • Convexity: second-order rate sensitivity. Important for large rate moves.

$$\Delta P \approx -D_{\text{mod}} \times \Delta y \times P + \frac{1}{2} \times C \times (\Delta y)^2 \times P$$

What to Watch

  • Curve risk: not just parallel shifts. Steepening, flattening, twist, butterfly
  • Real vs nominal rates: TIPS breakevens. Inflation expectations vs real rate moves
  • Central bank risk: FOMC, ECB, BOJ, BOE meetings. Forward guidance shifts
  • Rate vol: swaption vol, MOVE index (rates equivalent of VIX)

2.2 CREDIT RISK

Definition

Risk that a counterparty defaults or that credit spreads widen.

Metrics

Credit Spread DV01 (CS01):
$$\text{CS01} = \frac{\partial V}{\partial s} \times 0.0001$$

Where $s$ = credit spread. The P&L from a 1bp move in credit spreads.

Potential Future Exposure (PFE):
$$\text{PFE}{\alpha} = \text{Percentile}{\alpha}\left(\max(V_t, 0)\right)$$

  • The worst-case exposure to a counterparty at a future date
  • Used for OTC derivatives where the value can swing

Credit VaR:
- Loss distribution from rating transitions and defaults
- Uses transition matrices (probability of downgrade/upgrade/default)

What to Watch

  • Counterparty risk: who are you facing? If the PM trades OTC derivatives, the counterparty can default
  • Sovereign credit: European periphery, EM sovereign debt
  • Credit-equity correlation: in a crisis, credit and equity fall together. A "diversified" portfolio of credit + equities isn't diversified
  • CDS-bond basis: when CDS and bonds disagree on credit risk, someone is wrong

2.3 FX RISK

Metrics

  • Net FX exposure by currency: sum all positions denominated in each currency
  • Hedged vs unhedged: does the PM hedge the currency component of foreign positions?

What to Watch

  • Emerging market FX: can gap 10-20% overnight on capital controls, political crisis, or central bank intervention
  • Carry trade unwind: borrowing low-rate currency (JPY, CHF) to invest in high-rate (BRL, MXN). Works until it doesn't — then the unwind is violent (see JPY carry trade crash Aug 2024)
  • Dollar smile theory: USD strengthens in both risk-on (US growth outperformance) and risk-off (safe haven). Weakens in the middle (goldilocks global growth)
  • Hidden FX risk: a PM long European equities has hidden EUR/USD exposure even if they don't trade FX directly

2.4 COMMODITY RISK (Your Edge)

Unique Characteristics

  • Physical vs financial: commodity prices are driven by physical supply/demand, not just financial flows
  • Contango vs backwardation: futures curve shape affects roll return

$$\text{Roll yield} = \frac{F_1 - F_2}{F_1} \times \frac{365}{\text{days between contracts}}$$

  • Storage cost / convenience yield:

$$F = S \times e^{(r + u - y)T}$$

Where $u$ = storage cost, $y$ = convenience yield

  • Seasonality: natural gas (winter heating), crude oil (summer driving), agriculture (harvest)

What to Watch

  • Geopolitical supply disruption: Iran/Middle East (oil), Russia (gas), China rare earths
  • Weather: polar vortex (nat gas), drought (grains), hurricane season (gulf oil)
  • Inventory levels: EIA crude draws/builds, EU gas storage levels
  • Basis risk: the PM hedges WTI but the physical exposure is Brent or jet fuel — the spread can move against them
  • Position limits: CFTC/exchange limits on speculative positions in commodity futures

2.5 INFLATION RISK

Metrics

  • Breakeven inflation rate: difference between nominal yield and TIPS yield

$$\text{Breakeven} = y_{\text{nominal}} - y_{\text{real}}$$

  • Inflation beta: how the portfolio performs per unit of inflation surprise

What to Watch

  • Inflation regime: are we in a disinflationary (2010-2020) or inflationary (2021-2023) environment? Asset correlations behave completely differently
  • Stock-bond correlation: in low inflation, stocks and bonds are negatively correlated (bonds hedge equities). In high inflation, they become positively correlated (both fall together — 2022)
  • Wage-price spiral risk: if inflation becomes self-reinforcing, central banks overtighten, recession follows

2.6 GEOPOLITICAL RISK (Currently Elevated)

What to Watch — April 2026 Context

  • Iran conflict / Middle East escalation: oil supply disruption, shipping (Strait of Hormuz), risk-off contagion
  • US-China tensions: tech decoupling, Taiwan, tariffs, capital market restrictions
  • Russia-Ukraine: energy supply (European gas), sanctions, commodity flows
  • Election cycles: US midterms influence, European elections, EM political risk
  • Central bank divergence: Fed vs ECB vs BOJ policy divergence drives FX, rates, capital flows

Implementation

  • Don't try to predict geopolitics. Instead: scenario plan
  • For each geopolitical risk: "If X happens, what does it do to our portfolio?"
  • Map geopolitical events to market impacts: Iran escalation → oil +20% → risk-off equities -10% → vol spike → gold +10% → UST rally
  • Run the scenario through the stress testing framework weekly

PART 3: OPERATIONAL RISK

Operational risk is the risk of loss from failed processes, people, systems, or external events.

3.1 TRADE EXECUTION RISK

  • Fat finger trades: wrong size, wrong direction, wrong instrument. A misplaced zero turns a $1M trade into $10M
  • Slippage: getting a worse price than expected due to market impact, latency, or poor algo selection
  • Settlement failures: trade fails to settle (T+1 for equities, T+2 for FX), creating unexpected exposure
  • Implementation: Pre-trade checks (size limits, price reasonableness), 4-eyes principle (dual approval for large trades), post-trade reconciliation

3.2 MODEL RISK

  • Wrong model: using Black-Scholes for a product that needs stochastic vol (like SABR)
  • Stale parameters: model calibrated on old data that doesn't reflect current regime
  • Overfitting: backtest looks great, but the model was fit to noise, not signal
  • Implementation: Independent model validation (separate from the team that builds the model — your Natixis experience). Regular recalibration. Out-of-sample testing.

3.3 RECONCILIATION RISK

  • Position breaks: what the risk system thinks you hold ≠ what the prime broker says you hold
  • P&L unexplained: daily P&L doesn't match what Greeks predict — either the model is wrong, positions are wrong, or market data is wrong
  • Implementation: Daily position reconciliation between internal systems and prime broker. Investigate and resolve breaks same day. Track "P&L explain" ratio (should be > 95%).

3.4 COUNTERPARTY / PRIME BROKER RISK

  • Margin calls: prime broker demands more collateral. If you can't post it, forced liquidation
  • Rehypothecation: the prime broker re-uses your collateral. If THEY fail, you lose it (Lehman taught this lesson)
  • Concentration: using a single prime broker creates dependency risk
  • Implementation: Monitor margin utilization daily. Maintain excess margin buffer. Diversify across 2-3 prime brokers. Stress test: what if margin requirements increase 50%?

3.5 REGULATORY / COMPLIANCE RISK

  • Position limits: CFTC (commodities), SEC (equities), exchange limits
  • Short selling restrictions: some jurisdictions ban short selling in crisis. PM might be unable to hedge
  • Reporting requirements: 13F (quarterly, equities > $100M), Form PF (quarterly, systemic risk), EMIR (European trade reporting)
  • Insider trading / information barriers: discretionary PMs with industry contacts risk receiving MNPI (material non-public information)
  • Implementation: Automated position limit monitoring. Compliance pre-clearance for trades in restricted names. Training on MNPI policies.

3.6 PEOPLE / KEY PERSON RISK

  • PM departure: if a PM leaves, their book might need to be unwound (and unwinding IS the risk — fire sale)
  • Key man clause: investors may have the right to redeem if a senior PM leaves
  • Succession planning: what happens if the CIO or a founder leaves?

PART 4: TECHNOLOGY RISK

At Squarepoint especially, where technology is described as "integral to success."

4.1 SYSTEM RISK

  • Data feed failure: Bloomberg/exchange data stops flowing. Your risk numbers become stale. PMs are trading blind
  • Pricing engine failure: can't reprice the portfolio → can't calculate P&L or risk → can't monitor limits
  • Execution system failure: orders can't be sent. Or worse — orders sent but confirmations not received (ghost orders)
  • Implementation: Redundancy (backup data feeds, fallback systems). Circuit breakers. Manual override procedures. Disaster recovery testing.

4.2 DATA INTEGRITY RISK

  • Bad market data: a price spike that's actually a data error → triggers false risk alerts, or worse, triggers automated stops
  • Historical data errors: backtest results corrupted by survivorship bias, incorrect corporate actions (splits, dividends), stale prices for illiquid instruments
  • Position data errors: trade being booked in wrong account, wrong multiplier on a futures contract
  • Implementation: Data quality checks (range checks, velocity checks, cross-source validation). Flag prices that move > 3σ for manual review.

4.3 CYBERSECURITY RISK

  • Data breach: leaked positions = front-running by competitors
  • Ransomware: cripples trading operations
  • Social engineering: phishing targeting employees with access to trading systems
  • Implementation: Multi-factor auth, network segmentation, regular penetration testing, employee training. Not your daily job as risk manager, but you need to understand it for operational risk assessment.

4.4 LATENCY / PERFORMANCE RISK

  • Slow risk calculations: if your VaR takes 4 hours to compute, you're reporting yesterday's risk
  • Real-time monitoring lag: if your dashboard updates every 15 minutes, you miss the flash crash
  • Implementation: Optimize risk calculations (vectorized Python, kdb+ for data retrieval). Tiered approach: fast approximate risk (real-time) + full accurate risk (end-of-day).

PART 6: KEY RATIOS AND PERFORMANCE METRICS

Metric Formula What It Tells IC
Sharpe Ratio $\frac{R_p - R_f}{\sigma_p}$ Risk-adjusted return. Good: > 1.0. Great: > 2.0
Sortino Ratio $\frac{R_p - R_f}{\sigma_{\text{downside}}}$ Like Sharpe but only penalizes downside vol. Better for asymmetric strategies
Max Drawdown $\max_{t} \left(\frac{\text{Peak}_t - \text{Trough}_t}{\text{Peak}_t}\right)$ Worst peak-to-trough loss. IC sets limits on this per PM
Calmar Ratio $\frac{\text{Annual Return}}{\text{Max Drawdown}}$ Return per unit of worst-case loss
Win Rate $\frac{\text{Winning days}}{\text{Total days}}$ Not useful alone but flags style: a PM with 80% win rate + occasional huge loss = short vol strategy
Profit Factor $\frac{\sum \text{Winning P\&L}}{\sum \text{Losing P\&L}
Information Ratio $\frac{\alpha}{\sigma_{\text{residual}}}$ Alpha per unit of idiosyncratic risk. Measures PM skill vs their benchmark
Treynor Ratio $\frac{R_p - R_f}{\beta_p}$ Return per unit of systematic (market) risk
Tail Ratio $\frac{\text{Percentile}{95}}{\text{Percentile}{5}}$ Symmetry of returns. < 1 means fatter left tail (more crash risk)
VaR Utilization $\frac{\text{Current VaR}}{\text{VaR Limit}}$ How much risk budget the PM is using. > 80% = amber alert

PART 7: QUICK REFERENCE — Formulas Cheat Sheet

Options Pricing (Black-Scholes)

$$C = S \cdot N(d_1) - K e^{-rT} N(d_2)$$
$$P = K e^{-rT} N(-d_2) - S \cdot N(-d_1)$$

Where:
$$d_1 = \frac{\ln(S/K) + (r + \sigma^2/2)T}{\sigma\sqrt{T}}, \quad d_2 = d_1 - \sigma\sqrt{T}$$

Put-Call Parity

$$C - P = S - K e^{-rT}$$

SABR Implied Vol (Hagan Approximation)

$$\sigma_B(K,F) = \frac{\alpha}{(FK)^{(1-\beta)/2}} \cdot \frac{z}{x(z)} \cdot \left[1 + \left(\frac{(1-\beta)^2}{24}\frac{\alpha^2}{(FK)^{1-\beta}} + \frac{\rho\beta\nu\alpha}{4(FK)^{(1-\beta)/2}} + \frac{2-3\rho^2}{24}\nu^2\right)T\right]$$

Where $z = \frac{\nu}{\alpha}(FK)^{(1-\beta)/2}\ln(F/K)$ and $x(z) = \ln\left(\frac{\sqrt{1-2\rho z+z^2}+z-\rho}{1-\rho}\right)$

Monte Carlo VaR (Geometric Brownian Motion)

$$S_T = S_0 \exp\left[\left(\mu - \frac{\sigma^2}{2}\right)T + \sigma\sqrt{T} \cdot Z\right], \quad Z \sim N(0,1)$$

Portfolio Volatility (2 assets)

$$\sigma_p = \sqrt{w_1^2\sigma_1^2 + w_2^2\sigma_2^2 + 2w_1 w_2 \rho_{12}\sigma_1\sigma_2}$$

N-asset Portfolio Volatility

$$\sigma_p = \sqrt{w^T \Sigma w}$$

Exponentially Weighted Moving Average (EWMA) Volatility

$$\sigma_t^2 = \lambda \sigma_{t-1}^2 + (1-\lambda) r_{t-1}^2$$

RiskMetrics default: $\lambda = 0.94$ (daily), $\lambda = 0.97$ (monthly)

Duration-Based Hedge Ratio

$$N_{\text{hedge}} = \frac{D_{\text{portfolio}} \times V_{\text{portfolio}}}{D_{\text{hedge}} \times V_{\text{hedge}}}$$

Information Ratio

$$IR = \frac{\bar{\alpha}}{\sigma_{\alpha}} = \frac{R_p - R_b}{\text{Tracking Error}}$$

Kelly Criterion (Optimal Position Sizing)

$$f^* = \frac{p \cdot b - q}{b} = \frac{\mu}{\sigma^2}$$

Where $p$ = probability of win, $b$ = win/loss ratio, $q = 1-p$. In practice, use half-Kelly for safety.

PART 9: OVERLOOKED & ADVANCED RISKS

These are the risks that junior risk managers miss and that interviewers love to test on.

9.1 GAP RISK

  • Definition: The risk that prices jump discontinuously — overnight, over weekends, during market closures — bypassing your stop-loss
  • Markets are not continuous. They close at 4 PM and open at 9:30 AM. In between, geopolitical events happen.
  • Example: PM has a stop loss at -$2M on their position. On Friday the position is at -$1.5M. Over the weekend, war escalates. Monday open, the position is at -$8M. The stop was never triggered.
  • Why it matters: VaR and Greeks assume continuous price movements. Gaps break that assumption.
  • Implementation: Run overnight gap scenarios: "What if the market gaps ±5% on open?" Weekend risk reports before market close on Friday. Require PMs to reduce over weekends if gap exposure is large.

9.2 FUNDING / FINANCING RISK

  • Definition: The risk that the cost of borrowing to fund leveraged positions increases, or that financing becomes unavailable
  • Hedge funds use leverage. Leverage requires borrowing. Borrowing has a cost.
  • Repo rate risk: Short positions require borrowing securities (repo). If the repo rate spikes (hard-to-borrow), the cost of maintaining the short eats into P&L
  • Margin financing cost: Prime brokers charge financing rates on leveraged long positions. If rates spike (or PB raises rates), the carry cost of the book increases
  • Rehypothecation withdrawal: PB can change lending terms with short notice, forcing position reduction
  • Why it matters: A PM's trade might be profitable on a gross basis but unprofitable after financing costs. Rising rates make leveraged strategies more expensive across the board.
  • Implementation: Track daily financing cost by PM. Include financing in P&L attribution. Monitor prime broker lending terms. Stress test: what if financing cost doubles?

9.3 CROWDING / CROWDED TRADE RISK

  • Definition: The risk that too many market participants hold the same position, making exit impossible without massive losses
  • How to detect:
  • 13F filings: are all hedge funds in the same names?
  • Short interest data: extremely high short interest = crowded short
  • Options open interest concentration: everyone positioned the same way
  • Factor crowding: if all PMs tilt toward the same factor (e.g., momentum), the unwind is correlated
  • Why it matters: Crowded trades unwind violently. When one fund sells, prices drop, other funds hit stop-losses, they sell, prices drop more — a cascade. See: the 2021 GameStop short squeeze, 2007 quant quake.
  • Implementation: Monitor crowding indicators (short interest, 13F overlap, options OI). Flag to PM when a trade looks crowded. Stress test "crowded trade unwind" scenario.

9.4 BASIS RISK (Expanded)

  • Definition: The risk that a hedge doesn't track the underlying exposure perfectly
  • Examples:
  • Hedging jet fuel with crude oil futures — the crack spread can move against you
  • Hedging European equities with S&P futures — correlation breaks in a crisis
  • Hedging single stocks with ETF/index — the stock diverges from the index
  • Hedging credit with CDS — the CDS-bond basis can blow out (it went to 200bp+ in 2008)
  • Why it matters: The PM thinks they're hedged. They're not. The hedge can lose money on BOTH sides simultaneously.
  • Implementation: Track basis between every hedge and its underlying exposure. Report "hedge effectiveness" ratio. Stress test: what if the basis doubles?

9.5 ROLL RISK

  • Definition: The risk associated with rolling futures contracts from one expiry to the next
  • Why it exists: Most futures positions must be rolled before expiry (you don't want physical delivery of 1,000 barrels of oil). Rolling means selling the near contract and buying the far contract.
  • Cost:
  • In contango (far > near): rolling costs money — you sell low, buy high
  • In backwardation (near > far): rolling earns money — you sell high, buy low
  • The curve shape can change, turning a profitable carry trade into a loss
  • Extreme example: April 2020 — WTI front month went NEGATIVE (-$37/barrel) because nobody could take physical delivery and the roll was impossible
  • Implementation: Track roll dates for all futures positions. Calculate projected roll cost. Flag large positions approaching expiry. Monitor curve shape changes.

9.6 PIN RISK & ASSIGNMENT RISK

  • Pin risk: When an option expires exactly at-the-money. You don't know if you'll be assigned or not. This creates an uncertain Delta position overnight that you can't hedge.
  • Assignment risk: American-style options can be exercised early. The PM might suddenly find themselves with a position they didn't expect (especially around dividend dates for equity options — early exercise to capture the dividend).
  • Implementation: Monitor all options within 1% of strike approaching expiry. Flag American options near ex-dividend dates. Require PMs to close or roll options before the final day if possible.

9.7 DIVIDEND RISK

  • Definition: The risk from unexpected or changed dividend payments, particularly for equity derivatives
  • An option's price includes expected dividends. If a company cancels, increases, or changes its dividend timing:
  • Call holders lose if dividend increases (stock drops more on ex-date)
  • Put holders gain if dividend increases
  • Equity swap payments change
  • COVID March 2020: Hundreds of companies cancelled dividends simultaneously. This was an unmodeled shock to equity derivatives.
  • Implementation: Track expected dividends for all equity underliers. Flag any corporate action announcements that might affect dividends.

9.8 WRONG-WAY RISK

  • Definition: The risk that exposure to a counterparty increases at exactly the moment the counterparty's credit quality deteriorates
  • Example: You bought a CDS (credit protection) from Bank A on Bank B. But Bank A and Bank B are both European banks. If there's a European banking crisis, Bank B's credit deteriorates (your CDS should pay out) AND Bank A might default (so they can't pay you). The hedge fails exactly when you need it.
  • Opposite: Right-way risk is when exposure decreases as counterparty credit deteriorates.
  • Implementation: Map counterparty exposures against counterparty credit drivers. Flag trades where the two are correlated.

9.9 CONVEXITY RISK (Beyond Gamma)

  • Definition: The risk from nonlinear P&L profiles that aren't captured by first-order sensitivities
  • Delta and Vega are linear approximations. The ACTUAL P&L for large moves includes higher-order terms.
  • Where it bites:
  • Mortgage-backed securities: negative convexity (prepayment acceleration when rates fall)
  • Callable bonds: negative convexity above the call price
  • Convertible bonds: positive convexity (bond floor + equity upside)
  • Options near expiry: convexity explodes
  • Implementation: Always look at the FULL P&L graph, not just Greeks. Run scenario ladders (±1%, ±2%, ±5%, ±10%) and plot the P&L. If it's not close to linear, the Greeks are lying.

9.10 REDEMPTION / LIQUIDITY MISMATCH RISK

  • Definition: The risk that investors redeem (withdraw money) faster than the fund can liquidate positions
  • The problem: Investors might have quarterly or monthly redemption rights. But some positions take weeks to exit. If 30% of investors redeem simultaneously, you might need to fire-sale illiquid positions.
  • Why it matters: Forced liquidation in the worst market conditions = crystallizing losses. And the remaining investors bear the cost of the liquidation — creating incentive for MORE redemptions (run on the fund).
  • Defensive tools: Lock-up periods, gates (cap withdrawal at 10-25% per quarter), side pockets (segregate illiquid assets)
  • Implementation: Track investor redemption windows. Monitor fund-level liquidity against potential redemption scenarios. Maintain a liquidity buffer (cash or near-cash positions).

9.11 SKEW RISK

  • Definition: The risk from changes in the shape of the implied volatility surface — not just the level (Vega) but the slope (skew) and curvature (kurtosis)
  • ATM vol can be unchanged while skew shifts dramatically. If you're long OTM puts and skew flattens, you lose money even though "vol" didn't move.
  • Risk reversal: The price of a 25-delta call minus a 25-delta put. Measures directional skew.
  • Butterfly: The cost of OTM options vs ATM. Measures kurtosis pricing / wing richness.
  • Implementation: Monitor skew metrics (25D risk reversal, 25D butterfly) for every underlier where the PM has options. Flag significant skew changes. Decompose Vega into ATM Vega + skew Vega + wing Vega.

9.12 REGIME CHANGE RISK

  • Definition: The risk that the statistical properties of markets fundamentally change, invalidating models calibrated on historical data
  • Examples:
  • 2020-2024: zero rates → high rates. All rate-sensitive models needed recalibration
  • Stock-bond correlation flip: negative for 20 years, turned positive in 2022
  • Commodity supercycle: oil went from $20 (2020) to $120 (2022) to $80 — different regimes each time
  • Volatility regime: low vol (VIX 12-15) → high vol (VIX 25-40). Position sizing changes
  • Why it matters: VaR, correlations, factor models, and risk forecasts are all estimated from the RECENT past. If the regime changes, the recent past is the wrong dataset.
  • Implementation: Monitor for regime change indicators: structural breaks in vol, correlation, or mean levels. Use regime-switching models or at minimum, compare risk from different historical windows (last 6 months vs last 2 years vs last 5 years). If they diverge significantly, the regime is shifting.

9.13 SANCTIONS / ASSET FREEZE RISK

  • Definition: The risk that government sanctions make a position untradeable or worthless overnight
  • Example: Russia sanctions in Feb 2022 — investors holding Russian equities, bonds, or ruble positions were frozen. Some positions are STILL frozen years later.
  • Implementation: Monitor sanctioned entity lists (OFAC, EU). Flag any exposure to jurisdictions under sanction risk. Include "sanctions escalation" in geopolitical stress scenarios.

9.14 LEVERAGE RISK

  • Definition: Amplification of both gains and losses through borrowed money
  • Gross leverage = (Long exposure + |Short exposure|) / NAV
  • Net leverage = (Long exposure − |Short exposure|) / NAV
  • A portfolio with 3x gross leverage turns a 5% market move into a 15% portfolio move
  • Compounding effect: Leverage + compounding over multiple days can produce unexpected results (volatility drag / leverage decay)

$$\text{Leveraged return over N days} \neq \text{Leverage} \times \text{Unleveraged return over N days}$$

  • Implementation: Track gross and net leverage daily, by PM and fund-wide. Set IC limits on both. Monitor leverage against market vol — high leverage in high vol = danger.
