U — CASE STUDIES: BLOW-UPS & LESSONS

Learn from the dead so you don't join them

Depth: 🔴 DEEP

HOW TO USE THIS FILE

For each case study, internalize:
1. What happened — facts, timeline, magnitudes
2. The risk failure chain — the sequence of breakdowns that turned a loss into a catastrophe
3. What the RM should have caught — the specific signals that were visible BEFORE the blow-up
4. The systemic lesson — the principle that applies to ANY fund, ANY market
5. Your interview angle — the 60-second version that demonstrates risk judgment

The goal is NOT to memorize dates. It's to build a mental library of failure patterns so that when you see the SAME PATTERN forming today, you recognize it instantly.

PART 1: LTCM (1998) — LEVERAGE + CORRELATION + MODEL ARROGANCE

Timeline & Facts

Date Event
1994 Fund launches. Meriwether + Scholes + Merton (Nobel laureates). Initial capital: $1.25B.
1994-97 Annualized returns ~40%. Capital grows to $4.7B. Strategy: convergence/relative value across global bonds.
Early 1998 Leverage: ~25:1. $4.7B equity → $125B assets → $1.25T notional derivatives.
Aug 17, 1998 Russia defaults on domestic debt, devalues ruble.
Aug-Sep 1998 Global flight to quality. ALL spreads widen simultaneously. Every LTCM position loses.
Sep 23, 1998 Fed-organized rescue: 14 banks contribute $3.6B. LTCM wound down over 12 months.
Final loss ~$4.6B — essentially 100% of equity.

The Risk Failure Chain

High leverage (25:1)
    ↓
Illiquid positions (EM debt, off-the-run Treasuries, swap spreads)
    ↓
Russian default triggers global flight to quality
    ↓
ALL spread trades widen simultaneously (ρ → 1)
    ↓
"Diversified" portfolio becomes a single bet on spread compression
    ↓
Margin calls → can't liquidate without moving the market
    ↓
Every other convergence fund has the same positions → no buyers
    ↓
$4.6B loss, systemic bailout required

Quantitative Anatomy

Metric Value Why it matters
Leverage 25:1 (assets/equity) $1 of equity supporting $25 of positions
Effective leverage (notional) ~250:1 ($1.25T / $4.7B) True risk exposure was astronomical
Daily VaR (their model) $45M at 99% Model said max daily loss ~$45M
Actual daily losses in Aug-Sep $100-500M VaR was off by 3-10x
Correlation assumption ρ ≈ 0.1-0.3 across strategies In crisis, ρ jumped to 0.8-0.95
Monthly loss (August 1998) -$1.85B (-44% of equity) VaR model said this was ~20σ — "impossible"

What the RM Should Have Caught

Signal Available when What it meant Action
Leverage at 25:1 with illiquid assets Continuously No margin of safety — ANY adverse move is fatal Hard leverage cap (10:1 for illiquid), enforced daily
All positions loading on "spread compression" Q1 1998 Diversification was illusory — one factor drives everything Factor decomposition showing 85%+ variance from one factor
Funding from 28 counterparties but all with same margin terms Continuously In stress, all call margin simultaneously Stagger maturities, maintain undrawn credit lines
Liquidity of positions vs liquidation timeline Continuously Positions needed 3+ months to exit; fund could survive 3 weeks Liquidity-adjusted VaR; position limits based on days-to-exit, not notional
VaR model assuming normal returns and stable ρ Continuously Fat tails and crisis correlations make VaR useless at the extremes Supplement VaR with stress tests using historical ρ spikes (92, 94 bond rout)
Other funds replicating the same trades 1997-98 Crowding → when one unwinds, all must unwind → no exit Monitor market positioning; cap position as % of market open interest

The Deep Lesson

Leverage is a liquidation timer, not a return amplifier. LTCM's trades mostly converged eventually — many of their positions were profitable by 2000. But "eventually right" doesn't matter if you're dead in September. The RM's question is never "will this converge?" but "can we survive the path to convergence?"

$$\text{Survival} = f\left(\frac{\text{Equity cushion}}{\text{Maximum drawdown path}}\right)$$

If the maximum drawdown path exceeds equity at ANY point, the fund dies — regardless of terminal value.

PART 2: ARCHEGOS (2021) — COUNTERPARTY BLIND SPOTS + CONCENTRATION

Timeline & Facts

Date Event
2013 Bill Hwang's Tiger Asia pleads guilty to insider trading. Hwang banned from Hong Kong markets.
2020 Hwang's family office (Archegos) builds concentrated positions via Total Return Swaps (TRS) with 6 prime brokers.
Dec 2020-Mar 2021 ViacomCBS, Discovery, Baidu, GSX Techedu run up 100-400%. Archegos is the whale.
Mar 22, 2021 ViacomCBS announces stock offering → stock drops 9%.
Mar 24-25 Margin calls. Hwang can't meet them.
Mar 26 (Fri) Goldman, Morgan Stanley, Deutsche begin liquidating $20B+ in block trades.
Mar 29 (Mon) Credit Suisse and Nomura still holding.
Final losses Credit Suisse: $5.5B. Nomura: $2.9B. Goldman/MS: minimal (sold first). Total: ~$10B.

The Risk Failure Chain

Family office → no 13F filing → invisible to market
    ↓
Total Return Swaps → positions don't appear in equity filings
    ↓
6 PBs, each seeing only their slice → no one sees the aggregate ($30B+ notional)
    ↓
Massive concentrated position: 50%+ of float in some names
    ↓
ViacomCBS equity raise → stock drops → margin calls
    ↓
Hwang can't meet margin → forced liquidation
    ↓
ALL PBs need to sell the SAME stocks simultaneously
    ↓
First-mover advantage: Goldman exits fast (Fri), CS exits slow (Mon) → $5.5B loss

Critical Counterparty Risk Dimensions

Dimension What failed What should have existed
Aggregate visibility No PB knew Hwang's total exposure across all PBs Cross-PB information sharing agreements or concentration limits
Client due diligence CS gave Hwang favorable margin DESPITE SEC conviction Enhanced due diligence: prior enforcement = tighter terms, period
Concentration per name Some names: Archegos held >50% of float via swaps Position limit: max 10% of average daily volume per client per name
Margin methodology Static margin rates not reflecting crowding risk Dynamic margin: increase requirements as position grows relative to float
Liquidation protocol CS waited days to liquidate, hoping for recovery Pre-agreed automatic liquidation at margin breach. No discretion.
First-mover dynamics Goldman sold Friday night; CS waited until Monday Game theory: in multi-PB margin calls, speed = survival

Worked Example — Measuring Concentration Risk

Archegos held ~$10B of ViacomCBS through TRS across 6 PBs.
ViacomCBS market cap at peak: ~$60B. Free float: ~$30B. ADV: ~$500M.

Position as % of float: $10B / $30B = 33%
Days to exit at 10% participation: $10B / ($500M × 0.10) = 200 days
Days to exit at 25% participation: $10B / ($500M × 0.25) = 80 days

Conclusion: This position is fundamentally unliquidatable in a stress scenario. At any meaningful participation rate, the selling pressure would destroy the stock. This alone should have been a red flag at EVERY prime broker.

The Deep Lesson

You can't manage risk you can't see. Archegos exploited the seams between institutions. At Squarepoint, the analog is cross-PM exposure: PM A and PM B might each hold positions that look fine individually but create catastrophic aggregate concentration. The RM who only looks at individual books will miss the same failure that killed Credit Suisse's PB desk.

PART 3: BARINGS BANK (1995) — OPERATIONAL RISK + GOVERNANCE FAILURE

Timeline & Facts

Date Event
1992 Nick Leeson posted to Singapore. Role: arbitrage Nikkei 225 futures between SIMEX and Osaka.
1992-94 Leeson takes directional bets, hides losses in "Error Account 88888." Reports profits to London.
Jan 17, 1995 Kobe earthquake. Nikkei drops 1,000 points. Leeson's long Nikkei positions implode.
Feb 23, 1995 Leeson flees Singapore. Total losses discovered: £827M.
Feb 26, 1995 Barings Bank declared insolvent. 233 years old. Sold to ING for £1.

The Risk Failure Chain

Leeson runs BOTH front office (trading) AND back office (settlement)
    ↓
No separation of duties → he can hide his own trades
    ↓
Losses hidden in Error Account 88888 (since 1992)
    ↓
London sees reported "profits" from arbitrage → Leeson treated as star
    ↓
No one asks: "How is arb generating 20%+ returns?"
    ↓
Kobe earthquake (Jan 1995) → Nikkei crashes
    ↓
Leeson doubles down → writes strangles to finance margin on longs
    ↓
Losses explode: £827M → more than Barings' entire equity (£540M)
    ↓
Bank collapses. 233-year institution → sold for £1.

Operational Risk Red Flags

Red flag Why it should have been caught RM principle
Same person trading + settling Zero internal controls on position reporting Segregation of duties: trader can NEVER reconcile their own book
Arbitrage "profits" of £28M in 1994 Nikkei arb should make 2-5%, not 20%+ Too-good-to-be-true test: when returns exceed strategy capacity, investigate
Growing margin calls from SIMEX Arb should require constant margin; growing margin = directional position Independent margin reconciliation: match exchange margin calls to reported positions
Error account with £50M balance Error accounts should be near-zero, reconciled daily Error account policy: any balance > $X requires sign-off within 24 hours
Leeson requested £742M in "financing" from London £742M for an arb book? The notional makes no sense. Funding requests must reconcile with reported positions
Internal audit (1994) flagged dual role as "a significant risk" The audit was done AND IGNORED Audit follow-up: findings must have remediation deadlines + escalation

The Deep Lesson

Operational risk is the risk that nobody wants to talk about until it kills you. Every control that Barings lacked is now standard at fund administrators and prime brokers. But the principle remains: if you rely on a trader's self-reported P&L without independent verification, you're one rogue trader away from £827M. At Squarepoint, verify positions against PB statements, not trader reports.

PART 4: JP MORGAN "LONDON WHALE" (2012) — MODEL GAMING + HEDGE DRIFT

Timeline & Facts

Date Event
2011 Bruno Iksil in JPM's Chief Investment Office (CIO) builds credit derivative positions as "portfolio hedge."
Q4 2011 Position grows to ~$51B notional in CDX IG and HY tranches.
Jan 2012 CIO changes VaR model → reported VaR drops from $129M to $67M overnight.
Mar 2012 Position grows to ~$157B notional. Market participants notice → "London Whale."
Apr 6, 2012 Bloomberg/WSJ publish stories about "the Whale." JPM CEO Dimon calls it "a tempest in a teapot."
May 10, 2012 JPM discloses $2B in losses (later grows to $6.2B). Dimon: "egregious mistakes."
CIO total loss $6.2B

The Risk Failure Chain

CIO tasked with "hedging" JPM's overall credit portfolio
    ↓
Position starts as legitimate hedge → generates P&L
    ↓
Management likes the P&L → position treated as profit center
    ↓
Position grows: $51B → $157B notional (200% increase in 3 months)
    ↓
Size becomes too large → market participants front-run it
    ↓
VaR model changed (Jan 2012): $129M → $67M overnight
    ↓
New model hides true risk → limits not breached on paper
    ↓
Market moves against position → losses accelerate
    ↓
CIO marks positions using favorable internal prices (mark-to-model gaming)
    ↓
$6.2B loss + regulatory action + CEO credibility destroyed

Model Gaming — The Critical Lesson

Model change What it hid What should have happened
VaR formula revised → hazard weighting changed Risk looked 50% lower overnight Model changes require independent validation team approval BEFORE live deployment
Confidence interval adjusted Tail losses disappeared from reporting Any change that reduces reported risk by >10% triggers automatic review
P&L marks moved from external to internal pricing Losses understated by $660M Independent price verification (IPV) must use external/market prices, not trader marks

The "VaR Halving" test: If a risk metric drops >20% without any change in position, it's ALWAYS a red flag. Either the model changed (suspicious) or the market changed (investigate). A 50% drop with no position change = guaranteed model gaming or calculation error.

The "Hedge Drift" Problem

This case defines the concept of hedge drift — when a legitimate hedge morphs into a speculative position:

Phase Size Purpose P&L attribution RM should say
1 — Hedge $20B Offset bank's credit exposure ~$0 (hedge works) "Fine — hedge ratio is appropriate."
2 — Profitable hedge $51B Hedge + some P&L on basis +$400M "Hedge is now generating P&L. Size exceeds hedge need by 2x. Re-justify."
3 — Profit center $100B+ Actively trading credit for P&L +$1B cumulative "This is NOT a hedge. It's a proprietary position. Apply full risk limits."
4 — Trapped position $157B Too big to exit, market is front-running -$6.2B Crisis management.

RM principle: Any "hedge" generating more than ±10% of the hedged portfolio's P&L volatility has drifted into a directional trade. Reclassify and apply full limits.

PART 5: AMARANTH (2006) — CONCENTRATION + ILLUSORY DIVERSIFICATION

Timeline & Facts

Date Event
2005 Brian Hunter makes $1B+ from NG calendar spreads post-Hurricane Katrina. Star trader.
Jan 2006 Amaranth AUM: ~$9B. Multi-strategy fund. NG positions grow.
Aug 2006 Hunter's NG positions: ~$30B notional. Fund risk: >50% in one strategy.
Sep 14-20, 2006 NG winter-summer spread collapses. Losses: ~$6B in one week.
Sep 29, 2006 Fund liquidated. Lost 65% of NAV. Done.

The Risk Failure Chain

Hunter makes $1B in 2005 (Katrina) → management treats him as superstar
    ↓
"He's earned the right to size up" → limits RAISED to accommodate him
    ↓
$30B notional in NG calendar spreads → 50%+ of fund risk in one strategy
    ↓
Position = significant % of NG futures open interest → CAN'T EXIT
    ↓
NG spread moves against him in September
    ↓
Can't exit because position IS the market
    ↓
Other liquid positions sold to meet margin → those positions also lose
    ↓
$6B loss in one week → fund dead

Concentration Metrics That Should Have Screamed

Metric Threshold Amaranth actual Verdict
Single trader P&L as % of fund P&L < 30% > 70% CATASTROPHIC
Single strategy as % of fund VaR < 25% > 50% CATASTROPHIC
Position as % of NG open interest < 5% ~30% UNLIQUIDATABLE
Days to exit at 10% participation < 10 days > 60 days DEAD
Prior P&L used to justify current risk? Never "He made $1B last year" TEXTBOOK FAILURE

The "Moving Goalposts" Anti-Pattern

Date Risk limit Rationale What RM should have said
Jan 2005 $500M VaR Initial allocation "Appropriate for NG allocation."
Jun 2005 $750M VaR "Hunter is performing, deserves more" "Performance doesn't change risk. Market liquidity hasn't grown."
Dec 2005 $1.5B VaR "He made $1B this year" "STOP. Past profits are not risk capital. Limit stays at $750M."
Jun 2006 $2.5B VaR "This is our best strategy" "ESCALATE. We're a multi-strat fund with single-strat risk. IC override required."

The Deep Lesson

"He's earned the right to size up" is the most dangerous sentence in fund management. Past P&L is informative about the trader's skill but tells you NOTHING about future risk. The market doesn't know your YTD P&L. The next trade's risk is independent of the last trade's return. Any risk limit that gets raised because a trader is performing well is no longer a risk limit — it's a performance reward disguised as risk management.

PART 6: MARCH 2020 — TREASURY BASIS TRADE UNWIND

Timeline & Facts

Date Event
2018-20 Hedge funds build leveraged Treasury basis trades: long cash Treasuries, short Treasury futures. Leverage: 50-100x.
Feb 20, 2020 COVID sell-off begins. SPX starts falling. Treasuries initially rally (safe haven).
Mar 9, 2020 Oil crashes 30% (Saudi-Russia price war + COVID). Equities in freefall.
Mar 11-12, 2020 Treasury SELL-OFF begins. Cash Treasuries drop faster than futures. Basis WIDENS instead of converging.
Mar 12-16, 2020 Margin calls on basis trades. Forced selling of Treasuries. Bid-ask in on-the-run 10Y widens from 1/32 to 4/32.
Mar 15, 2020 Fed cuts to 0% + announces $700B QE. Not enough.
Mar 23, 2020 Fed announces UNLIMITED QE. Treasury market stabilizes.

The Risk Failure Chain

"Risk-free" convergence trade: long cash Treasury, short futures
    ↓
Leverage 50-100x (because spread is only 2-5bp)
    ↓
COVID sell-off → flight to quality → Treasuries initially RALLY (trade works)
    ↓
Then: PANIC selling → everyone sells everything INCLUDING Treasuries
    ↓
Cash Treasuries drop faster than futures → basis WIDENS → leveraged trade LOSES
    ↓
Margin calls on 50-100x leveraged positions
    ↓
Funds sell Treasuries to meet margin → more selling → basis widens further
    ↓
Treasury market liquidity EVAPORATES
    ↓
Fed must intervene with unlimited QE to prevent Treasury market collapse
    ↓
The "safest" asset class nearly broke the financial system

Leverage Math That Kills

Metric Normal March 2020
10Y Treasury bid-ask 1/32 ($312.50 per $1M face) 4-8/32 (up to $2,500)
Basis spread (cash-futures) 2-5bp -15 to -25bp (NEGATIVE — complete inversion)
Margin requirement 1-2% of notional 4-6% of notional (2-3x increase)
Fund equity as % of position 1-2% (at 50-100x leverage) ZERO — margin calls exceed equity
Market depth (10Y) $300M per 1bp < $50M per 1bp

The lethal formula:
$$\text{Leverage} \times \text{Adverse basis move} > \text{Equity}$$
$$50\text{x} \times 25\text{bp adverse} = 12.5\% \text{ of notional loss} \gg 1\text{-}2\% \text{ equity}$$

The fund is dead 5x over before the position has a CHANCE to converge.

The Deep Lesson

Path risk — not terminal risk — kills levered trades. The basis trade converges at maturity BY DEFINITION. Every fund that blew up would have been profitable if they could have held to maturity. But they couldn't, because margin calls forced them out. The RM question is always: "Can we survive the worst path to the destination?" If the answer requires "markets stay liquid" or "correlations stay stable," the answer is NO.

PART 7: NEGATIVE OIL (APRIL 2020) — MODEL ASSUMPTION FAILURE

Timeline & Facts

Date Event
Jan-Mar 2020 COVID lockdowns destroy oil demand. Global demand drops ~30%.
Mar 2020 Saudi-Russia price war adds supply. Oil collapses from $65 to $20.
Apr 2-17, 2020 Cushing, OK storage fills to 80%+. Front-month WTI at $20.
Apr 17 (Fri) May WTI futures: $18.27. Expiry: April 21.
Apr 20 (Mon) May WTI crashes to -$37.63 — the first negative commodity price in history.
Apr 21 (Tue) May contract expires. Anyone still holding must take physical delivery.

The Risk Failure Chain

COVID + price war → oil demand collapses 30%
    ↓
Storage fills up globally. Cushing at 80%+ capacity.
    ↓
May WTI = PHYSICAL delivery contract. Holding to expiry = receive 1,000 barrels.
    ↓
No physical storage available → no one wants delivery
    ↓
Sellers MUST exit May contract before expiry
    ↓
Buyers know sellers are desperate → bid collapses
    ↓
Price goes BELOW ZERO → you PAY someone to take oil
    ↓
Risk models with $0 floor: BROKEN. P&L calculations: WRONG. Greeks: UNDEFINED.

Model Failure Anatomy

Assumption Why it existed Why it failed Fix
"Oil can't go below $0" Commodity has intrinsic value Storage at capacity → holding cost > commodity value Remove hard floors; model storage cost as negative option
"Smooth price distribution" Normal/log-normal returns Physical delivery creates discontinuity at expiry Model physical delivery mechanics for near-dated contracts
"Futures converge to spot" Arbitrage keeps them aligned When arb is impossible (no storage), basis explodes Track storage capacity as a risk input
"Roll risk is small" Historically true for liquid contracts USO needed to roll 80K contracts in 4 days → front-ran Monitor ETF roll mechanics as a separate risk factor

USO ETF: The Amplifier

USO (United States Oil Fund) held ~80,000 near-month WTI futures contracts ($4B notional). It was forced to roll from May to June in a 4-day window. The market KNEW this.

Impact:
- Market makers sold May and bought June ahead of USO → widened the calendar spread by $15+
- USO's rolling losses were approximately $1.5B+ over the roll period
- Retail investors holding USO thought they were "buying cheap oil" — they were actually funding market-maker spread extraction

RM lesson: Any position large enough that its MECHANICS (roll, rebalance, expiry) are predictable to the market is at risk of being front-run. Track position as % of open interest AND as % of daily volume.

PART 8: UK GILT CRISIS (SEPTEMBER 2022) — MARGIN DOOM LOOP

Timeline & Facts

Date Event
Sep 6, 2022 New UK PM Liz Truss appoints Kwasi Kwarteng as Chancellor.
Sep 23, 2022 "Mini-budget": £45B in unfunded tax cuts announced. Markets react violently.
Sep 23-28, 2022 30Y gilt yield: 3.7% → 5.1% (+140bp in 3 days). GBP -5% vs USD.
Sep 28, 2022 Bank of England emergency intervention: temporary unlimited gilt purchases.
Oct 14, 2022 Kwarteng fired. Mini-budget reversed. Truss resigns Oct 20 (45 days PM).

The LDI Doom Loop

The crisis was CAUSED by pension fund risk management structures (LDI — Liability-Driven Investment):

Mini-budget → gilt yields spike +140bp in 3 days
    ↓
LDI funds (pension vehicle) used leveraged gilt positions to match liabilities
    ↓
Gilt price drops → margin calls on LDI funds
    ↓
LDI funds sell gilts to raise cash for margin
    ↓
Selling gilts → gilt prices drop further → MORE margin calls
    ↓
DOOM LOOP: sell → price drops → more margin → more selling
    ↓
Gilt liquidity evaporates (bid-ask widens 10x)
    ↓
BoE forced to intervene as buyer of last resort
    ↓
Without BoE, UK pension system faced SYSTEMIC FAILURE

The Margin-Driven Amplification

Day 30Y gilt yield LDI margin call (est.) Forced selling Net effect
Sep 23 3.7% → 4.0% £5B £5B of gilts sold Self-reinforcing
Sep 26 4.0% → 4.5% £15B £15B of gilts sold Accelerating
Sep 27 4.5% → 4.9% £25B+ Liquidation panic Systemic
Sep 28 4.9% → 5.1% (peaked) Total est: £50-65B BoE intervenes Stabilized

The horrifying math:

LDI funds had ~£1.5T in assets, levered 3-4x on gilts.
A 100bp yield rise ≈ 20% price decline on 30Y gilts.
20% × 3-4x leverage = 60-80% of equity wiped out.
These funds were managing pension money for millions of UK workers.

Connection to Multi-Strategy Fund Risk

The LDI crisis has a DIRECT parallel to multi-PM fund risk:

LDI crisis element Multi-PM fund analog
Leveraged position in one instrument (gilts) PM concentrated in one sector/factor
Margin call → forced selling → price moves against you Margin call → forced liquidation at worst prices
All LDI funds held the same position All PMs loading on the same factor (cross-PM correlation)
No one modeled "gilt yield +140bp in 3 days" Scenarios that seem impossible get excluded
Doom loop required external intervention (BoE) At fund level, CRO/IC must break the loop before it becomes terminal

The Deep Lesson

Margin structures create reflexive feedback loops that turn orderly drawdowns into existential crises. The RM's job is to BREAK THE LOOP before it accelerates:

  1. Maintain cash/liquidity buffers sized for 2x the worst historical margin call
  2. Pre-identify liquidation candidates BEFORE the margin call arrives
  3. Model margin requirements under stress, not just current conditions
  4. Never assume the central bank or any external party will rescue you

PART 9: VOLMAGEDDON (FEBRUARY 2018) — STRUCTURAL PRODUCT RISK

Timeline & Facts

Date Event
Feb 5, 2018 SPX drops 4.1% in regular session. VIX jumps from 17 to 37 (intraday peak: 50).
Feb 5, 4pm+ XIV (inverse VIX ETN) NAV drops 93%. Triggers "acceleration event" → product terminated.
Feb 6, 2018 Credit Suisse terminates XIV. $1.9B in investor capital → ~$100M.
Broader impact Short vol funds globally wiped out. $6B+ in total losses across short-vol strategies.

The Feedback Loop

SPX drops 4% → VIX jumps from 17 to 37
    ↓
Short VIX products (XIV, SVXY) must BUY VIX futures to rebalance
    ↓
Buying VIX futures → VIX rises further
    ↓
VIX rising further → more rebalancing needed → MORE buying
    ↓
FEEDBACK LOOP: short vol rebalancing AMPLIFIES the vol spike
    ↓
VIX reaches 50 intraday (from 17 → 194% increase)
    ↓
XIV NAV goes to ~$0 (inverse product: 2x levered × 194% VIX increase > 100% loss)
    ↓
Product terminates. $1.9B → $100M.

The Mechanical Death

XIV was a -1x daily inverse VIX futures ETN. Its rebalancing requirement:

$$\text{VIX futures to trade} = -1 \times \text{NAV} \times \frac{\Delta\text{VIX}}{\text{VIX}_{\text{prev}}}$$

When VIX doubles, XIV must buy VIX futures equal to 100%+ of its NAV. But it's already lost most of its NAV. The rebalancing demand exceeds the product's ability to survive.

The critical insight for risk managers: Any leveraged daily-rebalancing product has an embedded convexity trap. The bigger the move, the more it needs to rebalance, which amplifies the move, which requires MORE rebalancing. This is a mechanical doom loop — no human decision required.

RM Lesson

Short vol is picking up pennies in front of a steamroller — literally. XIV generated steady returns for years (VIX term structure in contango → short front-month profits from roll-down). But a single day destroyed the product entirely. At Squarepoint:

  • Any strategy that implicitly or explicitly shorts vol must be stress-tested for a VIX tripling scenario
  • Monitor "gamma contribution" — how much does our rebalancing amplify or dampen market moves?
  • Products with mechanical rebalancing rules (leveraged ETFs, risk parity, vol-targeting) create PREDICTABLE forced flows — use them as inputs, not exposures

PART 10: AUGUST 2024 CARRY UNWIND — MODERN CROWDING + CROSS-ASSET CONTAGION

Timeline & Facts

Date Event
Jul 31, 2024 Bank of Japan raises rates from 0-0.1% to 0.25%. JPY starts strengthening.
Aug 1, 2024 Weak US manufacturing ISM. Recession fears. Tech earnings disappoint.
Aug 2, 2024 US NFP misses (114K vs 175K exp). Unemployment jumps to 4.3% → triggers Sahm Rule.
Aug 5, 2024 Nikkei -12.4% (worst since 1987). VIX spikes to 65 intraday (from 23 → 183% increase). SPX -3%. JPY appreciates 7% in 3 days.
Aug 6-9, 2024 Recovery. Nikkei bounces +10%. VIX collapses to 20. Carry trades partially re-established.

The Contagion Chain

BoJ rate hike + US recession fears → JPY strengthens
    ↓
Carry traders (borrow JPY, invest in USD/EM assets) face losses
    ↓
JPY rising → carry trade P&L negative → margin calls
    ↓
Forced selling of carried assets (US equities, EM, crypto)
    ↓
Selling US equities → SPX drops → VIX spikes
    ↓
VIX spike → vol-targeting strategies must REDUCE exposure
    ↓
Vol-targeting selling → MORE equity selling → MORE VIX increase
    ↓
CTA trend-followers hit stop-losses → ADDITIONAL selling
    ↓
Nikkei -12% (carry unwind + Japanese exporter FX impact + margin)
    ↓
Cross-asset correlation → 1. Everything falls together.
    ↓
Recovery in 3 days once forced selling exhausts itself

Identifying The Cascade Pattern

Layer Mechanism Timing Detectable in advance?
1. Fundamental BoJ hike + weak US data Days before YES — rate decision was announced; data was scheduled
2. Carry unwind JPY appreciation forces selling of carried assets Hours PARTIALLY — CFTC data showed record JPY shorts
3. Systematic amplification Vol-targeting + CTA selling Minutes-hours YES — know the mechanical rules (vol target = vol up → sell)
4. Liquidity evaporation Market-makers widen spreads, depth disappears Minutes INDIRECTLY — pre-event market depth data
5. Recovery Forced selling exhausts, dip-buyers arrive 1-3 days PROBABILITY — most forced-selling-driven crashes recover quickly

The Key RM Lesson

The August 2024 crash was 90% mechanical, 10% fundamental. The actual news (BoJ hike, weak NFP) justified maybe a 2% sell-off. The other 10% was carry unwind → vol-targeting → CTA stops → liquidity death → correlation = 1.

Implication for Squarepoint: When you see a 2% equity sell-off that turns into 12%, don't ask "what fundamental news caused this?" Ask: "what MECHANICAL selling is amplifying this?" If the answer is carry unwind + systematic strategies, the move is likely to reverse within days. Your RM job: ensure the fund SURVIVES the mechanical cascade with enough capital to benefit from the recovery.

PART 12: INTERVIEW ANSWERS — CASE STUDIES

Q: "Walk me through a risk management failure that you've studied and what you learned."

"I'll use LTCM because it illustrates three overlapping failures that are directly relevant to a multi-PM fund.

LTCM ran convergence trades across global bond markets at 25:1 leverage with $1.25 trillion in notional derivatives. Their models showed diversified positions — different instruments, different countries, different maturities. But in August 1998, when Russia defaulted and triggered a global flight to quality, every single spread widened simultaneously. Correlation jumped from 0.2 to 0.95. Their 'diversified' portfolio was actually one trade: spread compression.

What kills me about LTCM is that their trades were mostly RIGHT. Many of those positions converged by 2000 and would have been profitable. But you can't profit if you're dead. Leverage is a liquidation timer — 25:1 on illiquid assets means you survive maybe 4% against you before margin calls force liquidation. And when every other convergence fund has the same positions, there are no buyers when you need to sell.

The RM lessons I take: First, factor decomposition is non-negotiable — 'how many independent bets do we actually have?' usually reveals the real number is 2-3, not 20. Second, leverage limits must be liquidity-adjusted — 10:1 on liquid instruments is fine; 3:1 on illiquid is already dangerous. Third, VaR is necessary but insufficient — it told LTCM their max daily loss was $45M; they lost $500M in a day. You need stress tests with stressed correlations, not just point-in-time VaR."

Q: "How would you apply the lessons from these case studies in your daily work?"

"I'd build a daily checklist based on the failure patterns I've studied. Every morning, I'd ask five questions:

One — Concentration: is any single name, sector, or factor responsible for more than 30% of house VaR? If yes, flag it. This is the Amaranth/Archegos check.

Two — Cross-PM correlation: are multiple PMs loading on the same factor without knowing it? I'd run a simple factor regression weekly. This is the LTCM check — illusory diversification.

Three — Model integrity: has any risk metric moved more than 20% without a corresponding position change? If VaR drops 50% overnight, something is wrong with the model, not the risk. This is the London Whale check.

Four — Margin adequacy: if our worst-case scenario hits, does our margin utilization exceed 85%? If yes, pre-identify what to liquidate BEFORE the margin call. This is the UK gilt / March 2020 check.

Five — Mechanical amplification: are there systematic strategies (CTAs, vol-targeting, ETF rebalancing) that would amplify a sell-off in our positions? If yes, size the forced-selling estimate and include it in our stress test. This is the Volmageddon / August 2024 check.

That's five questions, five minutes, every morning — and it covers over 90% of the failure patterns that have killed funds in the last 30 years."

Q: "What's the single most important lesson from studying these blow-ups?"

"That every catastrophic loss shares the same anatomy: a risk that was VISIBLE was either not measured, not communicated, or not acted upon.

Barings: the audit flagged Leeson's dual role — it was ignored. London Whale: the VaR model was gamed — nobody challenged it. Amaranth: the concentration was visible — limits were raised instead of enforced. Archegos: each prime broker saw their slice — nobody aggregated.

None of these were Black Swans. They were visible, measurable risks that the risk management function either couldn't see, chose not to see, or saw but couldn't stop. That tells me the RM's most important skill isn't modeling — it's the combination of SEEING the risk, COMMUNICATING it clearly to people who don't want to hear it, and having the INSTITUTIONAL authority to enforce action. The technical work is necessary but not sufficient. The human system around it is what determines whether the warning actually prevents the loss."
