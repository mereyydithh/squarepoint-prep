BULLET 1: Cross-Asset Risk Analytics Framework

What It Is (Plain English)

A bank has multiple trading desks — Rates, Equities, FX, Commodities, Credit. Each desk computes its own risk metrics (Greeks, VaR, stress P&L). The problem: if you ask "what's our TOTAL Delta exposure to a 50bp rate shock across the whole bank?" nobody can answer, because each desk computes Delta differently.

A cross-asset risk analytics framework solves this by:
1. Defining a common risk language (same bump sizes, same discounting, same conventions)
2. Building a normalization layer that takes each desk's raw Greeks and translates them into the common language
3. Aggregating the normalized Greeks at the portfolio level
4. Running cross-asset stress tests that capture correlation effects between asset classes

The Architecture (If You Designed It)

Layer 1: Data Ingestion

  • Each desk has its own pricing/risk engine (probably Murex, Calypso, or internal)
  • You need a data pipeline that extracts end-of-day (or intraday) Greeks from each desk's system
  • Format: position-level Greeks — for every trade, you need Delta, Gamma, Vega, Theta, Rho at minimum

Layer 2: Normalization

This is where the real quant work is. Problems you solve:

Problem A — Inconsistent Delta definitions:
- Rates desk computes DV01 (dollar value of 1bp move)
- Equities desk computes Delta as % sensitivity to 1% spot move
- Commodities desk computes Delta as $/unit move
- FX desk computes Delta per pip
- You define a common convention: e.g., "all Deltas expressed as P&L impact of a 1 standard deviation move in the underlying risk factor"

Problem B — Inconsistent vol surface models:
- Rates desk uses SABR for swaption vol
- Equities desk uses local vol or SVI
- Commodities desk uses seasonal vol models
- Vega from different models isn't directly comparable
- Solution: normalize to a common vol bump (e.g., P&L per 1 vol point parallel shift)

Problem C — Cross-effects (Vanna, Volga):
- Some desks compute cross-Greeks (Vanna = dDelta/dVol), some don't
- If you aggregate without cross-Greeks, your portfolio-level risk is wrong because you miss interaction effects
- Decision: mandate that all desks provide at least Delta, Gamma, Vega, and Vanna

Problem D — Discounting:
- Different desks may use different risk-free curves (OIS, SOFR, EURIBOR)
- Need to harmonize to a common discounting framework for consistent P&L aggregation

Layer 3: Aggregation Engine

  • Python + Polars (or kdb+) engine that:
  • Reads normalized position-level Greeks from all desks
  • Groups by risk factor (e.g., "USD rates 2Y", "EUR/USD spot", "Brent crude front month")
  • Sums across desks to get portfolio-level Greeks per risk factor
  • Computes portfolio VaR using the aggregated risk factor exposures + a cross-asset covariance matrix

Layer 4: Cross-Asset Stress Testing

  • Define scenarios: "risk-off" (equities -10%, rates -50bp, credit +100bp, USD strengthens, gold up)
  • Apply to ALL desks simultaneously
  • The key insight: correlation under stress ≠ correlation in normal markets
  • In normal times, equities and rates may have low correlation
  • In a crisis, everything correlates ("correlations go to 1")
  • Portfolio loss under stress is LARGER than sum of desk-level losses because of this

Layer 5: Reporting

  • Output to: Excel (for desk heads), dashboards (for risk team), regulatory reports (for FRTB)
  • Key reports: portfolio-level Greeks, VaR by risk factor, stress P&L by scenario, concentration analysis

Your Story (Defensible Version)

"At Natixis, I'm part of the team building a cross-asset risk analytics framework covering approximately €20 billion in notional across Rates, Equities, FX, Commodities, and Credit. My primary contribution has been on the commodities side — I understand the products and risk conventions from my BNP experience, so I've been working on the normalization layer for commodities Greeks, ensuring they aggregate consistently with the other asset classes. I've also been contributing to the cross-asset stress testing scenarios, particularly around commodity-rates correlation dynamics — something I have direct experience with from my work at BNP in New York."

Key language: "part of the team building" / "my contribution has been" / "contributing to" — not "I architected"

"What was the hardest normalization problem?" → "Reconciling how Delta is defined across asset classes — the rates desk uses DV01 in basis points while commodities uses dollar P&L per unit move. We standardized everything to P&L impact of a 1 standard deviation historical shock per risk factor, which made aggregation meaningful."

BULLET 2: SABR Volatility Surface Engine

What SABR Is (Full Explanation)

The Problem SABR Solves

When you trade options, you need to know the implied volatility at any strike and expiry — not just ATM. The "volatility surface" is a 3D map: strike × expiry → implied vol. Market data only gives you implied vol at traded strikes. You need a model to interpolate/extrapolate to any strike.

Black-Scholes assumes constant volatility. Reality has:
- Smile: deep OTM puts and calls have higher implied vol than ATM (excess kurtosis / fat tails)
- Skew: OTM puts have higher implied vol than OTM calls (negative skewness — markets crash more than they rally)

SABR is a model that captures both smile and skew using 4 parameters.

The SABR Model — The Math

SABR stands for Stochastic Alpha Beta Rho. It models two stochastic processes:

Forward price dynamics:
$$dF_t = \sigma_t F_t^\beta dW_1$$

Volatility dynamics:
$$d\sigma_t = \nu \sigma_t dW_2$$

Where:
- $F_t$ = forward price
- $\sigma_t$ = stochastic volatility (this is what makes it different from Black-Scholes — vol itself moves randomly)
- $W_1, W_2$ = correlated Brownian motions with $dW_1 \cdot dW_2 = \rho \, dt$

The 4 Parameters

α (Alpha) — Initial volatility level
- Sets the overall level of the vol surface
- Roughly corresponds to ATM implied vol
- Higher α → higher vol across all strikes
- Think of it as "where does the smile sit vertically"

β (Beta) — CEV exponent (0 ≤ β ≤ 1)
- Controls how vol relates to the forward level (the "backbone")
- β = 0: normal model — vol is independent of forward level (additive)
- β = 0.5: CIR-like — square root dynamics
- β = 1: lognormal model — Black-Scholes-like (multiplicative)
- In practice, β is usually fixed ex-ante based on market convention:
- Rates: β ≈ 0.5 (market convention, captures the fact that rate vol increases as rates increase, but less than proportionally)
- Equities: β ≈ 1.0 (lognormal, prices can't go negative)
- Commodities: β ≈ 0.5 to 1.0 depending on the product
- β is usually NOT calibrated — it's chosen. Only α, ρ, ν are calibrated to market data.

ρ (Rho) — Correlation between forward and vol (-1 ≤ ρ ≤ 1)
- Controls the skew (asymmetry of the smile)
- ρ < 0: when forward drops, vol rises → negative skew (typical for equities — "markets take stairs up and elevator down")
- ρ > 0: when forward rises, vol rises → positive skew (sometimes seen in commodities during supply shocks)
- ρ = 0: symmetric smile (no skew)
- This is the parameter that captures the real-world observation that "vol goes up when markets go down"

ν (Nu) — Vol-of-vol (ν ≥ 0)
- Controls the curvature of the smile (how much fatter the tails are)
- Higher ν → more curvature, fatter tails, higher implied vol for deep OTM options
- ν = 0: no stochastic vol → reduces to a simple CEV model with no smile
- Think of it as "how wild can vol itself get"

The Hagan Formula (How You Get Implied Vol from SABR Params)

Hagan et al. (2002) derived an analytical approximation for implied vol as a function of the 4 SABR parameters. This is why SABR is popular — you don't need Monte Carlo, you get a closed-form formula.

For a given strike $K$ and forward $F$, with time to expiry $T$:

$$\sigma_{impl}(K) \approx \underbrace{\frac{\alpha}{(FK)^{\frac{1-\beta}{2}} \left[1 + \frac{(1-\beta)^2}{24}\ln^2!\frac{F}{K} + \frac{(1-\beta)^4}{1920}\ln^4!\frac{F}{K}\right]}}_{\text{Base vol level (driven by } \alpha \text{ and } \beta\text{)}}$$

$$\times \quad \underbrace{\frac{z}{x(z)}}_{\text{Skew/smile adjustment}}$$

$$\times \quad \underbrace{\left[1 + \left(\frac{(1-\beta)^2 \alpha^2}{24(FK)^{1-\beta}} + \frac{\rho \beta \nu \alpha}{4(FK)^{\frac{1-\beta}{2}}} + \frac{2-3\rho^2}{24}\nu^2\right)T\right]}_{\text{Time-dependent correction}}$$

Where:

$$z = \frac{\nu}{\alpha}(FK)^{\frac{1-\beta}{2}}\ln\frac{F}{K}$$

$$x(z) = \ln\left(\frac{\sqrt{1-2\rho z + z^2} + z - \rho}{1-\rho}\right)$$

Intuition for each piece:
- First term (base vol): scales $\alpha$ by the moneyness-adjusted forward level — this is the "backbone" of the smile
- Second term $\frac{z}{x(z)}$: this is where skew ($\rho$) and smile ($\nu$) enter — it bends the vol curve around ATM
- Third term (time correction): small correction that grows with $T$, capturing how the smile shape evolves with maturity

You don't need to memorize this formula. But you need to know:
1. It's an analytical approximation (not exact)
2. It's fast (microseconds) — that's why SABR is production-friendly
3. It breaks down for very low strikes (can give negative implied vol or arbitrage)
4. The "corrections" you'd mention are fixes for the low-strike problem (e.g., Paulot 2015, Obloj 2008, or using the exact solution via numerical integration)

Calibration Process

What you calibrate: Given market-observed option prices at multiple strikes for one expiry, find (α, ρ, ν) that best fit the market implied vols. (β is fixed.)

How:
1. Fix β (e.g., 0.5 for rates)
2. For each expiry slice:
- Collect market implied vols at N strikes (e.g., 10-15 strikes from 25d put to 25d call)
- Use Levenberg-Marquardt optimization (nonlinear least squares) to minimize:

$$\min_{\alpha,\, \rho,\, \nu} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left(\sigma^{\text{market}}(K_i) - \sigma^{\text{SABR}}(K_i;\, \alpha,\, \rho,\, \nu)\right)^2$$

  • Output: calibrated $(\alpha, \rho, \nu)$ for that expiry
  • Repeat for each expiry → you have a term structure of SABR parameters
  • Interpolate between expiries for any maturity

C++ for the calibration core because:
- Levenberg-Marquardt requires many iterations, each evaluating the Hagan formula at all strikes
- For production (recalibrating every 30 min across hundreds of surfaces), speed matters
- C++ gives sub-millisecond per expiry slice
- Python is too slow for the inner loop but perfect for the API/orchestration layer

Python wrapper (pybind11): exposes the C++ engine to Python users. They call:

params = sabr_calibrate(forward=100, strikes=[90,95,100,105,110], 
                         market_vols=[0.22, 0.19, 0.17, 0.19, 0.21],
                         expiry=0.5, beta=0.5)
# returns: {'alpha': 0.035, 'rho': -0.25, 'nu': 0.45}

Known Limitations of SABR (Critical for Risk Manager)

  1. Negative density / arbitrage at low strikes: The Hagan approximation can give implied vols that, when converted to option prices, imply negative probability density. This means your vol surface is arbitrageable. Fix: use exact solutions or corrections.

  2. Single-expiry model: SABR calibrates each expiry independently. Nothing enforces consistency across expiries — your T=3M surface could cross your T=6M surface, which is a calendar spread arbitrage. Fix: post-calibration smoothing or joint calibration.

  3. No jumps: SABR is a pure diffusion model. Real markets have jumps (earnings, central bank decisions). For risk management, SABR may underestimate the probability of extreme moves. Fix: supplement with jump-diffusion models or add explicit tail scenarios.

  4. Parameter instability: Calibrated parameters can jump day-to-day, especially ν, making hedging based on SABR Greeks noisy. Fix: regularize calibration (penalize large day-to-day parameter changes).

The 15bps RMSE Improvement

RMSE (Root Mean Squared Error) of implied vol fit:
- Legacy model: e.g., RMSE = 45bps across all strikes
- SABR: RMSE = 30bps
- Improvement: 15bps

This was measured by:
1. Taking 18 months of historical market data
2. For each day, calibrating both models to end-of-day market vols
3. Computing the error (model vol - market vol) at each strike
4. Taking RMSE across all strikes and days
5. Comparing

BULLET 3: Cross-Asset Correlation Monitor

What It Is (Plain English)

A system that continuously monitors the statistical correlation between asset pairs (e.g., gold vs. US Treasury yields, oil vs. USD, equities vs. credit spreads) and flags when correlations break from their historical regime.

Why It Matters for Risk

Portfolios are built on correlation assumptions. If you're long gold as a hedge against your rates exposure, you're implicitly assuming gold and rates are negatively correlated. If that correlation flips, your hedge doesn't work — two positions that used to offset now move in the same direction, amplifying your loss.

What You Would Have Built

Architecture

Data Layer: kdb+ storing tick-level and daily price data for key assets
    ↓
Computation Layer: Python + Polars
    - Compute rolling correlations: 30-day, 60-day, 90-day windows
    - Simple: Pearson correlation of daily returns
    - Advanced: exponentially-weighted (gives more weight to recent data)
    - DCC-GARCH for dynamic conditional correlation (fancier)
    ↓
Detection Layer:
    - Compare current correlation vs. historical distribution
    - Flag when current reading is >2σ from long-term average
    - Chow breakpoint test for structural breaks
    ↓
Alert Layer:
    - Automated alerts when regime change detected
    - Dashboard showing correlation heatmap across all pairs
    - Report for risk committee

The Gold-UST Decoupling Story

What actually happened in Q4 2025 (real market event):
- Historically, gold and US Treasury yields are negatively correlated: yields down → gold up (both are "safe haven" flows)
- In late 2025, this relationship broke: gold was rising even as yields were rising
- Driver: central bank gold buying (China, Russia, Middle East — de-dollarization), which is a structural/geopolitical flow that overwhelms the traditional rates-gold relationship
- This was widely discussed in financial media (Goldman, JPM research notes)

How the monitor would catch it:
1. Rolling 30-day correlation between gold returns and 10Y UST yield changes turns positive (historically it's around -0.3 to -0.5)
2. System flags: "gold-UST correlation is at +0.15, which is a 3-sigma event vs. 10-year history"
3. You investigate: pull macro data, central bank reserve reports, ETF flows
4. Conclude: regime change driven by structural flows, not transient noise
5. Run portfolio impact: any portfolio using gold as a rates hedge has a 15% higher potential drawdown if rates reverse and gold doesn't offset

The 15% drawdown reduction:
- This means: by alerting portfolio managers early, they adjusted their hedging (reduced gold hedge ratio, added direct rates hedges), which meant when the correlation continued to diverge, the portfolio was protected
- "15%" = roughly the avoided drawdown vs. what would have happened with the old hedge allocation

Statistical Tests You'd Cite

Chow Test: Tests for a structural break in a time series. Split data at a candidate breakpoint, estimate the model (here, the correlation) on each sub-sample, test if they're significantly different. If p < 0.01, you have a structural break.

Rolling Z-Score:

$$Z = \frac{\rho_{\text{current}} - \mu_{\text{historical}}}{\sigma_{\text{historical}}}$$

If $|Z| > 2$ or $3$, the current correlation is anomalous relative to history.

BULLET 4: FRTB-SA / Sensitivity-Based Method

What FRTB Is (Plain English)

FRTB = Fundamental Review of the Trading Book. It's a Basel III/IV regulatory framework that overhauls how banks calculate market risk capital — i.e., how much capital they must hold against potential trading losses.

FRTB has two approaches:
1. Internal Models Approach (IMA): Bank uses its own VaR/ES models (harder to qualify, lower capital)
2. Standardized Approach (SA): Prescriptive, formula-based calculation (easier, but usually higher capital)

Most desks/banks use SA as baseline + try to qualify some desks for IMA.

FRTB-SA: Sensitivity-Based Method (SBM)

SBM computes capital charges based on three types of risk sensitivities:

1. Delta Risk Charge

  • Compute Delta sensitivity to each prescribed risk factor
  • Risk factors are grouped by risk class: GIRR (rates), CSR (credit spread), Equity, Commodity, FX
  • Within each risk class, sensitivities are multiplied by prescribed risk weights (like a regulatory multiplier)
  • Weighted sensitivities are then aggregated using prescribed correlation matrices (both within-bucket and cross-bucket)
  • Three correlation scenarios: "high", "medium", "low" → take the worst to be conservative

Example for GIRR (General Interest Rate Risk):
- Delta sensitivity = DV01 at each tenor point (3M, 6M, 1Y, 2Y, 3Y, 5Y, 10Y, 15Y, 20Y, 30Y)
- Risk weight for 10Y tenor = ~1.7% (prescribed by Basel)
- Weighted sensitivity = DV01 × risk weight
- Aggregate across tenors using prescribed correlation matrix

2. Vega Risk Charge

  • Compute Vega sensitivity to each prescribed vol risk factor (at each strike/expiry bucket)
  • Same structure: risk weights × sensitivities, aggregated with correlations
  • Captures risk from options positions

3. Curvature Risk Charge

  • Captures nonlinear (Gamma) risk that Delta doesn't capture
  • Computed by shocking each risk factor up and down, computing the P&L, and taking the worst-case net of the Delta component
  • Formula:

$$CVR_k = \max!\Big(0,\; \underbrace{V_i(+\text{shock})}\text{P\&L if up} - \underbrace{s_k \cdot \text{shock}}\text{Delta component},\; \underbrace{V_i(-\text{shock})}\text{P\&L if down} + \underbrace{s_k \cdot \text{shock}}\text{Delta component}\Big)$$

  • Important for portfolios with significant options positions

Total SBM Charge

$$\text{SBM} = \text{Delta Charge} + \text{Vega Charge} + \text{Curvature Charge}$$

Each component is computed per risk class, then summed across risk classes (with limited diversification benefit).

Add-ons

  • Default Risk Charge (DRC): For credit-sensitive instruments (bonds, CDS) — captures jump-to-default risk separately
  • Residual Risk Add-On (RRAO): For exotic instruments with risks not captured by SBM (e.g., correlation risk, gap risk)

Where €5M Capital Relief Comes From

Capital charges can be over-estimated if:
1. Sensitivities are computed too conservatively (e.g., bump size too large, capturing convexity in what should be a linear Delta)
2. Risk factor mapping is too coarse (grouping positions that could offset into different buckets, losing diversification)
3. Correlation parameters aren't optimized (FRTB prescribes correlations within ranges — choosing the most favorable scenario that's still compliant)
4. Netting is incomplete (failing to net long and short positions that are in the same regulatory bucket)
5. Curvature is over-estimated (the shock size or computation methodology is more conservative than required)

Your "recalibration" work = reviewing each of these areas, identifying where the bank's implementation was more conservative than the regulation requires, and tightening.
