P — MACRO FUNDAMENTALS

WHY things move — not just THAT they moved

Depth: 🔴 DEEP

WHY THIS MATTERS

A trader says "I'm adding to my long-duration position because the Fed is behind the curve." A quant shows a regime-switch model predicting stagflation with 72% probability. Constantine asks "where are we in the cycle, and what does that mean for cross-asset correlation?" If you can't engage with these statements — decompose the macro logic, challenge the assumptions, quantify the portfolio consequences — you're reading Bloomberg headlines, not managing risk.

Macro fundamentals are the connective tissue between individual trades and portfolio-level risk. Every position has embedded macro assumptions: a curve steepener assumes growth or term premium is rising. A long equity / short rates trade assumes Goldilocks. A PM who says "I have no macro view" is lying — they're just not aware of the macro embedded in their book.

PART 1: GDP & THE OUTPUT GAP

GDP Identity

$$Y = C + I + G + (X - M)$$

Component US share What drives it Lead indicators
C — Consumption ~68% Disposable income, wealth effect, credit conditions, confidence Retail sales, Michigan sentiment, credit card data
I — Investment ~18% Interest rates, CEO confidence, capacity utilization Durable goods orders, CAPEX surveys, permits
G — Government ~17% Fiscal policy, appropriations, defense spending CBO projections, Treasury issuance calendars
NX — Net exports ~-3% FX rates, relative growth, tariffs, supply chains Trade balance, container volumes, PMI export orders

Real vs Nominal:

$$\text{Real GDP growth} \approx \text{Nominal GDP growth} - \text{GDP deflator}$$

If nominal GDP grows 5% and the deflator is 4%, real growth is ~1%. The economy feels like it's growing but mostly from price increases. This distinction matters enormously for risk — nominal earnings can grow while real economic activity contracts.

The Output Gap

$$\text{Output Gap} = \frac{Y_{\text{actual}} - Y_{\text{potential}}}{Y_{\text{potential}}} \times 100$$

Gap Meaning Inflation pressure Policy response
Positive (>0) Economy above trend Inflationary — demand > supply Tighten
Zero At potential Neutral Hold
Negative (<0) Economy below trend Disinflationary — slack Ease

Why it matters for risk: The output gap determines whether inflation pressures are cyclical or structural. A positive output gap with rising inflation = overheating (rate hikes coming). A negative output gap with rising inflation = supply-side shock (policy dilemma). The distinction drives completely different portfolio outcomes.

Okun's Law — Growth to Unemployment

$$\Delta U \approx -0.5 \times (g_Y - g_Y^*)$$

For every 1% GDP growth above trend (~2%), unemployment falls ~0.5pp. This relationship is approximate but gives a quick sanity check: if someone claims GDP will grow 3% with rising unemployment, something is inconsistent.

The Taylor Rule — Policy Rate Framework

$$r^ = r_{\text{neutral}} + 0.5({\pi} - {\pi^}) + 0.5(y - y^*)$$

Where:
- $r^$ = prescribed policy rate
- $r_{\text{neutral}}$ = neutral real rate (~2.5% nominal currently)
- $\pi - \pi^$ = inflation gap (actual minus 2% target)
- $y - y^*$ = output gap

Worked example (April 2026):
- Neutral rate: 2.5%
- Core PCE: 3.2%, target 2.0% → gap = +1.2%
- Output gap estimate: +0.3%
- Taylor Rule: $2.5 + 0.5(1.2) + 0.5(0.3) = 2.5 + 0.6 + 0.15 = 3.25\%$
- Actual fed funds: 3.75%

Interpretation: The Fed is above the Taylor Rule prescription. But with headline inflation re-accelerating from oil, the "correct" rate is debatable. Hawks argue effective inflation gap is larger; doves argue the oil shock is supply-side and shouldn't be fought with rates.

Inflation Dynamics — The Phillips Curve

$$\pi_t = \pi^e + \beta(U^* - U_t) + \epsilon_t$$

Where:
- $\pi^e$ = inflation expectations
- $U^*$ = NAIRU (non-accelerating inflation rate of unemployment ≈ 4.0%)
- $U_t$ = actual unemployment
- $\epsilon_t$ = supply shocks

When $U_t < U^*$: Labor market tighter than sustainable → wage pressure → inflation rises.
When $U_t > U^*$: Slack in labor market → wage pressure eases → inflation falls.

The problem today: The Phillips curve has been flat for a decade — low unemployment didn't produce proportional inflation. Then 2021-2023 showed it can steepen violently when hit with simultaneous demand and supply shocks. The curve's slope is regime-dependent. Risk managers should not assume a stable relationship.

Inflation Transmission — Oil Shock → Sticky Inflation

Stage 1 (Immediate):    Oil spike → gasoline, heating, jet fuel ↑
Stage 2 (1-2 months):   Transport costs ↑ → shipping, trucking, rail
Stage 3 (2-4 months):   Food prices ↑ (transport + fertilizer inputs)
Stage 4 (2-4 months):   Business input costs ↑ → margins compress
Stage 5 (3-6 months):   Businesses pass costs to consumers → core CPI ↑
Stage 6 (6-12 months):  Workers demand wage increases to offset cost of living
Stage 7 (12-18 months): Wages ↑ → businesses raise prices → wage-price spiral

Stages 1-4 = cost-push (transitory if shock reverses). Stages 5-7 = demand-pull feedback (self-reinforcing). The Fed can look through Stages 1-4. If Stage 6 begins, they MUST respond or expectations de-anchor.

Monitoring the transition:
- Stage 1-2: Energy weight in CPI, PPI pipeline
- Stage 3-4: PPI finished goods, ISM Prices Paid
- Stage 5: Core CPI acceleration, trimmed mean
- Stage 6: Atlanta Fed Wage Tracker, ECI (Employment Cost Index), quits rate
- Stage 7: Michigan/NY Fed 1Y and 5Y inflation expectations, TIPS breakevens

PART 3: CENTRAL BANK POLICY — COMPARATIVE FRAMEWORK

The Fed's Dual Mandate

  1. Maximum employment (~4.0% NAIRU)
  2. Price stability (2.0% PCE target, symmetric)

When these objectives conflict — rising inflation AND weakening labor market — the Fed faces a dilemma. Historical precedent: they almost always prioritize inflation first (credibility), then pivot to growth once inflation is contained. Volcker (1980), Powell (2022-2023).

Global Central Bank Comparison

Central bank Mandate Target Current rate Balance sheet Key difference
Federal Reserve Dual (employment + inflation) 2% PCE 3.75% ~$7.5T (QT running) Dual mandate → more dovish toggle
ECB Price stability (primary) 2% HICP 3.25% ~€5T Single mandate → more hawkish on inflation
Bank of England Price stability (primary), growth (secondary) 2% CPI 4.50% ~£750B UK politics adds pressure; gilt market fragility (2022 LDI crisis)

Why Central Bank Divergence Matters for Risk

When central banks move at different speeds, FX rates move. The carry trade connects them:

$$\text{Carry} = r_{\text{invest}} - r_{\text{fund}} - E[\Delta \text{FX}]$$

If the Fed holds at 3.75% while the BoJ is at 0.50%, the rate differential incentivizes borrow-JPY/buy-USD. This keeps USDJPY elevated. If the BoJ hikes aggressively or the Fed cuts, the carry unwinds violently (see August 2024 JPY carry unwind — Nikkei -12% in 2 days).

For risk managers: Check PM books for embedded carry assumptions. A PM long EM local-currency bonds is implicitly short dollar strength and long EM central bank credibility. If the Fed signals higher-for-longer while EM central banks cut, that position gets crushed on FX.

Monetary Transmission Mechanism

Policy rate change
├── SHORT END (immediate)
│   ├── Money market rates (fed funds, SOFR, repo)
│   ├── Commercial paper, T-bill yields
│   └── Bank funding costs
├── FINANCIAL CONDITIONS (1-3 months)
│   ├── Mortgage rates → housing demand
│   ├── Corporate bond yields → capex decisions
│   ├── Equity valuations (discount rate effect)
│   ├── Credit availability (bank lending standards)
│   └── Dollar strength/weakness → trade competitiveness
├── REAL ECONOMY (3-12 months)
│   ├── Consumer spending (credit cost, wealth effect)
│   ├── Business investment (hurdle rate comparison)
│   ├── Housing construction (starts, permits)
│   └── Net exports (FX effect on competitiveness)
└── INFLATION (12-24 months)
    ├── Demand reduction → output gap widens
    ├── Labor market loosens → wage pressure falls
    └── Expectations adjust → self-reinforcing disinflation

The lag problem: Monetary policy operates with long and variable lags (Friedman). The full impact of a rate change takes 12-24 months. This means the Fed is always flying partially blind — they're reacting to data that reflects past policy, not current conditions.

PART 4: YIELD CURVE ANALYTICS

Term Structure Theory

The yield curve plots yields against maturity. Its shape encodes market expectations about growth, inflation, and risk:

$$y_n = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{t=1}^{n} E[r_t] + \text{Term Premium}_n$$

Theory Explanation Implication
Expectations hypothesis Long rates = average of expected future short rates Curve shape reflects rate path expectations
Liquidity preference Investors demand premium for holding longer maturities Curve normally upward-sloping (term premium > 0)
Preferred habitat Supply/demand at each maturity segment matters independently QE at long end can depress long rates
Market segmentation Different investors at different maturities → can create kinks Pension demand at 30Y → ultra-long bid

Curve Shapes and Their Signals

Shape What it signals Historical accuracy Portfolio implication
Normal (steep) Growth optimism, policy accommodation Standard in expansions Pro-risk, banks outperform (NIM ↑)
Flat Transition — late cycle, policy uncertainty Precedes regime shift Reduce risk, increase liquidity
Inverted (2Y > 10Y) Market expects rate cuts → recession coming 8/8 last recessions (12-18 month lead) Defensive, long duration (if recession materializes)
Bear steepening Long rates rising faster than short → term premium ↑ Fiscal concern, supply worry Risk-off for levered positions; mortgages crushed
Bull steepening Short rates falling faster → Fed cutting Early easing cycle Pro-risk transition, rate-sensitive rally
Bear flattening Short rates rising faster → Fed hiking Active tightening cycle Credit spreads widen, equity multiple compression
Bull flattening Long rates falling faster → flight to quality Risk-off, recession fear Defensive, TLT rallies, equities sell

Key Spreads to Monitor

Worked Example — Reading a Curve Move

Scenario: 10Y up 25bp, 2Y up 5bp (bear steepening)

What happened: Long-end sold off more than short-end.

Decomposition:
- Fed policy expectations (short-end): Barely moved → market doesn't expect near-term rate changes
- Term premium (long-end): Increased → either fiscal supply concerns, inflation risk premium, or foreign selling
- Real rate vs breakeven: Check if the 25bp was real rates (growth/fiscal) or breakevens (inflation)

Portfolio impact:
- Duration-sensitive positions: 10Y DV01 = $87.50/bp per $1M notional → $2,187.50 loss per $1M exposure on 25bp move
- Mortgage REITs: Crushed (funding at short end, assets at long end, spread compressed)
- Banks: Mixed — deposit costs stable (short end flat), loan rates higher, but credit risk up if term premium reflects growth concern
- Equities: Growth stocks hurt more (higher discount rate), value/commodity relatively better

PART 5: FISCAL POLICY & SOVEREIGN RISK

Fiscal-Monetary Interaction Matrix

Monetary tight Monetary easy
Fiscal expansionary CONFLICT: Deficit spending adds demand while Fed removes it → rates higher, dollar stronger, crowding out ALIGNED STIMULUS: Both adding demand → risk of overheating, asset prices ↑↑
Fiscal contractionary OVERKILL: Both removing demand → deep recession risk, deflation CONFLICT: Fiscal drag partially offsets easing → muddles recovery

Current US (April 2026): Fiscal expansionary (~6% deficit/GDP) + monetary restrictive (3.75% + QT). Classic conflict. Result: sticky inflation (fiscal keeps demand up), higher long-end rates (supply), and constrained Fed flexibility.

Debt Sustainability — The r-g Framework

$$\Delta \left(\frac{D}{Y}\right) = (r - g) \times \frac{D}{Y} + \text{Primary Deficit}$$

Where:
- $D/Y$ = debt-to-GDP ratio
- $r$ = average interest rate on government debt
- $g$ = nominal GDP growth rate
- Primary deficit = government deficit excluding interest payments

The key insight: If $g > r$ (growth exceeds interest costs), the debt ratio can be stabilized even with moderate primary deficits. If $r > g$ (interest costs exceed growth), the debt ratio spirals without primary surpluses.

Current US numbers:
- Debt/GDP: ~125%
- Weighted average interest on Treasury debt: ~3.2% (rising as old low-rate debt matures and is refinanced)
- Nominal GDP growth: ~4.5%
- Currently g > r, but the gap is narrowing as rates stay elevated and refinancing occurs

Why this matters for portfolio risk:
1. Term premium: If markets doubt fiscal sustainability, they demand higher yields on long bonds → bear steepening
2. Crowding out: Government bond supply competes with corporate issuance → wider credit spreads
3. FX impact: Fiscal dominance perception → currency weakness (see UK gilt crisis September 2022: Truss mini-budget → GBP -5% in days, 30Y gilt +150bp)
4. Rating downgrades: S&P downgraded US in 2011, Fitch in 2023. Future downgrades would trigger forced selling from mandated-investment-grade portfolios

The Gilt Crisis Case Study (September 2022)

What happened: UK PM Truss announced £45B unfunded tax cuts. Markets repriced UK fiscal trajectory. 30Y gilt yields spiked ~150bp in days. LDI (liability-driven investment) pension funds hit margin calls → forced selling of gilts → further price drops → doom loop.

RM lessons:
1. Fiscal credibility matters — markets can revolt against sovereign borrowers
2. Leverage + illiquidity + rates = toxic (LDI funds were levered 4-7x on gilt exposure)
3. Cross-asset contagion: gilt → GBP → UK equities → global rate vol → US Treasuries
4. Central bank as backstop: BoE intervened with £65B emergency purchasing — but only after the crash

PART 6: FX DYNAMICS & BALANCE OF PAYMENTS

FX Determination Framework

Three time horizons, three drivers:

Horizon Primary driver Key relationship
Short-term (days-weeks) Interest rate differentials, risk sentiment, positioning Higher rates → currency appreciation (carry)
Medium-term (months-quarters) Current account, relative growth, policy divergence BoP flows, portfolio rebalancing
Long-term (years) Purchasing power parity (PPP), productivity Currencies converge toward fair value

Interest Rate Parity

Covered interest rate parity (CIP):

$$F = S \times \frac{(1 + r_d)}{(1 + r_f)}$$

Where $F$ = forward rate, $S$ = spot rate, $r_d$ = domestic rate, $r_f$ = foreign rate. In theory, the forward premium/discount exactly offsets the interest rate differential, so there's no riskless profit. CIP deviations (basis) signal banking system stress or regulation-driven distortions.

Uncovered interest rate parity (UIP) — often violated:
The currency with the higher interest rate should depreciate to offset the carry. In practice, it doesn't (at least in the short run) — this is the carry trade's foundation.

The Carry Trade — Mechanics & Risks

$$\text{Carry P\&L} = \text{Notional} \times (r_{\text{invest}} - r_{\text{fund}}) + \text{FX P\&L}$$

Example (USDJPY carry):
- Borrow JPY at 0.50%, invest in USD at 3.75% → 325bp positive carry per year
- If USDJPY stays flat: pure carry profit
- Risk: If JPY appreciates 3.25%+ per year, the carry is wiped out
- Tail risk: Sudden JPY appreciation of 10-15% in days (August 2024: USDJPY fell from 161 to 141 in weeks)

RM implications:
- Carry trades are short vol — they earn steady returns until they blow up
- Portfolio scanning: Check for embedded carry across asset classes (EM bonds, basis trades, cross-currency positions)
- Crowding indicator: When the same carry trade is consensus, the unwind is violent

EM FX Vulnerability Framework

Factor Assessment metric Fragile signal
Current account CA/GDP ratio Deficit > 5% of GDP
FX reserves Reserves / short-term external debt < 100% (Guidotti rule)
External debt FX-denominated debt / GDP > 60%
Inflation differential Domestic CPI vs US CPI > 5pp → likely depreciation
Real rate differential Policy rate - CPI vs Fed rate - US CPI Negative real rate differential → outflow risk

The "Fragile Five" template: Any EM with twin deficits (fiscal + current account), high FX debt, and low real rates is vulnerable to a Fed-driven tightening in global dollar liquidity. History: 2013 taper tantrum hit BRL, INR, TRY, ZAR, IDR.

Dollar Smile Theory

The USD strengthens in two opposite scenarios:
1. US exceptionalism: US growth outperforms → capital inflows → USD ↑ (right side of smile)
2. Global crisis: Flight to safety → USD ↑ regardless of US fundamentals (left side of smile)
3. Middle (weakness): Global synchronized growth → risk appetite → capital flows to non-US markets → USD ↓

Current position on the smile: Debatable. Oil shock could push toward left side (risk-off, safe haven USD) OR right side (US relative outperformance if it's self-sufficient in energy). The ambiguity itself creates vol.

PART 7: BUSINESS CYCLE & MACRO REGIMES

The Four-Quadrant Framework

Growth ↑ Growth ↓
Inflation ↑ REFLATION — Pro-cyclical overheating. Equities +, commodities +, bonds -. STAGFLATION — Worst quadrant. Equities -, commodities +, bonds -. Nothing works except energy/commodities and CTA trend.
Inflation ↓ GOLDILOCKS — Best quadrant. Equities ++, bonds +, credit tight, vol low. DEFLATION — Risk-off. Equities -, bonds ++, credit widens, USD ↑.

Asset allocation implications by regime:

Regime Equities Duration Credit Commodities Vol FX
Goldilocks OW growth Neutral OW tight UW Sell Short USD
Reflation OW value/cyclical UW (rates up) Neutral OW Neutral EM +, commodity FX +
Stagflation UW UW (rates up) UW OW energy Buy USD +, commodity FX +
Deflation UW OW (rates down) UW (defaults) UW Buy USD ++ (safe haven)

Where are we now (April 2026)? Transitioning from Goldilocks (late 2025 — disinflation + moderate growth) toward Reflation or Stagflation depending on whether the oil shock kills growth. This is the crux of the current debate. If growth holds → Reflation (manageable). If growth cracks → Stagflation (dangerous).

Stock-Bond Correlation Regime

This is ONE of the most important concepts for portfolio risk:

Regime Stock-bond correlation Why Diversification
Growth-driven (pre-2020) Negative — stocks down → bonds up Rate cuts expected in downturn 60/40 works, bonds hedge equities
Inflation-driven (2022-2023) Positive — stocks down AND bonds down Inflation → rates up → both sell off 60/40 breaks, need alternatives
Current (2026) Unstable — oscillating between regimes Oil shock creates inflation-growth ambiguity Correlation can flip intra-day

$$\rho_{\text{stock,bond}} \approx f(\text{inflation vol}, \text{growth vol}, \text{policy regime})$$

Rule of thumb: When inflation is the dominant risk, correlation is positive. When growth is the dominant risk, correlation is negative. When both are risk, correlation is unstable and hedging is hardest.

RM action: Monitor rolling 3M stock-bond correlation daily. When it flips sign, reassess all hedge ratios. A portfolio hedged with duration in a negative-correlation regime will suffer if correlation turns positive — the hedge becomes an additional source of loss.

The Credit Cycle

Trough:   Banks cautious, spreads wide, defaults peaking
          → Survivors refinance at high rates
Recovery: Banks loosen, spreads tighten, defaults falling
          → Credit creation expands, animal spirits rise
Expansion: Easy money, tight spreads, covenants weaken
          → Leverage builds, zombie companies survive on cheap debt
Peak:     Spreads at cycle lows, leverage at highs, complacency
          → Any shock → defaults spike
Downturn: Banks tighten, spreads widen, defaults rising
          → Forced selling, margin calls, contagion

Key metrics:
- HY OAS: Current vs 5Y average → tighter = late cycle
- IG issuance: High issuance at tight spreads = peak behavior
- Default rate forecast: Moody's trailing 12M default rate
- Leverage ratios: Net debt/EBITDA for HY universe
- Senior Loan Officer Survey (SLOOS): Tightening standards predict recession with 4-6 quarter lead

How to Read an NFP Report

  1. Headline number (births-deaths adjustment matters): Large positive = strong labor market (hawkish), but check prior month revision — often revised ±50K
  2. Unemployment rate: U-3 (headline) vs U-6 (broad, includes underemployment). U-6 gap tells you about labor market slack
  3. Average Hourly Earnings (AHE): MoM > 0.4% = wage-push inflation concern. YoY > 4% with 2% productivity = unit labor costs rising
  4. Labor force participation rate: If UE falls because people leave the workforce (not because they got jobs), it's not a strong signal
  5. Sectoral breakdown: Government hiring doesn't count the same as private. Healthcare vs goods-producing tells you about the economy's composition

How to Read a CPI Print

  1. Headline MoM and YoY — the press headline. If MoM > 0.3%, markets react negatively
  2. Core MoM — strip food/energy. >0.2% = too hot for the Fed. Annualize it: 0.3% MoM ≈ 3.6% YoY pace
  3. Shelter — is it finally declining? 36% weight. The pipeline (new lease data, Zillow) leads CPI shelter by 12-18 months
  4. Supercore (core services ex-housing) — Powell's obsession. Driven by wages. If sticky, Fed stays hawkish
  5. Energy — how much is passing through to non-energy categories? Compare energy CPI to core CPI diffusion index
  6. Diffusion: How many categories are above 2% annualized? Broad-based > 60% = entrenched inflation

The Surprise Framework

$$\text{Surprise} = \text{Actual} - \text{Consensus}$$

Markets price consensus before the release. The move comes from the surprise, not the level. A CPI print of 3.5% that was expected at 3.5% = no move. A print of 3.3% vs 3.5% expected = dovish surprise → rates down, equities up.

Citi Economic Surprise Index: Aggregates surprises across all releases. Rising = data beating expectations. Falling = data disappointing. This tends to be mean-reverting — consensus catches up with 4-8 week lag.

PART 9: MACRO-TO-PORTFOLIO TRANSMISSION — THE RM FRAMEWORK

Why Risk Managers Need Macro

You are not a macro strategist. But every position in the portfolio has embedded macro assumptions:

Position Embedded macro assumption What breaks it
Long equities Growth continues, no recession GDP contraction, earnings miss

PART 10: SCENARIO ANALYSIS — MACRO-COHERENT STRESS TESTING

Building Macro-Coherent Scenarios

A stress test is only useful if the macro assumptions are internally consistent. "Equities -20% and rates -100bp" is a recession scenario. "Equities -20% and rates +100bp" is a stagflation scenario. They have completely different implications.

Scenario Construction Discipline

Each scenario must answer:
1. What is the shock? (Demand vs supply, domestic vs external)
2. What is the transmission mechanism? (Through rates, credit, FX, commodities?)
3. What is the policy response? (Accommodative, restrictive, or constrained?)
4. What are the second-order effects? (Contagion, feedback loops, non-linearities)
5. What is the timeline? (Acute shock = days, slow grind = months)

Worked Example — Hormuz Closure Scenario

Shock: Full Strait of Hormuz closure (20% of global oil transit).

Transmission:
1. Oil → $140-150 immediately (supply shock)
2. Energy CPI → +3pp within 2 months
3. Headline CPI → 6-7% YoY within 3 months
4. Core CPI → starts rising at month 3 as energy feeds through (transport, production)
5. Consumer spending → -2% (energy crowds out discretionary)
6. Corporate margins → compress 200bp (input costs, unable to fully pass through)
7. Earnings → -15% to -20% → SPX -25% to -30%
8. Fed → TRAPPED. Inflation says hike. Growth says cut. Likely holds and hopes for diplomatic resolution.
9. Credit → HY spreads +400bp as energy-intensive issuers face liquidity crunch

The Anatomy of a Macro Crisis

Phase 1: ACCUMULATION (months-years before)
         Leverage builds, risk premia compress, complacency
         └── Signs: VIX <15, HY OAS <350, yield-chasing, covenant-lite issuance

Phase 2: TRIGGER (days)
         Exogenous shock or endogenous crack exposes the accumulated fragility
         └── Signs: Single-name blow-up, unexpected data, geopolitical event

Phase 3: CONTAGION (days-weeks)
         Forced selling → margin calls → liquidity withdrawal → correlation spike
         └── Signs: Cross-asset correlation → 1.0, spreads gap, vol explodes

Phase 4: POLICY RESPONSE (days-weeks)
         Central bank intervenes, governments act
         └── Signs: Emergency meetings, new facilities, rate cuts, fiscal packages

Phase 5: RESOLUTION (months)
         Markets stabilize, fundamentals reassessed, new regime established
         └── Signs: Vol declines, sectoral differentiation returns, flows normalize

Lessons Across All Crises

  1. Leverage is the accelerant: Every major crisis involved excess leverage somewhere — banks (2008), hedge funds (LTCM), pension funds (UK 2022), family offices (Archegos)
  2. Correlations spike to 1 in a crisis: Diversification fails exactly when you need it. Cross-asset hedges that "worked" in normal times can fail simultaneously
  3. Liquidity disappears when you need it most: Market depth evaporates. Bid-ask spreads widen 5-10x. Forced sellers face no bids
  4. Policy response determines the trough: Markets bottom when policy intervention is sufficiently large and credible. "Whatever it takes" works; "measured response" doesn't
  5. The next crisis looks different from the last one: Regulators fix the previous vulnerability (banks after 2008). Risk migrates to wherever regulation isn't — shadow banking, private credit, levered LDI, basis trades

PART 12: INTERVIEW ANSWERS

Q1: "Walk me through how you'd think about the Hormuz oil shock from a risk management perspective"

Answer: "I'd structure this in three layers: immediate, medium-term, and tail.

Immediate — I'd look at our direct energy exposure, both longs and shorts. Then I'd map the second-order exposures: which PMs have positions in energy-intensive sectors like airlines, transport, or materials? What's our total portfolio sensitivity to a $10/barrel oil move? I'd want that DV01-equivalent for oil running by end of day.

Medium-term — the macro question is whether this stays as a cost-push shock that reverses or whether it feeds into core inflation via the wage-price transmission channel. I'd monitor the pipeline: PPI, ISM Prices Paid, then core CPI diffusion. If core CPI MoM starts exceeding 0.3% for two consecutive prints, we're in trouble because the Fed is trapped — they can't cut to support growth without re-igniting inflation, and they can't hike without deepening the growth shock.

Tail scenario — a full, prolonged closure means $140+ oil, recession probability jumps to 60%+, and we're in a stagflation regime where stock-bond correlation flips positive and traditional hedges fail. I'd run the scenario through our portfolio with stagflation correlation assumptions rather than recent correlations, because the correlation regime shift is where the real risk is hiding.

For the desk, I'd communicate this as: 'Our VaR number assumes recent correlations. If we enter a stagflation regime, effective portfolio risk could be 1.5-2x what VaR shows.' Then I'd recommend reviewing our hedge overlay — specifically, whether our duration hedges still work if both stocks and bonds sell off."

Q2: "The 10-year yield moved 15bp this morning. How would you decompose that?"

Answer: "I'd break it into three components.

First, expected policy rates — what's the market's revised expectation for the Fed path? I'd check fed funds futures across the curve. If the 2-year moved proportionally, the move is policy-driven, meaning the market is repricing the number of rate hikes or cuts.

Second, real rate versus inflation expectations — I'd compare the TIPS yield move to the nominal yield move. If the nominal 10Y moved 15bp and the TIPS 10Y moved 10bp, that's 10bp real rate increase and 5bp breakeven increase. Real rate moves suggest growth expectations or fiscal supply concerns. Breakeven moves suggest inflation repricing.

Third, term premium — the residual after accounting for expected rates and inflation. Using the ACM term premium model from the New York Fed, if most of the move is term premium, it suggests investor confidence in holding long-duration government bonds has shifted, possibly from fiscal supply concerns, foreign selling, or reduced QE expectations.

Then I'd map this to our portfolio: multiply the move by our aggregate DV01 for a first-order P&L estimate, then check which PMs have the most duration exposure and whether their positions are directional bets or hedged structures. A PM who's long a 2s10s steepener actually benefits from a bear steepening move, whereas a PM who's outright long 10Y duration is taking a loss."

Q3: "Where are we in the business cycle and what does that mean for portfolio risk?"

Answer: "I'd frame this using the growth-inflation quadrant. Right now we're in an unusual position — we were in Goldilocks territory in late 2025, with disinflation and moderate growth, but the oil shock is pushing us toward either Reflation or Stagflation, depending on whether growth holds up.

The key data to distinguish: if ISM holds above 50 and payrolls stay above 150K while inflation re-accelerates, we're in Reflation — which means equities can still work but positioning needs to rotate toward value and commodities, and duration should be underweight. If ISM breaks below 48 and claims start rising while inflation stays elevated, we're entering Stagflation — the worst quadrant for a balanced portfolio because stock-bond correlation turns positive and traditional diversification breaks.

For our book specifically, this regime ambiguity means I'd want to stress-test under BOTH scenarios and see which one causes more damage. I'd also want to check our hedge ratios — are we assuming negative stock-bond correlation? Because if we shift to a positive-correlation regime, our rate hedges could amplify losses rather than absorb them. That's the single biggest regime-change risk I'd flag to our PMs right now."
