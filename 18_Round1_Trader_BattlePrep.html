SECTION 1: GREEKS — HOW OPTIONS RISK IS MEASURED

Delta (Δ)

One-liner: How much the option price moves per $1 move in the underlying.

What it actually means: Delta is your directional exposure. A delta of 0.50 on a call means if the stock goes up $1, the option goes up $0.50. It also roughly approximates the probability of expiring in-the-money. Delta ranges from 0 to 1 for calls, 0 to -1 for puts.

Example: You own 100 call contracts on AAPL with delta 0.60 and a $100 multiplier. Your position delta is 100 × 0.60 × 100 = 6,000 shares equivalent. If AAPL moves up $2, you make ~$12,000 from delta alone.

Why traders care: Delta is the money. It's the first thing they look at — "what's my net delta?" tells them how much they make or lose for a given market move.

Deep dive: Risk Reference — Greek formulas | Derivatives Deep Dive — Part 4 Options

Gamma (Γ)

One-liner: How fast delta changes per $1 move in the underlying.

What it actually means: Gamma is the acceleration of your P&L. It tells you how unstable your delta is. High gamma means a small move in the underlying causes a large change in your delta — your directional exposure is shifting rapidly. Gamma is highest for at-the-money options near expiry.

Example: You're short 1,000 ATM puts with gamma of 0.05. If the stock drops $5, your delta changes by 0.05 × 5 = 0.25. You went from delta +0.50 (short puts) to delta +0.75 — you're getting longer as the market drops, which means your losses are accelerating. That's the short gamma trap.

Long gamma vs short gamma:
- Long gamma = you own options. Your delta improves in both directions (you get shorter when the market drops, longer when it rises). You pay theta for this luxury. You WANT big moves.
- Short gamma = you sold options. Your delta works against you in both directions. You collect theta as compensation. You WANT the market to sit still.

Why traders care: Short gamma concentration in the market creates cascading moves. If dealers are short gamma at a strike, they must buy as the market rises and sell as it falls — amplifying the move. This is the exact dynamics you mapped with the skew scanner at BNP.

Deep dive: Risk Reference — Greeks section | Probe Defense Guide — gamma/convexity probes

Vega (ν)

One-liner: How much the option price moves per 1% change in implied volatility.

What it actually means: Vega measures your exposure to changes in the market's fear level. When implied vol rises (people get scared), options get more expensive — if you're long vega, you profit. If you're short vega, you lose. Vega is highest for long-dated ATM options.

Example: You're long 500 straddles with vega of $200 per contract. If implied vol rises 3 points, you make 500 × $200 × 3 = $300,000 — even if the stock doesn't move at all.

The delta-vega interaction in commodities: When oil drops sharply, implied vol typically spikes. So a portfolio that's long delta AND long vega has a partial natural hedge — the vega gain offsets some of the delta loss. This is the hedging color you provided at BNP.

Deep dive: Risk Reference | Asset Classes And Portfolios — options strategies

Theta (Θ)

One-liner: How much value an option loses per day from time decay.

What it actually means: Options are wasting assets — every day that passes, the probability of a large move decreases, so the option becomes less valuable. Theta is the daily cost of being long options, or the daily income from being short options. Theta accelerates as expiry approaches — the last 30 days see the fastest decay.

Example: You're short a straddle collecting $500/day in theta. As long as the stock stays near the strike, you pocket $500 daily. But if the stock gaps 5% overnight, your gamma loss easily exceeds weeks of theta income. Short theta/gamma is picking up pennies in front of a steamroller — profitable most days, devastating on rare ones.

The theta-gamma trade-off: This is fundamental. You can't be long gamma without paying theta, and you can't collect theta without being short gamma. Every options strategy is fundamentally a bet on whether the realized move will exceed the implied move (gamma vs theta).

Deep dive: Risk Reference | Derivatives Deep Dive — Part 4

Rho (ρ)

One-liner: How much the option price moves per 1% change in interest rates.

What it actually means: Higher rates increase call values (because the present value of the strike payment decreases) and decrease put values. Rho is small for short-dated options but material for long-dated ones — a 2-year LEAP on a stock is significantly affected by a 100bp rate change.

Example: A 1-year call with rho of $0.50 gains $0.50 if rates rise 1%. On a $100M notional position, that's $500K — not trivial for a portfolio with long-dated options exposure.

SECTION 2: RISK MEASURES — HOW TO QUANTIFY PORTFOLIO RISK

Value at Risk (VaR)

One-liner: The maximum loss you'd expect to NOT exceed at a given confidence level over a given horizon.

What it actually means: "95% VaR of $10M over 1 day" means: on 95% of days, you lose less than $10M. On the other 5% of days? VaR says nothing. That's its fatal flaw — it tells you about normal days, not crisis days.

Three methods:

Method How it works Pros Cons
Historical Use actual past returns No distributional assumption Past may not repeat; limited by data window
Parametric Assume normal distribution, use mean/vol/correlation Fast, simple Assumes normality — underestimates tails
Monte Carlo Simulate thousands of scenarios Handles non-linear payoffs, fat tails Computationally expensive, model-dependent

The problem with VaR in practice: It assumes normal distributions, but market returns have fat tails. The 2008 crisis was a "25-sigma event" according to VaR models — which is statistically impossible. The model wasn't capturing reality.

Deep dive: Risk Reference — VaR section with formulas

Expected Shortfall (ES) / Conditional VaR (CVaR)

One-liner: The average loss GIVEN that you're beyond VaR — what actually happens in the tail.

What it actually means: If 95% VaR is $10M, ES asks: "when I DO lose more than $10M, how much do I lose on average?" The answer might be $18M. That's the number that matters in a crisis. ES replaced VaR under FRTB (Basel's new framework) precisely because it captures tail risk.

Why it's better: VaR can be gamed — a bank can restructure positions to have low VaR but catastrophic tail losses. ES can't be gamed the same way because it averages over the entire tail, not just one threshold.

Deep dive: Risk Reference — ES section

Stress Testing

One-liner: "What happens to our portfolio if [specific scenario] occurs?"

What it actually means: Unlike VaR which uses statistical distributions, stress testing uses defined scenarios — historical (replay March 2020) or hypothetical (oil hits $150 + Fed hikes 50bps). You reprice the entire portfolio under the stressed parameters and measure the P&L impact.

Three types:

Type Example Purpose
Historical replay March 2020 COVID crash "Would we survive a repeat?"
Hypothetical Oil $150 + rates +200bps + VIX 60 "What's our worst case from current risks?"
Sensitivity Equity -10%, -20%, -30% "How does our P&L scale with the magnitude of a move?"

What you built at BNP: A live stress-testing model that ran scenarios continuously across all client portfolios. Predefined scenarios (rate shifts ±50/100/200bps, equity drops 5/10/20%, commodity moves ±15-30%) plus ad-hoc scenarios. Showed P&L impact by asset class, by client, and identified the biggest risk contributors.

Deep dive: Risk Reference — stress testing section | Probe Defense Guide — stress model probe

Sharpe Ratio

One-liner: Return per unit of risk. (Return - Risk-free rate) / Volatility.

What it actually means: A Sharpe of 1.0 means you earn 1% excess return for every 1% of volatility. A Sharpe above 1.0 is good, above 2.0 is elite, below 0.5 is questionable. Most hedge funds target 1.0-1.5 net of fees.

Limitation: Treats upside and downside volatility equally. A fund that makes +5% and +10% alternating months has high vol but great returns — Sharpe penalizes it unfairly. That's why Sortino exists.

Sortino Ratio

One-liner: Like Sharpe but only penalizes downside volatility.

What it actually means: Uses downside deviation (only negative returns) instead of total volatility. A fund with lots of upside surprises and few drawdowns will have a much higher Sortino than Sharpe. More relevant for asymmetric strategies.

Maximum Drawdown

One-liner: The largest peak-to-trough decline in the portfolio.

What it actually means: If a fund goes from $100M to $85M to $92M to $78M, the max drawdown is ($100M - $78M) / $100M = 22%. This is the number that gets risk managers fired and PMs shut down. Institutional investors care about max drawdown more than any other metric because it represents the worst pain they endured.

Drawdown management: At a pod-level fund like Squarepoint, PMs typically have drawdown limits (e.g., -5% of allocated capital). Breach the soft limit → risk meeting and position reduction. Breach the hard limit → positions liquidated, potentially lose the allocation.

Deep dive: Hedge Fund Mechanics — Part 11: Drawdown & Risk Budgeting

Beta and Alpha

One-liner: Beta = sensitivity to the market. Alpha = return above what beta explains.

Beta: A portfolio with beta 1.3 to the S&P 500 moves 30% more than the index in both directions. To hedge to market-neutral, you'd sell $130 of S&P futures per $100 of portfolio.

Alpha: The residual return after removing beta exposure. If the S&P returns 10%, your beta contribution is 1.3 × 10% = 13%. If your portfolio returned 18%, your alpha is 18% - 13% = 5%. Alpha is what PMs get paid for. Alpha is what LPs invest in hedge funds for.

Why this matters for risk: A discretionary risk manager needs to decompose returns into beta and alpha continuously. If a PM's P&L is 90% beta, they're not adding value — the LP could buy an ETF cheaper.

SECTION 3: PORTFOLIO RISK CONCEPTS

Correlation

One-liner: How two assets move together. +1 = identical, -1 = opposite, 0 = independent.

What it actually means: Correlation determines whether diversification works. A portfolio of 10 assets with low correlation has much less risk than 10 assets with high correlation — even if each individual asset has the same volatility.

The crisis problem: Correlations are unstable. In calm markets, stocks and bonds are negatively correlated (diversification works). In a stagflation environment like right now — stocks AND bonds can sell off together. Correlation spiked to 0.8+ across multi-strategy books in Q1 2026 and blew up funds like Balyasny (-3.8%), ExodusPoint (-4.5%), and Caxton (-$1.3B).

Conditional correlation: Assets may have 0.2 correlation in normal markets but 0.8 correlation during a crisis. The correlation that matters for risk management is the conditional correlation — the one during the exact scenario you're trying to hedge against.

Deep dive: Market Intuition — cross-market relationships | Market Context Today — correlation map

Concentration Risk

One-liner: Too much exposure to a single name, sector, factor, or theme.

What it actually means: A portfolio can look diversified by number of positions but be concentrated in hidden ways. 20 tech stocks is 20 names but one bet (growth/rates). A long oil / short airlines / long energy services portfolio is three positions but one bet (oil goes up). Concentration risk is the enemy of survivability.

How a risk manager catches it:
- Single-name concentration: No position > X% of NAV
- Sector concentration: No sector > X% of gross
- Factor concentration: Run factor decomposition — are all positions loading on the same factor (momentum, value, growth)?
- Correlation-adjusted concentration: Two "different" positions that are 0.9 correlated are effectively one position

Basis Risk

One-liner: The risk that your hedge doesn't move exactly like the position it's supposed to protect.

What it actually means: You hedge a corporate bond portfolio by shorting Treasury futures. If credit spreads widen while Treasuries rally (flight to quality), your hedge loses money AND your portfolio loses money. You hedged interest rate risk but not credit risk — and the basis between the two moved against you.

Common basis risks:

Position Hedge Basis risk
Corporate bond Treasury futures Credit spread moves
Jet fuel exposure WTI crude futures Crack spread moves
Single stock Index futures Idiosyncratic risk not hedged
European option Exchange-traded futures Timing and settlement differences

Liquidity Risk

One-liner: The risk you can't exit a position at your expected price — or at all.

What it actually means: A position's paper value and its realizable value can diverge violently under stress. A $50M position in a liquid large-cap might take an hour to exit with minimal impact. A $50M position in an illiquid small-cap, EM bond, or structured product might take days and cost 2-5% in market impact.

When liquidity fails (cascade): Price drops → margin calls → forced selling → prices drop more → more margin calls → bid disappears entirely. March 2020 Treasury basis trade: funds with 50x leverage on cash-futures basis had to liquidate when repo lenders pulled funding. The Fed bought $1T of Treasuries to restore order.

Risk manager's rule: Always ask "what's funding this position?" Short-term funded, overnight repo, or leveraged margin positions blow up first in a liquidity crisis.

Deep dive: Hedge Fund Mechanics — margin, prime brokerage, repo

Drawdown Budget & Risk Budget

One-liner: How much a PM is allowed to lose (drawdown budget) and how much total risk they can take (risk budget).

What it actually means: The Investment Committee allocates risk to each PM. This can be expressed as:
- VaR budget: "Your daily 95% VaR can't exceed $5M"
- Vol budget: "Your annualized portfolio vol can't exceed 12%"
- Notional limits: "Your gross exposure can't exceed $500M"
- Drawdown limits: "If you lose 5% of your allocation, we review. At 8%, we cut. At 10%, we liquidate."

The risk manager's role: Monitor in real-time whether any PM is approaching their limits. The hard part is the conversation BEFORE the breach — "you're at 4.2% drawdown with 5% as the trigger. What's the plan?"

SECTION 4: DERIVATIVES — THE INSTRUMENTS

Futures

One-liner: Standardized contract to buy/sell an asset at a fixed price on a future date, traded on an exchange with daily margining.

Key mechanics:
- Daily mark-to-market: Your P&L settles every day via variation margin — you never accumulate large unrealized exposure
- Initial margin: The deposit you post upfront (~5-12% of notional) — this IS your leverage
- Cost of carry: Futures price = Spot + Cost of carry (interest, storage, insurance) - Convenience yield
- Contango: Futures > Spot (normal — cost of carry is positive). Rolling costs money.
- Backwardation: Futures < Spot (supply tightness). Rolling earns money.
- The -$37 WTI event (April 2020): Futures can go negative when physical delivery is impossible and storage is full

Example contracts:

Contract Exchange Multiplier Notional (approx)
ES (S&P 500) CME $50 × index ~$330K
CL (WTI Crude) NYMEX 1,000 barrels ~$115K
ZN (10-Year Note) CBOT $1,000 × price ~$110K
GC (Gold) COMEX 100 troy oz ~$465K

Deep dive: Derivatives Deep Dive — Part 2: Futures Full Picture

Options

One-liner: The RIGHT (not obligation) to buy (call) or sell (put) at a fixed price (strike) by a fixed date (expiry).

Moneyness:

State Call Put Delta (approx)
Deep ITM Spot >> Strike Spot << Strike ~0.90-1.00 / -0.90 to -1.00
ATM Spot ≈ Strike Spot ≈ Strike ~0.50 / -0.50
OTM Spot < Strike Spot > Strike ~0.05-0.30 / -0.05 to -0.30

Intrinsic vs Time Value:
- Intrinsic: The value if exercised NOW. Call: max(0, Spot - Strike). Put: max(0, Strike - Spot).
- Time value: Everything above intrinsic. Driven by volatility, time to expiry, and rates.
- Total option price = Intrinsic + Time value. At expiry, time value = 0.

American vs European vs Asian:

Type Exercise Where used Key difference
American Any time before expiry US equities, ETFs Can be exercised early, worth ≥ European
European Only at expiry Indices (SPX), FX Simpler to price (Black-Scholes applies directly)
Asian Payoff based on average price Commodities Lower effective vol because averaging reduces variance. Cheaper. This is what you priced at BNP Paris.

Put-Call Parity: Call - Put = Spot - PV(Strike). If this relationship breaks, there's an arbitrage. It's the fundamental constraint that links all option prices together.

Implied Volatility: The vol that makes the Black-Scholes formula reproduce the observed market price. It's not a forecast — it's a PRICE expressed in vol units. When you "buy vol," you're buying options because you think realized vol will exceed implied vol.

Skew: Implied vol varies by strike. For equities, puts are more expensive (higher IV) than calls — the demand for downside protection creates the "smirk." The steepness of skew tells you how much crash insurance the market is pricing.

Deep dive: Derivatives Deep Dive — Part 4: Options Full Picture | Risk Reference — Greeks and formulas

Swaps

One-liner: Agreement to exchange cash flows between two parties over time.

Interest Rate Swap (IRS):
- Most common: exchange fixed rate for floating rate (e.g., SOFR)
- "Receiving fixed" = you get fixed, pay floating. You profit if rates FALL
- "Paying fixed" = you get floating, pay fixed. You profit if rates RISE
- DV01 for a 5-year swap ≈ $45,000 per $100M notional per basis point

Total Return Swap (TRS):
- One party gets the total return of an asset (price + dividends), the other gets a financing rate (SOFR + spread)
- Used for leverage without owning the asset. Archegos used TRS to build $30B+ in concentrated stock positions with minimal disclosure — banks couldn't see the aggregate exposure across different prime brokers

Credit Default Swap (CDS):
- Insurance against default. Buyer pays periodic premium (spread), seller pays par on default.
- CDS spread of 250bps with 40% recovery → implied annual default probability ≈ 250 / (1-0.40) ≈ 4.2% (risk-neutral)
- The CDS-bond basis (difference between CDS spread and cash bond spread) is a common relative value trade

SECTION 5: HEDGING — STRATEGIES AND DECISION FRAMEWORK

Key Hedging Strategies

Strategy How it works Cost Upside cap? Best for
Buy puts Buy downside protection Premium (~3-5%/quarter) No Full protection, willing to pay
Collar Buy OTM puts + sell OTM calls Near zero (call finances put) Yes (capped) PM has a price target
Put spread Buy put + sell further OTM put Reduced (~half of naked put) No, but protection has a floor Budget-conscious protection
Sell futures Reduce net delta directly No premium, but gives up upside 1:1 Yes (fully reduces delta) Quick de-risk
Pairs/beta hedge Long the position + short a correlated asset Carry cost No Isolate idiosyncratic vs market risk

Decision Framework — Which Hedge to Use

  1. Time horizon: Short-term → futures or short-dated puts. Long-term → spreads or collars.
  2. Upside tolerance: PM willing to cap gains? → Collar. Not willing? → Puts or spreads.
  3. Cost tolerance: Zero cost? → Collar. Willing to pay? → Puts.
  4. What risk specifically? Directional → delta hedge. Volatility → vega hedge. Tail → OTM puts.
  5. Basis risk: How well does the hedge instrument correlate with the position?

The hedge that doesn't work: A simple stop-loss. In a gap-down — which is exactly when you need protection — a stop-loss doesn't execute at your price. You're exposed to the gap.

Deep dive: Asset Classes And Portfolios — options strategy table | Hedge Fund Mechanics — hedging mechanics

Dynamic Hedging & Rebalancing

One-liner: Continuously adjusting your hedge as the underlying moves, rather than setting it once and forgetting.

What it actually means: If you're delta-hedging an options book, your hedge changes every time the underlying moves (because delta changes via gamma). You need to rebalance your futures/stock position to stay hedged. How often you rebalance depends on:
- Transaction costs: More rebalancing = more costs
- Gamma magnitude: Higher gamma = delta changes faster = more frequent rebalancing needed
- Market conditions: In volatile markets, rebalancing is more frequent but also more expensive (wider spreads)

The hedging P&L: In theory (Black-Scholes world), continuous delta hedging of a long option position breaks even — you earn from gamma exactly what you pay in theta. In reality, discrete hedging + transaction costs + jumps mean the P&L depends on whether realized vol exceeds implied vol.

Conviction and Position Sizing

One-liner: Size should scale with conviction AND with how well you can define the downside.

What it actually means: High conviction with a clear stop-loss lets you size up. High conviction with undefined risk — say, shorting a heavily shorted name into a squeeze — requires smaller sizing no matter how right you think you are.

Framework in risk units: If a PM normally runs 1-2% of NAV per position, a high-conviction trade might go to 3-4% — but only if the IC-allocated risk budget can absorb it. If sizing up one position means the portfolio-level VaR or vol breaches the mandate, the answer is no regardless of conviction.

Red flag: PM wants to increase size on a position that's underwater. If the thesis hasn't changed AND new information supports the trade, averaging down can make sense. But " it has to come back" with no specific catalyst is hope, not a thesis.

When a Thesis Is Broken vs Just Being Early

Three tests:
1. Has the catalyst already happened and the position didn't respond? If you were long into earnings, earnings beat expectations, and the stock sold off — the thesis is broken. The market knows something you don't.
2. Has the fundamental assumption changed? If the trade was "oil supply tightens by Q2" and OPEC just announced a production increase, the thesis is broken. If oil is just volatile on the PATH to restriction, you're early, not wrong.
3. Is the time horizon expiring? A 3-month trade that hasn't worked after 2.5 months with the catalyst still ahead is early. The same trade at month 4 with no catalyst in sight is broken. Time decay applies to theses, not just options.

What a risk manager does: Challenge the PM — "walk me through again why this works." If the explanation is materially different from the original thesis, it's been refitted to justify the loss. That's the biggest behavioral red flag.

SECTION 6: VOLATILITY — THE TRADEABLE PRODUCT

Implied vs Realized Volatility

One-liner: Implied vol = what the market EXPECTS. Realized vol = what actually HAPPENED.

What it actually means:
- Implied vol is derived from option prices. It's not a forecast — it's a price. When people say "vol is cheap" they mean implied vol is low relative to what they think will actually happen.
- Realized vol (historical vol) measures actual past price fluctuations. Typically calculated as annualized standard deviation of log returns.
- The spread: Implied vol is almost always higher than subsequent realized vol — this is the vol risk premium. Option sellers systematically collect this premium. Option buyers pay it for insurance.

Example: VIX at 24 means S&P implied vol is 24% annualized. If the S&P subsequently realizes 18% vol, option sellers made money (they sold expensive insurance). If it realizes 30%, option buyers profited (the insurance paid off).

The Volatility Surface

What it is: A 3D map of implied volatility across all strikes (X-axis) and all tenors (Y-axis). Every listed option corresponds to one point on this surface.

Why it matters:
- Skew (across strikes): Puts are more expensive than calls → the market fears crashes more than rallies
- Term structure (across tenors): Short-term vol > long-term vol → market fears an imminent event. Long-term vol > short-term → calm now but uncertain ahead
- Smile: Both OTM puts AND OTM calls trade at higher implied vol than ATM — common in FX markets

Calibration: Fitting a model (SABR, Heston, local vol) to reproduce observed market prices across the surface. The calibrated model then prices exotic options that don't have direct quotes. This is what you used at BNP Paris and what you did with SABR at Natixis.

Deep dive: Probe Defense Guide — vol surface calibration probe | Natixis Defense Guide — SABR

Vol as a Tradeable Product

One-liner: Volatility itself can be bought and sold, independent of direction.

Instruments:
- Straddles/strangles: Buy both calls and puts → pure vol play. You profit if the market moves a lot in either direction.
- VIX futures/options: Trade expectations of future vol directly. VIX futures term structure is usually in contango (further out months higher) because of the vol risk premium.
- Variance swaps: Pay/receive the difference between implied and realized variance. Pure exposure to vol without delta.

Why this matters for risk: A PM who says "I'm not taking a vol view" might have vol exposure they don't realize. A concentrated long equity book is implicitly short vol — if vol spikes, equity typically drops. Understanding the portfolio's vol exposure is a key risk function.

SECTION 7: MARKET STRUCTURE — HOW THINGS ACTUALLY WORK

How a Trade Flows (Lifecycle)

PM decision → Order to broker/exchange → Execution → Clearing (CCP or bilateral)
    → Settlement (T+1 or T+2) → Position in portfolio → Daily P&L/margin
    → Eventually: close/expire/exercise/roll

Listed: Exchange-traded, CCP-cleared, standardized, daily margin, transparent pricing.
OTC: Bilateral, ISDA/CSA governed, bespoke terms, counterparty credit risk, less transparent.

Deep dive: Market Intuition — trade lifecycle

Margin Mechanics

Type What it is When it's called
Initial Margin (IM) Deposit to open a position (~5-12% of notional for futures) At trade inception
Variation Margin (VM) Daily P&L settlement — cash flows between winners and losers Every day
Maintenance Margin Minimum balance required. If equity falls below → margin call When positions move against you
Portfolio Margin Cross-offsets between correlated positions reduce total margin Sophisticated accounts

Margin call escalation:
1. Position moves against you → equity drops below maintenance
2. Broker/CCP issues margin call (typically T+1 morning)
3. You must deposit cash/collateral by deadline (usually same day)
4. If you don't → forced liquidation at market prices (usually the worst possible time)

Contango vs Backwardation

Contango: Futures price > Spot price. Normal for most commodities because of storage/financing costs. Rolling long positions in contango is expensive — you sell the expiring cheap contract and buy the more expensive next one ("negative roll yield").

Backwardation: Futures price < Spot price. Signals supply tightness or high convenience yield. Rolling long positions in backwardation is profitable — positive roll yield.

Current relevance: Oil is in steep backwardation because of Hormuz disruption — near-term supply is extremely tight, so the front contract trades at a massive premium to later months.

Deep dive: Derivatives Deep Dive — Part 2: Futures | Asset Classes And Portfolios

Leverage

One-liner: Using borrowed capital to amplify returns — and losses.

How hedge funds lever:
- Futures: ~10% margin = 10x leverage on notional
- Prime brokerage financing: Borrow against portfolio (typically 2-4x for long/short equity)
- Total return swaps: Synthetic exposure without owning the asset
- Repo: Short-term borrowing against collateral (typically overnight to 30 days)

Why leverage kills: A 5% move against a 10x levered position = 50% loss. Lehman died with 30x leverage. LTCM with 25x. Archegos with effectively 15-20x. Leverage turns survivable losses into fatal ones.

SECTION 8: ASSET CLASSES — WHAT MOVES THEM

Equities

Drivers: Earnings, economic growth (GDP, PMIs), interest rates, sector rotation, flows
Key metrics: P/E, EV/EBITDA, earnings yield vs bond yield, beta, sector exposure
Current context: S&P at 6,611, VIX 24 (elevated but not panic), worst Q1 since 2022, energy outperforming, tech underperforming

Deep dive: Asset Classes And Portfolios — equities section

Rates / Fixed Income

Drivers: Central bank policy, inflation expectations, economic growth, fiscal deficits, flight to quality
Key metrics: Yield, duration (sensitivity to rate changes), DV01, convexity, credit spread
The yield curve: 2Y-10Y spread measures curve shape. Inverted = recession signal. Steepening = growth expectations improving (or long-end selling off on inflation fears).
Current context: US 10Y at 4.35%, Fed at 3.75%, market split on whether Fed cuts April 29. Stock-bond correlation is POSITIVE (stagflation regime — both can sell off together).

Duration: How much a bond's price changes per 1% change in yield. A bond with duration 7 loses ~7% for every 1% rate rise. DV01 = duration × notional × 0.0001.

Convexity: Duration changes as rates move. Positive convexity (long bonds) means duration decreases as rates rise = losses decelerate. Negative convexity (MBS, callable bonds) means duration increases as rates rise = losses accelerate. This is the MBS risk manager's nightmare.

Deep dive: Asset Classes And Portfolios — rates section | Market Context Today

Commodities

Drivers: Supply/demand balance, geopolitics, weather (ags/power), inventory data, OPEC (oil)
Sub-classes: Oil, natural gas, power, emissions, base metals, precious metals, agriculture
Key concepts: Physical vs financial, storage costs, convenience yield, crack spreads, seasonal patterns

SECTION 10: RISK MANAGEMENT FRAMEWORKS

Position-Level Risk Assessment (use this framework for ANY position)

  1. Thesis: What's the trade? What's the PM betting on?
  2. Sizing: Notional as % of NAV. Gross and net exposure.
  3. Exposures: Delta, beta, sector, factor, concentration.
  4. Scenarios: What kills it? Historical replay + hypothetical.
  5. Hedging: What protection exists? Cost? Basis risk?
  6. Liquidity: How long to exit? Market impact estimate?
  7. Limits: Within VaR budget? Drawdown budget? Notional limits?

Portfolio-Level Risk Assessment

  1. Aggregation: Do individual positions compound or offset each other?
  2. Correlation: Are "diversified" positions actually correlated?
  3. Factor exposure: Are all positions loading on the same factor (momentum, growth, oil)?
  4. Tail risk: What's the worst case across the whole book? Do hedges work in that scenario?
  5. Liquidity profile: What % of the book can be liquidated in 1 day? 1 week?

Discretionary vs Systematic Risk Management

Dimension Systematic Discretionary
