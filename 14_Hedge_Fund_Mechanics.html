HEDGE FUND MECHANICS — OPERATIONS, STRUCTURE & PLUMBING

Everything Behind the Scenes That Makes a Fund Run

PART 1: FUND STRUCTURE & ECONOMICS

1.1 The LP/GP Structure

Every hedge fund has the same basic legal architecture:

Role Who What They Do
General Partner (GP) The fund manager (e.g., Squarepoint Capital) Makes all investment decisions. Bears unlimited liability. Earns management fee + performance fee.
Limited Partners (LPs) Investors — pensions, endowments, sovereign wealth funds, family offices, fund-of-funds Provide capital. Limited liability (can only lose what they invest). No say in day-to-day decisions.
Fund Vehicle The fund itself (usually a Cayman Islands LP or Delaware LP) Legal entity that holds the positions. Separate from the management company.
Management Company Operating entity Employs the people, pays the rent, runs the business. Funded by the management fee.

Why Cayman Islands? Tax-neutral jurisdiction — the fund itself pays no tax. LPs pay tax in their own jurisdictions. This avoids double taxation. ~70% of hedge fund AUM is domiciled offshore.

Onshore vs Offshore:
- Offshore fund (Cayman LP): For non-US investors, tax-exempt US investors (endowments, pensions)
- Onshore fund (Delaware LP): For US taxable investors
- Master-Feeder structure: Both feed into a single master fund that executes all trades. One portfolio, two entry points.

1.2 Fee Structure — How the Fund Makes Money

Management Fee (typically 1–2%)

$$\text{Management Fee} = \text{AUM} \times \text{Fee Rate}$$

  • Charged on committed capital or NAV, depending on the fund
  • Paid quarterly or monthly, regardless of performance
  • Covers operating costs: salaries, rent, technology, data, prime broker fees
  • Squarepoint likely charges ~1.5% management fee (systematic tends to be slightly lower than discretionary)

Example: $10B AUM × 1.5% = $150M/year in management fees. That's before a single trade is made.

Performance Fee (typically 15–20%)

$$\text{Performance Fee} = \text{Net Profits} \times \text{Incentive Rate}$$

  • Charged on net new profits above the high-water mark
  • Typically 20% for discretionary, 15-20% for systematic
  • This is where the real money is — both for the fund AND for your bonus

Example: Fund starts year at $10B, ends at $11.5B. Net profit = $1.5B.
Performance fee = $1.5B × 20% = $300M. That's the bonus pool.

High-Water Mark (HWM)

The HWM is the highest NAV the fund has ever reached. You only earn performance fees on profits above the HWM.

Year 1: Fund goes from $100 → $120. HWM = $120. Perf fee on $20.
Year 2: Fund drops from $120 → $105. HWM still = $120. NO perf fee.
Year 3: Fund goes from $105 → $130. HWM = $130. Perf fee only on $130 - $120 = $10.
         (The $15 recovery from $105 to $120 earns nothing — that's "climbing back to the watermark.")

Why this matters for risk managers: If the fund is below HWM, PMs are under massive pressure to recoup losses. This is when risk management is hardest — the temptation to increase risk to "make back" losses is enormous. Your job is to hold the line.

Hurdle Rate

Some funds require a minimum return before performance fees kick in. The hurdle is usually the risk-free rate (e.g., SOFR or T-bills).

$$\text{Performance Fee} = (\text{Return} - \text{Hurdle Rate}) \times \text{AUM} \times \text{Incentive Rate}$$

Example: Fund returns 12%, hurdle is 4% (risk-free), AUM is $5B.
Fee = (12% - 4%) × $5B × 20% = $80M

Hard hurdle = fee only on excess over hurdle. Soft hurdle = if you beat the hurdle, you earn fees on the full return (more common).

Clawback Provision

If a PM earns a performance fee, then subsequently loses money, the fund can "claw back" previously paid fees. In practice:
- Individual PM clawbacks are common in multi-PM platforms
- Fund-level clawbacks to LPs are rarer but exist in some structures
- Creates alignment: you can't take one big bet, collect your fee, then blow up

1.3 LP Terms — The Fine Print

Term What It Means Typical Range
Lock-up period Minimum time LP must keep capital in the fund before they can withdraw 1–3 years
Redemption notice How far in advance LP must notify before withdrawing 30–90 days
Redemption frequency How often LPs can redeem Monthly, quarterly, or annually
Gate provision Cap on how much capital can leave in a single redemption period Typically 10–25% of fund NAV per quarter
Side pocket Illiquid investments separated into a locked account — LP can't redeem until the position is liquidated Used for distressed or hard-to-value assets
Soft lock LP can redeem during lock-up but pays a penalty (e.g., 2–5% of withdrawal) Common in multi-strategy funds
Key person clause If a named principal leaves (CIO, founder), LPs can trigger redemption Almost universal

Why gates matter for risk: If a fund has a bad month and many LPs rush to redeem simultaneously, the gate prevents a forced liquidation spiral. Without gates, the fund would have to sell positions into a falling market to raise cash, making losses worse (see: Long-Term Capital Management, 1998).

PART 2: NAV — THE HEARTBEAT OF THE FUND

2.1 What Is NAV?

$$\text{NAV} = \text{Total Assets} - \text{Total Liabilities}$$

$$\text{NAV per Share} = \frac{\text{NAV}}{\text{Shares Outstanding}}$$

NAV is recalculated daily (for most liquid funds) or monthly (for less liquid strategies). It's the single number that determines:
- LP account values (what their investment is worth)
- Performance fees (calculated on NAV changes)
- High-water mark (highest NAV ever)
- Management fees (charged on NAV)
- Risk limits (often expressed as % of NAV)

2.2 NAV Calculation — Step by Step

1. Mark all positions to market (using end-of-day prices)
2. + Accrued income (dividends declared but not yet paid, bond coupons)
3. + Cash and cash equivalents (money market, T-bills, bank deposits)
4. - Accrued expenses (management fees, admin costs, legal, audit)
5. - Accrued performance fees (if applicable, crystallized or estimated)
6. - Borrowed funds / margin loans (prime broker financing)
7. - Payables (trades settled but not yet paid, pending redemptions)
= NAV

2.3 Valuation Challenges

Issue What Happens Risk Implication
Stale pricing A position hasn't traded in days — the "market price" is old NAV may not reflect true value. Common in illiquid credit, OTC derivatives, EM bonds.
Mark-to-model No market price exists, so you model the value Level 3 assets (IFRS/GAAP fair value hierarchy). Subjective. PM may have incentive to mark favorably.
Side pockets Illiquid positions separated, not included in redeemable NAV Protects liquid LPs but locks others into illiquid positions
Swing pricing NAV adjusted up/down on heavy redemption/subscription days to protect existing LPs Redeeming LPs bear the cost of their own liquidity, not continuing LPs
Equalization Adjusting for different LP entry dates so everyone pays fair performance fees Complex — if you entered at a higher NAV, you shouldn't pay perf fee on gains the fund already had before you joined

What to say if asked: "NAV is what keeps everything honest. It's the daily mark-to-market truth of the fund. The risk challenge is when positions are hard to mark — illiquid credit, exotic OTC structures, distressed debt — because that's where the gap between reported NAV and realizable value can be widest. As a risk manager, I'd be focused on the valuation policy for Level 2 and Level 3 assets and making sure we're conservative in our marks."

PART 3: PRIME BROKERAGE — THE FUND'S BACKBONE

3.1 What Does a Prime Broker Do?

A prime broker (PB) is the fund's operational backbone. The big PBs: Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, JPMorgan, BofA.

Service What It Means Why It Matters
Custody Holds the fund's assets (securities, cash) Segregation rules protect fund assets if PB goes bankrupt (mostly — Lehman showed the cracks)
Clearing & Settlement Executes and settles trades on behalf of the fund Fund trades through multiple executing brokers, all settle through the PB
Financing / Leverage Lends money to the fund for leveraged positions PB provides margin loans, repo financing, synthetic leverage (swaps). This is how the fund levers up.
Securities Lending Lends shares to the fund for short selling PB borrows from long-only holders (pensions, ETFs), lends to the fund. Charges a borrow fee.
Capital Introduction Introduces the fund to potential LPs PB has relationships with allocators. Helps smaller funds raise capital.
Reporting Daily position reports, margin reports, P&L, risk exposure Fund risk team uses PB reports as a data source (and to reconcile against internal books)
Technology Trade execution platforms, risk analytics, portfolio reporting Some PBs provide Aladdin-like tools as part of the relationship

3.2 How Leverage Works Through a PB

The PB extends a margin loan to the fund. The fund posts collateral (cash or securities), and the PB lends against it.

Example — Long equity on margin:
- Fund wants to buy $100M of equities
- PB requires 40% margin → fund posts $40M cash
- PB lends $60M at SOFR + 40bps
- Fund's leverage on this position = 100/40 = 2.5x

Example — Short selling:
- Fund wants to short $50M of TSLA
- PB locates shares (borrows from a pension fund holding TSLA)
- Fund receives $50M cash from the sale, posts it as collateral
- PB charges a borrow fee (say 3% annualized for TSLA — it's "general collateral," easy to borrow)
- If TSLA is "hard to borrow" (heavily shorted, small float), the fee could be 10-50%+
- Fund also posts additional margin (typically 30-50%) in case TSLA goes up

Short Selling Mechanics — Full Lifecycle

1. PM decides to short TSLA
2. Risk manager checks: is the borrow available? What's the cost? Does it fit within limits?
3. PB locates shares (from long holders — pensions, ETFs, mutual funds)
4. Fund borrows shares and sells them in the market → cash comes in
5. Fund posts margin (the cash from sale + additional buffer)
6. Daily mark-to-market: if TSLA goes up, fund owes more margin. If down, excess margin returned.
7. PM decides to close: fund buys shares back in market, returns them to lender
8. Profit = sell price - buy price - borrow cost - margin financing cost

RISKS:
- Short squeeze: stock spikes, forced to cover at a loss (GameStop, Jan 2021)
- Borrow recall: lender wants shares back, fund must cover or find new borrow
- Unlimited loss: stock can go up infinitely (vs. long position which can only lose 100%)
- Hard-to-borrow: borrow cost can spike overnight if supply tightens

3.3 Leverage Ratios — What They Measure

Ratio Formula What It Means Typical Range
Gross Leverage (Long MV + Short MV ) / NAV
Net Leverage (Long MV - Short MV ) / NAV
Long/Short Ratio Long MV / Short MV
Notional Leverage Total Notional / NAV Includes derivative notional (swaps, futures) Can be 10x+ for systematic; misleading without context

Why gross vs. net matters: A fund could be 3x gross (a LOT of positions) but 0x net (perfectly hedged). Gross tells you how much total risk is in the book. Net tells you the directional tilt. A risk manager needs both.

Example — Squarepoint discretionary team:
- NAV: $2B allocated to discretionary
- Longs: $3B, Shorts: $1.5B
- Gross leverage: ($3B + $1.5B) / $2B = 2.25x
- Net leverage: ($3B - $1.5B) / $2B = 0.75x (long-biased)

3.4 Multi-Prime — Why Funds Use Multiple PBs

After Lehman Brothers collapsed in 2008, funds learned not to rely on a single PB:

Reason Explanation
Counterparty risk If your only PB goes bankrupt, your assets are frozen (Lehman LPs waited years to recover)
Leverage competition Multiple PBs compete on margin terms — fund gets better financing rates
Best execution Different PBs are strong in different markets — Goldman for equities, JPM for rates, MS for EM
Operational resilience If one PB has a system outage, the fund can still trade through others

Most large funds use 3-5 prime brokers. Squarepoint almost certainly uses 3+ given their size (~$182B regulatory AUM).

PART 4: MARGIN & COLLATERAL — THE NUTS AND BOLTS

4.1 Types of Margin

Margin Type Where It Applies How It Works
Initial Margin (IM) Futures, cleared swaps, PB margin loans Upfront deposit required to open a position. Like a security deposit.
Variation Margin (VM) Futures (daily), cleared swaps, bilateral OTC (post-EMIR/Dodd-Frank) Daily cash transfer reflecting mark-to-market gains/losses. Winner receives cash, loser pays cash — every single day.
Maintenance Margin Futures exchanges Minimum margin level. If your margin falls below this due to losses, you get a margin call.
Portfolio Margin PB margin accounts Risk-based margin that considers hedging across positions. A hedged book gets lower margin than the sum of individual margins.
Regulatory Margin (UMR) Bilateral OTC derivatives post-2016 Uncleared Margin Rules require initial margin for non-cleared OTC derivatives. Phased in by counterparty size.

Variation Margin — The Daily P&L Settlement

This is the most important margin concept for a fund risk manager:

Day 0: Fund buys 100 ES futures at 5,500. Posts initial margin = 100 × $15,000 = $1.5M.
Day 1: ES closes at 5,520. Gain = 100 × 20 × $50 = +$100,000.
        → Clearinghouse PAYS the fund $100,000 in cash (variation margin).
Day 2: ES closes at 5,490. Loss = 100 × 30 × $50 = -$150,000.
        → Fund PAYS the clearinghouse $150,000 in cash.
Day 3: ES closes at 5,470. Loss = 100 × 20 × $50 = -$100,000.
        → Fund pays another $100,000.
        → Total margin balance may now be below maintenance margin → MARGIN CALL.

Key insight: Variation margin means futures P&L is settled in cash daily. The fund needs sufficient cash to meet these daily calls. A large adverse move on a concentrated futures book can create an enormous cash drain overnight — this is the liquidity risk that kills funds.

4.2 Collateral Management

What Can Be Posted as Collateral?

Collateral Type Typical Haircut Notes
Cash (USD) 0% Gold standard. No discount. Earns a small return (PB pays ~SOFR - spread).
US Treasuries 1–5% Nearly as good as cash. Short-dated bills ~1%, long bonds ~5%.
Agency MBS 3–10% Lower quality, higher haircut
Investment Grade Corp Bonds 5–15% Credit risk adds uncertainty
Equities (S&P 500 names) 15–30% Volatile, so requires larger buffer
EM Sovereign Bonds 10–25% Country risk, FX risk
Illiquid / High Yield 25–50%+ Or may not be accepted at all

Haircut = the discount applied. If you post $100M in Treasuries with a 2% haircut, the PB credits you with $98M of collateral value.

Rehypothecation

Definition: The PB's right to reuse the fund's posted collateral for its own purposes (e.g., lending it to another client, using it to meet the PB's own margin requirements).

Scenario Implication
With rehypothecation PB can reuse your collateral → lower financing costs for the fund, BUT higher counterparty risk (if PB defaults, your collateral might be gone)
Without rehypothecation (segregated) Your collateral sits in a separate account → safer but more expensive (PB charges more because they can't profit from reusing it)

Post-Lehman reality: Most sophisticated funds now insist on segregated collateral for at least a portion of their IM. The extra cost is worth the reduced counterparty risk.

4.3 Margin Calls in Practice

Timeline of a margin call:
T+0 (Evening):  PB runs end-of-day margin calculation. Fund is $5M under margin requirement.
T+0 (Night):    Margin call issued to the fund (automated, usually via a portal or SWIFT message).
T+1 (Morning):  Fund must meet the call. Options:
                 1. Post cash (wire transfer)
                 2. Post additional securities (with haircut)
                 3. Reduce positions (sell longs or cover shorts to lower margin requirement)
                 4. If fund fails to meet call → PB has the right to liquidate positions

ESCALATION IF UNMET:
- First missed call: PB reaches out, demands immediate resolution
- Repeated failure: PB starts liquidating positions WITHOUT the fund's consent
- Extreme scenario: PB terminates the relationship entirely, liquidates everything

As a risk manager, your job is to make sure the fund NEVER gets a margin call it can't meet. This means:
1. Monitoring margin utilization in real time (not just end-of-day)
2. Stress-testing the margin requirement under adverse scenarios
3. Maintaining a cash buffer (typically 10-20% above minimum margin)
4. Knowing which positions are margin-efficient vs. margin-intensive

PART 5: CENTRAL CLEARING & CCPs

5.1 How a CCP Works

A Central Counterparty (CCP) stands between buyer and seller, guaranteeing both sides of the trade.

WITHOUT CCP:    Fund A ←→ Bank B     (bilateral — each faces the other's credit risk)

WITH CCP:       Fund A ←→ CCP ←→ Bank B   (CCP guarantees both sides)

Post-2008 mandate (Dodd-Frank Title VII / EMIR): Most standardized OTC derivatives must now be cleared through a CCP. This includes:
- Interest rate swaps (LCH SwapClear dominates)
- CDS indices (ICE Clear Credit)
- FX NDFs (increasingly)

Still bilateral (not cleared): Exotic options, bespoke structures, many commodity OTC, total return swaps (TRS).

5.2 CCP Risk Waterfall

If a clearing member defaults, losses are absorbed in this order:

1. Defaulter's initial margin          ← first line of defense
2. Defaulter's default fund contribution ← their share of the mutual pool
3. CCP's own capital ("skin in the game") ← typically a small amount
4. Non-defaulting members' default fund  ← mutualized loss (your money!)
5. CCP assessment powers               ← CCP can demand more from survivors
6. CCP resolution / failure            ← nuclear option, central bank intervention

Why this matters for risk: A fund clearing through a CCP is exposed to mutualized default risk — if another clearing member blows up spectacularly and the defaulter's margin + default fund isn't enough, YOUR fund's default fund contribution can be used. This is rare but real (see: Nasdaq Clearing member default, 2018 — Einar Aas, $100M+ loss mutualized).

5.3 Bilateral vs. Cleared — Key Differences

Feature Bilateral OTC Centrally Cleared
Counterparty risk Direct to your counterparty To the CCP (credit rating of the CCP)
Collateral Negotiated (ISDA CSA) Standardized (CCP rulebook)
Margin IM + VM required post-UMR (since 2016-2022) IM + daily VM (always required)
Netting Bilateral netting only with that counterparty Multilateral netting across all trades at the CCP
Portability If counterparty defaults, you're stuck in litigation Positions can be ported to another clearing member
Transparency Opaque Trade reporting to repositories

PART 6: REPO & SECURITIES LENDING — FUNDING THE BOOK

6.1 Repo — Full Mechanics

Repo (Repurchase Agreement): You sell a security and simultaneously agree to buy it back at a slightly higher price on a future date. The difference is the repo rate — it's effectively a collateralized loan.

Day 0: Fund needs cash. Sells $100M of Treasuries to repo counterparty.
        Receives: $100M × (1 - haircut) = $98M cash.
Day 1 (or T+N): Fund buys back the Treasuries at $98M × (1 + repo rate × days/360).
        If overnight repo at 4.5%: cost = $98M × 0.045 × 1/360 = ~$12,250.
Repo Type Duration Use Case
Overnight repo 1 day, rolled daily Short-term cash need. Most liquid.
Term repo 1 week to 3 months Locking in financing for a known horizon
Open repo No fixed maturity, can be terminated by either party with notice Flexible ongoing funding
Tri-party repo Settled through a third party (BNY Mellon, JPM) Reduces settlement risk, standardized collateral management

Reverse Repo

The mirror image: you LEND cash and RECEIVE securities as collateral. Used when you have excess cash and want a safe return.

$$\text{Fund lends \$100M cash} \xrightarrow{\text{receives}} \text{Treasuries as collateral}$$

The Fed's Reverse Repo Facility (RRP) is literally this — money market funds lend cash to the Fed overnight and receive Treasuries. At its peak in 2022, the RRP had ~$2.5 trillion parked in it.

General Collateral (GC) vs. Special

Type What It Means Rate
General Collateral (GC) Any acceptable security (e.g., any Treasury) — the lender doesn't care which one GC repo rate ≈ Fed Funds rate (~4.25-4.50% currently)
Special Lender specifically wants a particular bond (e.g., the on-the-run 10Y) — because they need to deliver it Special repo rate < GC rate (sometimes near 0% or negative — you PAY to borrow that specific bond)

Why this matters: If you're short a specific Treasury and it goes "on special," your funding cost spikes. Risk managers need to monitor special repo rates for their short positions.

6.2 Securities Lending — The Short Selling Supply Chain

Long-only holder (pension, ETF) → PB (intermediary) → Hedge Fund (borrower)

Flow:
1. Pension fund holds 10M shares of AAPL
2. PB borrows shares from pension (pays a small fee)
3. PB lends shares to hedge fund (charges a larger fee — the spread is PB profit)
4. Hedge fund sells the borrowed shares short
5. Hedge fund posts cash collateral to the PB (typically 102-105% of market value)
6. PB invests the cash collateral and keeps the return (or shares with lender)

Borrow fee components:

$$\text{All-in borrow cost} = \text{Borrow fee} + \text{Margin cost} + \text{Dividend pass-through}$$

Borrow Type Fee Range Examples
Easy to borrow (ETB) 0.25–0.50% annualized AAPL, MSFT, large-cap liquid names
General collateral 0.50–2.00% Mid-cap, moderately traded
Hard to borrow (HTB) 2–10%+ Small-cap, heavily shorted, limited float
Very hard to borrow 10–100%+ Squeeze candidates (GME was 50%+ at peak)

Dividend pass-through: When you're short a stock and it pays a dividend, YOU owe the dividend to the lender. This can be significant for high-dividend stocks or around ex-dividend dates.

PART 7: HEDGING MECHANICS — HOW FUNDS MANAGE EXPOSURE

7.1 Dynamic Hedging

Most hedge funds don't set a hedge and forget it — they dynamically rebalance to maintain a target exposure.

Delta Hedging (Options Books)

You're long 100 call options on SPY, each with delta = 0.55.
Portfolio delta = 100 × 0.55 × 100 shares/contract = 5,500 shares equivalent.
To be delta-neutral: short 5,500 shares of SPY.

Next day: SPY moves up, delta increases to 0.62.
New portfolio delta = 6,200 shares.
You're only short 5,500 → you're now net long 700 shares.
Action: sell 700 more shares to rebalance to delta-neutral.

Gamma tells you how fast delta changes — high gamma = frequent rebalancing needed = higher transaction costs.

The hedging cost paradox: Perfect delta hedging in theory is free (Black-Scholes assumes continuous hedging). In practice, you can only hedge discretely, and each rebalance costs a bid-ask spread. The more volatile the market, the more you rebalance, the more it costs. This is gamma scalping cost — and it's real money.

Beta Hedging (Equity Portfolios)

Portfolio: $200M in stock picks, beta to S&P = 1.3
Market exposure: $200M × 1.3 = $260M equivalent S&P risk
Hedge: Short $260M of ES futures → portfolio beta ≈ 0 (market-neutral)

Cost: only futures margin (~5-12% of notional) + roll cost every quarter

Why not perfect: Beta changes over time. Your stock picks might become more or less correlated with the market. This requires ongoing monitoring — "is the hedge still working?"

Cross-Hedging & Basis Risk

When you can't hedge with the exact same instrument:

Scenario Hedge Instrument Basis Risk
Long crude oil cargo (physical) Short WTI futures WTI vs. physical grade spread can move 5-10%
Long EM bonds Short US Treasury futures Spread to Treasuries can blow out in crisis
Long single stock Short sector ETF Idiosyncratic moves aren't hedged
Long GBP interest rate swap Short gilt futures Swap vs. gilt spread (swap spread risk)

Basis risk = the risk that the hedge doesn't move perfectly with the position. It's ALWAYS there when cross-hedging. Risk managers must quantify it and stress-test it.

7.2 Common Hedge Fund Hedging Strategies

Strategy How It Works Risk It Addresses
Pair trade hedge Long undervalued stock A, short overvalued stock B in the same sector Market risk (sector beta cancels, left with alpha)
Tail hedge (OTM puts) Buy far OTM puts on SPX as insurance Crash protection. Costs ~0.5-2% of NAV annually.
Collar Long portfolio + buy OTM puts + sell OTM calls Floorprotection funded by capping upside
Correlation hedge Trade options on correlation (dispersion trade) Hedges against correlation spike in a crisis
VIX calls Buy VIX calls (vol goes up when market crashes) Convex tail hedge — cheaper when VIX is low
FX hedge Fund denominated in USD, positions in EUR → forward/swap to hedge FX Currency risk on non-USD holdings
Duration hedge Short Treasury futures or receive fixed in IRS to reduce duration Interest rate risk on bond portfolios

Tail Hedging — The Risk Manager's Insurance Policy

$$\text{Cost of tail hedge} \approx 0.5-2\% \text{ of NAV annually}$$

The dilemma: Tail hedges bleed money every year they're not needed (theta decay on puts). But when they pay off, they pay off 5-20x. The risk manager's job is to determine:
1. How much to spend on tail protection
2. What strike/tenor to use (1-month ATM puts vs. 3-month 10% OTM)
3. When to monetize (sell the puts when they're in profit vs. hold through the event)

What to say if asked: "Tail hedging is like fire insurance. You hope you never need it, and it costs you every year. But when the building burns, you're either the fund that survives or the one on the front page. The right level depends on the fund's drawdown tolerance, LP terms, and how much the IC is willing to pay in drag. I'd frame it as: how much annual P&L are we willing to sacrifice for what level of crash protection?"

PART 8: OPTIONS STRATEGIES — DEEPER MECHANICS

Asset Classes And Portfolios covers the strategy table. Here's the deeper risk-management view.

8.1 P&L Profiles & Greeks by Strategy

Bull call spread Buy ATM call + sell OTM call Strike diff - premium Premium paid +Delta, limited. Gamma concentrated near strikes.
Bear put spread Buy ATM put + sell OTM put Strike diff - premium Premium paid -Delta, limited
Straddle Buy ATM call + ATM put Unlimited Total premium Delta ≈ 0, long gamma, long vega

8.2 Volatility Trading — The Risk Manager's Perspective

Discretionary funds trade volatility as a standalone strategy:

Long volatility (buying options/variance swaps):
- Profits when realized vol > implied vol
- Positive gamma (benefits from large moves in either direction)
- Positive vega (benefits from rising implied vol)
- Costs theta (options decay daily)
- Risk: vol stays low, premium drains away

Short volatility (selling options/variance swaps):
- Profits when realized vol < implied vol
- Negative gamma (gets hurt by large moves)
- Negative vega (gets hurt by rising implied vol)
- Earns theta (premium income daily)
- Risk: tail event causes catastrophic loss. This is what blew up XIV in Feb 2018 (short VIX ETN lost 96% in one day).

Variance swap — pure bet on realized vs. implied vol:
$$\text{P\&L} = \text{Notional} \times (\sigma^2_{\text{realized}} - \sigma^2_{\text{strike}})$$

Dispersion trade — sell index vol, buy single-stock vol:
- Profits when correlation is lower than implied (stocks move independently)
- Loses when correlation spikes (all stocks fall together — crisis)
- Classic "picking up pennies in front of a steamroller" if not sized properly

What to say if asked: "As a risk manager, my biggest concern with vol strategies is the asymmetry — short vol earns steady small premiums but has unlimited downside. The risk management framework needs to decompose every position into its vega, gamma, and theta contributions and stress-test under scenarios where implied vol doubles overnight and correlation goes to 1. That's what happened in March 2020 — VIX went from 15 to 82 in three weeks."

PART 9: PROFIT MECHANICS — HOW A HEDGE FUND ACTUALLY MAKES MONEY

9.1 Sources of Return

Source Description Example
Alpha Returns from skill — stock selection, timing, insight PM buys company before positive earnings surprise. Factor model can't explain the return.
Beta Returns from market exposure Fund is long equities → makes money because the market went up. Not skill.
Carry Income from holding a position Bonds pay coupons. Short vol earns theta. Dividend stocks pay income.
Roll yield Profit from rolling futures in contango/backwardation Oil in backwardation → rolling from expensive front month to cheap back month = profit
Convergence Mispricing narrows over time Basis trade: cash bond vs. futures price converges to zero at delivery
Liquidity premium Compensation for holding illiquid assets Distressed debt bought at $0.30 on the dollar → recovers to $0.70
Complexity premium Compensation for operating in markets that are difficult to analyze Structured credit, exotic derivatives — fewer participants, wider spreads

Alpha Decomposition — Is the PM Actually Good?

$$r_{\text{portfolio}} = \alpha + \beta_{\text{market}} r_{\text{market}} + \beta_{\text{sector}} r_{\text{sector}} + \beta_{\text{rates}} r_{\text{rates}} + \epsilon$$

If alpha is zero or negative, the PM isn't adding value — the return is all from factor exposure (beta) that could be replicated cheaply with ETFs.

Key metrics:

Metric Formula What It Tells You
Sharpe Ratio $(\bar{r} - r_f) / \sigma$ Risk-adjusted return. Above 1.0 = good. Above 2.0 = excellent.
Sortino Ratio $(\bar{r} - r_f) / \sigma_{\text{downside}}$ Like Sharpe but only penalizes downside volatility
Information Ratio $\alpha / \sigma_{\alpha}$ Alpha per unit of tracking error. Measures consistency. Above 0.5 = strong.
Max Drawdown Peak-to-trough decline Worst loss from peak. Tells you the pain tolerance needed.
Calmar Ratio Annualized return / Max drawdown Return per unit of worst-case pain. Above 1.0 = good.
Hit Rate % of winning trades Doesn't matter alone — a 30% hit rate with 5:1 win/loss ratio is excellent
Win/Loss Ratio Average win / Average loss Combined with hit rate, tells you expected value per trade

9.2 Common Discretionary Strategies

Strategy How It Works Risk Profile
Global Macro Take views on macro themes using rates, FX, commodities, equities Directional. Big moves = big profits/losses. Soros, Bridgewater.
Long/Short Equity Long undervalued stocks, short overvalued. Market-neutral or long-biased. Alpha depends on stock selection skill. Net exposure drives market sensitivity.
Event-Driven Trade around corporate events: M&A, earnings, restructuring, spin-offs Merger arb spread risk, deal breaks, idiosyncratic.
Credit / Distressed Buy cheap debt of troubled companies, profit from recovery or restructuring Illiquidity risk, binary outcomes (recovery vs. zero), legal complexity.
Relative Value Exploit mispricings between related instruments (yield curve, basis, swap spreads) Carry + convergence. Leverage amplifies. Can blow up if mispricing widens before converging (see: LTCM).
Vol Arbitrage Trade implied vs. realized volatility Gamma exposure, tail risk, correlation risk.
Multi-Strategy Multiple PMs running different strategies under one fund. Squarepoint is here. Diversification across strategies. Risk: correlation spike across pods in crisis.

PART 10: DRAWDOWN MECHANICS & RISK BUDGETING

10.1 Drawdown — Definition and Math

$$\text{Drawdown}_t = \frac{\text{NAV}_t - \text{HWM}_t}{\text{HWM}_t}$$

Where $\text{HWM}_t = \max(\text{NAV}_0, \text{NAV}_1, \ldots, \text{NAV}_t)$

Month 1:  NAV = $1.00B    HWM = $1.00B    DD = 0%
Month 2:  NAV = $1.08B    HWM = $1.08B    DD = 0%
Month 3:  NAV = $0.95B    HWM = $1.08B    DD = -12.0%    ← in drawdown
Month 4:  NAV = $0.90B    HWM = $1.08B    DD = -16.7%    ← max drawdown deepens
Month 5:  NAV = $1.00B    HWM = $1.08B    DD = -7.4%     ← recovering but still below HWM
Month 6:  NAV = $1.10B    HWM = $1.10B    DD = 0%         ← recovered, new HWM

10.2 Drawdown-Triggered Actions

Most funds have predefined escalation protocols:

Drawdown Level Action Who Decides
-5% Risk committee review. PM explains thesis. Increased monitoring frequency. Risk manager + CRO
-10% Mandatory position reduction (e.g., cut gross by 25%). Halt new position initiation. CRO + IC
-15% Hard stop. All positions reduced to 50% of normal size. Review of continued capital allocation. IC + CEO/CIO
-20%+ Possible capital reallocation to other PMs. PM may lose their allocation entirely. IC

At multi-PM platforms (like Squarepoint): PMs typically have individual drawdown limits of 5-10%. If breached, they get cut immediately. The platform can reallocate that capital to a PM who's performing. This is the "pod model" — capital flows to the PMs with edge and away from those who are losing.

10.3 Risk Budgeting

The IC allocates risk budgets across PMs and strategies. Think of it as a total risk pie that gets divided.

Total fund VaR budget: $50M (1-day 99% VaR, ~1.5% of NAV)

Allocation:
PM A (Macro):           $15M VaR (30% of budget)
PM B (Equity L/S):      $12M VaR (24%)
PM C (Credit):          $8M VaR  (16%)
PM D (Vol Trading):     $10M VaR (20%)
Diversification benefit: -$5M    (-10%)  ← because PMs aren't perfectly correlated
                       ________
Residual / buffer:      $10M     (20%)  ← risk budget headroom for opportunities

The risk manager's role:
1. Monitor each PM's actual risk vs. budget in real time
2. Escalate when a PM approaches or breaches their limit
3. Estimate the diversification benefit (if it shrinks — PMs are getting more correlated — that's a warning)
4. Recommend reallocation when PMs consistently under- or over-utilize their budget
5. Stress-test the TOTAL portfolio for scenarios where diversification breaks down (2008, March 2020)

PART 11: REGULATORY FRAMEWORK — WHAT YOU NEED TO KNOW

11.1 Key Regulations

Regulation Jurisdiction What It Does Impact on Risk Manager
Dodd-Frank (Title VII) US Mandates clearing of standardized OTC derivatives, margin requirements for uncleared swaps, trade reporting to SDRs Must track which counterparties require cleared vs. bilateral, monitor UMR compliance
EMIR EU European equivalent of Dodd-Frank. Clearing, margin, trade reporting. Same impact for EU-facing trades. EMIR Refit (2024) simplified reporting.
Volcker Rule US Prohibits banks from proprietary trading. Why Squarepoint exists — the nQuant team left Barclays because of this. Doesn't apply directly to hedge funds, but shapes counterparty landscape (banks became less aggressive).
MiFID II / MiFIR EU Transparency, best execution, position reporting, investor protection Position limits on commodity derivatives. Pre/post-trade transparency for some instruments.
AIFMD EU Regulates Alternative Investment Fund Managers. Capital requirements, risk management, depositary rules. Risk management function must be independent. Regular risk reporting to regulators.
Form PF US (SEC) Quarterly filing for large private fund advisers. Detailed reporting of strategies, exposures, leverage, counterparties, liquidity. Risk team often prepares the data. Must report: gross/net exposure, VaR, largest positions, counterparty concentrations.
13F Filing US (SEC) Quarterly disclosure of US equity holdings for managers with >$100M in US equities Public filing. Competitors can see your positions (with a ~45-day delay).
Form SHO / Reg SHO US (SEC) Short selling rules: locate requirement (must have reasonable belief shares can be borrowed before shorting) Risk must verify locates. "Naked shorting" (shorting without a locate) is illegal.
UMR (Uncleared Margin Rules) Global (BCBS/IOSCO) Initial margin requirements for non-cleared OTC derivatives. Phased in 2016-2022 by notional threshold. Major operational burden. Must segregate IM, calculate ISDA SIMM, post margin to custodian.

11.2 What to Say If Asked About Regulation

"The regulatory landscape shapes every aspect of fund operations. Dodd-Frank and EMIR pushed most standardized swaps to central clearing, which reduced counterparty risk but increased margin requirements — that's a direct cost to the fund. UMR means even bilateral OTC trades now require initial margin, which locks up capital. The risk manager needs to understand these constraints because they directly affect how much leverage the fund can take and what the financing costs are. Form PF and 13F mean our positions aren't fully private — so position sizing and disclosure timing matter from a market impact perspective as well."

PART 12: OPERATIONAL RISK & CONDUCT RISK

The JD explicitly mentions these. Most candidates ignore them.

12.1 Operational Risk

Risk Type Example Mitigation
Trade error Wrong size, wrong direction, wrong instrument Pre-trade checks, four-eyes principle, order management system (OMS) controls
Settlement failure Trade doesn't settle on time (counterparty doesn't deliver) T+1 monitoring, reconciliation, fails tracking
Technology failure System outage during market hours Redundancy, disaster recovery, failover testing
Model risk Pricing model gives wrong value → leads to bad trading decisions Independent model validation, challenger models, backtesting
Data quality Bad market data feeds, stale prices, incorrect corporate actions Data reconciliation, multiple data sources, anomaly detection
Key person risk Star PM leaves → fund loses capital and LP confidence Key person clauses, succession planning, diversified alpha sources
Vendor risk Critical vendor (data, clearing, technology) goes down Multi-vendor strategy, contractual SLAs, monitoring
Cyber risk Data breach, ransomware, phishing targeting fund employees Encryption, access controls, monitoring, employee training, incident response plan

12.2 Conduct Risk

Risk Type Example Why It's Serious
Insider trading Trading on material non-public information (MNPI) Criminal offense. Jail time. Fund reputation destroyed.
Market manipulation Spoofing, layering, painting the tape Dodd-Frank made these explicitly illegal. CFTC/SEC enforce aggressively.
Front-running Trading ahead of a large client order you know is coming Violates fiduciary duty. SEC monitors trade timestamps.
Unauthorized trading PM exceeds mandate or hides positions Risk of rogue trader scenario (Barings/Leeson, Societe Generale/Kerviel). Controls: real-time limit monitoring, independent trade confirmation.
Conflicts of interest PM has personal positions that conflict with fund positions Mandatory personal account disclosure, pre-clearance for personal trades
LP communication Misleading LPs about strategy, risk, or performance SEC/FCA enforcement. Legal liability.

What to say if asked: "Operational and conduct risk are as important as market risk — arguably more dangerous because they can shutdown a fund entirely. A single insider trading charge or a rogue trader event can destroy a fund overnight regardless of performance. The risk manager's role includes monitoring for outlier trading patterns, ensuring limit adherence isn't just reported but enforced, maintaining independence in escalation (the ability to go directly to the CRO or IC without being blocked by the PM), and fostering a culture where people raise concerns early. It's about systems, controls, AND culture."
