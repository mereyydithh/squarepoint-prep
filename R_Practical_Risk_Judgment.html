R — PRACTICAL RISK JUDGMENT

The difference between knowing risk and DOING risk

Depth: 🔴 DEEP

WHY THIS MATTERS

A junior RM who knows every formula but can't navigate a live trading floor is useless. A junior RM who can read a blotter in 5 minutes, sense when a PM is rationalizing a losing position, flag the one cross-asset risk that matters today, and communicate it in 30 seconds to a busy Head of Risk — that person is invaluable from week one.

This domain is the JUDGMENT layer. Technical knowledge gets you in the room. Judgment keeps you there.

At Squarepoint, you'll sit between discretionary PMs who think in conviction and narrative, and a systematic infrastructure that thinks in quantitative constraints. Your job is to bridge both — translate quant frameworks into actionable PM guidance, and translate PM intuition into risk-budgeted positions. Every section below is designed for immediate practical application.

PART 1: READING A RISK REPORT — THE 5-MINUTE SCAN

The Position Blotter

When you sit down at 7:00 AM, you'll see something like this on your screen:

# Name Dir Notional ($M) % NAV Sector Delta Gamma Vega DV01 ($K) P&L Today ($K) P&L MTD ($K) Days Held
1 XOM Long 80 8.0% Energy 1.00 — — — +950 +2,100 34
2 AAPL Long 45 4.5% Tech 0.85 0.02 — — -320 -850 22
3 SPX Jun 5200P Short 30 3.0% Index +0.35 -0.04 -180 — -410 -600 15
4 TLT Long 25 2.5% Rates 1.00 — — 38 -150 -400 40
5 BUND Jun 130C Long 15 1.5% EU Rates 0.45 0.01 65 12 +30 -120 18
6 BRL/USD Short 20 2.0% EM FX — — — — +80 -200 28
7 CVX Long 35 3.5% Energy 1.00 — — — +420 +900 34
8 NVDA Jun 900C Long 12 1.2% Tech 0.55 0.03 95 — -180 -500 10

Portfolio summary:
- Gross: $262M | Net long: $182M (net/gross = 69% long-biased)
- NAV: $1,000M
- VaR (95%, 1d): $8.2M (limit: $10M)
- Margin utilization: 72%
- MTD P&L: +$330K

The Systematic Scan — 8 Checks in 5 Minutes

1. CONCENTRATION (30 seconds)
- XOM at 8.0% NAV → large single-name. Combined with CVX at 3.5% → 11.5% in energy. Any energy-specific shock (oil reversal, earnings miss) causes 10%+ portfolio impact.
- Rule of thumb: >5% NAV in one name = flag. >15% in one sector = escalate.

2. DIRECTION (30 seconds)
- Net long $182M on $1B NAV → 18.2% net equity exposure
- But the short puts ADD to effective long when market falls. At SPX -5%, those puts' delta increases → effective long exposure could reach 25%+
- Question: is this intentional directional allocation or drift?

3. GREEKS — ACCELERATION RISK (45 seconds)
- Short gamma via SPX puts (-0.04) = PM gets LONGER as market falls. This is the toxic convexity profile.
- Short vega (-180) via puts + long vega (+95 + 65) via calls = net short vega -20. Manageable, but if vol spikes 5 points, that's -$100K from vega alone.
- Key calculation: if SPX drops 3% in a day:

$$\text{New put delta} \approx 0.35 + 0.04 \times \frac{0.03 \times 5200}{1} \approx 0.35 + 6.24 = \text{capped at } {\sim}0.65$$

Put delta roughly doubles → adds ~$9M to effective long exposure → total portfolio gets 50% longer at the worst possible time.

4. CORRELATION — ARE "DIFFERENT" POSITIONS THE SAME BET? (45 seconds)
- XOM + CVX = both long oil → obvious energy concentration
- But also: BRL/USD short → Brazil is an oil exporter → if oil rallies, BRL strengthens → the short BRL position LOSES money. So this is partially hedging the energy longs... or is it an independent macro view?
- TLT long = supposed equity hedge → but in a stagflation scenario, equities AND bonds sell off. The "hedge" becomes additional loss. Check: is the stock-bond correlation currently positive or negative?

5. P&L TRAJECTORY — WHAT'S BLEEDING? (30 seconds)
- AAPL: -$850K MTD, held 22 days. That's -1.9% on the position. Modest, but what's the thesis? Is this a conviction hold or a stale position?
- NVDA calls: -$500K MTD in only 10 days, with 0.55 delta on a long call = time decay is eating this. At what point does the PM cut?
- BRL: -$200K, held 28 days. No signs of thesis working. Time-horizon question.

6. HEDGE EFFECTIVENESS (30 seconds)
- TLT hedge: $25M notional with DV01 of $38K. For a $182M net equity portfolio, this provides partial duration offset but minimal equity hedge UNLESS stock-bond ρ < 0.
- Quick check: rolling 21d SPX/TLT correlation. If ρ > 0 (currently likely due to oil shock), TLT is NOT hedging — it's an independent risk source.

7. LIQUIDITY (30 seconds)
- All names are liquid large-cap or deep options markets. No liquidity flags today.
- But: in a stress scenario, bid-ask on deep OTM puts widens 3-5x. The short puts become harder to close at fair value.

8. STRESS — WORST SINGLE DAY (30 seconds)
- Scenario: SPX -5%, oil -10%, rates +20bp (stagflation shock)
- Quick estimate: Equity: -$182M × 5% × beta(~1.1) = -$10M. Energy: XOM+CVX -$115M × 5% extra = -$5.8M. Puts: gamma adds $9M long exposure, then loses on that too. TLT: -$38K × 20 = -$760K.
- Combined worst case: -$16M+ → BREACHES $10M VaR limit. Flag.

After the 5-minute scan, you produce:

PART 2: RISK PRIORITIZATION FRAMEWORK

The Urgency Matrix

Low probability (<10%) Medium probability (10-40%) High probability (>40%)
High impact (>$5M) MONITOR — stress tests, contingency plans. Don't dismiss because "unlikely." These are the cases that end careers. PREPARE — active hedge, position size review, IC discussion within 24h ACT NOW — reduce, hedge, or get explicit IC sign-off. No debate.
Medium impact ($1-5M) ACCEPT — standard operating risk WATCH — daily monitoring, pre-defined action triggers ADDRESS — within 1-2 days, ensure intentional
Low impact (<$1M) IGNORE — don't waste bandwidth ACCEPT — limits handle this ROUTINE — manage through standing limits

The Escalation Ladder

Severity Trigger Who to tell When How
GREEN Approaching soft limit (70-80%) PM only As it happens Verbal: "FYI, approaching [limit]"
YELLOW Breaching soft limit / unusual risk concentration PM + note for desk head Within 1 hour Message: "At 85% of [limit]. Recommendation: [action]"
ORANGE Approaching hard limit (90%+) / correlation breakdown PM + desk head + risk committee Same day Formal: documented escalation with alternatives
RED Hard limit breach / margin call / system failure Everyone up to CRO Immediately Call + written documentation. Timestamp everything.

Worked Example — Prioritizing 5 Simultaneous Risks

At 10:15 AM, you notice all of these:

# Issue Impact Probability Priority
1 XOM approaching 10% NAV (limit: 10%) Medium High (current drift) YELLOW — talk to PM in next 30 min
2 Overnight EM crisis → BRL gap open -4% Small ($400K) Already happened GREEN — inform PM, it's small
3 SPX -2% in first 45 min, gamma adding exposure High ($3M+ at risk) Real-time ORANGE — prepare recommendation now
4 AAPL earnings tonight — PM hasn't mentioned it Medium Known catalyst YELLOW — ask PM about overnight risk plan

You handle #5 first (you can't manage risk you can't measure), then #3 (active P&L risk), then #1 and #4 (medium-priority conversations), and #2 last (small, already happened).

Concrete Good-vs-Bad Examples

Situation: Oil up 8%

Bad RM Good RM
"Oil is up 8%. Here's the P&L." "Oil up 8%. Your net P&L is +$900K, mostly XOM/CVX. But here are two second-order risks you should know about:"
(Stops there) "1. Your short SPX puts are now deeper short gamma — if the market sells off on stagflation fear, your puts add $9M to your long exposure at exactly the wrong moment."
"2. TIPS breakevens widened 8bp, which means the market is repricing rate cut expectations. If fed funds futures reprice to 'no cuts this year,' your TLT and BUND positions lose another $500K."
"Recommendation: roll the SPX puts down 100 strikes to flatten gamma. Cost: $200K. Or add a 5100/5000 put spread as a crash hedge — cost: $350K."

Situation: PM wants to double a losing position

Bad RM Good RM
"That will breach your concentration limit." "I want to understand the thesis first. When you entered AAPL at $185, the thesis was iPhone cycle + AI services. It's now at $172 — what's changed in the fundamental story?"
(End of conversation) [If PM says nothing has changed]: "OK, I can support adding if the thesis is intact. But let me show you the portfolio impact: VaR goes from $8.2M to $9.7M. You'll be at 97% of limit. And if AAPL reports earnings and misses, combined with your existing positions, max loss is $4.2M on that name alone — that's 42% of your VaR budget on one event. Alternative: add $15M instead of $45M, which captures 80% of the thesis with half the concentration risk."
[If PM says "it has to come back"]: "That's not a thesis — that's hope. I need to flag this to the desk head. I'm not blocking you, but I need an explicit sign-off with that rationale documented."

Situation: Vol running 30%+ above target

Bad RM Good RM
"The portfolio's realized vol is above target." "Realized vol is running 18% vs your 12% target, for 8 straight days. I decomposed it:"
"• 60% from market vol increase (VIX moved from 16 to 22 — that's beta, not your fault)"
"• 40% from XOM/CVX concentration, which is running 3x the vol of the rest of the book right now"
"If you want to bring vol back to target: trimming XOM by 25% does it. If you want to keep the position because the thesis is strong, we need IC sign-off on elevated vol. Which path do you prefer?"

The Core Principle

Your job is not to minimize risk. Your job is to maximize INFORMED risk-taking.

Every dollar of risk should be:
1. Intentional — the PM explicitly chose to take this risk (not drift, not accident)
2. Sized — within the risk budget, with buffer for stress
3. Understood — at the portfolio level, including second-order effects and cross-asset transmission
4. Compensated — the expected return justifies the risk (Sharpe > 0 on marginal capital)
5. Hedgeable — there's a way out if the thesis is wrong (or the PM consciously doesn't hedge and accepts the tail)

PART 5: COMMUNICATION — THE ART OF BEING HEARD

How to Talk to Traders / PMs

Rule 1: Lead with P&L impact, not Greek letters.
- ❌ "Your gamma is -0.04 on the SPX puts"
- ✅ "If SPX drops 2%, your short gamma adds $9M to your long exposure and your P&L swing is -$3.5M"

Rule 2: Speak their language.
- ❌ "The portfolio's standard deviation is elevated"
- ✅ "Your book moves $2.3M per 1% SPX move — that's up from $1.5M last week"

Rule 3: Time it right.
- 🟢 Pre-market and post-close: deep conversations, thesis reviews, structural adjustments
- 🟡 During market hours: alerts, quick answers, pre-trade checks
- 🔴 During active trading/execution: only urgent alerts (limit breach, margin call, system failure)

Rule 4: Be definitive, not wishy-washy.
- ❌ "Maybe we should think about potentially reducing some of the energy exposure..."
- ✅ "I'd recommend reducing XOM by $15M. Here's why: [30 seconds]. Your call."

Rule 5: Pick your battles. Seriously.
- If you flag 10 things a day, you get tuned out by day 3
- Flag the 1-2 things that TRULY matter. Earn credibility through precision, not volume.
- The PM should think: "When this person speaks up, I listen, because they only speak up when it matters."

The Pushback Framework (without being annoying)

Step 1: Validate the thesis first
"I understand the trade is [X] because you believe [Y]. I think the thesis has merit."

Step 2: Add the risk dimension
"My concern isn't the thesis — it's the sizing relative to what else is in the book."

Step 3: Bring data, not opinions
"Under the current scenario set, this position alone generates a -$15M loss in a March 2020 replay. Combined with the rest of the book, total drawdown is -$22M, which breaches your 5% NAV limit."

Step 4: Offer alternatives
"Instead of $45M notional, what about $25M plus a call spread overlay? You get 80% of the upside thesis with 55% of the risk. VaR stays within limit."

Step 5: Escalate with documentation if needed
"If you want to proceed at $45M, I respect your judgment, but I need to flag this to the desk head with the scenario numbers. Not blocking — just ensuring visibility."

Talking to Senior Risk (CRO / Head of Risk / Constantine)

Format: Situation → Quantification → Recommendation → Trade-offs. 90 seconds max.

Template:

"PM [name] is at [X]% drawdown against [Y]% limit. The concentration is in [sector/factor], currently [Z]% of NAV. The concern is [specific scenario]: if [event], portfolio loss is estimated at $[number], which would [breach/approach limit]. I recommend [action A]. PM prefers [action B] because [their rationale]. I'm requesting [visibility/IC review/limit exception]."

Example:

"PM Chen is at 4.2% drawdown against a 5% limit. Concentrated in energy — XOM + CVX = 11.5% NAV — and short SPX gamma. If oil reverses 10% and SPX drops 5% simultaneously, estimated loss is $16M, which breaches the VaR limit and approaches the drawdown limit. I recommend trimming energy by 25% and rolling the puts. PM disagrees — believes oil hasn't peaked. Requesting IC review before the close."

What they want to hear:
1. Numbers — not "it's getting risky" but "$16M loss scenario"
2. Context — how does this compare to limits, historical, other PMs
3. Your judgment — are you concerned or just reporting? If concerned, say so explicitly
4. What you need — a decision, visibility, or approval. Be specific.

PART 6: PRE-TRADE RISK ANALYSIS

When a PM Wants to Put on a New Trade

Every new trade request should trigger a rapid risk check before execution:

The Pre-Trade Checklist (2 minutes):

Check Calculation Output
VaR impact Marginal VaR: ΔVaR from adding this position "VaR goes from $8.2M → $9.7M. You're at 97% of limit."
Concentration New position size as % NAV, combined with same-sector exposure "This takes energy to 14.5% NAV. Above our 12% soft limit."
Margin impact Additional margin required + new margin utilization "Margin utilization goes from 72% → 81%. Leaves thin buffer."
Correlation How does this position correlate with existing book? "ρ = 0.7 with rest of energy book. Not diversifying."
Factor overlap Does this add new factor exposure or amplify existing? "This adds another $800K of oil beta. You already have $2.1M."
Liquidity Can we exit this position in 1 day at <1% impact? "ADV is $50M. $35M position = 70% ADV. Exit takes 2-3 days."
Worst case If this position goes wrong, what's the max loss? "If it gaps -15%, that's -$5.3M. Combined with existing book: -$21M."

The delivery:
"Adding $35M long in [name]. Here's the impact: VaR +$1.5M to $9.7M (97% of limit). Energy concentration goes to 14.5% (above 12% soft limit). Margin to 81%. The position is correlated 0.7 with your existing energy book — so you're amplifying, not diversifying. If you still want to proceed, I'd recommend either (a) trimming CVX by $15M to make room, or (b) getting IC sign-off on the limit exception. Which do you prefer?"

PM Says: "What if I restructure it as options?"

You should be able to immediately respond:

Structure Risk profile vs equity Advantage Disadvantage
Long stock ($35M) Full delta, no optionality Full upside, no time decay Full downside, max margin
Long call (ATM, 3M) Delta ~0.5, long gamma, long vega Defined loss, less margin Premium cost ($1.5M), time decay
Call spread (ATM/+15%) Delta ~0.35, defined upside Cheaper than call, defined risk Capped upside at 15%
Risk reversal (sell put, buy call) Delta ~0.7, short downside gamma Zero premium Naked downside risk, margin-intensive
Collar on existing Caps upside and downside No premium, defined range No participation above cap

Your job: Help the PM find the structure that best expresses their thesis within the risk budget. "If your thesis is 'XOM goes up 10% in 2 months with limited downside,' a call spread captures that at 40% of the marginal risk. Want me to price it?"

Limit Interaction — The Non-Obvious Part

Limits interact. A portfolio can be within every individual limit and still be dangerously risky:

Example:
- VaR: 92% of limit (fine individually)
- Energy concentration: 14% (fine individually, under 15% limit)
- Short gamma: moderate (within Greeks limit)
- Margin: 78% (fine individually)

BUT: In a stagflation scenario (oil -10%, SPX -5%, rates +20bp):
- Energy concentration → -$6M
- Short gamma adds effective long exposure → -$4M more
- Margin call triggered → forced to sell at worst prices → -$2M additional slippage
- Total: -$12M on a $10M VaR limit → breach

Lesson: The RM's job is to check limits INDIVIDUALLY and IN COMBINATION. The scenario analysis catches what individual limits miss.

Monthly Limit Calibration

Limits should not be static. Review monthly:

Question If YES
Has market vol regime changed? VaR limit may need tightening (same $ limit means more market risk in high-vol)
Has the PM's strategy evolved? Greeks and factor limits may need adjusting
Are correlations structurally different? Concentration limits may need tightening (higher ρ = less true diversification)
Has the PM hit drawdown? Reduce capital allocation / risk limits proportionally
Has the PM demonstrated consistent alpha? May warrant limit increase with IC approval

PART 9: MARGIN AND LIQUIDITY MANAGEMENT

Margin Framework

$$\text{Margin utilization} = \frac{\text{Total initial margin required}}{\text{Available margin (equity − haircuts)}}$$

Utilization Status Action
< 60% Comfortable No action needed
60-75% Normal operating range Monitor daily
75-85% Elevated Flag to PM: "Running tight. One bad day = margin call."
85-95% Danger zone Active discussion: which position to trim for buffer?
> 95% Margin call imminent Emergency: identify most expendable position. Act within hours.

The Margin Call Protocol

When a margin call arrives:

  1. Verify the call — check the numbers. Margin systems have errors. Confirm with PB/clearing.
  2. Quantify the shortfall — how much do we need to post or liquidate?
  3. Triage positions — rank by: (a) conviction, (b) liquidity, (c) contribution to margin requirement
  4. Simulate liquidation options:
Option Capital freed Market impact Strategy damage
Sell AAPL ($45M, liquid) $12M margin Low (0.1% ADV) Moderate — removes a conviction position
Close SPX puts ($30M, liquid) $18M margin Low Low — reduces risk and margin simultaneously
Trim XOM by 30% ($24M) $8M margin Medium (1.5% ADV) High — reduces best-performing position
  1. Recommend — "I'd close the SPX puts first — frees $18M margin, reduces our biggest risk (short gamma), and has minimal market impact. That covers the margin call with buffer. If we need more, trim AAPL $15M."
  2. Execute — coordinate with PM and trading desk
  3. Document — timestamp everything. Post-mortem: why did we get here? How do we prevent recurrence?

Liquidity Risk Management

$$\text{Days to exit} = \frac{\text{Position size}}{\text{ADV} \times \text{participation rate}}$$

Participation rate Context Market impact estimate
5% Minimal impact, patient exit ~5-10bp
10% Normal, moderate impact ~15-25bp
20% Aggressive, visible ~40-75bp
25%+ Fire sale, price-sensitive ~100bp+

Portfolio liquidity profile example:

Position Notional ADV Days @ 10% Days @ 20% Classification
XOM $80M $2.5B 0.3 0.2 Highly liquid
AAPL $45M $10B 0.05 0.02 Ultra-liquid
Small-cap name $15M $25M 6.0 3.0 ILLIQUID — flag
SPX puts $30M Deep market 0.1 0.05 Liquid (but spread widens in stress)

Key insight: Liquidity is a fair-weather friend. A position that trades $25M/day normally might trade $5M/day in a crisis as market-makers pull back. Always stress-test liquidity:

$$\text{Stressed days to exit} = \text{Normal days} \times \frac{1}{\text{liquidity stress factor (0.3-0.5)}}$$

That small-cap position that's "6 days to exit" in normal conditions could be 15-20 days in a crisis. If the PM needs to get out during a sell-off, they're trapped.

PART 10: SCENARIO DESIGN FOR PRACTICAL RISK

Building Scenarios That Actually Help PMs

Bad scenarios are arbitrary: "SPX -20%." Good scenarios are macro-coherent stories that connect:

Element Must specify Why
Trigger What causes the scenario? Determines speed and cross-asset mechanics
Macro path Growth, inflation, policy response Ensures internal consistency
Cross-asset Rates, FX, credit, commodities Cascades are where real risk lives
Time horizon Acute (1 day), short (1 week), medium (1 month) Some exposures are only dangerous over time
Magnitude Calibrated to historical analogues Prevents both over- and under-estimation
Correlation assumption Which correlation regime? The same portfolio has dramatically different losses under different ρ assumptions

Presenting Scenarios to PMs

Do NOT present a 10-row table and say "here are the numbers."

Instead:

"I ran 10 scenarios. Three things stood out:

First, your biggest single risk isn't a market crash — it's an oil reversal. Scenario 5 (oil -25%) costs you $8.2M. That's worse than a 12% equity crash because your portfolio is so concentrated in energy.

Second, in the inflation shock scenario, your TLT hedge COSTS you $760K instead of helping, because stock-bond correlation is positive in that regime. Your effective hedge ratio is about 30% of what you think it is.

Third, the short gamma on SPX puts creates asymmetric exposure: you get longer as the market falls. In any scenario with SPX -5% or more, your effective portfolio delta is 50% higher than what the blotter currently shows.

My recommendation: [specific trade adjustments]."

PART 11: THE RISK MANAGEMENT TOOLBOX — SITUATIONAL PLAYBOOK

Playbook 1: Approaching Drawdown Limit

Situation: PM is at 4.2% drawdown against 5% limit.

Step 1 — Quantify remaining budget: $1B × (5% - 4.2%) = $8M remaining risk budget. At current VaR ($8.2M), one average bad day could breach the limit.

Step 2 — Identify P&L drivers: What positions are causing the drawdown? Where is P&L concentrated?

Step 3 — Present options:
| Option | Risk reduction | P&L impact | PM acceptance |
|---|---|---|---|
| Reduce all positions pro-rata by 20% | VaR: $8.2M → $6.6M | Reduces recovery potential equally | Low — blunt instrument |
| Cut losing positions only | VaR: variable | Crystallizes losses but stops bleeding | Medium — emotional resistance |
| Hedge with index puts | VaR: $8.2M → $6.0M | Costs $300K premium but preserves upside | High — retains thesis |
| Cut the position most correlated with primary loss driver | VaR: targeted reduction | Most efficient risk reduction per $ | High — surgical, smart |

Step 4 — Recommend: "I'd recommend reducing XOM by 25% (cuts VaR by $1.5M, low market impact) and buying a 2-week put spread on SPX ($300K cost, caps tail at -$5M). This preserves your oil thesis at reduced sizing while capping your drawdown exposure. Total cost: $300K in premium. Budget freed: $2.3M in VaR."

Playbook 2: Event Day (CPI, FOMC, NFP)

T-minus 1 day:
- Map PM exposures to potential outcomes (beat, meet, miss)
- Run scenarios for ±1σ and ±2σ surprises
- Identify: "If [X], which positions gain/lose the most?"
- Prepare recommendations for each outcome: "If hot CPI: [action]. If cold CPI: [no action needed]."

T-minus 30 minutes:
- Send PM the scenario summary: "In 30 minutes, CPI prints. Your exposure: [summary]. If hot, estimated impact: -$X. If cold: +$Y. I recommend [Z] if you want to de-risk ahead."

T-zero:
- Watch the number. Immediately compare to consensus.
- Within 2 minutes, estimate PM-specific P&L impact.
- If the print is in the tail (>2σ surprise), reach out immediately: "Core CPI +0.5%. This is a 2σ miss. Your estimated hit is -$4.7M across [positions]. Recommend: [action]."

T-plus 15 minutes:
- Check if the reaction is following the "expected" cross-asset pattern or something unusual
- If unusual (markets not reacting as expected to the data), that's information — the narrative may be shifting

Playbook 3: Overnight Geopolitical Shock

You wake up to: "Missiles reported near Strait of Hormuz. Oil futures +12%. Equity futures -3%."

  1. Assess direct exposure (5 min): Energy longs, EM FX, shipping/transport, defense. Quick P&L estimate.
  2. Assess second-order (15 min): Cross-asset cascade: oil → rates → equities → credit → FX. Run stagflation scenario.
  3. Check liquidity (5 min): Will markets open normally? Any positions in affected regions? Circuit breaker risk?
  4. Prepare 1-page brief (15 min):
  5. Direct P&L impact: +$Y (energy) / -$Z (equity, FX)
  6. Net impact: $[number]
  7. Scenarios: escalation path vs de-escalation path
  8. Recommendations: "Hold energy longs (thesis confirmed). Reduce SPX short gamma immediately (dangerous in vol spike). Consider adding USDJPY hedge for carry unwind risk."
  9. Be at desk before PM arrives. Hand them the page. "Here's where we stand."

Playbook 4: Liquidity Event

Situation: A position's bid-ask spread has widened 5x. Volume has dropped 80%.

  1. Mark check: Is the position marked correctly? In illiquid conditions, mid-market marks can be misleading. Check: last trade price, dealer quotes, comparable names.
  2. Size the problem: What % of NAV is this position? At current liquidity, how many days to exit?
  3. Impact estimate: If we sell into this market, what's the expected slippage?
  4. Alternatives: Can we hedge the exposure synthetically (correlated ETF, index, options) rather than selling the illiquid position directly?
  5. Communicate: "The bid-ask on [name] has widened from 5bp to 25bp. We can't exit our $15M position without significant impact (~100bp = $150K slippage). I recommend hedging with [ETF/index] at 10bp cost while we wait for liquidity to normalize."

PART 12: INTERVIEW ANSWERS

Q1: "Walk me through how you'd read a risk report for a discretionary PM's portfolio"

Answer: "I'd do a systematic 8-check scan, prioritized by what can hurt us most.

First, concentration — is any single name more than 5% of NAV or any sector more than 15%? I want to know what one surprise can do.

Second, direction — what's the net exposure? A book that's 70% net long is making a directional bet whether the PM intends it or not. I'd check if that's deliberate.

Third, Greeks — specifically short gamma and short vega. Short gamma is the most dangerous because it makes the portfolio longer when the market falls. I'd calculate: if the market drops 3%, how much does our effective exposure increase? If it doubles, that's a flag.

Fourth, correlation — are positions that look different actually the same bet? I'd run a quick factor decomposition. If 60% of the book is explained by one factor, everything falls together.

Fifth, I'd check hedge effectiveness. If the PM has TLT as a hedge, but we're in a positive stock-bond correlation regime, that hedge isn't working — it's an independent source of risk. I'd flag that immediately.

Sixth, P&L trajectory — anything bleeding for more than 2 weeks without a catalyst gets a thesis review.

Seventh, liquidity — can we exit every position in less than 2 days at under 50bp impact?

Eighth, stress — what's the worst plausible single-day scenario? I'd run it through the current book and check: does it breach any limits?

The output of all this is a 1-page morning summary for the PM with the top 3 risks and my specific recommendation for each."

Q2: "A PM wants to add $40M to a losing energy position. What do you do?"

Answer: "First, I'd want to understand the thesis — not challenge it, but genuinely understand it. What was the entry thesis, and what has changed? If the stock is down 15% and the PM says 'nothing has changed, the thesis is intact, and the market is mispricing this,' that's one conversation. If the PM says 'it has to come back,' that's a very different conversation — that's hope, not a thesis, and I'd flag it.

Assuming the thesis is intact, I'd run the numbers. Adding $40M takes this name to [X]% of NAV — is that within concentration limits? It takes energy sector exposure to [Y]% — within sector limits? What's the VaR impact — how much risk budget does this consume? And critically, what's the portfolio impact if this position AND the rest of the energy book move against us? I'd present the correlated tail loss.

Then I'd offer alternatives. 'Instead of $40M in stock, what about $20M in stock plus a $20M-notional call spread? You get 80% of the upside conviction with 55% of the downside risk, and VaR stays within budget.' Or: 'If you want the full $40M, I'd recommend trimming CVX by $15M to make room — you're effectively concentrating the energy bet in your highest-conviction name.'

If the PM wants to proceed at full size and it breaches a limit, I'd say: 'I respect your conviction, but this triggers a limit discussion. I need to flag it to the desk head with the scenario numbers. Not blocking, but ensuring visibility.'"

Q3: "How do you distinguish between a genuine risk issue and normal noise?"

Answer: "Three filters.

First, magnitude relative to limits. If a daily P&L move is 1% of VaR, that's noise. If it's 30% of daily VaR, something structural may have shifted on the day. I track a trailing ratio of realized P&L to predicted P&L from the previous night's VaR — if realized consistently exceeds predicted, the risk model is under-estimating something.

Second, persistence. One day of elevated vol or an unusual correlation is noise. Five consecutive days is a regime shift. I track rolling 5-day metrics against 60-day norms. If the 5-day realized vol is more than 1.5x the 60-day average, I flag it. Same for correlations — if a hedge that was -0.3 correlated is now +0.1, that's not noise, that's a structural shift.

Third, source identification. I decompose the unexpected P&L. Did it come from delta (market moved)? Gamma (market moved a LOT)? Vega (vol changed)? Correlation (assets moved together unexpectedly)? Or is it unexplained residual? If the residual is growing, there's something the model isn't capturing, and THAT is the genuine risk issue — what you can't measure is more dangerous than what you can.

When I find a genuine issue, I apply the urgency matrix: high impact × high probability = act now. High impact × low probability = monitor with scenarios. Most 'noise' is low impact regardless of probability — I let those go. The skill is not flagging everything. It's flagging the right thing at the right time."
