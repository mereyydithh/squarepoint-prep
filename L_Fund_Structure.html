L — FUND STRUCTURE & MECHANICS

LP/GP, fees, NAV, redemptions, prime brokerage, margin, leverage, repo, CCPs, multi-strategy pod economics, and the structural risks that live inside the plumbing

Depth: 🟡 WELL

WHY FUND STRUCTURE IS A RISK DISCIPLINE

Most people think "fund structure" is legal paperwork — LPs, GPs, offering memoranda. For a risk manager, fund structure IS risk. Every structural design choice creates a risk channel:

  • Fee structure creates behavioral incentives (HWM pressure → risk-seeking after drawdowns)
  • Liquidity terms create liquidity mismatch risk (quarterly redemptions vs daily-traded assets, or worse — illiquid assets)
  • Leverage amplifies market moves and creates fragility
  • Prime brokerage introduces counterparty risk AND pro-cyclical margin risk
  • Multi-strategy pod structure creates internal allocation and correlation risks

The RM who understands the plumbing catches risks that pure market-risk analysis misses. The margin call that kills a fund isn't a market risk problem — it's a STRUCTURAL risk problem.

PART 1: FUND LEGAL STRUCTURE

The LP/GP Model

Almost every hedge fund uses a limited partnership structure:

Role Who Rights Liability Economics
General Partner (GP) Fund management company ALL investment decisions, fund governance, operational control Unlimited liability Management fee + performance fee
Limited Partners (LPs) Pensions, endowments, family offices, SWFs, insurance cos, HNW individuals Capital providers; receive returns; NO say in investment decisions Limited to investment Net returns after fees

Why limited partnership? Two reasons:
1. Tax: Partnerships are pass-through entities — no entity-level taxation. Profits and losses flow directly to the partners' tax returns.
2. Liability: LPs risk only what they invest. The GP bears unlimited liability — which is why many GPs are themselves structured as LLCs or LLPs.

The Investment Manager vs the GP:
- The GP is often a shell entity. It doesn't employ anyone.
- The Investment Manager (e.g., Squarepoint Capital LLP) is a separate entity that has the investment team, infrastructure, and licenses. The GP delegates investment authority to the IM via an Investment Management Agreement (IMA).
- This separation matters for RM: risk limits, mandate constraints, and compliance obligations are defined in the IMA.

Fund Vehicles

Vehicle Jurisdiction Tax treatment Typical LP base Key features
Offshore fund Cayman, BVI, Ireland Tax-neutral at entity level Non-US institutions, tax-exempt US (endowments, pensions) Most flexible; Cayman is ~70% of HF industry
Onshore fund Delaware LP or LLC Pass-through (K-1 reporting) US taxable investors More regulatory burden; ERISA considerations for pensions
Master-feeder Offshore + onshore feed → single Cayman master Master is tax-neutral; feeders distribute based on investor type Both US and non-US investors Most large funds use this structure
Managed account Investor's own custody Per investor Large SWFs, sovereign funds, $500M+ allocators Full transparency, custom terms, LP controls liquidation
Fund of funds Varies Additional fee layer Smaller institutions accessing multiple HFs Diversification; but double layer of fees (fund-of-fund fee + underlying fund fee)
UCITS Ireland, Luxembourg EU-regulated European retail and institutional Strict leverage, liquidity, concentration limits; daily NAV; limited to eligible assets

Master-Feeder Structure — How It Works

  [Offshore Feeder]     [Onshore Feeder]     [Managed Account]
  (Cayman LP)           (Delaware LP)         (Separate custody)
        \                     |                    /
         \                    |                   /
          --------→  [MASTER FUND]  ←-----------
                     (Cayman LP)
                          |
                  [Prime Broker(s)]
                          |
                      [MARKETS]
  • ALL trading happens in the Master Fund. Feeders are just holding companies that invest into the master.
  • Each feeder has its own NAV, fee schedule, and redemption terms — because different investor classes have different needs.
  • The master fund aggregates all capital and trades as a single pool → better execution, simpler risk management, consolidated margin.

RM implication: Risk is managed at the MASTER fund level. But liquidity stress must be analyzed at the FEEDER level — because a wave of redemptions from the offshore feeder doesn't affect onshore LPs' capital, but it DOES force trades in the master fund that affect everyone's NAV.

Squarepoint's Structure

Squarepoint Capital LLP is a London-headquartered global investment manager running both systematic and discretionary strategies. Key structural features:

  • LLP structure: Limited Liability Partnership under UK law. Partners have limited liability (unlike a traditional GP with unlimited liability).
  • Multi-strategy: Multiple trading teams (pods) under one umbrella, each with allocated capital and risk budgets.
  • The discretionary desk you'd join is one team within this larger structure — operating under centrally-set risk limits but with autonomy over investment decisions.
  • Global operations: Offices in London, New York, Singapore, Montreal — trading across time zones and asset classes.

PART 2: FEE STRUCTURES

The Classic "2 and 20"

Component Rate Basis Payment frequency
Management fee 2% per annum (charged as ~0.167% monthly or 0.5% quarterly) AUM at start of period Monthly or quarterly
Performance fee 20% of net new profits Net P&L above HWM Annually (crystallized at year-end)

Management fee covers: Salaries, rent, technology, data feeds, compliance, legal, operations — the cost of keeping the lights on. It's charged REGARDLESS of performance.

Performance fee is conditional: Only charged on net new profits above the high-water mark.

Modern Fee Compression

The "2 and 20" era is largely over for most strategies:

Fund type Typical 2025 fees Trend
Large multi-strat (Citadel, Millennium, Squarepoint) 0% mgmt / 20-30% perf + pass-through No management fee; high perf fee; costs passed to LPs
L/S equity single-manager 1.5% / 17.5% Compressed from 2/20
Quant/systematic 1-2% / 20% Less compression (capacity-constrained)
Macro 1.5-2% / 20% Relatively stable
Fund of funds 1% / 10% (on top of underlying fund fees) Heavy compression

Pass-Through Fee Model (Multi-Strat Standard)

The dominant model for multi-strategy funds like Citadel, Millennium, Balyasny, and likely Squarepoint:

How it works:
1. No management fee (or minimal, e.g., 0.5%)
2. Performance fee: 25-30% of net profits
3. All operating costs passed through to LPs: Technology ($200M+ at large firms), data ($50M+), salaries for non-investment staff, office space, compliance
4. The fund charges these as expenses deducted from NAV rather than as a management fee

Why this exists: Large multi-strats argue that pass-through aligns incentives better. The management fee is a FIXED cost — the firm gets paid even if performance is flat. Pass-through makes ALL costs variable and transparent. LPs pushed for this.

The catch: Total cost to LPs can be HIGHER than 2/20. A firm with $20B AUM passing through $1B of expenses = 5% effective management fee. Plus 25% performance. LPs pay more — but they get transparency.

High-Water Mark (HWM) — Worked Example

The HWM prevents the GP from earning performance fees on the SAME dollar of profit twice.

Year Start NAV End NAV P&L HWM New profit above HWM Perf fee (20%) NAV after fees
1 $100.00 $120.00 +$20.00 $120.00 $20.00 $4.00 $116.00
2 $116.00 $102.00 −$14.00 $120.00 $0 (below HWM) $0 $102.00
3 $102.00 $115.00 +$13.00 $120.00 $0 (still below HWM) $0 $115.00
4 $115.00 $130.00 +$15.00 $130.00 $10.00 (only $120→$130) $2.00 $128.00

Year 3 is the key insight: The fund gained +$13 (a 12.7% return!) but earned ZERO performance fee. The PM made a strong return but is still below the HWM set in Year 1. This is the "HWM treadmill."

RM implications of HWM:

Situation Behavioral risk RM response
PM is 15% below HWM Desperate to recover → may increase risk, concentrate, or swing for the fences Monitor position size, concentration, gross exposure for any ramp-up
PM just cleared HWM Temptation to "lock in" the year by reducing risk Less dangerous, but check for disposition effect (cutting winners to protect the fee)
PM is well above HWM in November May "coast" in December to protect performance fee crystallization Monitor for sudden degrossing or risk reduction at year-end

Crystallization & Equalization

Crystallization: The date when performance fees are actually CALCULATED and deducted from NAV. Usually annually (December 31 or fund anniversary). Some funds crystallize quarterly.

Equalization problem: LPs who invest at different times have different HWMs.

LP Entry date Entry NAV HWM
LP A Jan 2023 $100 $100
LP B Jul 2024 $115 $115

If fund goes from $115 → $130: LP A owes perf fees on all $15 of profit. LP B owes perf fees on the $15 above THEIR entry point. Different fees for the same period.

Solution: Equalization shares (or series accounting). The fund issues "equalization shares" that track each LP's individual HWM and ensure fair fee allocation. Operationally complex but mathematically necessary.

Hurdle Rate

Some funds must earn above a minimum return (the hurdle) before taking performance fees:

Hurdle type Definition Example
Hard hurdle Performance fee only on returns ABOVE the hurdle Fund earns 12%, hurdle is 5%. Perf fee on 7% only.
Soft hurdle If return exceeds hurdle → perf fee on ENTIRE return Fund earns 12%, hurdle is 5%. Perf fee on full 12%.
Catch-up After hurdle, GP gets 100% of next tranche until "caught up" to full 20% Common in PE; rare in HFs

Modern trend: More LPs demanding hurdles, especially risk-free rate hurdles. "If T-bills pay 5%, you shouldn't earn 20% on the first 5% of return — that's beta, not alpha."

PART 3: NET ASSET VALUE (NAV)

What NAV Is

$$\text{NAV} = \text{Total Assets} - \text{Total Liabilities}$$

$$\text{NAV per share} = \frac{\text{NAV}}{\text{Shares outstanding}}$$

NAV is the "share price" of the fund. LPs subscribe (buy in) and redeem (cash out) at NAV. Every P&L number, every return, every fee — ALL flow through NAV.

Daily NAV Calculation Process

Step What happens Who does it
1. Position download All positions extracted from OMS/PMS Operations / middle office
2. Mark to market Listed instruments: closing prices. OTC: marks from PBs, dealers, or models Fund administrator + pricing committee
3. Cash & collateral Add: cash balances, collateral posted, receivables, accrued interest Operations
4. Subtract liabilities Management fees accrued, expenses, financing costs, payables Operations
5. Side pocket adjustment Illiquid positions marked separately; may use independent valuation Valuation committee
6. NAV published Final NAV per share published to LPs and administrators Fund administrator

Valuation Hierarchy (ASC 820 / IFRS 13)

Level Input type Examples Reliability
Level 1 Quoted prices in active markets NYSE/NASDAQ stocks, listed futures, ETFs Highest — observable, verifiable
Level 2 Observable inputs other than Level 1 OTC derivatives priced from yield curves, comparable bonds, dealer quotes Moderate — requires judgment
Level 3 Unobservable inputs Private equity, distressed debt, bespoke structures, illiquid CDO tranches Lowest — model-dependent, subject to manipulation

RM concern: Level 3 assets are where "mark to model" becomes "mark to myth." If 20% of the fund's NAV is Level 3, the NAV itself is unreliable. A PM can show 10% returns when the underlying assets haven't actually been priced by a market — they've been priced by a MODEL that the PM influences.

Detection: Track Level 3 as a % of NAV over time. If it's growing → the fund is accumulating illiquid positions. Ask: "If we had to liquidate Level 3 assets in a stress scenario, what's the realistic haircut?"

Side Pockets

When a fund holds an asset that becomes illiquid (e.g., a corporate bond where the issuer enters restructuring), the fund moves it to a side pocket:

  • Side pocket has SEPARATE NAV from the main fund.
  • LPs cannot redeem side pocket assets until they're liquidated or valued.
  • New investors don't participate in the side pocket — they only invest in the main fund.
  • Prevents new LPs from "buying into" unknown illiquid positions.

Abuse risk: In 2008, some funds moved performing assets into side pockets to prevent redemptions. This is a governance failure. The RM / compliance function should ensure side pocket criteria are objective and pre-defined.

PART 4: LIQUIDITY TERMS — SUBSCRIPTIONS, REDEMPTIONS, GATES, LOCK-UPS

The Fundamental Tension

A hedge fund is an open-ended vehicle that invests in sometimes-illiquid assets. This creates a structural mismatch:

Fund promises LPs Fund's actual portfolio
Liquidity Quarterly redemptions with 45-day notice Some positions trade 10% of ADV daily; some OTC with 3-month unwind
Speed Cash within 60 days of redemption date Forced selling of concentrated positions = market impact

This mismatch killed dozens of funds in 2008. LPs requested redemptions. Funds couldn't liquidate fast enough. Fire-sale prices. NAV collapsed. More redemptions. Death spiral.

Subscription Terms

Parameter Typical terms Notes
Minimum investment $1M–$25M Higher for managed accounts ($100M+)
Subscription frequency Monthly or quarterly Some daily (UCITS)
Notice period 5–30 days before subscription date
Documentation Subscription agreement, AML/KYC, accredited investor verification

Redemption Terms

Parameter Typical L/S equity Typical multi-strat Typical credit/distressed
Frequency Quarterly Quarterly or annual Semi-annual or annual
Notice period 45–90 days 60–90 days 90–180 days
Settlement T+30 to T+60 after redemption date T+30 to T+90 T+90 or longer
Holdback 5–10% held for audit completion 5–10% 10–20%

Holdback: The fund pays 90–95% of proceeds at settlement and withholds 5–10% until the annual audit confirms final NAV. This protects against overpayment if NAV was miscalculated.

Lock-Up Periods

Type Duration Liquidity Penalty
Hard lock-up 1–3 years ZERO — LP cannot redeem N/A
Soft lock-up 1–2 years LP CAN redeem but pays penalty 2–5% early redemption fee
Rolling lock-up Resets each time LP adds capital New capital is locked for the full period Varies

Why lock-ups exist: They give the PM time to implement the strategy without worrying about forced liquidation. A credit fund buying distressed bonds at 50 cents needs 2–3 years for the recovery thesis to play out. If LPs can redeem quarterly, the PM can never hold the position.

Gates — The Emergency Brake

When aggregate redemption requests exceed a threshold (usually 15–25% of NAV per quarter), the fund invokes a gate:

Worked example:
- Fund NAV: $2B
- Gate: 20% of NAV per quarter ($400M max)
- Redemption requests received: $700M from 15 LPs

Without gate With gate
Fund must liquidate $700M in positions in 60 days Fund processes $400M pro rata (each LP gets 57% of their request)
Market impact on selling $700M → 5–10% price discount Remaining $300M queued for next quarter
NAV drops below where redemptions were calculated NAV preserved better for remaining LPs
Death spiral risk Orderly wind-down

Controversy: Gates punish LPs who need their money. In 2008, many institutional LPs (who had their own cash needs — pensions paying retirees) were gated and couldn't access capital for 12+ months. This led to a permanent shift toward managed accounts for the largest allocators.

Suspension of Redemptions

The nuclear option. The fund board suspends ALL redemptions.

When it happens:
- 2008 GFC: 30%+ of hedge funds gated or suspended
- Market crisis makes fair NAV determination impossible
- Fire-sale liquidation would destroy remaining LP value

Legal basis: Typically requires board (or GP) determination that redemptions would be "materially detrimental to remaining LPs."

RM Responsibilities in a Redemption Scenario

Phase RM action
Pre-notice Maintain a position-level liquidity profile: how many days to unwind each position at 10%/20%/30% of ADV
Notice received Calculate total cash needed vs liquid asset base. Stress test: what if 2× the expected redemptions come in?
Liquidation planning Identify the LEAST impactful liquidation order. Sell liquid positions first. Avoid concentrated names where we're >5% of ADV.
Execution Monitor market impact during liquidation. Adjust if realized impact exceeds estimates.
Post-redemption Re-assess remaining portfolio: has the redemption concentrated the book? Are risk limits still appropriate for the smaller NAV?

PART 5: PRIME BROKERAGE

What a Prime Broker Does

Service What it means Risk to fund
Custody Holds the fund's securities and cash Counterparty risk: PB insolvency (Lehman)
Financing (margin lending) Lends cash for leveraged longs Financing can be withdrawn with 24hr notice
Securities lending Provides shares to borrow for shorts Recall risk: lender can demand shares back
Trade settlement Settles trades between fund and exchanges/counterparties Operational risk if settlement fails
Reporting Daily position, P&L, margin reports Data accuracy risk
Capital introduction Introduces fund to potential LPs Conflict of interest (PB favors clients that generate revenue)
Synthetic access (TRS) Provides exposure via total return swaps instead of physical ownership Counterparty risk + PB discretion on margin and termination

The Lehman Lesson (2008)

When Lehman Brothers filed for bankruptcy on September 15, 2008:

  • Lehman's Prime Brokerage clients had assets CUSTODIED at Lehman. Those assets were frozen in the bankruptcy estate.
  • Some funds couldn't access their own securities for MONTHS.
  • Rehypothecated assets (assets the fund had pledged as collateral, which Lehman then re-pledged to its own creditors) were lost in the waterfall.
  • Funds that had 100% of their assets at Lehman PB faced existential risk.

Result: The entire industry moved to multi-prime models.

Multi-Prime Model

Benefit How it works
Counterparty diversification No single PB has >30–40% of the fund's assets
Competitive pricing PBs compete on financing rates, stock borrow availability, margin terms
Business continuity If PB A has operational issues, positions at PB B and C continue trading
Negotiating leverage "PB A offered us 10bp better financing — can you match?"
Information leakage reduction No single PB sees the entire portfolio (important for multi-strats that guard position information)

Typical setup for a large multi-strat ($10B+ AUM):
- 3–5 prime brokers (Goldman, Morgan Stanley, JPMorgan, BofA, Barclays)
- Assets allocated roughly: 30% / 25% / 25% / 20%
- Separate execution brokers (electronic platforms, agency brokers)
- Fund administrator (independent NAV calculation — not the PB)

How PBs Make Money from Hedge Funds

Revenue source Typical magnitude Fund's cost
Financing spread Fed Funds + 30–80bp Annual cost on leveraged long book
Securities lending 25bp for general collateral; 5–30% for hard-to-borrow Annual cost on short book
Execution commissions $0.003–0.01 per share (equities) Per-trade cost
Synthetic financing (TRS spread) SOFR + 40–100bp Annual cost for swap-based access
Cross-selling FX, rates, credit — profitable flow Spread/commission on OTC trades

RM relevance: Financing costs are a direct drag on P&L. A fund running $3B gross at 50bp average financing cost = $15M/year in financing alone. The RM should monitor financing costs vs returns, and flag strategies where financing costs eat a large portion of the alpha.

PB Risk Monitoring — What the RM Tracks

Metric Why Red flag
PB CDS spread Widening CDS = market pricing PB default risk higher CDS > 200bp sustained
Rehypothecation level How much of our collateral has the PB re-pledged? Approach to contractual limit
Financing rate changes PB silently increasing rates = de-risking their HF exposure Rate pop of >25bp without market justification
Margin requirement changes PB increasing IM = they want less exposure to us Sudden IM increase > 20%
Stock borrow availability Recalls or decreased inventory = PB de-risking or funding pressure Multiple recall notices in a week

PART 6: MARGIN AND LEVERAGE

Types of Margin

Margin type Purpose Set by Timing
Initial Margin (IM) Collateral to OPEN a position Exchange (listed) or PB/CCP (OTC) At trade inception
Variation Margin (VM) Daily MTM adjustment — winner receives, loser pays Exchange or CCP Daily (sometimes intraday)
Maintenance Margin Minimum equity to KEEP a position open Exchange or PB Continuous
Additional Margin Extra margin demanded by PB for concentrated or illiquid positions PB (discretionary) Anytime — PB can call this at will

The PB discretion problem: For listed instruments, exchange-set margins are transparent and rules-based. For OTC and PB-financed positions, the PB sets margin AT THEIR DISCRETION. They can INCREASE margin requirements unilaterally — and they tend to do so EXACTLY when the fund is already under stress.

Leverage Metrics

Metric Formula What it captures
Gross leverage (Long MV + |Short MV|) / NAV Total exposure relative to capital
Net leverage (Long MV − |Short MV|) / NAV Directional exposure
Notional leverage Total notional / NAV Used for derivatives (can be misleading)
Regulatory leverage Various (Basel III, AIFMD) Standardized for reporting
Risk-adjusted leverage Gross exposure × portfolio vol / NAV Accounts for how much RISK the leverage creates

Example: Fund with $500M NAV:

Long Short Gross Net Gross leverage Net leverage
Equities $600M $400M $1,000M $200M 2.0× 0.4×
Rates $300M $200M $500M $100M 1.0× 0.2×
Total $900M $600M $1,500M $300M 3.0× 0.6×

This fund is 3.0× gross leveraged but only 0.6× net. The directional risk is modest, but the TOTAL exposure is significant — any sharp move in the long or short book separately (e.g., a short squeeze) can create large P&L swings.

Leverage by Strategy Type

Strategy Typical gross leverage Why Key risk
Long/short equity 1.5–3.0× Moderate notional; alpha from stock selection Factor crowding
Multi-strategy 3–6× Diversified strategies; higher Sharpe → more leverage Correlation across pods
Stat arb 5–15× Low per-trade edge → need leverage for returns Model breakdown
Fixed income RV 10–50× Tiny spread → massive notional needed Liquidity/basis blow-out
Global macro 3–10× Concentrated bets via futures and options Event risk
Credit 1–3× Illiquid assets → limited leverage deployed Mark-to-market + redemption mismatch

Margin Call Mechanics — Worked Example

Setup: Fund has $500M equity, $1.5B gross exposure (3× leverage), maintenance margin = 25% ($375M equity required).

Day Market move Equity impact Fund equity Margin status Action
Mon — — $500M $125M buffer Normal
Tue −2% on longs −$12M $488M $113M buffer Normal
Wed −3% on longs −$18M $470M $95M buffer Watch
Thu −5% adverse (shorts rally, longs sell off) −$45M $425M $50M buffer Warning
Fri −4% adverse + short squeeze −$40M $385M $10M buffer — approaching margin call PB calls demanding $50M additional margin by Monday AM

The RM's margin buffer rule:

$$\text{Margin buffer} = \text{Current equity} - \text{Maintenance margin requirement}$$

$$\text{Buffer ratio} = \frac{\text{Buffer}}{\text{Equity}} = \frac{\$500M - \$375M}{\$500M} = 25\%$$

Minimum recommended buffer: 15–25% of equity. If buffer drops below 15% → begin voluntary de-risking BEFORE the PB forces you to sell.

Why pro-active matters: If the fund waits for the PB's margin call, the PB chooses WHICH positions to liquidate. They'll sell your most liquid positions first — which are often your BEST positions (highest alpha, most liquid markets). The fund is left with the illiquid dregs.

Pro-Cyclicality of Margin

The most dangerous feature of the leverage system: margins increase when you can least afford it.

Market condition What happens to margin Fund impact
Low vol, markets calm PBs offer low margins, easy financing, generous leverage Fund levers up, adds positions
Vol spike, stress begins PBs increase IM requirements, recall stock borrow, tighten terms Fund must POST more collateral or sell positions — exactly when selling is most costly
Crisis PBs increase margins aggressively, cut financing lines, recall all flexible lending Forced liquidation at worst possible prices

This is why LTCM failed. This is why Archegos failed. This is why Bear Stearns failed. The leverage was available in good times and withdrawn in bad times — the OPPOSITE of what funds need.

RM response: Always stress-test margin under STRESS conditions, not just current conditions. "We have $125M of margin buffer today. If PBs increase requirements by 50% (which they did in March 2020), how much buffer remains?" If the answer is zero → reduce leverage NOW.

PART 7: CENTRAL COUNTERPARTIES (CCPs) & CLEARING

Post-2008 Regulatory Framework

Dodd-Frank (US) and EMIR (EU) mandated central clearing of standardized OTC derivatives:

Before 2008 After 2010+
Bilateral OTC — each fund faced its dealer directly CCP sits between buyer and seller (novation)
Counterparty risk: if dealer defaults → fund loses CCP absorbs default risk via margin + default fund
No standardized margin — negotiated bilaterally Standardized IM/VM set by CCP
Opaque — no one knew total exposures CCP has full visibility into positions

How the CCP Default Waterfall Works

If a clearing member defaults:

Layer Source Size (example)
1 Defaulter's initial margin $500M
2 Defaulter's default fund contribution $50M
3 CCP's own capital ("skin in the game") $100M
4 Non-defaulting members' default fund contributions $5B
5 CCP recovery tools (VM haircutting, additional assessments) Uncapped

RM concern: Layer 4 means your fund (as a clearing member or through your clearing broker) could be assessed for SOMEONE ELSE'S default. This is mutualized loss — you pay for another firm's failure. The RM should know the fund's maximum exposure to CCP default fund assessments.

CCP Margin Pro-Cyclicality

CCPs use models (SPAN, PRISMA, VaR-based) that INCREASE margin when vol spikes:

VIX level Typical equity IM 10% move impact
12 (calm) 5% of notional $50M IM on $1B
25 (elevated) 10% of notional $100M IM on $1B
40 (stress) 15–20% of notional $150–200M IM on $1B

Going from VIX 12 to VIX 40: IM triples. The fund needs $150M MORE in cash — at the exact moment when every other fund also needs cash and liquidity is scarce.

PART 8: REPO & SECURITIES FINANCING

Repurchase Agreement (Repo) Mechanics

A repo is a short-term collateralized loan disguised as a sale-and-repurchase:

Step What happens
T+0 Fund "sells" $100M of Treasuries to dealer. Receives $98M cash (2% haircut).
T+1 (or term end) Fund "repurchases" Treasuries for $98M + interest ($98M × repo rate × days/360).
Net effect Fund BORROWED $98M using Treasuries as collateral. Paid the repo rate.

Repo Rate Components

$$\text{Repo rate} \approx \text{Risk-free rate} - \text{Collateral premium (if special)} + \text{Credit spread (of borrower)}$$

Collateral type Typical haircut Typical repo rate
US Treasuries (on-the-run) 1–2% SOFR flat to +5bp
US Treasuries (off-the-run) 2–5% SOFR +5 to +15bp
Agency MBS 3–5% SOFR +10 to +30bp
Investment-grade credit 5–8% SOFR +25 to +75bp
Equities (margin loan equivalent) 25–50% SOFR +40 to +150bp
High-yield / distressed 15–30% SOFR +100 to +300bp

Reverse Repo (the other side)

Repo Reverse Repo
Fund... Borrows cash, pledges securities Lends cash, receives securities
Why Finance long positions Earn interest on cash; obtain specific securities for shorting
Risk Collateral locked up; rollover risk Cash is at risk if counterparty defaults and collateral declines

Securities Lending

Separate from repo. Used specifically for short selling:

Step What happens
1 Fund wants to short Stock X. Doesn't own it.
2 PB locates Stock X from a lender (pension fund, index fund, insurance co).
3 Fund borrows Stock X. Posts cash collateral (typically 102–105% of stock value).
4 Fund sells Stock X in the market (establishing the short).
5 Fund pays a borrow fee to the lender (25bp to 30%+ annualized depending on availability).
6 To close: fund buys Stock X in the market, returns it to lender, gets collateral back.

Hard-to-borrow (HTB) stocks: When everyone wants to short the same stock, borrow fees spike. A stock with 25% short interest might cost 5–10% annualized to borrow. A stock on threshold list with 80% utilization → 20–50%+ annualized. This cost DIRECTLY reduces the short's profitability.

Recall risk: The lender can recall (demand return of) the borrowed shares at any time. If the fund can't find a replacement lender → forced buy-in. Forced buy-ins on HTB names in stressed markets can cause explosive short squeezes (GME, Jan 2021).

Repo Risks for RM

Risk Description Mitigation
Rollover risk 80%+ of repo is overnight. If counterparty refuses to roll → must raise cash immediately. Diversify repo counterparties. Maintain some term repo (7-day, 30-day) alongside overnight.
Haircut risk Counterparty increases haircut from 2% to 5% → must post $30M more collateral on $1B position. Monitor haircuts daily. Stress-test with 2× haircuts.
Collateral downgrade Counterparty stops accepting certain collateral classes. Keep "free" unencumbered high-quality assets.
Fire-sale spiral Can't roll → forced to sell repo'd securities → selling pressure → prices drop → haircuts increase elsewhere → cascade. Maintain liquidity buffer: 10–20% of assets in unencumbered cash or T-bills.

PART 9: MULTI-STRATEGY POD ECONOMICS

One-liner: Understanding how multi-strats allocate capital, charge costs, and manage pods is critical because Squarepoint operates this model.

The Pod Model

Feature Description
Structure Multiple independent trading teams ("pods") under one fund umbrella
Capital Each pod gets an allocation from the central pool (e.g., $200M–$1B per pod)
P&L Each pod's P&L tracked independently
Risk limits Centrally set: VaR limit, max drawdown, gross/net exposure, concentration
Risk management Central RM team monitors all pods + aggregates firm-wide risk
Infrastructure Shared: execution, technology, operations, compliance, PB relationships

PM Compensation in Multi-Strats

Component Typical
Base salary $200K–$500K (relatively modest)
P&L payout 10–20% of net PnL above costs

This is NOT the sum of individual pod risks because of correlation:

$$\sigma_{\text{firm}} = \sqrt{\sum_i \sigma_i^2 + 2\sum_{i<j} \rho_{ij}\sigma_i\sigma_j}$$

PART 11: INTERVIEW APPLICATION

"Explain how a fund's structure affects risk management."

"Fund structure creates risk channels that pure market-risk analysis doesn't capture.

First, liquidity mismatch. The fund offers quarterly redemptions, but some positions take weeks to unwind at reasonable market impact. If 20% of LPs redeem in one quarter, we need $100M+ in cash within 60 days. The RM must maintain a position-level liquidity profile — for every position, how many days to liquidate at 10%, 20%, and 30% of ADV — and ensure the aggregate is comfortably inside our redemption capacity.

Second, leverage fragility via pro-cyclical margin. At 3× gross, a 5% adverse move costs 15% of NAV. But it's worse than that — because the PB increases margin requirements exactly when you're losing money. In March 2020, some PBs doubled margin requirements in a single week. The RM must stress-test margin not at current levels but at 1.5× to 2× current levels.

Third, fee structure creates behavioral risk. A PM who's 15% below the high-water mark needs to recover that entire amount before earning performance fees. That creates incentive to increase risk — gamble to recover faster. The RM needs to monitor for risk increases that correlate with distance-below-HWM."

"Walk me through what happens during a margin call."

"A margin call means the fund's equity has dropped below the PB's maintenance margin requirement. There are three phases.

First, the call itself. The PB notifies us — usually before market open — that we need to post additional collateral within 24 hours, sometimes same day. The amount is typically the shortfall plus a buffer.

Second, the response. We have three options: post cash, post additional securities as collateral, or reduce positions to lower the margin requirement. The RM's job is to pre-plan this — know which positions to sell that minimally impact the portfolio's alpha-generating capability. You want to sell the liquid, low-conviction positions first. If you wait for the PB to liquidate, they choose — and they'll sell your most liquid positions regardless of your conviction.

Third, the aftermath. After meeting the call, the RM must reassess: is the portfolio now appropriate for the reduced capital base? Have risk limits auto-adjusted? And critically — if the market continues to move against us, do we have buffer for ANOTHER margin call? Because margin calls often come in clusters."

"How does multi-strategy fund structure affect firm-level risk?"

"The main issue is correlation. In normal markets, pods running different strategies — equity L/S, credit, macro, stat arb — appear diversified. Their correlations might be 0.2–0.3, so firm-level VaR is significantly lower than the sum of pod VaRs. The CRO budgets for this diversification benefit.

But in stress, correlations spike. In March 2020, equity and credit correlations jumped from 0.3 to 0.85. The diversification benefit that was reducing firm VaR by 30% effectively vanished. If the CRO was relying on that benefit to stay within leverage limits, the firm is suddenly over its risk budget.

The RM's job is to run firm-level stress tests using CRISIS correlations, not normal-market correlations. And to ensure that capital allocation across pods accounts for the scenario where diversification breaks down. If firm-level VaR under crisis-correlation assumptions exceeds limits, the response is to reduce gross across ALL pods — not just the one that's losing."
