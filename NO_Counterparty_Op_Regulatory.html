NO — COUNTERPARTY, OPERATIONAL & REGULATORY RISK

The non-market risks that can still kill a fund

Depth: 🟢 AWARE

WHY THIS MATTERS

You won't get a deep dive question on FRTB or CVA formulas. But you need to know these exist — because:
1. They come up in "what types of risk exist beyond market risk?" answers
2. They show you understand the FULL risk landscape, not just Greeks and VaR
3. Operational and counterparty risk have killed more funds than bad trades

PART 1: COUNTERPARTY RISK

What Is It?

The risk that the other side of your trade can't pay what they owe you. If you buy a swap from Bank X and Bank X goes bankrupt, your swap is worthless even if the trade was profitable.

Key Concepts

Credit Valuation Adjustment (CVA)

  • The market price of counterparty credit risk.
  • CVA = Expected Exposure × Probability of Default × Loss Given Default
  • Translation: "How much is at risk × how likely they default × how much we'd lose if they do."
  • Practically: CVA is a deduction from the value of an OTC derivative position. If the counterparty is riskier, the trade is worth less to you.

Debit Valuation Adjustment (DVA)

  • The mirror image: the market price of YOUR OWN credit risk to counterparties.
  • Controversial because: as your own credit deteriorates, DVA creates an accounting gain. "We're closer to bankruptcy, so our liabilities are worth less, so we book a profit." This is technically correct but perverse.

Wrong-Way Risk

  • When your exposure to a counterparty INCREASES at the same time as their creditworthiness DECREASES.
  • Classic example: You buy a credit default swap (CDS) on Italian debt from an Italian bank. If Italy gets into trouble, the bank's credit also deteriorates — exactly when you need them to pay you the most.
  • This is what makes counterparty risk non-linear and dangerous.

Netting & Collateral

Netting agreements (ISDA Master Agreement):
- If you have multiple trades with the same counterparty, netting means you only owe/are owed the NET amount.
- Without netting: you owe them $10M on Trade A, they owe you $8M on Trade B → you owe $10M (if they default you lose $8M AND still owe $10M).
- With netting: net position is $2M. Much less risk.

Credit Support Annex (CSA):
- The collateral agreement under ISDA. Specifies:
- How often collateral is exchanged (daily/weekly)
- Minimum transfer amount
- Eligible collateral (cash, government bonds, etc.)
- Thresholds (below which no collateral is required)

Central Clearing (CCP):
- Post-2008 reform: standardized OTC derivatives must be cleared through a CCP (like LCH, CME).
- CCP sits between buyer and seller → eliminates bilateral counterparty risk.
- BUT creates concentration risk: if the CCP fails, EVERYTHING fails. The CCP is "too important to fail."

For Your Interview

"Counterparty risk is the risk that the other side of an OTC trade can't pay. It's managed through ISDA netting, CSA collateral, and central clearing. The key concept I'd flag is wrong-way risk — when your exposure increases exactly when the counterparty's creditworthiness decreases. That's a correlation most simple models miss."

PART 2: OPERATIONAL RISK

What Is It?

Risk of loss from inadequate or failed internal processes, people, systems, or external events.

The Big Categories

People Risk

  • Rogue trader (Barings, Société Générale / Kerviel)
  • Key-person dependency (what if the head of risk quits?)
  • Human error (wrong notional on a trade, fat-finger)

Process Risk

  • No separation of duties (Barings: Leeson did trading AND back office)
  • Inadequate reconciliation (positions not matched between front and back office)
  • Missing or incomplete documentation for OTC trades

Technology Risk

  • System outage during trading hours
  • Data feed failures (pricing based on stale data)
  • Cybersecurity (theft, ransomware, data breach)

External Events

  • Natural disasters, pandemics (COVID forced work-from-home → new op risk)
  • Legal/regulatory actions against the firm
  • Third-party vendor failures

The Operational Risk Framework (for an RM answer)

Layer What it covers Example
Prevention Controls that stop errors before they happen 4-eye principle (two approvals), automated limit checks
Detection Controls that catch errors quickly Daily reconciliation, trade matching, anomaly alerts
Response What to do when an error occurs Incident response procedures, escalation, root cause analysis
Recovery Getting back to normal Business continuity, backup systems, disaster recovery

Key Operational Controls at a Hedge Fund

  1. Trade reconciliation: Front office positions must match back office and prime broker records DAILY.
  2. Pricing verification: Marks must be independently verified — not just using the broker's mark.
  3. Access controls: WHO can enter trades, WHO can modify limits, WHO can override alerts.
  4. Segregation of duties: The person who puts on a trade cannot be the person who reconciles it.

For Your Interview

"Operational risk is about controls — making sure the right people are doing the right things and that errors are caught quickly. The lesson from Barings is that no amount of market risk management matters if one person can both trade and reconcile their own positions. As a junior RM, I'd make sure I have independent access to position data and that I'm doing daily reconciliation against the PB blotter."

PART 3: REGULATORY LANDSCAPE

Basel Framework (Banks)

Not directly applicable to a hedge fund, but important to understand because:
- Prime brokers operate under Basel → their balance sheet constraints affect YOUR margin terms
- Basel capital requirements influence HOW MUCH leverage PBs will extend to you
- FRTB (below) changes how PBs calculate market risk capital → may make some trades more expensive

Basel III (current)

  • Capital ratios: minimum CET1 ratio of 4.5% + buffers
  • Leverage ratio: 3% minimum (limits total balance sheet leverage)
  • Liquidity requirements: LCR (liquid assets to cover 30-day outflows), NSFR (stable funding for illiquid assets)
  • Supplementary Leverage Ratio (SLR): constrains bank balance sheets → reduces PB capacity → matters for hedge funds trying to get leverage

FRTB (Fundamental Review of the Trading Book)

  • New market risk capital standard replacing old VaR-based approach.
  • Key change: Expected Shortfall (ES) instead of VaR. Why? ES captures tail risk better — it's the average loss beyond VaR, not just the threshold.
  • Practically for you: PBs may reprice their services as FRTB increases capital charges. Some strategies (concentrated, illiquid) may become more expensive to finance.

Dodd-Frank (US Post-2008)

  • Volcker Rule: banks can't do proprietary trading. Led many risk professionals to move from banks to hedge funds (where you CAN trade).
  • Clearinghouse mandates: standardized swaps must go through CCPs.
  • Swap dealer registration: reporting requirements for OTC derivatives.
  • Impact on hedge funds: more transparency, higher costs, but also better counterparty infrastructure.

EMIR (European equivalent of Dodd-Frank)

  • Trade reporting: all OTC derivatives must be reported to a trade repository.
  • Central clearing: mandatory for certain standardized OTC derivatives.
  • Risk mitigation: timely confirmation, portfolio reconciliation, margin requirements for non-cleared trades.

MiFID II (European — Markets in Financial Instruments)

  • Best execution: must prove you got the best price for clients.
  • Transparency: pre- and post-trade reporting.
  • Research unbundling: research payments must be separated from execution commissions. Changed how funds pay for research.

SEC (US — for hedge funds)

  • Form PF: quarterly or annual reporting for private funds (AUM, leverage, exposures, concentration).
  • 13F: quarterly filing of long equity positions for managers with >$100M AUM. BUT: short positions NOT reported. Total return swaps NOT reported (this is the Archegos gap).
  • Regulation SHO: short-selling rules (locate requirement, circuit breakers).

For Your Interview

"The regulatory landscape is mostly relevant to us through PB constraints — Basel and FRTB determine how much leverage our prime brokers can offer and at what cost. EMIR and Dodd-Frank drive central clearing and reporting requirements for our OTC book. And the 13F reporting gap — where TRS don't appear — is exactly what enabled Archegos. The regulation is catching up but there are still blind spots."
