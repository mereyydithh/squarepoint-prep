CVA/DVA, wrong-way risk, ISDA/CSA, operational controls, rogue traders, technology risk, Basel III/IV, FRTB, Dodd-Frank, EMIR, MiFID II, and why the non-market risks are the ones that actually kill funds

WHY NON-MARKET RISKS KILL MORE FUNDS THAN BAD TRADES

Every fund that has ever blown up catastrophically was destroyed by a non-market risk — or by market risk combined with structural/operational failures:

Fund Market loss? What ACTUALLY killed it
Barings (1995) Nikkei adverse move Operational: Leeson controlled BOTH trading AND back office. No segregation of duties.
LTCM (1998) Spread widening Counterparty + structural: Leverage 25×+ with no margin buffer. PBs withdrew financing.
Amaranth (2006) Nat gas spread Concentration + operational: Single trader controlled >50% of fund risk. No position limit enforcement.
Knight Capital (2012) N/A (no market event) Technology: Erroneous code deployed to production. Lost $440M in 45 minutes.
Archegos (2021) ViacomCBS drop Counterparty opacity: TRS positions invisible across PBs. No aggregate exposure monitoring.

Market risk is what you lose money ON. Operational, counterparty, and regulatory risk is what PREVENTS you from managing that market risk.

PART 1: COUNTERPARTY RISK — FUNDAMENTALS

Definition

Counterparty risk = the risk that the entity on the other side of your trade can't fulfil its obligations. If you trade a swap with Bank X and Bank X defaults, your in-the-money position is worth zero — even though you were right about the trade.

Where Counterparty Risk Lives

Instrument Counterparty risk Why
Listed futures/options Minimal CCP (exchange) stands between buyer and seller
OTC derivatives (bilateral) High Direct exposure to the other party
Total Return Swaps (TRS) High Counterparty is the PB/dealer providing the swap
Repo / reverse repo Moderate Collateralized, but haircut and collateral quality matter
Securities lending Moderate Borrower may fail to return shares; cash collateral may be insufficient
Bank deposits Low but non-zero FDIC covers only $250K; fund cash balances can be $100M+
Prime brokerage custody Moderate Lehman 2008 — assets held at PB frozen in bankruptcy

Key Metrics

Potential Future Exposure (PFE)

PFE measures the MAXIMUM expected exposure to a counterparty at a given confidence level over a given time horizon:

$$\text{PFE}_{95\%} = \text{Current MTM} + \text{Potential increase at 95th percentile over horizon}$$

Trade Current MTM 95th percentile potential increase PFE (95%)
5Y interest rate swap (receive fixed) +$2M +$8M $10M
3Y equity TRS +$5M +$12M $17M
FX forward (6 month) −$1M +$4M $3M

PFE captures the fact that even a trade currently LOSING money could become an exposure if it swings in your favor — because then you're owed money by the counterparty.

Expected Exposure (EE)

EE is the AVERAGE exposure over the life of the trade (not the peak):

$$\text{EE} = \mathbb{E}\left[\max(V_t, 0)\right]$$

EE is used for CVA calculation (below). PFE is used for counterparty limit monitoring.

PART 2: CREDIT VALUATION ADJUSTMENT (CVA) AND DVA

CVA — The Market Price of Counterparty Risk

CVA answers: "How much less is this derivative worth because my counterparty might default?"

$$\text{CVA} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \text{EE}(t_i) \times \text{PD}(t_{i-1}, t_i) \times \text{LGD} \times \text{DF}(t_i)$$

Where:
- EE(tᵢ) = Expected Exposure at time tᵢ
- PD(tᵢ₋₁, tᵢ) = Probability of Default during the interval
- LGD = Loss Given Default (typically 60% for senior unsecured)
- DF(tᵢ) = Discount Factor

Simplified intuition:
$$\text{CVA} \approx \text{Expected Exposure} \times \text{Probability of Default} \times \text{Loss Given Default}$$

Worked example:

Input Value
Average expected exposure over 5Y $15M
Counterparty 5Y CDS spread 200bp (implies ~2% annual PD)
Cumulative 5Y PD ~10%
LGD 60%
CVA $15M × 10% × 60% = $900K

This means the derivative is worth $900K LESS than its risk-free value because of the counterparty's credit risk.

Who bears CVA? Both parties. The fund adjusts its mark. The dealer adjusts their mark. CVA is a real economic cost that must be charged to the trading desk or absorbed as a portfolio-level cost.

DVA — The Mirror Image

DVA = the market price of YOUR OWN credit risk to counterparties.

$$\text{DVA} \approx \text{Counter-party's Expected Exposure to YOU} \times \text{YOUR PD} \times \text{LGD}$$

The perverse accounting result: As your own credit deteriorates → your DVA increases → you book an accounting GAIN. "We're closer to defaulting, so our liabilities are worth less, so — congratulations — we made money."

Your credit quality DVA P&L impact
Improving DVA decreases Loss (liabilities worth more)
Deteriorating DVA increases Gain (liabilities worth less)

This is technically correct under mark-to-market but economically bizarre. Many firms and regulators are uncomfortable with it.

Funding Valuation Adjustment (FVA)

FVA accounts for the cost of FUNDING the collateral required by derivatives positions:

$$\text{FVA} = \text{Expected Exposure} \times (\text{Funding Spread} - \text{OIS Rate}) \times \text{Duration}$$

If the fund borrows at SOFR + 100bp to post collateral, and the derivative is collateralized at OIS, the fund incurs a funding cost that reduces the trade's value.

Why this matters for RM: A trade that looks profitable on a risk-free basis may be UNPROFITABLE after CVA + FVA. The RM should ensure that the PM's P&L reflects these adjustments — or at minimum, that the risk budget accounts for them.

PART 3: WRONG-WAY RISK

Definition

Wrong-way risk (WWR) occurs when your exposure to a counterparty INCREASES at the same time as their creditworthiness DECREASES. The correlation between exposure and default probability is POSITIVE — exactly when you need them most, they're least able to pay.

Types

Type Definition Example
Specific WWR Direct causal link between counterparty and reference entity Buy CDS on Italian sovereign debt from an Italian bank
General WWR Exposure increases in scenarios where counterparty credit also worsens Long emerging market via TRS with an EM-exposed bank; EM crisis → exposure rises AND bank weakens

Classic Wrong-Way Risk Scenarios

Trade Counterparty Scenario What happens
Buy CDS on Italy from Unicredit Italian bank Italy sovereign stress CDS payout owed to you increases; Unicredit's ability to pay collapses
Long oil via TRS with energy-focused bank Energy bank Oil crash TRS losses accrue to counterparty; bank's other portfolios also bleeding
FX forward: short USD/buy TRY from Turkish bank Turkish bank Turkish lira crisis Your exposure grows as TRY crashes; bank may fail
Interest rate swap: receive fixed from a highly-leveraged bank Levered bank Rate spike Bank's bond portfolio loses value; your swap exposure to them increases

Modeling Wrong-Way Risk

Standard CVA models assume exposure and default probability are INDEPENDENT. Wrong-way risk means they're CORRELATED:

$$\text{CVA}{\text{WWR}} = \text{CVA}{\text{standard}} \times \text{WWR multiplier}$$

Typical WWR multipliers: 1.3–2.0× for general WWR; 2.0–5.0× for specific WWR.

RM action: When approving an OTC trade, ask: "In a scenario where this counterparty is under stress, does our exposure to them INCREASE?" If yes → wrong-way risk. Apply higher CVA, consider collateralization, or find a different counterparty.

PART 4: ISDA, NETTING, AND COLLATERAL

The ISDA Master Agreement

The International Swaps and Derivatives Association (ISDA) Master Agreement governs virtually all bilateral OTC derivative transactions:

Component What it covers
Master Agreement Standard legal terms: default events, termination events, close-out netting
Schedule Customized terms: specific elections, modifications to standard terms
Credit Support Annex (CSA) Collateral terms: what, when, how much
Confirmations Individual trade terms under the master framework

Close-Out Netting — Why It's Critical

Without netting:
- You owe Bank X: +$10M on Trade A
- Bank X owes you: −$8M on Trade B
- If Bank X defaults: you STILL owe $10M on Trade A, but you can only claim $8M as an unsecured creditor on Trade B
- Net loss: potentially the full $8M (recovery rate on unsecured claims in bankruptcy: ~40 cents on the dollar → loss = $8M × 0.6 = $4.8M)

With netting:
- Net position: you owe Bank X $2M
- If Bank X defaults: your exposure is $2M, not $8M
- Netting REDUCES counterparty exposure by 50–90% in practice

Legal enforceability: Netting is ONLY valid if legally enforceable in the counterparty's jurisdiction. In some jurisdictions, bankruptcy "cherry picking" laws allow the administrator to enforce profitable trades and reject unprofitable ones — destroying the netting benefit. ISDA has worked to get netting legal opinions in 70+ jurisdictions.

Credit Support Annex (CSA) — Collateral Mechanics

CSA parameter Typical terms RM concern

Threshold $0 – $10M (below this, no collateral required) Higher threshold = more unsecured exposure
Minimum transfer amount (MTA) $250K – $1M Prevents nuisance calls; but creates exposure in the gap
Eligible collateral Cash (USD, EUR, GBP), G7 government bonds Equities, corp bonds → haircuts apply; non-cash adds complexity
Haircuts Cash: 0%. G7 bonds: 1–5%. Corporate bonds: 5–15%. Equities: 15–30% After haircuts, collateral may not fully cover exposure
Independent amount (IA) Additional margin beyond MTM — similar to initial margin Protects against gap between collateral calls
Rehypothecation Can the receiving party re-use the collateral? Yes → counterparty risk on YOUR collateral. No → safer but ties up assets.

PART 5: CENTRAL CLEARING AND CCPs

Post-2008 Mandate

Dodd-Frank (US) and EMIR (EU) mandated central clearing for standardized OTC derivatives:

Before (bilateral) After (cleared)
Direct exposure to each dealer CCP stands between all parties
Negotiated collateral (CSA) Standardized margin (IM + VM)
Counterparty risk concentrated in banks Counterparty risk concentrated in CCPs
Opaque — regulators couldn't see total exposures Transparent — CCP has full position data

Major CCPs

CCP Products Region
LCH (SwapClear) Interest rate swaps (70%+ of global IRS) Global (London HQ)
CME Clearing Futures, options, IRS, CDS US
ICE Clear CDS, energy, equity derivatives US / EU
Eurex Clearing Euro-denominated derivatives EU
JSCC Yen IRS, CDS Japan

CCP Default Waterfall

If a clearing member defaults, losses are absorbed in this order:

Layer Source Typical size Who bears it
1 Defaulter's initial margin $500M+ Defaulter
2 Defaulter's default fund contribution $50M+ Defaulter
3 CCP's own capital ("skin in the game") $100M–$500M CCP shareholders
4 Non-defaulting members' default fund contributions $5B+ collectively All other members (mutualized loss)
5 CCP recovery tools: VM gains haircutting, additional assessments, partial tear-ups Uncapped All members + end-users

Layer 4 is critical for hedge funds: Your fund (through its clearing broker) pays into the default fund. If someone ELSE defaults, YOUR money covers THEIR losses. This is mutualized risk — the opposite of bilateral where you only face your own counterparties.

Layer 5 is existential: VM gains haircutting means the CCP can take AWAY your profits to cover the default. Partial tear-ups mean the CCP can unilaterally cancel your trades.

CCP Risks for the RM

Risk Description RM response
Margin pro-cyclicality IM increases in stress → fund needs cash exactly when cash is scarce Stress-test margin at 1.5–2× current levels
Concentration CCP is a single point of failure for $100T+ notional Monitor CCP health; diversify across CCPs where possible
Default fund exposure Fund pays for others' defaults Know maximum assessment; build into liquidity buffer
Model risk CCP's risk model determines margin → if wrong, under-margined Monitor CCP margin methodology changes
Operational CCP technology failure → can't process trades/margin BCP: what if CCP is down for the day?

PART 6: OPERATIONAL RISK — THE SILENT KILLER

Definition

Operational risk = risk of loss from inadequate or failed internal processes, people, systems, or external events. Basel II defines it formally. For a hedge fund, it's everything that goes wrong that ISN'T a market view being wrong.

The Operational Risk Taxonomy

1. People Risk

Risk Example Control
Rogue trading Nick Leeson at Barings — unauthorized positions hidden in error account #88888 Segregation of duties, independent reconciliation, position limits
Unauthorized trading Jérôme Kerviel at SocGen — €50B in hidden futures positions Daily P&L attribution, independent position verification
Fat finger Deutsche Bank accidentally transferred €28B to a single counterparty (2018) Pre-trade checks, size limits, 4-eye principle
Key-person risk Star PM leaves → strategy collapses, LP redemptions follow Documentation of strategy, succession planning, institutional knowledge transfer
Fraud Madoff — fictitious trades, fake statements for decades Independent custody, third-party NAV calculation, audited statements
Insider trading PM trades on MNPI from management meetings Information barriers, compliance monitoring, trade surveillance

2. Process Risk

Risk Example Control
Failed reconciliation Front office shows +$5M P&L; back office shows +$3M. Who's right? Daily 3-way reconciliation (front office ↔ back office ↔ PB)
Settlement failure Trade doesn't settle on T+2 → counterparty exposure extends Settlement monitoring, fail-tracking, escalation
Incorrect trade booking Wrong notional, wrong sign (long vs short), wrong maturity Trade confirmation, automated booking checks, independent review
Documentation gaps OTC trade executed without signed ISDA confirmation Confirmation tracking, legal review, escalation for unsigned trades
NAV miscalculation Wrong marks, missing accruals, incorrect fee calculation Independent fund administrator, pricing committee, Level 3 review

3. Technology Risk

Risk Example Control
System outage Trading system down during market hours → can't manage risk Redundancy, failover, BCP drills
Bad code deployment Knight Capital: erroneous algorithm deployed; $440M loss in 45 minutes Code review, staged rollout, kill switch, regression testing
Data feed failure Pricing engine using stale prices → wrong marks, wrong risk Multiple data vendors, automated stale-data detection
Cybersecurity Ransomware locks trading systems; data theft exposes positions Network security, penetration testing, incident response, encryption
Algo malfunction Systematic strategy sends orders 10× intended size Pre-trade size checks, circuit breakers, real-time monitoring

4. External Event Risk

Risk Example Control
Natural disaster Hurricane destroys data center Geographic redundancy, cloud backup, remote capability
Pandemic COVID-19 → entire firm works from home overnight WFH infrastructure, secure remote access, tested BCP
Third-party failure Bloomberg terminal outage; data vendor delivers corrupted data Vendor diversification, SLA monitoring, backup data sources
Legal/litigation Fund sued by LP for NAV misrepresentation Legal review, compliance documentation, D&O insurance
Regulatory action SEC investigation, license revocation Compliance function, regulatory reporting, proactive engagement

The Knight Capital Case Study — Technology Risk in Detail

Date: August 1, 2012

What happened:
1. Knight Capital deployed new software for NYSE's Retail Liquidity Program
2. A technician failed to deploy the new code to ONE of 8 servers
3. Old code on that server executed a defunct test function — the "Power Peg" flag — which was designed to BUY at the ask and SELL at the bid (the opposite of normal trading)
4. At market open (9:30 AM), the server began aggressively buying high and selling low across 154 stocks
5. By 9:58 AM (28 minutes), the error was identified
6. By 10:15 AM (45 minutes), trading was halted

Result: Knight Capital lost $440 million in 45 minutes. The firm had ~$365M in capital. It was dead.

Root causes:
| Failure | What went wrong |
|---|---|
| Code deployment | No automated deployment system — manual copy to 8 servers |
| Testing | No integration test with the new code + old code on same network |
| Monitoring | No real-time P&L monitoring flagged the losses for 28 minutes |
| Kill switch | No circuit breaker that would halt trading when P&L hit a threshold |
| Code hygiene | Dead code ("Power Peg") left in production for 8 years — never removed |

RM lessons:
1. Technology IS market risk if it can generate unintended positions
2. Every trading system needs a kill switch with automatic triggers
3. Real-time P&L monitoring must flag anomalies within SECONDS, not minutes
4. Code review, automated deployment, and staged rollout are not optional

PART 8: REGULATORY LANDSCAPE

Basel Framework (Banks) — Why It Matters for Hedge Funds

You're not a bank. You won't be regulated under Basel. But your PRIME BROKERS are. Their capital constraints DIRECTLY affect you:

Basel requirement Impact on PB Impact on YOUR fund
CET1 ratio (4.5%+) PB must hold capital against risk-weighted assets Higher capital charge for PB → higher financing cost for you
Leverage ratio (3%) Limits PB's total balance sheet regardless of risk weights PBs cut low-margin businesses (e.g., repo financing) → less capacity for you
LCR (Liquidity Coverage Ratio) PB must hold liquid assets against 30-day stress outflows PB may treat your deposits as "runnable" → penalizing you
NSFR (Net Stable Funding Ratio) PB must match illiquid assets with stable funding Short-term financing to HFs is "unstable" → PB charges more or reduces capacity
SLR (Supplementary Leverage Ratio) Constrains bank balance sheets including derivatives exposure Particularly impacts repo-heavy strategies and large derivative books

FRTB (Fundamental Review of the Trading Book)

The new market risk capital standard replacing the internal models approach:

Feature Old approach (VaR-based) FRTB
Risk metric VaR (99%, 10-day) Expected Shortfall (97.5%, variable horizon)
Tail sensitivity Cuts off at VaR — ignores what's beyond ES captures average loss beyond VaR
Liquidity horizon Single 10-day horizon Instrument-dependent: 10 days (liquid) to 120 days (illiquid)
Non-modellable risk factors (NMRFs) Not explicitly captured Must capitalize NMRFs separately (often large)
P&L attribution test Not required Desks must prove their model's P&L matches actual P&L
Boundary Trading book vs banking book somewhat flexible Stricter boundary → less regulatory arbitrage

Impact on hedge funds:
- Strategies using ILLIQUID instruments (credit, distressed, structured products) → PBs face higher capital charges → higher financing costs to you
- NMRFs mean exotic/bespoke OTC products become more expensive to hold for dealers → wider spreads to you
- PBs may EXIT certain businesses entirely if capital charges make them uneconomical

The 13F Reporting Gap — The Archegos Problem

What 13F captures What 13F MISSES
Long equity positions (stocks, ETFs, convertibles, call options) Short positions — no public reporting
Held at quarter-end with >$200K market value TRS / synthetic positions — not beneficial ownership
Manager-level aggregation Cross-PB exposure — each PB sees only their slice

Archegos exploited ALL three gaps:
1. Used TRS → no 13F filing → market didn't know about $20B in concentrated positions
2. Used 5 different PBs → no single PB saw the total picture
3. Regulators had no visibility until the collapse

Post-Archegos reforms (SEC, 2023-2025):
- Proposed: expanded 13F to include shorts and TRS
- Proposed: enhanced Form PF with more granular position data
- Status: partially implemented, still evolving

PART 9: REGULATORY RISK — THE RISK OF REGULATION ITSELF

Beyond COMPLIANCE with regulation, there's the risk that regulation CHANGES in ways that damage your strategy:

Regulatory change risk Example Impact
Short-selling bans EU banned short-selling during COVID (March 2020) L/S equity funds couldn't hedge → forced to go net long or degross entirely
Position limits CFTC commodity position limits tightened Commodity funds must reduce positions
Margin increases EMIR margin requirements for non-cleared derivatives Bilateral OTC trades become more expensive
Tax changes Transaction tax (FTT) proposals in EU Would significantly impact high-turnover systematic strategies
Reporting burden New reporting requirements → compliance cost increase Diverts resources from investing to reporting
Strategy prohibition Volcker Rule eliminated bank prop trading Banks exited → reduced liquidity AND reduced competition for HFs

RM responsibility: Track regulatory pipeline. When regulation is PROPOSED (not just enacted), begin assessing impact:
1. Which positions/strategies are affected?
2. What's the compliance cost?
3. Does this create forced selling/buying that moves markets?
4. Can we benefit from others' forced compliance? (Regulatory arbitrage)

PART 11: INTERVIEW APPLICATION

"What types of risk exist beyond market risk?"

"Three main categories. Counterparty risk — the risk that the other side of an OTC trade defaults. This is managed through ISDA netting agreements, CSA collateral posting, and central clearing via CCPs. The subtle version is wrong-way risk, where your exposure to a counterparty increases exactly as their credit deteriorates — buying CDS on Italian debt from an Italian bank is the classic example.

Operational risk — the risk of internal failures in people, processes, and technology. This is what killed Barings — Leeson controlled both trading and back office with no segregation of duties. And Knight Capital — $440M lost in 45 minutes because of a failed code deployment. As an RM, I'd focus on daily 3-way reconciliation, independent pricing verification, and ensuring no single person controls the full trade lifecycle.

Regulatory risk — both compliance risk and the risk that regulation changes and impacts our strategy. Short-selling bans during COVID forced L/S funds to degross. FRTB is increasing capital charges for PBs, which flows through as higher financing costs. And the 13F reporting gap — where TRS positions are invisible — is exactly what enabled Archegos. That gap is slowly being closed but isn't fully fixed yet."

"How would you monitor counterparty risk?"

"Three layers. First, ongoing monitoring — daily net exposure by counterparty, daily collateral coverage ratios, and daily CDS spread tracking for each major counterparty. If CDS widens 100bp in a week, that's an early warning.

Second, structural protection — ensuring we have enforceable ISDA netting, CSA collateral with daily margin calls and low thresholds, and where possible, central clearing.

Third, wrong-way risk screening. At every new trade approval, I'd ask: in a stress scenario where this counterparty is weakening, does our exposure to them increase? If yes, we need to either collateralize more heavily, find a different counterparty, or adjust the CVA charge to reflect the correlation.

And at the portfolio level, I'd monitor single-counterparty concentration. No single counterparty should hold more than 5% of our net exposure. This is the Lehman lesson — when one counterparty fails, you need to survive without them."

"Walk me through the key operational controls you'd implement."

"The foundation is segregation of duties — the person who trades cannot reconcile their own positions. That's Barings, and it's non-negotiable.

On top of that: daily 3-way reconciliation between front office, back office, and the prime broker. If those three don't match, SOMETHING is wrong — and we find it before it becomes a problem.

For pricing: independent mark verification. We don't just take the PB's marks — we get independent sources. For Level 3 assets especially, where there's no observable market price, we need a pricing committee and potentially third-party valuations.

For technology: real-time P&L monitoring with automatic alerts. Knight Capital lost $440M in 45 minutes because nobody noticed the bleeding for 28 minutes. We need circuit breakers that halt trading automatically if daily P&L breaches a threshold — not alerts that require a human to notice and act.

And the 4-eye principle: every material decision — trade entry above a threshold, limit override, cash transfer — requires two approvals from different people."
