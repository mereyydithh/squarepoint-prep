PART 1: HOW COMMODITY MARKETS ACTUALLY WORK

Cash vs Physical vs Futures — The Three Layers

Layer What it is Who trades it Example
Physical (spot) Actual barrels, tonnes, bushels changing hands for near-term delivery Producers (Shell, Saudi Aramco), consumers (airlines, refiners), physical trading houses (Vitol, Trafigura, Glencore) A refiner buys 500,000 barrels of crude for delivery next week at the Cushing terminal
Cash / Forward OTC contracts for physical delivery at a negotiated future date, priced vs a benchmark Physical traders, large consumers, producers locking in prices An airline buys jet fuel forward for Q3 delivery tied to Platts assessment
Futures Exchange-traded standardized contracts for a specific commodity, quantity, delivery point, and date Financial investors, hedge funds, CTAs, banks, producers hedging, specs A fund buys 100 lots of Dec-26 Brent ICE futures

One-liner: Physical is the real stuff. Cash/forward is the OTC deal. Futures is the exchange-traded, standardized, margined version. Financial players mostly trade futures. Physical players trade all three and arbitrage the gaps between them.

Why this distinction matters:
- Futures prices CONVERGE to physical prices at expiry (otherwise there's free money). But BEFORE expiry, futures can trade at premiums or discounts to physical — this is the basis.
- A "paper" trader at a hedge fund sees futures prices. A physical trader at Vitol sees actual cargo economics — shipping costs, refinery margins, storage constraints. Sometimes they diverge, and the physical trader has an edge.
- As an RM at Squarepoint, you're almost certainly dealing with futures and swaps, not physical. But understanding the physical layer helps you understand WHY futures behave the way they do.

Price Discovery and Benchmarks

Commodities don't have a single "price" like a stock. They have a web of benchmarks:

  • Exchange settlement prices: ICE Brent, NYMEX WTI, COMEX Gold — official daily settlement from the exchange
  • Price reporting agencies (PRAs): Platts (S&P Global), Argus, ICIS — these agencies collect transaction data and publish reference prices (assessments) used to price physical contracts worldwide
  • Why Platts matters: Most physical oil contracts globally are priced as "Platts Dated Brent + premium/discount." If Platts assesses Dated Brent at $82, and your contract is Dated Brent +$0.50, you pay $82.50. The Platts window (4:00-4:30 PM London time) is when physical traders submit bids/offers that set this benchmark.

How These Benchmarks Relate

Dated Brent (physical) ←→ ICE Brent Futures (financial) ←→ WTI (US)
                                                          ←→ Dubai/Oman (Asia)
  • Brent-WTI spread: Reflects the Atlantic Basin supply/demand balance. Normally Brent trades $2-5 above WTI (more global demand, fewer logistical bottlenecks). In 2011, WTI collapsed to $20+ discount when Cushing was flooded with Canadian/shale crude and pipeline capacity was insufficient.
  • Brent-Dubai spread (EFS): Reflects the light-sweet vs heavy-sour differential and the relative supply balance between the Atlantic and Pacific basins.

Crude Oil Quality Matters

Not all oil is the same. Two key specifications:

Spec What it measures Impact
API gravity Density. Higher API = lighter crude. WTI ~39.6° (light). Arab Heavy ~27° (heavy). Light crude yields more valuable products (gasoline, diesel). Heavy crude yields more residual fuel oil. Light commands a premium.
Sulfur content "Sweet" (<0.5% sulfur) vs "Sour" (>0.5%). WTI is sweet. Most Middle East crude is sour. Sweet crude is cheaper to refine (less desulfurization needed). IMO 2020 shipping fuel regs increased the sweet premium.

Why this matters for risk: If a PM is trading the "Brent-Dubai spread," they're not just trading oil — they're trading the light-sweet vs heavy-sour quality differential, which is driven by refinery configurations, regulatory changes, and regional demand patterns.

WTI Delivery Mechanics and the -$37 Event

WTI futures expire to PHYSICAL delivery at Cushing, Oklahoma:
- Cushing is a landlocked storage hub with ~76 million barrels of capacity
- At expiry, if you're still long, you must take delivery of oil AT Cushing
- April 2020: COVID crushed demand → storage at Cushing was ~80% full → no one could accept delivery → sellers had to PAY buyers to take oil off their hands → WTI went to -$37.63/bbl
- This can NEVER happen to Brent futures because they're cash-settled — no physical delivery obligation

RM lesson: Always know whether a contract is physically or cash-settled, and monitor position sizes relative to open interest as expiry approaches. CME now has negative price functionality permanently.

PART 3: REFINED PRODUCTS — WHAT OIL BECOMES

A barrel of crude yields different products depending on the refinery configuration. This is the "crack spread."

ULSD (Ultra-Low Sulfur Diesel) Heating oil / diesel. Formerly called "Heating Oil" on NYMEX. NYMEX ULSD (HO) 42,000 gallons (1,000 barrels) Winter peak (heating demand). Also industrial demand (trucking, shipping).

Crack Spreads — The Refiner's Margin

One-liner: A crack spread is the difference between the price of refined products and crude oil — it's the refiner's gross profit margin.

Crack spread Formula What it tells you
Simple gasoline crack RBOB price − Crude price Margin on turning crude into gasoline
Simple diesel crack ULSD price − Crude price Margin on turning crude into diesel
3-2-1 crack (2 × RBOB + 1 × ULSD) − 3 × Crude oil, divided by 3 Approximate US Gulf Coast refiner margin (assumes 3 barrels crude → 2 barrels gasoline + 1 barrel diesel)
Gasoline-diesel spread RBOB − ULSD Relative value within the products complex

Why crack spreads matter:
- When cracks are wide: Refiners are printing money → they maximize crude throughput → increases crude demand → supports crude prices
- When cracks compress: Refiner margins squeezed → maintenance shutdowns (cut throughput) → reduces crude demand → weighs on crude
- Seasonal pattern: Cracks typically widen before driving season (gasoline demand) and before winter (heating oil demand)
- 2022 example: Diesel cracks exploded to $70+/bbl (normally $15-25) due to Russia sanctions removing refinery capacity + low diesel inventories. Signaled massive supply disruption.

PART 4: NATURAL GAS — REGIONAL MARKETS

Natural gas is NOT one global market. It's three separate regional markets connected by LNG:

US Natural Gas

Feature Detail
Benchmark Henry Hub (HH) — pipeline delivery point in Erath, Louisiana
Exchange NYMEX (CME Group)
Contract size 10,000 MMBtu (million British thermal units)
Pricing unit $/MMBtu
Current range ~$2-4/MMBtu (historically low due to shale revolution)
Key drivers Weather (heating/cooling degree days), storage injections/withdrawals (EIA Thursday report), LNG export volumes, associated gas production from shale oil drilling, power generation demand

Key US gas concepts:
- Basis differentials: Henry Hub is the benchmark, but actual gas prices vary by location. Permian Basin gas trades at deep discount to HH (associated gas with no pipeline capacity). Northeast gas (Algonquin) trades at massive premium in winter (heating demand, pipeline constraints).
- Storage report: EIA releases weekly storage data every Thursday at 10:30 AM ET. The market reacts to the deviation from consensus: a build of +80 Bcf when the market expected +90 Bcf is bullish (less gas injected than expected = supply tighter).
- Shoulder months: March-April and October-November — transition between heating and cooling seasons. Prices typically lowest here.
- Associated gas: Gas produced as a byproduct of oil drilling. When oil prices rise → more shale drilling → more associated gas produced → gas prices FALL even though energy demand is up. This decoupled US gas from oil prices.

US Gas Hubs and Delivery Locations

One-liner: The US gas market is a web of interconnected pipeline hubs — Henry Hub sets the benchmark, but every region has its own hub price that reflects local supply, demand, and pipeline capacity.

Henry Hub — the benchmark:
- Physical delivery point in Erath, Louisiana, where 13 interstate and intrastate pipelines interconnect
- Why it became the benchmark: massive pipeline connectivity means gas can flow in and out efficiently, making it a natural meeting point for supply and demand
- NYMEX NG futures settle to physical delivery at Henry Hub
- All other US locations price as a basis differential to HH (e.g., "SoCal Citygate at HH + $0.35")

Major US Gas Hubs:

Hub Location Key characteristic Typical basis to HH
Henry Hub (HH) Erath, Louisiana THE benchmark. 13 pipelines converge. Gulf Coast supply center. — (is the benchmark)
Waha West Texas (Permian Basin) Massive associated gas production from Permian oil drilling. Often oversupplied — pipelines can't move gas out fast enough. Deep discount (sometimes negative — producers PAY to get rid of gas)
Dominion South Appalachia (Marcellus/Utica) Major shale gas production hub. Cheap gas but constrained by pipeline takeaway to demand centers. Discount — reflects pipeline bottlenecks out of Appalachia
Algonquin Citygate New England delivery The demand endpoint for Northeast heating/power. Infamous for winter spikes when pipeline capacity is maxed out. Sharp premium in winter ($10-30+ vs HH in polar vortex events), near parity in summer
SoCal Citygate Southern California West Coast delivery for power generation. Vulnerable to pipeline outages (Aliso Canyon storage leak 2015). Moderate premium reflecting transport costs
Chicago Citygate Midwest Major demand center for heating + industrial. Well-connected by interstate pipelines. Small premium or parity
AECO Alberta, Canada Canadian gas. Connects via pipelines to US Midwest and West Coast. Often at discount to HH. Discount — long on supply, dependent on US export capacity
Transco Zone 6 (NY) New York City delivery Urban Northeast demand center. Extremely high winter premiums. Premium, especially winter
Katy Hub Southeast Texas Reflects Gulf Coast industrial and LNG export demand near Freeport and Sabine Pass LNG terminals. Near parity or slight premium

Why basis matters for risk:
- A PM trading "US natural gas" at Henry Hub is NOT exposed to the same price as the gas burning in a New England power plant in January. In the 2014 polar vortex, HH traded at $4.50/MMBtu while Algonquin Citygate hit $78/MMBtu — that's a 17x difference at the same moment in time.
- Basis risk example: If you hedge a New England power plant's gas cost using HH futures, you're exposed to the Algonquin-HH basis. If a winter storm hits and pipelines into New England are maxed, HH might move $0.20 while Algonquin moves $30. Your hedge covers almost nothing.
- Pipeline constraints are the driver: Most basis blowouts happen because gas physically can't get from where it's produced (Appalachia, Permian, Gulf Coast) to where it's consumed (New England, California) fast enough. Pipeline capacity isn't just infrastructure — it's a tradable constraint.

LNG export hubs (increasingly important):
- Sabine Pass (Cheniere, Louisiana) — first US LNG export terminal. 6 trains, ~4.2 Bcf/d capacity.
- Freeport LNG (Texas) — ~2 Bcf/d. Forced offline by fire Jun-Feb 2022/23, contributed to US gas price drop.
- Cameron, Calcasieu Pass, Corpus Christi — expanding US LNG capacity.
- When US LNG exports increase → more domestic gas demand → tightens HH → supports price. LNG export volumes are now a primary driver of HH pricing.
- Feed gas = the gas flowing into LNG terminals for liquefaction. Daily feed gas data is watched closely — a sudden drop signals operational issues.

US Power Markets

One-liner: Electricity can't be economically stored at scale, so power prices are set in real-time by the marginal cost of the generator needed to meet demand at that exact moment.

Market (ISO/RTO) Region Key feature
PJM Mid-Atlantic + Midwest (13 states) Largest US power market. Heavy coal/gas mix. Capacity market.
ERCOT Texas (isolated grid) NOT connected to the rest of the US. No capacity market → extreme price spikes. Feb 2021 freeze → prices hit $9,000/MWh (cap).
CAISO California Heavy renewables (solar). "Duck curve" — oversupply midday, sharp ramp at sunset.
NYISO New York Congested transmission. NYC prices can diverge massively from upstate.
ISO-NE New England Winter gas/power price spikes (pipeline constraints, competing heating demand).
MISO Midwest Wind-heavy. Low prices in windy periods.
SPP Central US (Plains) Very wind-heavy. Negative prices (generators pay to dispatch) when wind oversupplies.

How power prices work:
- Merit order / supply stack: Generators are dispatched from cheapest to most expensive. Renewables (zero marginal cost) → nuclear → coal → gas (CCGT) → gas (peaker). The marginal unit (last one needed to meet demand) sets the price for ALL power that hour.
- Heat rate: The efficiency of converting gas to electricity. Measured in MMBtu/MWh. A heat rate of 7 is efficient (combined cycle). A heat rate of 10+ is inefficient (peaker). Implied heat rate = power price / gas price — tells you whether it's profitable to burn gas to make electricity.
- Spark spread: Power price − (Gas price × Heat rate) = Generator's margin. If negative, it's uneconomic to run.
- Dark spread: Power price − (Coal price × Emission cost) = Coal generator's margin.
- Congestion: Transmission lines have limited capacity. When a cheap generator can't deliver power to a high-demand area because wires are full, the high-demand area pays more (locational marginal pricing / LMP).
- Capacity markets (PJM, ISO-NE, NYISO): Generators are paid just to be available, even if they don't run. Ensures reliability. ERCOT has NO capacity market, which is why Texas has both lower average prices and more extreme spikes.
- Renewable duck curve: In CAISO, midday solar floods the market → prices drop (sometimes negative) → at 5 PM, solar dies and everyone turns on AC → prices spike as gas peakers ramp. This 3-hour ramp is the most volatile part of the day.

Negative power prices: Yes, power prices can go negative. When wind or solar output exceeds demand plus transmission capacity, generators must PAY to stay connected (because they have contractual take-or-pay obligations or receive renewable subsidies per MWh that exceed the negative price penalty). This is increasingly common in ERCOT, CAISO, and SPP.

European Natural Gas

Feature Detail
Benchmark TTF (Title Transfer Facility) — virtual trading hub in the Netherlands
Exchange ICE Endex
Contract size 1 lot = 1 MWh per day for the delivery period
Pricing unit €/MWh (NOT $/MMBtu — you must convert to compare to HH)
Conversion 1 MMBtu ≈ 0.293 MWh. So $3/MMBtu ≈ €9.5/MWh at current FX (rough).
NBP UK's National Balancing Point — historically the benchmark, but TTF has overtaken it for European price discovery since ~2020

Key European gas concepts:
- TTF has become the global LNG benchmark. When the Europe 2022 energy crisis hit (Russia cut gas), TTF spiked from ~€30/MWh to €340/MWh. This repriced ALL global LNG because cargoes were diverted to Europe for the premium.
- NBP vs TTF: NBP is UK-specific (connected to the continent via interconnectors but separated by the channel). NBP trades at a spread to TTF reflecting UK storage levels, interconnector flows, and LNG terminal capacity.
- LNG import dependency: Europe pivoted from Russian pipeline gas (40% of supply pre-2022) to seaborne LNG. This means European gas prices are now linked to global LNG shipping economics: freight rates, liquefaction costs, and Asian LNG demand compete for the same cargoes.
- Storage: European gas storage (Gasunie, Storengy) follows a fill/draw cycle: inject April-October, withdraw October-March. EU now mandates 90% fill by November 1. Storage levels are THE anxiety metric for European gas prices.
- JKM (Japan-Korea Marker): Asian LNG spot benchmark, assessed by Platts. When JKM > TTF, LNG cargoes go to Asia. When TTF > JKM, cargoes come to Europe. This is the "LNG arbitrage."

Asian Natural Gas (LNG)

Feature Detail
Benchmark JKM (Platts Japan-Korea Marker)
Pricing Spot: JKM ($/MMBtu). Long-term contracts: historically oil-indexed (e.g., 14.85% × JCC + $0.50)
Major importers Japan, South Korea, China, India, Taiwan
Key feature Most LNG is sold under long-term contracts (10-20 years) with oil-linked pricing — NOT spot. But spot share is growing.

Oil-linked LNG pricing explained: Historically, Asian LNG buyers signed long-term contracts priced as a percentage of crude oil (JCC — Japan Customs-cleared Crude). A typical "slope" is 14-15%: if JCC oil = $80/bbl → LNG price = 0.145 × $80 = $11.60/MMBtu. This means Asian gas prices move with oil, not with Henry Hub — a completely different dynamic.

PART 5: BASE METALS — THE LME

London Metal Exchange (LME) — How It Works

The LME is unique:
- Open-outcry ring trading (still exists alongside electronic). The "ring" is where official prices are set.
- Daily prompt dates: Unlike other futures where you trade monthly contracts, the LME allows trading for any business day up to 3 months out, then monthly to 63 months. This gives extreme flexibility for hedging physical delivery.
- 3-month price is the benchmark. "LME Copper" usually means the 3-month forward price.
- Physical delivery via LME-registered warehouses worldwide. Metal stored in LME warehouses can be tendered for delivery.
- Warehouse queues: Historically, long waiting times to withdraw metal from LME warehouses created artificial tightness (Goldman's aluminum warehouse controversy in 2013).

Copper — "Doctor Copper" Deep Dive

One-liner: Copper goes into everything — wiring, plumbing, electronics, EVs, grid infrastructure — so its price is a real-time read on global industrial health.

Why copper matters disproportionately:
- China consumes 50%+ of global copper. When China stimulus is announced, copper rallies before GDP data confirms it.
- Green energy transition is structurally bullish: An EV uses 4x more copper than an ICE car. Offshore wind farms use 6-10 tonnes per MW. Grid upgrades for electrification are copper-intensive.
- Supply is constrained: Major mines (Escondida, Grasberg) are mature. New mine development takes 10-15 years from discovery to production. Grade decline is real (ore quality is deteriorating).
- Concentrate market: Most copper is mined as concentrate (30% copper), then smelted. Treatment/refining charges (TC/RCs) reflect the tightness between miners and smelters.

US copper futures: COMEX (CME Group). Contract size = 25,000 lbs (~11.3 tonnes). Priced in USD cents/lb. To convert: LME $/tonne ÷ 2,204.6 × 100 = COMEX cents/lb. (e.g., $9,000/tonne ≈ 408 c/lb)

The Nickel Squeeze of March 2022

The LME nickel market broke:
- Chinese tycoon Xiang Guangda (Tsingshan) was massively short nickel (hedging physical production)
- Russia-Ukraine war spiked nickel (Russia = 10% of global supply) → prices doubled in days
- Tsingshan faced margin calls it couldn't meet → forced buying → nickel spiked from $25K to $100K/tonne in 24 hours
- LME cancelled trades and suspended nickel trading for a week — unprecedented
- Destroyed LME's credibility as a venue (many participants moved hedging to CME)
- RM lesson: Short hedges on physical production can blow up if margin calls arrive faster than physical revenue. Liquidity in LME nickel has never fully recovered.

Gold — The Real Rate Relationship

Key insight: Gold is NOT an inflation hedge. Gold is a REAL RATE hedge. Higher real rates (nominal rate minus inflation) = lower gold. Lower real rates = higher gold.

Why? Gold has no yield. It just sits there. When real rates are high, you're giving up a meaningful return by holding gold instead of TIPS. When real rates are low or negative, there's no opportunity cost to holding gold — and it has no counterparty risk.

The formula that drives gold: Watch 10-year TIPS yield (real yield). When it falls, gold rises. When it rises, gold falls. This correlation was very strong 2006-2021. It temporarily broke in 2022-2024 due to massive central bank buying (China, Turkey, Poland buying gold regardless of rates).

Gold contract details:
- COMEX 100 oz contract: ~$250,000 notional at $2,500/oz. Initial margin ~$10,000 (~4%).
- London gold fix: LBMA Gold Price, set twice daily (10:30 AM and 3:00 PM London). This is the price used by central banks, ETFs, and physical contracts.
- Gold ETFs (GLD, IAU): Hold physical gold in vaults. When inflows increase, the ETF buys physical gold → supports price. When outflows occur, they sell → weighs on price. ETF flow data is a leading indicator.

PART 7: AGRICULTURE

Agricultural commodities are fundamentally different from energy and metals because supply is weather-dependent and seasonal — you can't decide to "produce more wheat" right now the way you can turn on an oil well.

Key US agriculture concepts:
- WASDE Report: USDA's World Agricultural Supply and Demand Estimates, released monthly. The single most market-moving ag report. Supply/demand revisions of even 1-2M tonnes can move prices 3-5%.
- Crush spread (soybeans): Soybean processors buy beans and crush them into meal + oil. Crush margin = (meal revenue + oil revenue) − bean cost. When crush margins are high, processors buy more beans → supports bean prices.
- Ethanol blend wall: ~40% of US corn goes to ethanol. The RFS mandate creates a floor under corn demand. But E10 (10% ethanol) is roughly the max blend for standard cars — beyond that, demand is political.
- Key growing season: April-June planting, July-August pollination/critical weather period, September-November harvest. A Midwest drought in July can spike corn/soy 30%+ in weeks.

PART 9: KEY SPREADS AND RELATIVE VALUE TRADES

These are the trades that commodity desks actually focus on — not outright direction:

Spread What it is What it tells you
Calendar spread (time spread) Same commodity, different delivery months (e.g., Jun WTI vs Dec WTI) Curve shape (contango/backwardation), storage economics, supply expectations
Crack spread Products vs crude (e.g., RBOB − WTI) Refining margin. Wide = refiner profit → runs at capacity → pulls crude
Spark spread Power price − (Gas price × Heat rate) Gas-fired power generation margin
Crush spread Soy products (meal + oil) − soybeans Soybean processing margin
Frac spread NGL price − gas price Whether it's economic to extract NGLs from gas
Brent-WTI Atlantic Basin crude supply balance US export capacity, Cushing storage, pipeline economics
Brent-Dubai (EFS) Atlantic vs Pacific basin balance, light-sweet vs heavy-sour differential Asian refinery demand, Middle East supply, arbitrage flows
JKM-TTF Asian vs European LNG price LNG cargo diversion direction
Location basis (gas) Regional hub vs Henry Hub (e.g., Algonquin vs HH) Pipeline capacity, local supply/demand, weather
Gold-Silver ratio Gold price / Silver price Risk appetite. Ratio >80 = stressed (silver underperforming). Ratio <65 = risk-on.

PART 11: INTERVIEW APPLICATION

If asked "Tell me about commodity markets":

"Commodity markets have a physical layer that financial markets don't — real barrels need to go somewhere, real tonnes need warehouse space. This creates unique dynamics.

In crude oil, there are two main benchmarks: WTI for US delivery at Cushing (physically settled) and Brent for the global market (cash-settled). The fact that WTI is physically settled is why it went negative in April 2020 — there was nowhere to put the oil. Brent can't go negative the same way.

Natural gas is regional, not global. Henry Hub in the US trades at $3/MMBtu while TTF in Europe traded at the equivalent of $60+ during the 2022 energy crisis. LNG is what connects these markets — cargoes flow to wherever the premium is highest.

I focus on the curve shape — contango versus backwardation tells you about storage economics and supply tightness. I'd flag to a PM if they're holding a large long in deep contango because the roll cost bleeds their return every month regardless of where spot goes.

For risk, the key questions are: position size relative to open interest (are we the market?), delivery obligations approaching expiry, and whether what looks like diversified commodity exposure is actually one correlated macro bet."
