ASSET CLASSES, INSTRUMENTS & PORTFOLIO CONSTRUCTION

Complete Reference for a Discretionary Risk Manager

PART 1: EQUITIES

1.1 The Asset Class

Equities represent ownership in a company. Equity markets are the most liquid and widely traded asset class globally.

Key indices:
- US: S&P 500, Nasdaq 100, Russell 2000, Dow Jones
- Europe: Euro Stoxx 50, FTSE 100, DAX, CAC 40
- Asia: Nikkei 225, Hang Seng, KOSPI, ASX 200
- EM: MSCI EM, Bovespa, Nifty 50

Key characteristics:
- Long-term risk premium: equities return ~7-10% annually over long horizons, compensating for volatility
- Fat-tailed distribution: equity returns are NOT normally distributed — crashes are more frequent and severe than a bell curve predicts
- Correlation to macro: equities are sensitive to growth expectations, earnings, rates, and risk appetite
- Highly liquid (large caps): SPY trades $30bn+/day. Single-name large caps $100M-1bn/day
- Illiquid (small caps, EM): Beware — a $500M position in a small cap takes weeks to exit

1.2 Instruments

Cash Equities (Stocks)

  • Buy/sell shares on exchange
  • Settlement: T+1 (US), T+2 (Europe)
  • Costs: Commission, exchange fees, stamp duty (UK: 0.5%)
  • Risk: Delta 1 (linear exposure to the stock price)
  • Dividend: Receive dividend if long, pay dividend if short
  • Short selling: Need to borrow shares from a lender (via prime broker). Borrow cost varies — some stocks are "hard to borrow" (10%+/year)

Equity Futures

  • Standardized contracts trading on exchanges (CME, Eurex, ICE)
  • Key contracts: ES (S&P 500, $50/point), NQ (Nasdaq, $20/point), RTY (Russell, $50/point), FESX (Euro Stoxx, €10/point)
  • Margin: ~5-12% of notional (much less than cash equity)
  • Use: Quick beta exposure, hedging, leverage. No need to borrow shares for synthetic short
  • Roll: Quarterly (Mar/Jun/Sep/Dec). Must roll before expiry.
  • Basis: Futures ≠ spot. Basis = futures price − spot index. Driven by cost of carry (rates − dividends)

$$F = S \times e^{(r-q)T}$$

Where $r$ = risk-free rate, $q$ = dividend yield, $T$ = time to expiry

Equity Options

  • Right (not obligation) to buy (call) or sell (put) a stock/index at a strike price by expiry
  • American style: Exercise any time (single stock options)
  • European style: Exercise only at expiry (index options — SPX)
  • Pricing: Black-Scholes (see Risk Reference), but real-world pricing uses vol surface (skew + term structure)
  • Key strategies:
Strategy Construction Use Risk Profile
Long call Buy call Bullish, limited risk Pay premium, unlimited upside
Long put Buy put Bearish or hedge Pay premium, profit if stock drops
Covered call Long stock + sell call Income, mild bullish Capped upside, still have downside
Protective put Long stock + buy put Insurance Pay premium for downside protection
Straddle Buy ATM call + ATM put Bet on volatility (either direction) Pay premium, profit from big moves
Strangle Buy OTM call + OTM put Cheaper vol bet Wider breakevens, less premium
Risk reversal Buy call + sell put (or vice versa) Directional, zero premium Synthetic long/short with skew exposure
Butterfly Buy 1 low strike, sell 2 middle, buy 1 high Bet price stays near middle Low cost, limited profit, pinning bet
Iron condor Sell OTM put + sell OTM call, buy further OTM wings Bet on low vol / range-bound Collect premium, limited risk
Calendar spread Sell near-dated, buy far-dated (same strike) Bet on term structure Profit if near vol collapses, far holds

Equity Swaps (Total Return Swaps — TRS)

  • OTC contract: one party pays the total return of an equity/index, the other pays a financing rate (SOFR + spread)
  • Use: Synthetic equity exposure without owning the shares. Avoids stamp duty, settlement, and sometimes disclosure requirements
  • Risk: Counterparty risk (OTC), financing rate risk, and the underlying equity risk
  • Regulatory note: Archegos blew up in 2021 using TRS to build massive concentrated positions without public disclosure. Regulators now require more transparency.

ETFs (Exchange-Traded Funds)

  • Baskets of securities trading like a single stock
  • Use in risk: Quick hedging instrument. Sell SPY to reduce equity beta instantly.
  • Key risk: ETF price can deviate from NAV in stress (flash crashes, illiquid underlying assets)
  • Leveraged ETFs: 2x, 3x exposure. Suffer from volatility drag over time. NOT the same as 2x/3x spot exposure over more than a day.

PART 2: FIXED INCOME / RATES

2.1 The Asset Class

Fixed income = debt instruments. Governments and corporations borrow money by issuing bonds. The bond market is MUCH larger than equities (~$130T global vs ~$100T equities).

Key concepts:
- Yield: The return you receive for holding the bond. Inversely related to price — when yields rise, bond prices fall
- Coupon: Periodic interest payment (fixed or floating)
- Maturity: When the principal is returned
- Yield curve: The plot of yields across different maturities (2Y, 5Y, 10Y, 30Y). The shape tells you about economic expectations

Yield curve shapes:
| Shape | What It Means |
|---|---|
| Normal (upward sloping) | Healthy economy, investors demand more yield for locking up money longer |
| Flat | Uncertainty, possible transition between regimes |
| Inverted (downward sloping) | Recession signal — market expects rate cuts, flight to long-end |
| Steep | Economic recovery expectations, or central bank anchoring short end low |

2.2 Instruments

Government Bonds

Instrument Country Details
US Treasuries (USTs) US T-bills (< 1Y), T-notes (2-10Y), T-bonds (10-30Y). Benchmark for global risk-free rate. Most liquid bond market on earth. ~$25T outstanding
Bunds Germany Benchmark for Eurozone. 2Y Schatz, 5Y Bobl, 10Y Bund, 30Y Bund
Gilts UK UK government bonds
JGBs Japan Japanese government bonds. BOJ yield curve control creates unique dynamics
TIPS US Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities. Principal adjusts with CPI. Real yield instrument.

Treasury Futures

  • Standardized contracts on CME for USTs
  • Key contracts:
  • ZT (2Y note), ZF (5Y note), ZN (10Y note), ZB (30Y bond), UB (ultra-long bond)
  • Each has a cheapest-to-deliver (CTD) bond that determines the DV01
  • DV01 per contract (approximate):
  • ZT: ~$38/bp, ZF: ~$47/bp, ZN: ~$65/bp, ZB: ~$130/bp
  • Use: Duration management, curve trades, hedging rate exposure

Interest Rate Swaps (IRS)

  • OTC contract: one party pays fixed, the other pays floating (SOFR, EURIBOR)
  • Key use: Managing rate exposure. Paying fixed = short rates. Receiving fixed = long rates.
  • Swap rate vs Treasury yield: The swap spread (swap rate − treasury yield) reflects credit/funding conditions

$$\text{PV}{\text{fixed leg}} = R \sum{i=1}^{n} \Delta_i \cdot D(t_i)$$
$$\text{PV}{\text{float leg}} = \sum{i=1}^{n} L_i \cdot \Delta_i \cdot D(t_i)$$

Where $R$ = fixed rate, $L_i$ = forward SOFR rates, $\Delta_i$ = day count fraction, $D(t_i)$ = discount factor

The swap rate $R$ is set so $\text{PV}{\text{fixed}} = \text{PV}{\text{float}}$ at inception.

Swaptions

  • Options on interest rate swaps
  • A payer swaption gives the right to enter a swap paying fixed (bet on rates rising)
  • A receiver swaption gives the right to enter a swap receiving fixed (bet on rates falling)
  • Priced using normal (Bachelier) model or SABR
  • Key risk: Rate vol. MOVE index measures UST implied vol.

Repo (Repurchase Agreements)

  • Short-term collateralized lending. You sell a bond and agree to buy it back tomorrow/next week at a slightly higher price. The difference is the repo rate.
  • Use: Funding long bond positions (borrow cash against bonds) or covering short positions (borrow bonds against cash)
  • Risk: Repo rate spikes (Sep 2019: repo rate spiked from 2% to 10% overnight). Counterparty risk.

Corporate Bonds

  • Debt issued by companies. Higher yield than government bonds to compensate for credit risk.
  • Investment grade (IG): BBB- and above. Relatively liquid, tight spreads.
  • High yield (HY): BB+ and below. Higher spreads, less liquid, higher default risk.
  • Spread: = Corporate yield − Treasury yield. Widens in stress, tightens in calm.
  • Risk: Credit risk (default), spread risk (widening), rate risk (duration), liquidity risk (corporate bonds are much less liquid than govies)

Money Market

  • Short-term instruments: T-bills, commercial paper, certificates of deposit, repo
  • Very low risk, very low return. Used for cash management and funding.

PART 3: FOREIGN EXCHANGE (FX)

3.1 The Asset Class

FX is the largest market in the world: ~$7.5 trillion/day in volume. It's the plumbing of global finance — every cross-border transaction involves FX.

Key currency pairs:
| Pair | Name | What Drives It |
|---|---|---|
| EUR/USD | "Euro" | ECB vs Fed policy, Eurozone growth, trade balance |
| USD/JPY | "Dollar-Yen" | BOJ policy, carry trade, risk appetite |
| GBP/USD | "Cable" | BOE policy, UK political risk, Brexit legacy |
| USD/CHF | "Swissy" | Safe haven flows, SNB intervention |
| AUD/USD | "Aussie" | China demand, commodities, risk appetite |
| USD/CAD | "Loonie" | Oil prices, Fed vs BOC |
| USD/CNH | "Offshore Yuan" | PBOC policy, trade tensions, capital controls |
| NZD/USD | "Kiwi" | Dairy prices, RBNZ, risk sentiment |

DM vs EM:
- Developed Market (DM): G10 currencies. High liquidity, free floating primarily. Spreads in fractions of a pip.
- Emerging Market (EM): BRL, MXN, TRY, ZAR, INR, IDR. Less liquid, subject to capital controls, central bank intervention, political risk. Spreads can be 10-100x wider than DM.

3.2 Instruments

FX Spot

  • Exchange one currency for another at today's rate. Settlement T+2.
  • Use: Immediate exposure. But also creates settlement risk (Herstatt risk — counterparty defaults between when you pay one leg and receive the other)

FX Forwards / NDFs

  • Agree to exchange currencies at a future date at a rate agreed today
  • Forward rate reflects the interest rate differential between the two currencies:

$$F = S \times \frac{1 + r_d \cdot T}{1 + r_f \cdot T}$$

or continuously compounded: $F = S \times e^{(r_d - r_f)T}$

Where $r_d$ = domestic rate, $r_f$ = foreign rate

  • NDF (Non-Deliverable Forward): For currencies with capital controls (CNY, INR, BRL). Cash settled in USD, no physical delivery of the restricted currency.
  • Use: Hedging future FX exposure, carry trade implementation

FX Swaps

  • Simultaneous spot + forward transaction (buy EUR/sell USD spot, sell EUR/buy USD in 3 months)
  • Use: Rolling FX hedges forward, funding in foreign currency
  • Risk: Basis swap spread (the cost of swapping currencies beyond the interest rate differential) — went haywire during COVID

FX Options

  • Vanilla: calls/puts on currency pairs
  • Convention quirks: FX options are quoted in delta space (25D call, 25D put) not strike space. Vol is quoted in terms of:
  • ATM vol: Straddle (delta-neutral vol)
  • Risk reversal (RR): 25D call vol − 25D put vol (measures directional skew)
  • Butterfly (BF): Average of 25D wings − ATM vol (measures kurtosis, tail pricing)
  • Barrier options: Common in FX. Knock-in/knock-out at specific levels. Cheaper than vanilla but discontinuous payoff at the barrier = pin risk
  • Digital options: Pay a fixed amount if spot is above/below strike at expiry. Discontinuous payoff creates massive Gamma near strike at expiry.

Cross-Currency Basis Swaps

  • Swap floating rate in one currency for floating rate in another, including exchange of principal
  • The basis = deviation from covered interest rate parity. Should be zero in theory. In practice, it's not (funding stress, regulatory capital, etc.)
  • Use: Long-term FX funding, exploiting cross-currency basis dislocations

4.2 Key Commodity Concepts

The Futures Curve

Unlike equities, commodities have a term structure — prices for delivery at different future dates.

Contango: Forward price > spot price. Normal when storage costs exist and supply is ample.
$$F > S \implies \text{Contango}$$

Backwardation: Spot price > forward price. Happens when there's a supply shortage — buyers pay a premium for immediate delivery.
$$S > F \implies \text{Backwardation}$$

Cost of Carry Model

$$F = S \times e^{(r + u - y)T}$$

Where:
- $r$ = risk-free rate (financing cost)
- $u$ = storage cost (% of price)
- $y$ = convenience yield (the value of physically holding the commodity — high when supply is tight)
- If $r + u > y$: contango
- If $r + u < y$: backwardation

Roll Return

When futures contracts expire, positions must be rolled:
- In contango: Sell low (near), buy high (far) → negative roll return (costs money)
- In backwardation: Sell high (near), buy low (far) → positive roll return (earns money)

$$\text{Roll return} \approx \frac{F_{\text{near}} - F_{\text{far}}}{F_{\text{near}}} \times \frac{252}{\text{business days between contracts}}$$

Crack Spreads (Refining Margins)

The relationship between crude oil and refined products:

$$\text{3:2:1 Crack} = \frac{2 \times \text{Gasoline} + 1 \times \text{Heating Oil}}{3} - \text{Crude Oil}$$

This is the refiner's margin. Relevant because your BNP clients were corporates hedging refining exposure.

Spark Spread (Power Generation)

$$\text{Spark Spread} = \text{Power Price} - \frac{\text{Gas Price}}{\text{Heat Rate}}$$

The profit margin for a gas-fired power plant. Relevant for the fire drill (long gas / short power).

Basis

The difference between related commodity prices:
- Location basis: WTI (Cushing, OK) vs Brent (North Sea) vs Dubai
- Quality basis: Low-sulfur vs high-sulfur crude
- Timing basis: Spot vs specific forward month

4.3 Instruments

Commodity Futures

  • The dominant instrument. Standardized, exchange-traded, margin-based
  • Physical settlement: The contract ACTUALLY delivers the commodity (WTI delivers crude oil to Cushing). Most financial participants close before expiry
  • Cash settlement: No physical delivery, just cash difference (Brent ICE is cash-settled)
  • CFTC position limits: Speculative position limits to prevent market manipulation. Must be monitored.

Commodity Options

  • Options on commodity futures (not on spot, typically)
  • Asian options: Payoff based on AVERAGE price over a period (common in energy — month-average pricing for physical contracts)

$$\text{Payoff}{\text{Asian call}} = \max\left(\frac{1}{n}\sum{i=1}^n S_{t_i} - K, 0\right)$$

  • Spread options: Option on the spread between two commodities (e.g., crack spread option). Very common in energy.
  • Swing options / storage options: Give the holder the right to take varying amounts of a commodity (natural gas storage). Complex to value — Monte Carlo / dynamic programming required.

Commodity Swaps (OTC)

  • Fixed-for-floating: One party pays a fixed price, the other pays the floating market price. Used by producers to lock in revenue and by consumers to lock in costs
  • This is what you priced at BNP Paris for corporate clients (airlines hedging jet fuel, utilities hedging gas)
  • Basis swaps: Swap between two related commodities or two delivery locations

ETFs / ETNs

  • Commodity ETFs (GLD for gold, USO for oil, UNG for nat gas)
  • Warning: Commodity ETFs that hold futures (not physical) suffer from contango roll cost. USO lost 80%+ during COVID while oil spot was flat to higher long-term.

Crude Oil Grades — Not All Oil Is the Same

Oil varies on two key dimensions:

1. API Gravity (Density): Light vs Heavy
| Grade | API Gravity | What It Means |
|---|---|---|
| Light crude (WTI, Brent) | > 31° API | Easier to refine, produces more gasoline/jet fuel. Costs more. |
| Medium crude (Dubai, Arab Medium) | 22-31° API | Middle ground. |
| Heavy crude (Canadian oil sands, Venezuelan Merey) | < 22° API | Thick, tar-like. Hard to refine. Needs specialized "complex" refineries. Trades at a discount. |

2. Sulfur Content: Sweet vs Sour
| Grade | Sulfur Content | What It Means |
|---|---|---|
| Sweet crude (WTI, Brent, Nigerian Bonny Light) | < 0.5% sulfur | Less processing needed. Meets environmental standards more easily. Premium price. |
| Sour crude (Arab Heavy, Russian Urals, Canadian) | > 0.5% sulfur | Needs desulfurization at the refinery ("cracking" + "coking"). Discounted. |

Why this matters financially:
- The WTI-Brent spread reflects transport/quality/supply differences (WTI is landlocked at Cushing; Brent is seaborne)
- The light-heavy differential widens when complex refinery capacity is tight
- Sour crude discounts widen when environmental regulations tighten (IMO 2020 shipping fuel rules)
- Today: Iranian crude is medium-sour. The war has removed sour crude supply → sour discounts narrowing → refineries designed for sour crude are scrambling

Crack Spreads — Refining Margins in Detail

Spread Formula What It Represents
Simple 1:1 crack Gasoline price ($/bbl) − Crude price ($/bbl) Gasoline refining margin
3:2:1 crack [2 × Gasoline ($/bbl) + 1 × ULSD ($/bbl)] / 3 − Crude ($/bbl) US Gulf Coast refining proxy — "standard" benchmark
5:3:2 crack [3 × Gasoline + 2 × ULSD] / 5 − Crude More realistic US refinery yield
2:1:1 crack [1 × Gasoline + 1 × ULSD] / 2 − Crude European refinery proxy

Unit conversion for cracks: Gasoline is priced in cents/gallon (RBOB), crude in $/barrel. To compute the crack:

$$\text{Gasoline (per barrel)} = \text{Price in cents/gallon} \times 42 \div 100$$

So if RBOB = 300 ¢/gal → $300 × 42 / 100 = $126/bbl$

If WTI = $115/bbl → 1:1 gasoline crack = $126 − $115 = $11/bbl

Today's crack spreads are elevated because:
1. Crude supply constrained (Hormuz) → but refined product demand unchanged
2. Refineries near Gulf coast have been running at high utilization
3. Shortage of sour crude feedstock → some refineries can't run at full capacity

Natural Gas — A Completely Different Market

Concept Details
Pricing US: Henry Hub in $/MMBtu. Europe: TTF in €/MWh. Asia: JKM (Japan-Korea Marker) in $/MMBtu
Not globally fungible Unlike oil (ships everywhere), gas requires pipelines or LNG terminals. Regional prices can diverge wildly
LNG (Liquefied Natural Gas) Gas cooled to -162°C, shrinks to 1/600th volume, loaded on tankers. Connects markets but with ~$2-5/MMBtu of transport + liquefaction cost
Storage Critical seasonal factor. US gas storage (EIA weekly report every Thursday). Injections in summer (Apr-Oct), withdrawals in winter (Nov-Mar). Storage levels vs 5-year average = key signal
Heat rate Gas-to-power conversion: 1 MWh of electricity requires ~7-10 MMBtu of gas (depending on plant efficiency). Heat rate = MMBtu/MWh
Basis Henry Hub is the benchmark. Regional basis = local price − Henry Hub. Can be negative if pipeline capacity is constrained
Seasonality Massive. Winter = heating demand. Summer = power (air conditioning). Shoulder months = low vol. A polar vortex in January can move gas +30% in a day

Gold — Not Just a Metal

Gold trades as a financial asset, not a commodity:
- No industrial consumption (unlike copper, oil) — demand is investment, central banks, jewelry
- No storage degradation — an ounce of gold lasts forever
- Price driven by: real interest rates (inverse relationship), USD strength (inverse), central bank reserves, geopolitical fear
- Gold futures (COMEX) are one of the most liquid futures contracts in the world
- EFP (Exchange for Physical): mechanism to convert futures position into physical delivery at LBMA vaults in London
- LBMA (London Bullion Market Association): sets the "London fix" twice daily — the global benchmark price for physical gold

The Gold Forward Rate (GOFO): The rate at which you can borrow dollars by lending gold. When GOFO turns negative, it signals physical supply stress (people won't lend gold).

4.8 Physical vs Financial — Key Differences a Risk Manager Must Know

Dimension Financial Market Physical Market
Settlement Cash or roll before delivery Actual physical delivery to/from storage
Liquidity High (electronic, screen-traded) Lower (brokered, relationship-based, phone markets)

Example (right now): Brent crude financial contracts trade at $111/bbl. But physical cargoes of Middle Eastern sour crude are trading at much wider premiums to Dubai benchmark because refiners are competing for limited supply after Hormuz closure. The physical market is tighter than the financial market shows. A risk manager aware of this recognizes the financial price may still have upside.

PART 5: CREDIT

5.1 The Asset Class

Credit = the risk that a borrower doesn't repay their debt. Credit markets include corporate bonds, loans, and credit derivatives.

Key measures:
- Credit spread: Yield above the risk-free rate that compensates for default risk
- Rating: S&P/Moody's ratings indicate credit quality (AAA → D)
- Default rate: Historical average ~1-2%/year for IG, ~3-5%/year for HY (much higher in recessions)

Credit spread hierarchy:
| Rating | Spread Range (typical) | Example |
|---|---|---|
| AAA | 20-50bp | Johnson & Johnson, Microsoft |
| AA | 40-80bp | Large banks, strong corporates |
| A | 60-120bp | Most investment-grade corporates |
| BBB | 100-250bp | Riskiest IG — "BBB cliff" risk (downgrade to HY = forced selling) |
| BB | 200-400bp | "Fallen angels," better quality HY |
| B | 350-600bp | Leveraged companies, cyclical businesses |
| CCC and below | 600-2000bp+ | Distressed, near-default |

5.2 Instruments

Corporate Bonds

  • Covered in Rates section. Key additions:
  • Callable bonds: Issuer can redeem early. Creates negative convexity for the holder.
  • Convertible bonds: Can be converted to equity. Has equity option embedded — positive convexity.
  • Covenants: Legal restrictions on the issuer (max leverage, min coverage). Violation = event of default.

Credit Default Swaps (CDS)

  • Insurance against default. Buyer pays a quarterly premium (spread), seller pays par if the reference entity defaults.
  • CDS spread reflects the market's view of default probability

$$\text{Approximate default probability} = \frac{\text{CDS spread}}{\text{1 − Recovery Rate}}$$

Typical recovery rate assumption: 40% for senior unsecured.

  • So a 200bp CDS spread implies ~$\frac{0.02}{0.60}$ ≈ 3.3% annual default probability
  • Use in discretionary fund: Hedging credit risk, expressing credit views, relative value between credits

CDS Indices

Index Market Details
CDX IG US Investment Grade 125 names, 5Y
CDX HY US High Yield 100 names, 5Y
iTraxx Main European IG 125 names, 5Y
iTraxx Crossover European HY ~75 names, 5Y
  • More liquid than single-name CDS. Used for quick credit market hedging or beta exposure.

Leveraged Loans

  • Bank loans to sub-investment-grade companies. Floating rate, senior secured.
  • Less liquid than bonds. Trade in "secondary" market with settlement in T+7 to T+20.
  • Used by CLOs (Collateralized Loan Obligations).

PART 6: VOLATILITY AS AN ASSET CLASS

6.1 Why Vol Matters

Volatility is not just a Greek (Vega) — it's a tradeable asset class. Discretionary PMs often take explicit vol views.

VIX — The "Fear Index"
$$\text{VIX} = 100 \times \sqrt{\frac{2}{T}\sum_i \frac{\Delta K_i}{K_i^2}e^{rT}Q(K_i)}$$

In practice, VIX represents the 30-day expected annualized vol of S&P 500. Historical range: 10-80+.

VIX term structure:
- Contango (normal): Long-term VIX > short-term VIX. Market is calm.
- Backwardation (crisis): Short-term VIX > long-term VIX. Market is panicking NOW.
- The shape of the VIX term structure is itself a signal about market fear.

6.2 Vol Instruments

VIX Futures

  • Traded on CBOE. Cash-settled.
  • Problem: VIX futures ≠ VIX spot. They trade at a premium (in contango) that decays. Long VIX futures bleeds money in calm markets.
  • Lesson of 2018 (Volmageddon): XIV (inverse VIX ETN) was wiped out when VIX spiked from 13 to 50 in one day. $2B of value destroyed.

Variance Swaps

  • Payoff based on REALIZED variance vs a fixed strike (implied variance):

$$\text{Payoff} = \text{Notional}{\text{vega}} \times \frac{(\sigma{\text{realized}}^2 - K_{\text{var}}^2)}{2K_{\text{var}}}$$

Where $K_{\text{var}}$ is the variance strike (implied vol level) and $\sigma_{\text{realized}}$ is the actual realized vol.

  • Convexity: Variance swaps are convex in volatility — they pay out MORE per unit of vol increase than they lose per unit of vol decrease. This means long variance swap positions disproportionately benefit from vol spikes.
  • Use: Pure vol exposure without Delta risk. The "cleanest" vol trade.

Volatility Swaps

  • Similar to variance swaps but linear in vol (not convex):

$$\text{Payoff} = \text{Notional} \times (\sigma_{\text{realized}} - K_{\text{vol}})$$

  • Harder to hedge (no clean replication), so less liquid and wider bid-ask vs variance swaps.

Straddles / Strangles (Options)

  • Already covered in equities. The most common way to trade vol is buying/selling ATM straddles.
  • Gamma P&L = the realized vol component
  • Theta = the cost of holding the straddle
  • If realized vol > implied vol, the Gamma P&L exceeds Theta cost = long straddle is profitable

Dispersion Trading

  • Sell index vol, buy single-stock vol (or vice versa)
  • Profits from the difference between implied correlation and realized correlation
  • If implied correlation is high but stocks end up diverging: short index vol + long single-name vol wins

PART 7: PORTFOLIO CONSTRUCTION

7.1 The Principles

Portfolio construction is the art of combining individual trades into a portfolio that maximizes risk-adjusted return. For a discretionary fund risk manager, you're not constructing the portfolio yourself — the PMs are. But you're evaluating whether the COMBINED portfolio makes sense.

Core Questions You Ask:

  1. Is the risk budget efficiently allocated? Are PMs with the best risk-adjusted returns getting enough capital?
  2. Is there hidden concentration? Do individual PM books aggregate into unintended fund-level bets?
  3. Is the portfolio diversified? Not just by asset class — by factor, by geography, by time horizon.
  4. Is the leverage appropriate? Given current market vol, is gross exposure too high/low?
  5. Can we get out? If we need to liquidate 50% in a week, what's the cost?

7.2 Position Sizing

Volatility-Based Sizing

The most common approach in discretionary funds: size each trade so that it contributes equal RISK, not equal capital.

$$\text{Position size (contracts)} = \frac{\text{Target risk budget}}{\text{Instrument volatility} \times \text{Contract value}}$$

Example:
- Target risk per trade: $500K daily VaR
- Crude oil: 2% daily vol, contract value = $80,000 (1,000 barrels × $80)
- Position size = $500,000 / (0.02 × $80,000) = 312.5 contracts ≈ 312 contracts

Risk Parity

Allocate capital so each position/asset contributes EQUALLY to total portfolio risk:

$$\text{Risk contribution}_i = w_i \times \frac{(\Sigma w)_i}{\sigma_p}$$

Set all risk contributions equal → solve for weights.

Implication: Lower-vol assets get higher weight, higher-vol assets get lower weight. Bonds get more capital than equities in a risk parity framework.

Kelly Criterion (Reviewed)

Optimal bet size to maximize long-run wealth:

$$f^* = \frac{\mu}{\sigma^2}$$

Where $\mu$ = expected return, $\sigma^2$ = return variance.

In practice, use half-Kelly or quarter-Kelly — full Kelly has too much drawdown risk.

Maximum Position Limits

IC typically sets hard limits:
- Max single position: 5-10% of PM's allocation
- Max sector: 20-30% of PM's allocation
- Max single factor exposure: defined by factor model

7.3 Diversification — What Actually Diversifies

True Diversifiers (Low Correlation in Normal AND Stress Times)

Asset A True Diversifier Why
Equities Long-dated USTs (when inflation is controlled) Flight to quality — stocks crash, bonds rally
Equities Managed futures / trend following Historically makes money in equity bear markets
Equities Long vol (VIX calls, tail hedges) Vol and equities inversely correlated
Credit Rate duration Credit spreads widen when rates fall (recession expectations)
Commodities Equities (sometimes) Depends on regime — correlated in risk-on, uncorrelated in supply shocks

False Diversifiers (Correlations Converge in a Crisis)

What Looks Diversified What Actually Happens in Crisis
US equities + European equities Both crash together (correlation → 0.9+)
IG credit + equities Both sell off (risk-off)
Long gold + long AUD AUD is risk-on, gold is risk-off — in a crash, AUD drops, partially offsetting gold
HY bonds + equities HY bonds ARE equities with a coupon — correlation → 0.8+ in stress
Multiple PMs "doing different things" If they're all implicitly long growth/risk, they all lose together

Measuring Diversification

Diversification ratio:
$$DR = \frac{\sum_i w_i \sigma_i}{\sigma_p}$$

  • DR = 1 means no diversification (everything perfectly correlated)
  • DR = 2 means diversification halves the risk vs the sum of individual risks
  • Higher is better. But check STRESSED diversification ratio too.

Effective number of bets:
$$N_{\text{eff}} = \frac{1}{\sum_i p_i^2}$$

Where $p_i$ = proportion of total risk from position $i$. If one position is 80% of the risk, $N_{\text{eff}}$ ≈ 1.5 even if you have 20 positions. This number should be high.

7.4 Hedging — Constructing Protection

Hedge Ratio

$$h = \rho \times \frac{\sigma_{\text{position}}}{\sigma_{\text{hedge instrument}}}$$

Minimum variance hedge ratio: Minimize portfolio variance by choosing optimal hedge size.

Static vs Dynamic Hedging

Static Dynamic
What Put hedge on, leave it Continuously adjust the hedge
When Known, specific exposure Delta hedging options, changing portfolio
Cost Lower (trade once) Higher (continuous trading, slippage)
Risk Hedge decays as market moves Model risk, execution risk

Cost of Hedging

Hedging is NOT free. Common costs:
- Options premium: Buying puts for protection costs 1-3% of the portfolio per year
- Carry: Being short an asset in backwardation costs the roll yield
- Opportunity cost: Capital locked in hedges can't be used for alpha
- Negative carry: Shorting a high-yielding asset costs the yield differential

The Risk Manager's Role: Help PMs determine whether the hedge is worth the cost. Often the answer is to hedge PARTIALLY — reduce tail risk without eliminating all upside.

7.5 Portfolio-Level Risk Aggregation

Step 1: Normalize Exposures

Convert everything to a common risk unit:
- All positions → dollar Delta equivalent
- All positions → VaR contribution
- All option positions → include Gamma, Vega adjustments

Step 2: Aggregate Across PMs

  • Sum all PM exposures by: asset class, sector, geography, currency, factor, tenor
  • This is where you find hidden concentration: PM A long oil, PM B long energy stocks, PM C long CAD = fund is 3x overweight energy

Step 3: Run Fund-Level Risk Metrics

  • Total fund VaR (not just sum of individual — accounts for diversification)
  • Marginal VaR by PM: How much risk does each PM add to the fund?
  • Component VaR: What proportion of fund VaR is each PM responsible for?

Step 4: Stress Test the Aggregate

  • Run scenarios on the TOTAL portfolio
  • The result might be different from summing individual PM stress test results (because of cross-PM correlations and offsets)

Step 5: Present to IC

IC cares about:
1. Total fund risk vs risk budget: are we using 60% of our risk allocation or 95%?
2. Biggest risk concentrations: "The fund is effectively 2x levered to a rates sell-off"
3. Tail scenarios: "In a 2008-style event, the fund loses $X"
4. Recommendations: "PM A should trim their rates position" or "We need a portfolio-level tail hedge"

7.6 Risk Budgeting

How IC Allocates Risk

The total fund has a risk budget (e.g., total VaR of $20M, max drawdown of 5%). IC allocates this across PMs:

$$\sum_{i=1}^{n} \text{Risk Budget}_i \leq \text{Total Fund Risk Budget}$$

But because of diversification, you can allocate MORE risk in aggregate than the total budget:

$$\sum_{i=1}^{n} \text{VaR}_i > \text{Total Fund VaR}$$

The difference is the diversification benefit. IC manages this by:
1. Setting individual PM VaR limits
2. Monitoring fund-level VaR (which benefits from diversification)
3. Adjusting PM limits if diversification benefit shrinks (correlations rising)

Signal for IC to Act

  • If the diversification benefit drops from 40% to 15%: all PMs are betting on the same thing → need to reduce or diversify
  • If one PM is consuming 60% of fund VaR: concentration risk → rebalance

Your Role in These Scenarios

You run these BEFORE they happen. Every week, you update stress scenarios with the current portfolio. When IC asks "what if oil goes to $120 this month?" you should already have the answer ready. That's the difference between reactive and proactive risk management — and it's exactly the "risk is the art of being proactively ready" philosophy from your NYC mentor.
