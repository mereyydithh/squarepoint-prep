Risk Concepts

Q6. What is VaR? How do you compute it?

VaR is the maximum loss not exceeded at a given confidence level over a given horizon. 95% 1-day VaR of $1M means: on 95% of days, losses won't exceed $1M — but on the remaining 5% they will, and VaR says nothing about how bad those days are.

Three methods:
- Historical: sort past returns, take the 5th percentile directly. No distributional assumption.
- Parametric: assume normality, compute mean and std, apply z-score (1.645 for 95%).
- Monte Carlo: simulate thousands of paths, take the percentile of simulated outcomes.

Q7. What are the limitations of VaR?

  • Ignores everything beyond the threshold — tells you nothing about the size of tail losses
  • Historical VaR assumes the past represents the future — misses new risk regimes
  • Parametric VaR assumes normality — real returns are fat-tailed and skewed
  • Not sub-additive: portfolio VaR can exceed the sum of individual VaRs (not coherent)
  • Easily gamed: a strategy can show low VaR while taking on large tail risk
  • Lookback period matters enormously — a calm 2-year window gives false comfort

Q8. What is CVaR / Expected Shortfall? Why is it better?

CVaR is the average loss in the cases where VaR is breached — the expected value of the tail. If 95% VaR is -$1M, CVaR tells you: on the 5% of bad days, you lose on average $X. It's more informative because it characterises the shape of the tail, not just the threshold. It's also sub-additive (coherent). Limitation: still model-dependent and harder to backtest than VaR.

Q9. What is the difference between market risk and operational risk?

  • Market risk: losses from market price moves — equities down, rates up, vol spike, FX move. Driven by external market forces.
  • Operational risk: losses from failures in processes, systems, or people — wrong order entered, settlement failure, system outage, rogue trader. Driven by internal mechanics and human error.

Both are real and material — operational losses can be as large as market losses (e.g. Barings, Société Générale).

Q10. What is stress testing? How does it differ from VaR?

Stress testing applies predefined extreme shocks to a portfolio and measures the P&L impact. Unlike VaR, it's not probabilistic — it asks "what happens if X occurs" not "what's the 95th percentile loss."

Two types:
- Historical scenarios: GFC 2008, COVID March 2020, 1987 crash — replay observed market moves
- Hypothetical scenarios: US-China trade war escalation, Middle East escalation, mass QE unwind

VaR gives a daily probabilistic estimate under normal conditions. Stress testing prepares you for the extremes VaR can't capture, and informs liquidity planning, collateral requirements, and portfolio construction decisions.

Q11. What is a risk limit? How would you set one?

A risk limit is the maximum exposure a trader or desk is authorised to take. Limits cascade: firm-level → desk-level → individual trader. Types: notional (gross/net), VaR budget, delta limit, concentration limit (single-name or sector).

Setting a limit: start with the desk's capital allocation, determine the acceptable loss given the firm's risk appetite, back into the VaR or notional equivalent. For a new trader, start conservative — limits can be increased as the track record develops. Always set both a daily limit and a drawdown stop-loss level.

Q12. What is drawdown? How do you use it to monitor a strategy?

Drawdown is the percentage decline from a peak to a subsequent trough. Max drawdown is the largest such decline over a period. It captures sustained losses that VaR misses — a strategy can have low daily VaR but a catastrophic drawdown if losses accumulate over weeks.

In monitoring: track rolling drawdown on a daily basis. If a strategy is approaching its drawdown limit, investigate whether the strategy is behaving as designed or whether market regime has changed and the model is stale.

Q13. What is the Sharpe ratio? What's a good Sharpe?

Sharpe = annualised excess return / annualised volatility. It measures return per unit of risk. A Sharpe of 0.5–1.0 is acceptable, 1.0–2.0 is strong, above 2.0 is exceptional (or suspicious). Always assess Sharpe alongside max drawdown — a high Sharpe strategy that has large drawdowns is riskier than it appears.

Q14 & 15. What is correlation? Why does it matter in a portfolio?

Correlation measures how much two assets move together (-1 to +1). In a portfolio context, holding five assets doesn't mean five independent exposures — if three are highly correlated, the effective exposure is much smaller than it appears. This overstates diversification and understates concentration risk.

Critical point: in a crisis, correlations converge toward 1. Assets that appeared uncorrelated begin moving together — exactly when you need diversification most, it disappears. This is why stress tests must assume correlation breakdown.

Options & Greeks

Q16. What is delta? What does a delta of 0.6 mean?

Delta has two interpretations:
- Hedge ratio: for every $1 move in the underlying, the option value moves $0.60. To delta-hedge, you take an offsetting position of 0.60 shares per option.
- Moneyness proxy: roughly approximates the probability the option expires in-the-money. Strictly, the risk-neutral ITM probability is N(d2), but delta = N(d1) is commonly used as a quick approximation since they're close for short-dated ATM options.

Net portfolio delta = total dollar move of the portfolio per $1 move in spot. A risk manager monitors net delta to ensure the book is within its directional exposure limit.

Q17. What is gamma? Why does a risk manager care?

Gamma is the rate of change of delta — how much delta shifts when spot moves. It determines how quickly your hedge goes stale.

  • Long gamma: delta adjusts automatically as spot moves — you're naturally rehedged, but you pay for it via theta (time decay). Large moves benefit you.
  • Short gamma: you collect theta but are exposed to large sudden moves. If spot gaps, your delta hedge is wrong and you lose on both legs.

Risk managers care because short gamma positions can generate large losses in volatile markets — exactly when you least want it.

Q18. What is vega? How does a vol spike affect a long options book?

Vega is the dollar change in option value per 1 percentage point move in implied volatility. A long options book has positive vega — when vol spikes, option values increase, generating a profit. The risk manager monitors aggregate vega to understand the book's sensitivity to volatility regime changes. In the current environment (VIX ~30), vega exposure is a primary risk driver.

Q19. What is theta? Who benefits?

Theta is daily time decay — the amount an option loses in value per day, all else equal. Theta is highest (fastest decay) in the final 30 days before expiry, lowest for long-dated options. Option sellers (short premium) collect theta — it's the "rent" they earn for taking on gamma risk. Option buyers pay theta. A risk manager tracks net theta to understand the daily carry cost or gain of the book.

Q20. If a trader is long gamma, is that good or bad to monitor?

Long gamma is a cleaner risk profile to monitor — the position self-adjusts as spot moves, reducing the risk of runaway losses. The main risk is theta bleed (daily cost) and vega exposure. Short gamma is harder to monitor and more dangerous in crisis conditions — losses can accelerate non-linearly as spot moves. For a risk manager, short gamma positions require more frequent rehedge monitoring and tighter limits.

Scenarios & Judgment

Q21. A trader is at 90% of their daily VaR limit — what do you do?

Proactively reach out — don't wait for a breach. Understand what's driving the exposure: is it a single concentrated position or broad market move? Check what upcoming events (data, Fed, earnings) could push them through the limit. Flag to senior risk manager. Monitor intraday, don't wait for EOD reports. If they're adding risk, that's a conversation; if it's mark-to-market movement, assess whether it's transient.

Q22. A trader breaches their limit at market open — walk me through your response.

Market open breaches are often driven by overnight fills and early volatility — the mark may recover within minutes. Don't immediately force a position unwind. Steps:
1. Verify the breach is real, not a stale price or system error
2. Flag immediately to senior risk manager with full context
3. Speak to the trader — understand their view and intended action
4. Analyse what caused it: new position or market move?
5. Document everything — paper trail is non-negotiable
6. Monitor closely through the first hour; if still in breach by mid-morning, escalation required

Q23. Two strategies are highly correlated — why is that a problem?

One effective exposure, not two — the firm is taking more concentration risk than the limit structure implies. More critically, if one strategy is discretionary and the other systematic, an algo may react to a market move automatically, selling exactly when the discretionary trader wants to buy — compounding the loss. Crowded positioning across correlated strategies can create violent unwinds. At the portfolio level, you must adjust notional limits to reflect the true net exposure.

Q24. A model shows low VaR but the trader is taking large directional bets — what's happening?

Several possibilities:
- The VaR lookback window captures a calm period — the model is underestimating the current volatility regime
- The directional bets are in instruments or tenors not well captured by the model (illiquid, model blind spot)
- The trader has structured positions to appear hedged on the model's risk factors while retaining the actual directional exposure

Action: don't trust the model number. Go to the raw positions, stress test them manually against realistic scenarios, and present the discrepancy to senior risk management. The worst thing a risk manager can do is defer to a model showing green when the book looks obviously risky.

Q25. Markets gap down 5% at open — what's your first call?

  1. Pull live P&L across all discretionary books immediately
  2. Identify who has the largest net long delta exposure — they're most impacted
  3. Call those traders: do they have hedges in place? Are they intending to add or reduce?
  4. Calculate estimated P&L impact at current spot vs limit thresholds
  5. Escalate to senior risk management with a one-page summary: net delta, estimated loss, limit status, trader actions
  6. Monitor continuously — a 5% gap can reverse or extend; the first 30 minutes of trading is critical

Markets

Q26. What's your view on current market conditions?

The volatility regime has shifted — what was a low-vol environment in 2024 is now structurally higher. For a discretionary book, this means delta exposure is more expensive to hold, vega exposure is more valuable, and risk limits that were calibrated for a calm environment may need revisiting.

Q27. VIX at 30 — what does that mean for a discretionary options book?

Implied vol at 30 prices in approximately 1.9% daily moves in SPX. Options are expensive — buying protection costs more, selling premium collects more but with higher risk. A long options book is profitable on the vega position. The key risk manager question is whether the book's gamma and vega positions are sized for a sustained high-vol regime or were calibrated for a calmer environment.

Q28. How does a tariff shock affect equity vol?

Tariffs introduce earnings uncertainty — margins compress, supply chains reprice, and the growth outlook becomes less predictable. This raises the equity risk premium and pushes implied vol up even before actual earnings impact. Sector dispersion also increases (domestic vs. international earners behave differently), making correlation assumptions less reliable. For a discretionary book with cross-asset exposure, tariff risk can manifest through equities, FX, and commodity prices simultaneously.

Counterparty & Credit Risk

Q29. What is counterparty credit risk?

The risk that a counterparty to a contract defaults before final settlement, leaving you with an unrealised gain you can't collect. It applies to OTC derivatives (swaps, forwards, OTC options), repo, securities lending — anything where settlement is in the future and the contract has positive MTM in your favour. Listed/cleared products (futures, exchange options) push this risk to the central counterparty (CCP) via daily margining, which is why post-Dodd-Frank a lot of swaps were forced into clearing.

Q30. What is current exposure vs potential future exposure (PFE)?

  • Current Exposure (CE) = max(MTM, 0). The replacement cost today if the counterparty defaulted right now. Backward/present-looking.
  • Potential Future Exposure (PFE) = a high quantile (typically 95% or 99%) of the future exposure distribution at a given horizon, simulated forward. It answers "how bad could the exposure plausibly get before this trade matures?"

PFE is generated by Monte Carlo: simulate market factors forward, revalue the netting set at each step, take the quantile of positive MTM at each horizon. The PFE profile is a curve over time — usually peaks somewhere in the middle of a swap's life (diffusion has built up but amortisation hasn't dominated yet).

Q31. How are PFE, EPE, and EAD related?

  • PFE(t) = quantile of positive exposure at time t (tail of the distribution)
  • EE(t) = expected exposure at t = E[max(MTM_t, 0)]
  • EPE = average of EE(t) over the life of the trade (mean of the curve)
  • EAD (Exposure at Default, Basel) ≈ alpha × EEPE, where alpha is typically 1.4

PFE is what you use for setting counterparty limits and trade approval. EPE feeds CVA (credit valuation adjustment) — the price you charge for counterparty credit risk in the trade.

Q32. How do you mitigate counterparty risk?

  • Netting (ISDA Master Agreement): aggregate all trades with one counterparty into a single net exposure rather than gross
  • Collateral (CSA): post variation margin daily based on MTM moves; initial margin for residual gap risk
  • Central clearing: novate the trade to a CCP (LCH, CME); the CCP becomes counterparty to both sides
  • Credit limits: per-counterparty PFE caps, monitored pre-trade
  • Diversification: don't concentrate exposure to one bank
  • CDS hedging: buy protection on names you have large exposure to (less common buy-side, more common bank treasury)

Q33. What is wrong-way risk?

When your exposure to a counterparty increases at the same time the counterparty's credit quality deteriorates — the two are positively correlated. Classic example: buying CDS protection on Italy from an Italian bank. If Italy's sovereign credit blows out, your protection is worth more (bigger exposure) but the bank is also more likely to default. Or: Russian bank counterparty on a long-RUB FX forward in 2022 — RUB collapsed, your MTM gain on the trade went up while the bank's solvency simultaneously deteriorated. Specific wrong-way risk is usually banned at the trade-approval stage.

Option Strategies

Q34. Walk me through a covered call.

Long stock + short call (typically OTM). You collect the call premium, capping upside above the strike. Used by long-only managers to enhance yield on holdings they're comfortable being called away. Risk profile: keeps full downside, gives up upside above strike. Net delta is below 1 (long 100 shares + short ~30-40 delta call = ~60-70 net delta).

Q35. Protective put?

Long stock + long put (usually OTM). The put acts as insurance: floors your downside at the strike, costs you the premium. Equivalent payoff to a long call (put-call parity). Used to hedge a concentrated long position when you don't want to sell (tax reasons, conviction, mandate). The cost is the drag — buying puts continuously is expensive in steep contango vol surfaces.

Q36. What's a vertical spread? When would you use it?

Buy one option, sell another of the same type and expiry, different strikes.
- Bull call spread: long lower-strike call, short higher-strike call. Bullish, cheaper than outright call, capped upside.
- Bear put spread: long higher-strike put, short lower-strike put. Bearish, capped downside.

Used when you have a directional view but want to cheapen the trade or define max loss. You give up the tail upside in exchange for a lower premium and known risk.

Q37. Straddle vs strangle?

  • Straddle: long ATM call + long ATM put (same strike, same expiry). Bet on a large move in either direction. Expensive (two ATMs).
  • Strangle: long OTM call + long OTM put (different strikes). Cheaper, but needs a bigger move to pay off.

Both are long vol / long gamma trades — used ahead of binary events (earnings, central bank, election) when you expect a move but don't know the direction. Risk: theta bleed. If the event passes without a move, IV crushes and both legs lose.

Q38. What's a butterfly? An iron condor?

  • Butterfly: e.g. long 1 K1 call + short 2 K2 calls + long 1 K3 call (K1 < K2 < K3, equal spacing). Max profit if spot pins exactly at K2 at expiry. Defined risk, defined reward, cheap. Bet on low realised vol or pinning.
  • Iron condor: short OTM put spread + short OTM call spread. Profit if spot stays in the wings. Short vol trade with defined max loss. Common for premium-collection strategies in low-vol regimes.

Q39. Risk reversal? Collar?

  • Risk reversal: long OTM call + short OTM put (or reverse). Synthetic forward exposure with skew bet baked in. The skew of the surface determines whether you pay or collect premium. Standard FX trade for expressing directional view.
  • Collar: long stock + long OTM put + short OTM call. Floors the downside, caps the upside. Often structured zero-cost (call premium funds the put). Used by executives with concentrated stock positions to lock in a band.

Q40. Calendar spread?

Long a longer-dated option + short a shorter-dated option, same strike. Profits from the front-month decaying faster than the back-month. Long vega, near-zero net delta at inception. Used to express a view on the term structure of vol — e.g., front-month is overpriced relative to back-month around a known event.

Products & Instruments

Q41. What instruments would you expect to see in a discretionary cross-asset book?

  • Equities: cash equities (single names, ETFs, ADRs), index futures (ES, NQ, EuroStoxx, Nikkei)
  • Listed options: index options (SPX, SX5E), single-name options
  • Rates: government bonds (USTs, Bunds, OATs, JGBs), bond futures (ZN, ZB, RX), interest rate swaps, swaptions, OIS/SOFR
  • FX: spot, forwards, NDFs (for EM), FX options
  • Credit: corporate bonds (IG, HY), CDS single-name and indices (CDX IG, iTraxx Main)
  • Commodities: futures (WTI, Brent, gold, copper, nat gas), commodity options
  • Volatility: VIX futures and options, variance swaps

A risk manager needs to map every position to its primary risk factors (delta, DV01, vega, CS01, FX delta) and aggregate consistently across asset classes.

Q42. What's the difference between a future and a forward?

  • Future: exchange-traded, standardised, daily margined (no counterparty risk via CCP), liquid, cash or physical settlement.
  • Forward: OTC, customisable (size, date, underlying), no daily margining → counterparty risk accumulates, less liquid, typically physical settlement.

Pricing under no-arbitrage is the same: F = S × e^((r-q)T). But forwards and futures can diverge slightly when rates are stochastic and correlated with the underlying (futures convexity adjustment).

Q43. How does a swap work? What's an interest rate swap?

A swap is an agreement to exchange cash flows. Vanilla IRS: one party pays fixed, the other pays floating (SOFR, EURIBOR), on a notional that's never exchanged. The PV at inception is zero (par swap rate is set so both legs are equal). Over time, as rates move, the swap accrues MTM. DV01 of a 5Y receiver swap with $100M notional ≈ $50K. Swaps are now mostly cleared (LCH SwapClear) post-Dodd-Frank, removing bilateral counterparty risk.

Q44. Bond futures — what's the cheapest-to-deliver?

Bond futures (ZN, ZB, RX) are physically settled against a basket of deliverable bonds. The short can choose which bond to deliver, and rationally picks the one that minimises their cost — the cheapest-to-deliver (CTD). The CTD has the highest implied repo rate among deliverables. The future tracks the CTD's price, scaled by a conversion factor. Risk manager point: a CTD switch can cause the future's effective duration to jump, so DV01 isn't static — it depends on which bond is currently CTD.

Q45. What's a CDS? How does it work?

Credit Default Swap. Protection buyer pays a periodic premium (the spread, in bps of notional). Protection seller pays par minus recovery if a credit event (bankruptcy, failure to pay, restructuring) occurs on the reference entity. Settled via auction (cash settlement) or physical delivery of a defaulted bond.

Example: 5Y CDS on company X trades at 200bps. On $10M notional, buyer pays $200K/year. If X defaults with 40% recovery, buyer collects $10M × (1 - 0.40) = $6M.

CDS indices (CDX.IG, iTraxx Main) are baskets of 125 names — used for macro credit views, much more liquid than single-name CDS.

Q46. ETF vs index future — when use which?

  • Index future (e.g. ES): high leverage (~5% margin), low transaction cost, no expense ratio, but expires quarterly → roll cost (contango drag), no dividend stream (dividends are priced into the future)
  • ETF (e.g. SPY): no expiry, dividends paid through, smaller minimum size, but full notional capital required, expense ratio, slight tracking error

Discretionary PMs: futures for tactical exposure with leverage, ETFs for buy-and-hold or smaller allocations. A risk manager treats both as delta-1 equity exposure but tracks them separately for liquidity and roll risk.

Q47. What's a variance swap?

A swap that pays the difference between realised variance over a period and a strike variance, multiplied by a notional. Pure exposure to realised vol — no path dependency, no daily delta hedging required (unlike a vol-traded straddle). Long var swap = long realised vol. Used by vol traders to express clean vol views without strike or skew exposure. Replication uses a strip of OTM puts and calls weighted 1/K² — which is why the VIX index methodology looks the way it does.

Volatility Surface

Q48. What is the volatility smile / skew?

Empirically, implied vol is not constant across strikes (as Black-Scholes assumes) — it varies. In equity indices, OTM puts trade at higher IV than OTM calls (a downward-sloping curve in strike) — that's "skew," priced in since the 1987 crash. In FX and single-name equities you see a more symmetric "smile." The shape of the surface tells you what the market is pricing for tail risk. Risk managers monitor skew because a steepening skew often precedes vol spikes — it's a market-implied stress signal.

Q49. What's the term structure of vol? Contango vs backwardation in vol?

The IV curve across maturities at fixed moneyness (usually ATM).
- Contango (upward-sloping): long-dated vol > short-dated vol. The "normal" regime — calm now, more uncertainty later. VIX futures usually in contango → systematic short-vol strategies earn the roll.
- Backwardation (downward-sloping): short-dated vol > long-dated. Stress regime — market expects the storm to pass. Happens during crises (March 2020, Aug 2024).

Inversion of the VIX curve is a real-time stress indicator.

Q50. What is realised vs implied vol? When does the spread matter?

  • Realised vol: backward-looking, computed from historical price moves (typically annualised stdev of log returns).
  • Implied vol: forward-looking, extracted from option prices.

The spread (IV − RV) is the variance risk premium — what option sellers earn on average for taking on tail risk. Persistently positive (option buyers overpay for protection). When IV << RV, options are cheap relative to actual market chop — buying gamma is attractive. Risk managers track this because a book that's systematically short vol is collecting the premium but exposed to the tail.

Q51. What's vanna and volga? Why do they matter?

Second-order Greeks:
- Vanna = ∂Delta/∂Vol = ∂Vega/∂Spot. How delta moves when vol changes (or vega when spot changes). Matters for skewed positions — a vol spike shifts your delta.
- Volga (vomma) = ∂Vega/∂Vol. Convexity of vega. OTM options have high volga — they gain value disproportionately in a vol spike.

A risk manager monitoring an exotic or barrier book needs vanna/volga because BS Greeks alone don't capture how the position behaves through a stress event.

Liquidity & Funding

Q52. Market liquidity vs funding liquidity — what's the difference?

  • Market liquidity: ability to exit a position without moving the price against you. Measured by bid-ask spread, depth, ADV.
  • Funding liquidity: ability to finance positions — access to cash, repo, prime brokerage. Margin calls, haircut increases, withdrawal of credit lines.

The two are linked: a funding liquidity squeeze (e.g., LTCM 1998, Bear Stearns 2008) forces fire-sales, which destroys market liquidity, which forces more sales — the doom loop. A risk manager has to monitor both: position-level liquidity (how fast can I exit) and firm-level funding (can I survive a margin call).

Q53. What is initial margin vs variation margin?

  • Variation margin (VM): daily MTM movement. If you lose $1M today, you post $1M cash to the counterparty/CCP overnight. Settles the realised exposure.
  • Initial margin (IM): a buffer posted upfront to cover potential losses between the last VM settlement and the close-out of the position if you default. Sized as a quantile (e.g., 99% 5-day VaR for cleared swaps; SIMM for uncleared).

Risk implication: a vol spike inflates IM requirements (margin call) even if you haven't lost money — that's a funding shock. Archegos partly blew up because IM doubled across multiple prime brokers simultaneously.

Q54. What is repo? Why does it matter for risk?

Repo = sale of a security with agreement to buy it back later at a higher price. Economically, a collateralised loan: cash for bonds for a few days. The "haircut" is the lender's protection — lend $98 against $100 of collateral.

Why it matters: hedge funds finance long bond positions via repo. If repo rates spike, or haircuts widen, the levered bond carry trade implodes (basis trade in March 2020, again in 2023). The September 2019 repo spike (rates to 10%) was a flash funding crisis with no obvious trigger — this is why the Fed runs the standing repo facility now.

Q55. What's the basis trade and why is it a systemic risk concern?

Hedge funds buy cash Treasuries, sell Treasury futures, and capture the basis (cash trades cheap to futures because futures embed financing). Levered ~50-100x via repo. Profitable in normal times — tiny spread × massive leverage. In March 2020, when futures sold off and repo seized up, funds were forced to unwind, dumping cash Treasuries → Treasury market broke → Fed had to intervene with $1T+ in purchases. SEC and Fed are now scrutinising it. For a discretionary book, the lesson: levered convergence trades can destroy you in a liquidity event even if the trade is "right."

P&L Attribution & Validation

Q56. What is P&L attribution? Why does a risk manager care?

Decomposing daily P&L into the contributions from each risk factor:
- Delta P&L: spot move × delta
- Gamma P&L: ½ × gamma × (spot move)²
- Vega P&L: vol move × vega
- Theta: -theta × dt
- Carry/financing: rates accrual
- Residual: unexplained

A clean attribution should leave a small residual. A large residual means either the model is wrong, the Greeks are stale, or there's a position you don't know about. P&L attribution is a daily sanity check that the risk view of the book matches the actual P&L — a key control on rogue trading and model error.

Q57. What is VaR backtesting?

Compare ex-ante VaR forecasts to actual realised P&L. For 95% 1-day VaR, you expect ~5% of days to breach (12-13 days/year). Count the breaches:
- Too few → VaR is too conservative (capital inefficient)
- Too many → model underestimates risk

Formal tests: Kupiec POF (number of breaches), Christoffersen (independence — breaches shouldn't cluster). Basel "traffic light": green (≤4 breaches/year), yellow (5-9), red (10+) → capital multiplier increases. A red zone forces model review and regulatory escalation.

Q58. What's a model risk? How do you manage it?

Model risk = the risk that a model produces wrong answers and drives bad decisions. Sources: wrong assumptions (normality), bad calibration (stale data), implementation bugs, misuse outside the model's domain.

Management:
- Independent validation: a separate team revalues using a different model
- Backtesting: compare model output to realised
- Benchmarking: compare to industry standard or alternative model
- Reserves: hold capital against model uncertainty
- Use limits: cap exposure to instruments where model confidence is low

The 2012 JP Morgan "London Whale" loss involved a VaR model swap that halved reported risk — classic model governance failure.

Math & Stats

Q59. Why annualise vol by √T (square root of time)?

Under Brownian motion, returns are independent and identically distributed, so variance scales linearly with time: Var(r_T) = T × Var(r_1). Standard deviation scales with √T. So daily vol × √252 ≈ annual vol.

Caveat: assumes IID returns — breaks down with autocorrelation (momentum/mean-reversion) or fat tails. In practice the √T rule is a useful approximation but not exact.

Q60. If returns are normal, what's the probability of a 3-sigma move?

About 0.27% (two-tailed) — roughly once every 370 trading days, ~1.5x per year. A 4-sigma move: 0.006%, once every ~16,000 days, ~63 years. A 5-sigma: once in ~3.5 million days.

Punchline: real markets see "5-sigma events" several times per decade. Returns are NOT normal — they have fat tails. Risk managers know that the normal distribution underestimates tail probabilities by orders of magnitude, which is why historical and Monte Carlo VaR with realistic distributions matter.

Q61. Two assets each with vol 20%, correlation 0.5, equal-weighted — portfolio vol?

σ²_p = w₁²σ₁² + w₂²σ₂² + 2 w₁ w₂ ρ σ₁ σ₂
= 0.25(0.04) + 0.25(0.04) + 2(0.5)(0.5)(0.5)(0.2)(0.2)
= 0.01 + 0.01 + 0.01 = 0.03
σ_p = √0.03 ≈ 17.3%

If correlation were 1: portfolio vol = 20% (no diversification). If 0: ~14.1%. If -1: 0%. The diversification benefit comes entirely from correlation < 1.

Q62. What's a Brownian motion? Why use it for asset prices?

Continuous-time random process with: starts at 0, independent increments, normally distributed increments with variance proportional to time, continuous paths. Geometric Brownian Motion (GBM) for prices: dS/S = μ dt + σ dW. Used because:
- Tractable (closed-form solutions like Black-Scholes)
- Captures drift + volatility cleanly
- Log-normal prices ⇒ no negative prices

Limitations: constant vol (no smile), normal log returns (no jumps), continuous paths (no gaps). Real markets jump and have stochastic vol — modern models (Heston, SABR, jump-diffusion) extend GBM to address these.

Markets & Microstructure

Q63. What's the bid-ask spread? What drives it?

Spread = ask − bid. The compensation a market maker requires to take the other side of your trade. Drivers:
- Adverse selection: risk that you're informed → spread widens
- Inventory risk: market maker holds the position until they offset → vol drives spread
- Order processing cost: fixed minimum
- Competition: more market makers → tighter spreads

In stress, spreads explode (March 2020 Treasury market: 10x normal). For a risk manager, position sizing must account for execution cost in stress, not just normal-day spread.

Q64. What is market impact? How do you size around it?

Market impact = the price move you cause by trading. Square-root law (empirical): impact ≈ k × σ × √(volume / ADV). Trading 10% of ADV in a name with 30% vol moves the price ~10%×30%/√10 ≈ 1% against you. Implications:
- Large positions can't be exited at "screen price"
- Liquidation cost is a separate risk, not captured by market vol
- Liquidity-adjusted VaR adds an exit-cost component

Squarepoint runs systematic strategies — market impact modelling is core to their alpha capture.

Q65. What is slippage?

Difference between expected execution price and actual fill. Sources: bid-ask spread, market impact, latency, adverse selection on resting orders. A risk manager looks at slippage as a hidden cost that erodes strategy P&L over time — strategies that look profitable on paper can be unprofitable after realistic execution costs.

Factor Models & Beta

Q66. What is beta? How do you compute it?

Beta = Cov(r_asset, r_market) / Var(r_market). Slope of the regression of asset returns on market returns. Interpretation:
- β = 1: moves with the market
- β > 1: amplifies market moves (high-beta stocks: tech, cyclicals)
- β < 1: dampens (utilities, staples)
- β < 0: inverse (gold sometimes, VIX always)

Risk manager use: a "market-neutral" book should have aggregate beta near zero. If beta drifts, the book has unintended directional exposure.

Q67. What's a factor model? Name the standard ones.

A model decomposing asset returns into systematic factor exposures + idiosyncratic. r_i = α_i + Σ β_ij × F_j + ε_i.

  • CAPM (1-factor): market only
  • Fama-French 3-factor: market + size (SMB) + value (HML)
  • Carhart 4-factor: + momentum
  • Fama-French 5-factor: + profitability + investment
  • Barra-style risk models: 50+ factors (industry, country, style)

For a discretionary equity book, factor decomposition tells you: how much of P&L is alpha vs beta exposure? A trader who claims alpha but has 80% of returns explained by momentum factor isn't picking stocks, they're riding a factor.

Q68. What is PCA in a risk context?

Principal Component Analysis: decompose the covariance matrix of returns into orthogonal components ranked by variance explained. For a yield curve, typically:
- PC1 (~80-90%): level shift (parallel)
- PC2 (~5-10%): slope (steepening/flattening)
- PC3 (~1-5%): curvature (butterfly)

Used to: reduce dimensionality (hedge with 2-3 instruments instead of 10), build factor-based risk limits, identify regime changes (eigenvalue distribution shifts in crisis).

Backtesting & Strategy Validation

Q69. What biases ruin a backtest?

  • Look-ahead bias: using info not available at the time (e.g., today's revised earnings to trade yesterday)
  • Survivorship bias: testing only on companies still listed today (delisted = bankrupt = your strategy "avoided" them by accident)
  • Data snooping / p-hacking: testing 1000 strategies, picking the best — guaranteed to find spurious "alpha"
  • Overfitting: too many parameters relative to sample size — fits noise, not signal
  • In-sample vs OOS leakage: tuning on the test set
  • Transaction cost omission: ignoring slippage, market impact, financing
  • Regime bias: strategy backtested only in bull markets

A risk manager validating a new strategy demands an out-of-sample test with realistic costs and an explanation of why the strategy should work (economic rationale), not just "the numbers look good."

Q70. What's overfitting? How do you detect it?

A model that captures noise rather than signal — high in-sample fit, poor out-of-sample. Detection:
- Train/test split with degraded test performance
- Cross-validation
- Penalised regression (Lasso, Ridge) — punish large coefficients
- Number of parameters vs sample size (rule of thumb: <1 parameter per 10 obs)
- Robustness checks: small data perturbation should not change conclusions

Every quant strategy is at risk. Overfit risk in a backtest = model risk in production. Risk managers ask: "show me the equity curve on data the model has never seen."

Hedging Strategies & Cost

Q71. How do you hedge a long equity book?

Options ordered by cost and precision:
- Index futures short (ES, SX5E): cheapest, removes broad market beta. Residual = stock-specific risk + factor tilt.
- Sector ETF shorts: hedges sector concentration if your longs are clustered
- Single-name shorts (pair trade): if you have idiosyncratic conviction
- Index put options: defined cost, kicks in only on downside, leaves upside. Premium drag in calm markets.
- VIX calls: convex payoff in vol spikes — but VIX futures are in contango most of the time → bleed
- Tail hedge funds / structured products: outsource the hedge, pay an explicit fee

Risk manager perspective: every hedge has a cost (spread, theta, basis risk). The right hedge depends on what risk you're trying to remove and at what price.

Q72. What is a tail hedge? Pros and cons of long puts vs long VIX?

Tail hedge = position designed to pay off in extreme downside, not normal moves.
- Long OTM SPX puts: precise downside payoff, expensive theta bleed if nothing happens. Cost ~1-2% of notional/year for 25-delta puts.
- Long VIX calls or futures: convex payoff in vol spikes, but VIX futures in persistent contango → roll cost ~5-15%/year. You pay for the optionality.
- Long gold / long Treasuries: cheap "soft" hedges that work in some crises (deflationary) but fail in others (stagflation)

Universa-style tail hedging argues you should accept the bleed because the payoff in a tail event funds decades of losses. But for a discretionary book, the bleed is real P&L that competes with active strategy returns — most funds don't run permanent tail hedges.

Crisis Case Studies

Q73. What lessons did Archegos teach risk managers?

Archegos (March 2021) — Bill Hwang's family office blew up, took $10B+ from prime brokers (Credit Suisse, Nomura, Morgan Stanley).

Mechanics: held massive concentrated long positions in a few names (ViacomCBS, Discovery, Chinese ADRs) via Total Return Swaps (TRS) across multiple prime brokers. Each PB only saw their slice — none saw the aggregate exposure. When the names started falling, margin calls hit simultaneously, Archegos couldn't meet them, PBs unwound the TRS positions into a falling market.

Lessons:
- TRS hide leverage from public disclosure (no 13F filing required)
- Prime brokers had no visibility into total client exposure across the street
- Concentration risk: single positions were 10%+ of float
- Margin models underestimated correlation in stress
- Post-Archegos: SEC pushing for swap position disclosure, PBs raised IM significantly

Q74. LTCM 1998 — what happened and what's the takeaway?

Long-Term Capital Management ran arbitrage trades (convergence) at 25-30x leverage. Russia defaulted Aug 1998 → flight to quality → spreads widened instead of converging → losses → margin calls → forced unwinds in illiquid markets → death spiral. NY Fed organised a $3.6B bailout to prevent systemic contagion.

Lessons:
- Liquidity dries up exactly when you need to exit
- Models assuming normal correlations fail in crisis (correlations → 1)
- Leverage amplifies survivable losses into terminal ones
- Even Nobel laureates with PhDs blow up
- Position size matters more than position quality

Q75. UK LDI crisis 2022 — what happened?

UK pension funds used Liability-Driven Investment strategies — long Gilts + leveraged via repo to match long-duration liabilities. Sept 2022 mini-budget caused Gilt yields to spike 100bps in days → mark-to-market losses → margin calls → forced Gilt selling → yields spiked further → death spiral. BoE intervened with emergency Gilt purchases.

Lessons:
- Levered duration trades have non-linear blow-up risk in rate spikes
- "Hedging" liabilities with leverage adds new risk
- Coordinated forced selling creates feedback loops
- Risk managers must model the funding side, not just the asset side

Behavioural / Situational

Q76. A senior trader pushes back hard on your risk view. How do you handle it?

Don't escalate emotionally; escalate analytically. Steps:
1. Listen — they may know something you don't (e.g., a hedge offsetting the position you flagged)
2. Restate their argument back to them to confirm understanding
3. Show your data — exact position, the limit, the breach calculation
4. Ask: "If you're right, what would change my numbers?" Test their thesis against the model
5. If still in disagreement, escalate jointly to senior risk — not behind their back

A risk manager isn't there to be liked or to win arguments. The job is to be right and to make the firm's risk explicit. Traders respect risk managers who do their homework and don't fold under pressure.

Brain Teasers

Q79. You flip a fair coin until you see two heads in a row. Expected number of flips?

Let E = expected flips. Two states by progress:
- From "0 heads in a row": flip → with p=½ go to "1 head", with p=½ stay at 0. E₀ = 1 + ½ E₁ + ½ E₀
- From "1 head in a row": flip → with p=½ done, with p=½ back to 0. E₁ = 1 + ½ × 0 + ½ E₀

Solve: E₁ = 1 + ½ E₀. Substitute: E₀ = 1 + ½(1 + ½ E₀) + ½ E₀ = 1.5 + ¾ E₀ → ¼ E₀ = 1.5 → E₀ = 6.

Answer: 6 flips.

Q80. Two assets, both with Sharpe 1, correlation 0.5 — what's the Sharpe of an equal-weighted portfolio?

Equal weights w = ½. Both have same μ and σ (since same Sharpe). Portfolio:
- Mean return: μ_p = μ
- Variance: σ²_p = ½ σ² + ½ × 0.5 × σ² = 0.75 σ² → σ_p = σ × √0.75 ≈ 0.866σ

Sharpe_p = μ / (0.866σ) = 1 / 0.866 ≈ 1.155.

Generalises: combining uncorrelated equal-Sharpe strategies improves Sharpe by √n. With correlation 0.5, you get less than √2 lift but still a real improvement. This is the math behind multi-strat funds.

Q81. A stock is at 100, you sell a 105 call for $2 and buy a 95 put for $1. What's your max loss and max gain?

This is a "risk reversal" (synthetic short with band):
- If stock at expiry > 105: short call assigned → lose (S − 105) on call, +$2 premium, put expires → -$1. Net: -(S − 105) + 1. Loss grows linearly above 105 — unlimited max loss.
- If 95 ≤ S ≤ 105: both options expire worthless. Net P&L = +$2 − $1 = +$1 (max gain region).
- If S < 95: long put pays (95 − S), call expires, premiums net +$1. Net: (95 − S) + 1. Gains as stock falls — max gain unbounded on downside (if no stock position).

Note: this is a payoff question, not a directional bet — interviewer wants to see you draw the payoff cleanly. If asked the same with long stock + this collar overlay, max loss = stock @ 100 → put @ 95 = -$5 + $1 net premium = -$4, max gain = stock @ 100 → call @ 105 = +$5 + $1 = +$6.
