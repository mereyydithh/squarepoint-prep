INSIDE A DISCRETIONARY FUND

What It Looks Like, What Risk Does, and Your Stack

THE PEOPLE — Who Sits Where

A discretionary trading floor is organized around pods or teams, each led by a PM (Portfolio Manager).

The Investment Committee (IC)

  • Senior leadership (at Squarepoint: founders + CIO Michael Gottlieb)
  • Sets the overall risk budget: how much capital each PM gets, max drawdown, strategy guidelines
  • Meets regularly (weekly/monthly) to review performance, adjust allocations, approve new strategies
  • The IC is the "board of directors" for the trading business — PMs answer to them

Portfolio Managers (PMs)

  • Each PM runs a "book" — a portfolio of positions based on their thesis/strategy
  • At a discretionary fund, the PM is the alpha generator. They decide WHAT to trade and WHY
  • Examples of discretionary strategies:
  • Global macro: trading rates, FX, commodities based on macro views (central bank policy, inflation, geopolitics)
  • Relative value: long one asset vs short another based on a pricing dislocation (long Brent/short WTI, long UST 5Y/short 10Y)
  • Event-driven: trading around catalysts (earnings, M&A, regulatory changes, elections)
  • Fundamental L/S equity: long undervalued / short overvalued stocks based on deep research
  • At Squarepoint specifically, PMs likely lean quantitative-discretionary — they have a thesis but use Squarepoint's data/quant platform to validate and size it

Quantitative Researchers (Quants)

  • Support the PMs with analytical horsepower
  • Build models to test PM hypotheses: "Is this mean-reverting? What's the vol regime? How crowded is this trade?"
  • Run backtests, scenario analysis, factor decomposition
  • At Squarepoint, the quants ARE the platform — they bridge the systematic infrastructure with discretionary PMs

Traders (Execution)

  • Execute the PM's orders in the market
  • Responsible for best execution — minimizing slippage, market impact
  • In some setups (like this one), traders and PMs overlap — the JD mentions collaborating with both

Risk Manager (YOU)

  • Sits ACROSS all the PMs, not inside one pod
  • Reports to the IC, not to the PM — this independence is critical
  • The PM wants to make money. The risk manager makes sure the PM doesn't blow up the fund doing it

Analysts

  • Junior researchers who support PMs with sector expertise, data analysis, idea generation

THE DAILY RHYTHM OF A DISCRETIONARY FUND

Pre-market (6:30-8:00 AM)

  • Overnight news review: what happened in Asia/Europe, macro releases, central bank commentary
  • Risk team pulls overnight P&L, checks for limit breaches, flags positions that moved significantly
  • Risk team prepares the morning risk report: portfolio exposures, Greeks, concentration, VaR, stress test results

Morning huddle (8:00-8:30 AM)

  • PM + risk + quant + traders gather (or call in if global)
  • PMs share their view: "I'm adding to the short euro position because ECB minutes were dovish"
  • Risk flags concerns: "Your rates exposure is at 85% of limit" or "that position is correlated with your FX book"
  • Quick, informal — this is the "spontaneous huddles" Squarepoint mentions in their culture page

Trading day (8:30 AM - 4:00 PM)

  • PMs trade. Risk monitors in real-time
  • Risk manager is watching: position sizes, P&L swings, Greeks migration, correlation shifts, news events that could impact the book
  • If something moves sharply — a geopolitical event, surprise data release — the risk manager immediately assesses impact across ALL PM books
  • Ad-hoc conversations: PM comes to your desk — "I want to put on a $200M position in nat gas, what does that do to our portfolio?" You run the numbers, stress test it, give them an answer
  • Risk manager maintains the risk dashboard — a real-time view of all exposures, limits utilization, P&L

Post-market (4:00-6:00 PM)

  • End-of-day reconciliation: confirm all positions are correct
  • Update stress tests with closing prices
  • Prepare any IC materials if there's a review coming
  • Flag any limit breaches for escalation

Weekly/Monthly

  • IC meeting: Risk presents portfolio-level view to senior leadership. Total fund exposure, biggest risks, tail scenarios, how close each PM is to their limits
  • Strategy review: Deep-dives on each PM's book — attribution, risk-adjusted returns, drawdown analysis
  • Stress testing: Run hypothetical scenarios (what if oil goes to $120? What if the Fed hikes 100bp? What if US-China war?)

THE RISK MANAGER'S ROLE — What You Actually Do

1. Position-Level Risk Monitoring

  • Track every position across every PM's book
  • Calculate Greeks: Delta, Gamma, Vega, Theta (and cross-Greeks: Vanna, Volga for options-heavy books)
  • Monitor position sizes vs limits set by IC
  • Flag concentration risk: "PM A and PM B both have massive long gold exposure — the combined fund is overweight"

2. Portfolio-Level Risk Aggregation

  • Aggregate risk ACROSS all PMs to see the total fund picture
  • A PM might think their book is hedged, but combined with other PMs, the fund could be net long credit risk
  • This is the portfolio construction part of the JD — you help the IC see how individual PM books combine
  • Metrics: net/gross exposure, beta, sector/geography/factor concentration, VaR, Expected Shortfall

3. Stress Testing & Scenario Analysis

  • Design and run scenarios: historical (2008 GFC, COVID crash, 2022 rate shock) and hypothetical (Iran war escalation, oil embargo, Fed emergency cut)
  • For each scenario: what happens to each PM's P&L? What happens to the total fund?
  • Present findings to IC — "If treasuries sell off 100bp, PM A loses $15M, PM B makes $8M, net fund impact is -$7M"

4. Limit Monitoring & Enforcement

  • IC sets limits: max loss per PM (e.g., $5M/day, $20M/month), max gross exposure, max sector concentration, max VaR
  • You monitor these in real-time
  • When a PM approaches a limit, you notify them. When they breach, you escalate to IC
  • This is where the independence matters — you can't be afraid to tell a PM to cut their position

5. Challenging the PM's Thesis

  • This is the human judgment part that doesn't exist in systematic
  • PM says: "I'm shorting euro because ECB will cut." You ask: "What if they don't? What's your stop loss? How crowded is this trade? What's the convexity of your position?"
  • You're not second-guessing their alpha — you're making sure the risk/reward is appropriate and the position sizing makes sense
  • Good risk managers ask the questions the PM doesn't want to ask themselves

6. Trade-Level Risk Assessment (Pre-Trade)

  • Before a large trade goes on, risk evaluates it: what does it do to portfolio VaR? Does it breach any limits? What's the worst-case scenario? How liquid is it (can we get out)?
  • You might say: "This trade is fine on its own, but it makes the fund 2x levered to a rates move when combined with PM B's book"

7. P&L Attribution & Performance Analysis

  • Break down each PM's returns: how much came from the market moving (beta), how much from their specific view (alpha)?
  • Identify which trades are working, which are bleeding
  • Present to IC so they can decide: does this PM get more capital or less?

8. Risk Framework Development (the "builder" part)

  • Since the discretionary team is NEW at Squarepoint, you'd be helping BUILD:
  • The risk report templates
  • The limit structure
  • The escalation process
  • The stress testing scenarios
  • The risk dashboard
  • Integration with Squarepoint's existing systematic risk infrastructure
  • This is the greenfield/builder angle that maps to your NYC experience

  • Become the person PMs come to BEFORE they trade, not just the person who monitors AFTER

HOW RISK IS DIFFERENT: Systematic vs. Discretionary

Systematic Risk Discretionary Risk (YOUR ROLE)
Who decides The model The PM + risk manager together
Risk limits Hard-coded in optimizer Negotiated with IC, enforced by risk
Your counterpart An algorithm A human trader with a thesis
Key skill Model building, calibration Judgment, communication, pushback
When things go wrong System alert → automatic deleverage You call the PM, challenge the position, escalate to IC
Daily work Monitor model outputs, recalibrate Review positions, stress test, discuss with trader
Alpha attribution Statistical decomposition Understanding the trade narrative
Your value-add Better risk model = better portfolio optimization Better risk culture = PMs make better decisions

Bottom line: In systematic, risk is engineering. In discretionary, risk is judgment. You're interviewing for the judgment seat.

YOUR CHEAT SHEET: What to Say if Asked "What Do You Think the Day-to-Day Looks Like?"

"I'd expect the day starts with reviewing overnight moves, pulling P&L and exposure data, and preparing for the morning huddle with the PMs. During the day it's real-time monitoring — tracking positions against limits, running stress tests on new trades PMs want to put on, and aggregating risk across the book at the portfolio level. Post-market is reconciliation and reporting. And since this is a new team, a big part of the role would be building the infrastructure — the risk reports, the dashboard, the limit framework, the stress testing scenarios — essentially creating the risk management operating system for the discretionary business.

What excites me is that at Squarepoint, I'd get to build this on top of a world-class quantitative platform. Most discretionary risk teams start from Excel. Here, you have the data, the models, the compute — you just need to adapt it for human-judgment strategies. That's the bridge I want to build."
