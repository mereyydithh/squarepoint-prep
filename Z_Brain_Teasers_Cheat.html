ONE-LINERS

Concept One-liner
Conditional probability The answer changes based on WHAT you condition on — "at least one heads" ≠ "first flip heads." Always draw the sample space.
Bayes' theorem Prior × Likelihood / Evidence. A 99% accurate test with 0.01% prevalence has ~1% PPV. Base rates dominate.
Expected value Probability-weighted average outcome. Positive EV ≠ good bet if variance is extreme (St. Petersburg).
Geometric vs arithmetic +20% then −20% = −4%, not 0%. Geometric return ≈ arithmetic − σ²/2. Vol destroys compounding.
Gambler's ruin With a slightly negative edge, you go broke with near certainty. Even with positive edge, overleveraging kills.
Fermi estimation Decompose → estimate each factor → multiply → sanity check. Errors cancel across multiplicative factors. ±3x is a good Fermi estimate.
Optimal stopping Observe the first 37% (1/e), then pick the next one that beats all you've seen. Works for hiring, entry timing, and hedge shopping.
Ergodicity Positive EV per trial but negative geometric growth → you still go broke. Kelly criterion fixes this.
Average speed trap Average speed = total distance / total time (harmonic mean), NOT the arithmetic mean of speeds. You can never average 2× your first-lap speed.

1 P(both heads | at least one heads) 1/3 Condition removes TT; 3 remain, only 1 is HH
2 3 cards (R/R, B/B, R/B), see red, P(other red)? 2/3 Count FACES (3 red), not cards. 2 of 3 red faces are R/R
3 Monty Hall: switch or stay? Switch (2/3) Your initial 1/3 doesn't change. The other 2/3 concentrates
4 Birthday problem: n for >50%? 23 $\binom{23}{2}$ = 253 pairs. It's pairs, not individuals
5 Two dice, P(sum = 7)? 1/6 6 favorable out of 36. Most likely sum.
6 E[max of two dice] 4.47 P(max=k) = (2k−1)/36. Compare to E[single] = 3.5
7 E[flips for 2 consecutive heads] 6 Solve E = 1.5 + 0.75E. For n heads: E = 2^(n+1) − 2
8 Gambler's ruin: P(broke), you=$a, opponent=$b b/(a+b) You $10 vs casino $990 → 99% ruin
9 Coupon collector: E[boxes] for all n n·H_n Last few take disproportionately long. H_10 ≈ 2.93
10 Derangement: P(nobody gets own hat) 1/e ≈ 37% Converges fast. Even n=4 gives 37.5%
11 Drunk walk: E[steps to return to 0] ∞ Returns with P=1 but expected time = ∞
12 Secretary problem: P(hiring best) 1/e ≈ 37% Reject first n/e, then take next that beats all
13 St. Petersburg: E[payout] ∞ But median → 0. Expected utility is finite (~$4)
14 Two envelopes: switch? No gain The paradox assumes a uniform prior that doesn't exist
15 E[draws to first ace] from 52-card deck 10.6 (N+1)/(k+1) = 53/5

FERMI ESTIMATION — FRAMEWORK

1. CLARIFY  → What exactly? Units? Timeframe?
2. DECOMPOSE → Break into factors you CAN estimate
3. ESTIMATE  → Each factor with anchor + adjustment
4. MULTIPLY  → Combine factors
5. SANITY    → Does it pass the smell test? ±3x OK.

EXPECTED VALUE — KEY FORMULAS

Distribution E[X] Var[X] Use
Bernoulli(p) p p(1−p) Single breached/not breached
Binomial(n,p) np np(1−p) VaR breaches in 250 days
Geometric(p) 1/p (1−p)/p² Flips to first success
Poisson(λ) λ λ Rare events per period
Uniform(a,b) (a+b)/2 (b−a)²/12 Flat prior
Exponential(λ) 1/λ 1/λ² Time between events
Normal(μ,σ²) μ σ² Returns (approx)

Linearity: E[aX + bY] = aE[X] + bE[Y] — ALWAYS, even if dependent.

Variance: Var[aX + bY] = a²Var[X] + b²Var[Y] + 2ab·Cov[X,Y]

THE 60-SECOND INTERVIEW PROTOCOL

  1. Restate (5s): "So I need to find..."
  2. Classify (5s): Probability? Estimation? Logic? Optimization?
  3. Simplify (10s): Try n=2 or n=3 first
  4. Identify the trick (10s): Conditional? Count faces? Extra dimension?
  5. Solve (20s): Clear steps, speak aloud
  6. Sanity check (10s): "Is this between 0 and 1? Does n=1 case work?"

Common Traps

Trap Fix
Forgetting to condition properly Draw the full sample space
Counting objects not faces "What exactly am I counting?"
Ignoring base rates (Bayes) Prior × Likelihood / Evidence
Arithmetic ≠ geometric returns Always multiply: 1.2 × 0.8 = 0.96
Infinite mean = good bet Check median and variance
"It's 50/50" Challenge: are priors really equal?

KEY INTERVIEW LINES

On probability: "The trick is almost always about WHAT you're conditioning on. I restate the sample space explicitly — if the answer is 1/2, I'm probably wrong."

On estimation: "I don't need the exact answer. I decompose into factors I can anchor, multiply through, and sanity-check. If I'm within 3x, I've demonstrated the thinking they want."

On being stuck: "Let me try a smaller case — n=2 or n=3 — and see if the pattern generalizes."

Connecting to risk: "This is exactly like [risk analog] — the same mathematical structure shows up in portfolio correlation, VaR calibration, and position sizing."
