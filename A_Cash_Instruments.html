A — CASH INSTRUMENTS & ASSET CLASSES

What exists, how it works, how it moves, and how it hurts you

Depth: 🔴 DEEP

PART 1: EQUITIES

The Instrument

A share of equity = ownership claim on a company's residual cash flows after all obligations (debt, taxes, operating costs) are paid. You're last in line — that's why equities are the riskiest part of the capital structure.

What Drives Equity Prices

Price = Earnings × Multiple. That's it. Everything else is just a more detailed version of this.

Driver What it means Example
Earnings growth Company makes more money Revenue up 10%, margins stable → EPS up 10% → stock up ~10%
Multiple expansion Market pays more per dollar of earnings P/E goes from 15x to 18x → stock up 20% even with flat earnings
Multiple compression Market pays less Rates rise → discount rate up → future earnings worth less today → P/E falls
Sentiment / flows Supply and demand for the stock Fund liquidation forces selling regardless of fundamentals

Equity Risk Factors

When you own a stock, you're exposed to:
- Market risk (beta): Broad equity market goes down, your stock probably goes down too. SPX beta of 1.2 → if SPX drops 5%, expect this stock to drop 6%.
- Sector risk: Energy stocks move with oil. Banks move with rates. Tech moves with growth expectations.
- Idiosyncratic risk: Company-specific events — earnings miss, CEO departure, product failure, fraud.
- Factor risk: Size (small vs large cap), value (cheap vs expensive), momentum (winners vs losers), quality (high vs low profitability).

Equity Metrics to Know Cold

Metric Formula What it tells you
P/E Price / EPS How much you're paying per $ of earnings. High P/E = expensive or high growth.
EV/EBITDA Enterprise value / EBITDA Better than P/E for comparing companies with different capital structures.
Free Cash Flow Yield FCF / Market Cap Cash generation relative to price. Higher = cheaper.
Dividend Yield Annual dividend / Price Income component of return.
Short Interest Shares short / Float Crowding indicator. >20% of float short = squeeze risk.
Beta Cov(stock, market) / Var(market) Sensitivity to broad market moves.

Sector Rotation & What Drives It

  • Risk-on: Growth, tech, small caps, cyclicals outperform. Money moves into higher-beta stocks.
  • Risk-off: Utilities, healthcare, consumer staples, large caps outperform. Defensive rotation.
  • Rising rates: Financials benefit (net interest margin expands). Long-duration equities (unprofitable tech) suffer.
  • Falling rates: Growth stocks benefit (discount rate falls → future cash flows worth more today).
  • Oil spike: Energy outperforms. Airlines, transports, consumer discretionary underperform.

Crowding Risk

When too many funds hold the same positions:
- 13F filings show overlap — if 40 hedge funds own the same stock, that's crowded.
- Crowded longs: vulnerable to a "crowded unwind" — if one fund sells, others follow.
- Crowded shorts: vulnerable to a "short squeeze" — stock rises, shorts cover, forcing it higher.
- GameStop (2021) is the extreme version. But milder squeezes happen regularly.

PART 2: FIXED INCOME / RATES

The Instrument

A bond = a series of fixed coupon payments plus return of principal at maturity. You're lending money; the issuer is borrowing.

The Yield Curve

The most important chart in finance. Plots yields by maturity.

  • Normal curve (upward sloping): Long-term rates > short-term rates. Investors demand more yield for locking up money longer. Economy is healthy.
  • Flat curve: Short and long rates similar. Economy slowing; market unsure about future.
  • Inverted curve: Short-term rates > long-term rates. Market expects rate CUTS → recession signal. Every US recession since 1970 was preceded by an inversion (2s10s spread going negative).

Key Rates and What They Mean

Rate What it is Who sets it
Fed Funds Rate Overnight lending rate between banks The Fed (FOMC)
2-Year Treasury Market's expectation of Fed policy over next 2 years The market
10-Year Treasury Benchmark risk-free rate, drives mortgages, corporate bonds The market
30-Year Treasury Longest duration, most sensitive to inflation expectations The market
SOFR Secured overnight financing rate (replaced LIBOR) Market (repo-based)

Duration & Convexity

Duration = sensitivity of bond price to a 1% change in yield.
- A bond with duration of 7 means: if yields rise 1%, bond price falls ~7%.
- Modified duration for precision: ΔP/P ≈ -D × Δy
- Longer maturity → higher duration → more rate sensitivity.
- A zero-coupon bond's duration = its maturity (no coupons to shorten it).

DV01 (Dollar Value of a Basis Point) = how much the position gains/loses for a 1bp move in yield.
- DV01 = Duration × Price × 0.0001
- A portfolio with $500K DV01 loses $500K if yields rise 1bp.

Convexity = the curvature. Duration is the first-order approximation; convexity is the second-order correction.
- ΔP/P ≈ -D × Δy + ½ × C × (Δy)²
- Positive convexity: bonds actually fall LESS than duration predicts when yields rise, and rise MORE when yields fall. This is a good thing — it's why "long bonds, long convexity" is desirable.
- Negative convexity: mortgage-backed securities (prepayment risk). When rates fall, homeowners refinance, and you get your principal back early (at the worst time — when you want to stay invested at the old higher rate).

Credit Spreads

The extra yield above the risk-free rate that a corporate bond pays. Compensates for default risk.

Rating Typical spread (bps) What it means
AAA/AA 20-60 Very low default risk
A 60-120 Low default risk
BBB 120-200 Lowest investment grade — watch carefully
BB 200-400 High yield ("junk"). First to blow out in stress.
B 400-700 High default probability
CCC and below 700+ Distressed. Trading on recovery value.

Spread widening = credit risk increasing = risk-off. IG spreads widening 20bps in a day is notable. HY spreads widening 100bps is a crisis signal.

BBB cliff risk: The largest pool of corporate bonds is BBB-rated. If they get downgraded to BB ("fallen angels"), IG-only funds are forced to sell → supply flood in HY → spreads blow out → cascade.

PART 3: FOREIGN EXCHANGE

The Instrument

An FX rate = the price of one currency in terms of another. EUR/USD = 1.08 means one euro costs $1.08.

What Drives FX

  1. Interest rate differentials — money flows to higher-yielding currencies (carry trade).
  2. Growth differentials — stronger economy → stronger currency (attracts capital).
  3. Central bank policy — rate hikes strengthen, cuts weaken.
  4. Risk appetite — risk-on weakens safe havens (USD, JPY, CHF); risk-off strengthens them.
  5. Capital flows — trade balance, FDI, portfolio flows.
  6. Intervention — central banks directly buying/selling (Japan, China, Switzerland).

Key Pairs and Their Personalities

Pair What it reflects
EUR/USD Global risk & rate differential. Most liquid pair.
USD/JPY Risk appetite + carry trade. JPY strengthens in crisis.
GBP/USD UK economy + Brexit residuals + BOE policy
USD/CNH China policy. Managed float — watch for devaluation signals.
USD/TRY, USD/ZAR EM risk barometer. Blow out in risk-off.
DXY Dollar index (basket: EUR 57%, JPY 14%, GBP 12%, CAD 9%, SEK 4%, CHF 4%)

Carry Trade

Borrow in a low-rate currency (JPY at 0.5%), invest in a high-rate currency (MXN at 10%). Earn the differential (9.5% annualized). Works until it doesn't.

When carry unwinds:
- Risk event → JPY strengthens → you lose on FX more than you earned in carry.
- Carry unwind is FAST and VIOLENT because it's crowded and leveraged.
- October 2022 JPY, Q3 2024 JPY carry unwind — both crushed funds.

FX Risk for a Discretionary Fund

  • Any non-USD position has embedded FX exposure.
  • Long European stocks = long EUR implicitly. If EUR/USD falls 3%, your stock return is reduced by 3% when converted back to USD.
  • Hedging: FX forwards (most common), FX options (for tail protection), natural hedge (matching assets and liabilities in same currency).

PART 4: COMMODITIES

The Instrument

Commodities trade via futures (mostly). Physical delivery matters because storage constraints drive pricing (see Case Studies — negative oil).

Major Commodities and What Drives Them

Commodity Key drivers Why it matters for risk
Crude Oil (WTI, Brent) OPEC supply, demand (China, US), geopolitics (Hormuz), inventory Oil moves everything — inflation, rates, equities, EM
Natural Gas Weather, storage, LNG exports, seasonality Extremely volatile. Amaranth died here.
Gold Real rates (inverse), USD (inverse), safe haven demand, central bank buying Hedge portfolio. But only works when real rates cooperate.
Copper Industrial demand (China), construction, EV transition "Dr. Copper" — leading indicator of global growth
Wheat/Corn/Soy Weather, trade policy, fertilizer costs Food inflation → political instability → EM risk

Futures Curve Dynamics

Contango: Front month cheaper than back months. Normal for storable commodities (cost of carry = storage + insurance + financing). Market expects higher future prices.

Backwardation: Front month MORE expensive than back months. Signals tight current supply (scarcity premium). Producers are getting paid MORE for immediate delivery.

Roll cost/yield:
- If you're long a futures contract and it expires, you "roll" to the next month.
- In contango: you sell cheap front month, buy expensive back month → LOSE money on the roll (negative roll yield). This is why commodity ETFs (like USO) bleed value in contango markets.
- In backwardation: you sell expensive front month, buy cheap back month → MAKE money on the roll (positive roll yield).

Commodity Risk Specifics

  • Physical delivery: WTI expires to physical delivery at Cushing, OK. If storage is full: price goes negative.
  • Basis risk: Brent vs WTI spread can move independently of "oil" direction. If you hedge WTI risk with Brent, the basis can blow out.
  • Seasonality: NG is seasonal (winter heating demand). Gasoline is seasonal (summer driving). These patterns create predictable but sometimes extreme moves.
  • Unit conversion: Know the basics — 1 barrel = 42 US gallons. 1 lot of WTI futures = 1,000 barrels.

PART 5: CREDIT

The Instrument

Credit = the risk of a borrower defaulting. Traded via:
- Corporate bonds (cash market)
- Credit Default Swaps (CDS) (synthetic — buy/sell protection against default)
- Leveraged loans (bank loans to sub-IG companies, floating rate)
- CLOs (securitized pools of leveraged loans)

Credit Spread Components

Spread = Default probability × Loss given default + Liquidity premium + Risk premium

CDS Mechanics (Know This)

  • Buyer of protection: pays a quarterly premium (spread) to the seller. If the reference entity defaults, the seller pays the buyer (par minus recovery).
  • Seller of protection: earns the spread, but bears the default risk. Economically equivalent to being long the credit (long the bond).
  • CDS spread rising = credit risk increasing = risk-off for that name.
  • Basis: CDS spread vs cash bond spread. When basis is large, there's a relative value opportunity (or a structural reason for the gap).

Credit Signals to Watch

Signal What it means Action
IG spreads +20bps in a day Material stress in investment-grade credit Check portfolio duration, credit exposure
HY spreads +100bps Crisis territory — defaults expected to rise Reduce credit exposure, check counterparty risk
CDS-bond basis blowout Cash and synthetic markets diverging — liquidity stress Monitor for forced selling, margin pressure
BBB downgrade wave Fallen angels flood HY market Cascading spread widening, forced selling by IG funds
Default rate rising above 3% Economic downturn underway Reduce HY exposure, increase hedges

Investment Grade vs High Yield

Feature IG HY
Rating BBB- and above BB+ and below
Typical spread 50-200bps 300-800bps
Default rate <1% 3-10% (cycle dependent)
Duration risk Higher (longer maturities) Lower (shorter maturities, but default risk dominates)
Correlation to equities Low in normal times, rises in stress High — HY trades like equities in a crisis

PART 6: CROSS-ASSET CONNECTIONS

This is where it all comes together. Assets don't move independently — everything is connected.

The Master Cascade Chain

Oil spike → Inflation expectations rise
  → Rates rise (to fight inflation)
    → Dollar strengthens (higher US rates attract capital)
      → EM currencies weaken (capital outflows)
        → EM stocks and bonds sell off
    → Equity multiples compress (higher discount rate)
      → Growth/tech stocks hit hardest (long duration equity)
    → Credit spreads widen (recession fears)
      → HY bonds sell off
        → CLO tranches face downgrades
  → Gold: ambiguous (inflation positive, but real rates rising is negative)
  → Commodity importers (Japan, Europe, India) weakened

Regime Table

Regime Equities Bonds Dollar Gold Spread Vol
Risk-on (growth + easy money) ↑ ↓ (yields up) ↓ ↓ Tight Low
Risk-off (panic) ↓ ↑ (flight to quality) ↑ ↑ Wide High
Stagflation (inflation + slow growth) ↓ ↓ (inflation kills bonds) ↑ ↑ Wide High
Goldilocks (moderate growth + low inflation) ↑ Stable Stable ↓ Tight Low

The Key Correlation to Know

Stocks and bonds are negatively correlated (stocks down, bonds up) in most post-2000 environments. This is the foundation of 60/40 portfolios.

BUT: In 2022 (inflation shock), stocks AND bonds both fell. The correlation flipped positive. This destroyed the "bonds hedge my equity risk" assumption. If you're running a portfolio that relies on stock-bond negative correlation for diversification, you need to stress test for what happens when that breaks.

PART 7: INTERVIEW APPLICATION

If asked "Walk me through the major asset classes and their risk characteristics":

"I think about 5 core asset classes: equities, rates, credit, FX, and commodities. Each has its own risk profile but they're deeply interconnected.

Equities are driven by earnings and multiples — but multiples are driven by rates, which connects equities to the rates market. When rates rise, equity multiples compress, especially for long-duration growth stocks.

In rates, the key risk measure is duration — sensitivity to yield changes. A bond with duration 7 loses 7% for every 1% rate increase. The shape of the yield curve tells you about growth and recession expectations.

Credit sits between equities and rates — in normal times it behaves like bonds (spread income), but in stress it behaves like equities (default risk, forced selling, correlation spike).

FX is driven by rate differentials and risk appetite. Any non-USD position has embedded FX exposure that needs to be monitored.

Commodities are unique because of physical delivery constraints and storage costs. The futures curve (contango vs backwardation) creates roll costs that can dominate returns.

The RM's job is understanding how a shock to one asset class cascades to others — like an oil spike flowing through inflation to rates to equity multiples to credit spreads. That's where scenario analysis becomes critical."
