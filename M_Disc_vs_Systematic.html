M — DISCRETIONARY VS SYSTEMATIC

How each approach works, how risk management differs, and where you sit at Squarepoint

Depth: 🟡 WELL

PART 1: THE TWO PARADIGMS

Systematic (Quantitative)

Decisions are made by ALGORITHMS. Humans build models; models make trades.

Aspect Systematic approach
Signal generation Statistical models, machine learning, factor analysis
Execution Automated. Algorithms route orders optimally.
Holding period Milliseconds (HFT) to weeks (medium-frequency stat arb)
Positions Hundreds to thousands. Highly diversified.
Edge Speed, data processing, statistical patterns across many securities
Human role Build and maintain models. Monitor model behavior. Research new signals.
Risk Model risk, overfitting, regime change, crowded trades

Discretionary (Fundamental)

Decisions are made by HUMANS. PMs research, form views, and decide what to trade.

Aspect Discretionary approach
Signal generation Fundamental research, earnings models, macro views, sector expertise
Execution Human-directed. Trader works the order.
Holding period Days to months (sometimes years)
Positions 20-80 typically. Concentrated, high-conviction.
Edge Deep fundamental insight, variant perception, informational edge
Human role Everything — idea generation, research, thesis formation, sizing, execution, exit
Risk Behavioral biases, concentrated positions, key-person risk

PART 2: HOW RISK MANAGEMENT DIFFERS

This is the CORE of what your interviewers want to know: why does a discretionary RM do different things than a systematic RM?

Systematic Risk Management

What the models handle:

  • Position sizing (automated, risk-optimized)
  • Exposure limits (coded into the system)
  • Stop-losses (algorithmic, no emotions)
  • Factor neutrality (models are factor-neutral by construction)

What the RM focuses on:

Focus area Why
Model risk Is the model overfitting? Has the regime changed?
Tail risk Models optimize for normal markets. What happens in 5-sigma events?
Crowding Are our signals correlated with every other quant fund? Will everyone unwind at once?
Technology risk System failures, data feed errors, execution glitches
Correlation monitoring Are positions more correlated than the model assumes?
Liquidity Can we actually execute the model's desired trades at the assumed cost?

How it feels:

  • Mostly monitoring dashboards and systems.
  • Interventions are rare but critical (model break, flash crash, regime change).
  • Less interpersonal — you're watching algorithms, not talking to PMs.

Discretionary Risk Management

What the PM handles:

  • Idea generation, research, thesis development
  • Trade structuring (entry/exit timing)
  • Conviction assessment

What the RM focuses on:

Focus area Why
Position sizing PM may over-size based on conviction or anchoring. RM pushes back.
Concentration Portfolios can become concentrated. RM monitors and flags.
Drawdown management PMs feel losses personally. RM runs the broken-thesis framework.
Factor drift PM may unknowingly accumulate factor bets (all long growth, all long beta).
Correlation PM may think they're diversified but all positions are correlated to one macro factor.
Behavioral biases Anchoring, disposition effect, overconfidence. RM is the behavioral check.
Scenario analysis What happens to this portfolio in a recession? Rate shock? Sector rotation?

How it feels:

  • Very interpersonal. You're sitting next to the PM, having daily conversations.
  • Constant dialogue: "Why are you holding this? What's your catalyst? When do you cut?"
  • You ARE the risk system much more than any dashboard — judgment matters.

Side-by-Side Comparison

Dimension Systematic RM Discretionary RM
Main tool Dashboards, model monitoring Conversations, judgment
Key risk Model failure, crowding Behavioral bias, concentration
Position count 1000+ positions 20-80 positions
Interaction style Engineer-to-model Human-to-human
Speed of decisions Seconds (automated stops) Hours/days (judgment-based)
Data dependency Extremely high Moderate
Override authority Rare (kill switch) Frequent (daily dialogue)
Biggest nightmare Quant quake (Aug 2007) Single-name blow-up, PM revenge trading

PART 3: SQUAREPOINT'S MODEL

Systematic Core

Squarepoint was founded and built as a systematic fund. The DNA is quantitative:
- Multiple systematic strategies across asset classes
- Technology-driven infrastructure
- Data-centric decision making
- Risk systems built for automated monitoring of thousands of positions

Discretionary Build-Out

Squarepoint is adding discretionary teams — this is the team you'd join. This is strategically important:

Why Squarepoint wants discretionary:

  1. Diversification of alpha: Systematic and discretionary alphas are largely uncorrelated. Having both improves the fund's Sharpe ratio.
  2. Capture different opportunities: Some trades (activist situations, special situations, macro theme trades) can't be captured by models. You need human judgment.
  3. LP demand: Allocators like multi-strategy platforms that offer diversified alpha sources.
  4. Regime robustness: When systematic models break (regime change, crowding), discretionary PMs may still perform — and vice versa.

What makes this challenging:

  1. Culture clash: Systematic quants think in data and statistics. Discretionary PMs think in stories and conviction. The RM bridges these worlds.
  2. Infrastructure mismatch: Risk systems built for thousands of positions need to be adapted for a concentrated 40-position book where each position requires qualitative context.
  3. Risk language: Systematic risk = "the model's expected shortfall breached 2.5 standard deviations." Discretionary risk = "the PM is anchoring to a losing thesis." You need to speak BOTH languages.
  4. Trust building: The firm's leadership is systematic. The discretionary PM needs to prove they can generate alpha within the firm's risk framework before getting more capital.

PART 4: YOUR ROLE — THE BRIDGE

As the RM for the discretionary desk inside a systematic firm, you're uniquely positioned:

Translating Between Worlds

You translate From To
The PM's conviction "I think XOM is a buy because..." "This adds $2M incremental VaR, 15% sector concentration, and 0.3 portfolio beta"
The risk system's output "Portfolio stress test shows -$8M in a 2008 replay" "PM, if we get a credit crunch, your most levered names will get hit hardest — here are the three you should trim"
Firm-wide risk to PM context "CRO wants discretionary VaR under $5M" "You can run this portfolio at current gross but need to reduce your correlation to systematic book's positions"

Daily Workflow (Likely)

Time Activity
7:00 AM Review overnight moves. Check Asian/European session impact on positions.
7:30 AM Pre-market risk report: P&L, VaR, exposure changes, limit utilization.
8:00 AM Morning meeting with PM. Review: positions, catalysts today (earnings, data), risk metrics.
9:30 AM-4:00 PM Live market monitoring. Real-time P&L, delta exposure. Flag intraday limit breaches.
Throughout day Dialogue with PM on new ideas, sizing, exits.
4:30 PM End-of-day reconciliation. Updated risk reports. Overnight exposure review.
Weekly Portfolio deep-dive: factor exposures, concentration, stress scenarios, correlation with systematic book.

What Makes You Valuable

  1. Quantitative fluency: You understand the risk system, can speak the quant language to the CRO.
  2. Human judgment: You can read the PM's behavior, catch biases, push back constructively.
  3. Bridge credibility: You help the discretionary PM build trust with the systematic-oriented leadership.
  4. Risk culture ambassador: You bring the firm's rigorous risk discipline to a team that might otherwise operate more loosely.

PART 5: HYBRID MODELS AND THE FUTURE

The Industry Trend

The boundary between systematic and discretionary is blurring:

Trend Example
Discretionary PMs using quant tools Factor-based screening, sentiment analysis, alt data
Systematic funds adding human overlays Quant models + discretionary override in extreme events
"Quantamental" Fundamental research enhanced by data science and machine learning
Multi-strategy platforms Citadel, Millennium, Point72 — run both systematic and discretionary pods

What This Means for You

  • Don't position yourself as JUST a discretionary RM.
  • Show that you understand both worlds.
  • Emphasize your ability to use quantitative tools to support discretionary decision-making.
  • The future RM is quantitatively sophisticated but emotionally intelligent.

PART 6: KEY DIFFERENCES IN RISK EVENTS

August 2007 Quant Quake — Systematic Risk Event

  • Multiple quant funds had similar factor models.
  • One fund started unwinding → positions were correlated across funds → cascade.
  • All selling the same names → prices crash → more margin calls → more selling.
  • Lesson: CROWDING is existential risk for systematic funds.
  • RM response: Monitor strategy overlap with industry. Measure uniqueness of signals.

Archegos (March 2021) — Discretionary Risk Event

  • Bill Hwang ran concentrated, leveraged positions through total return swaps.
  • Positions were invisible (swaps don't appear in 13F filings).
  • When one position (ViacomCBS) dropped, margin calls triggered unwind of everything.
  • Banks lost $10B+: Credit Suisse, Nomura.
  • Lesson: Concentration + leverage + opacity = catastrophic risk.
  • RM response: Monitor concentration limits, leverage, transparency to prime brokers.

COVID March 2020 — Both

  • Systematic: models had never seen -30% in 3 weeks. Many signals broke.
  • Discretionary: PMs who held through were rewarded by the massive recovery. PMs who panic-sold locked in losses.
  • RM lesson: Stress tests must include unprecedented scenarios. The PM who has a plan BEFORE the crisis makes better decisions during it.

PART 7: INTERVIEW APPLICATION

If asked "Why do you want to do discretionary risk management specifically, especially at a systematic firm?"

"Discretionary RM is where judgment matters most. In a systematic setup, the model handles most risk decisions — the RM is a monitor. In discretionary, I'm in daily dialogue with the PM, pushing back on sizing, flagging concentration, running scenario analysis. That human element is what excites me.

Being at Squarepoint specifically is an advantage because the risk infrastructure is world-class — built for systematic strategies where precision matters. I get to use that quantitative toolkit to support a discretionary PM. For example, I can run factor decomposition on the PM's portfolio to show exposure they might not see, or run systematic-style stress tests on a 40-position concentrated book.

I'm also aware that this desk is new, which means the RM role goes beyond monitoring — it's about building credibility with the firm's leadership, establishing the risk framework for a new strategy, and acting as the bridge between the discretionary PM's fundamental thinking and the firm's quantitative culture. That's a unique opportunity."
