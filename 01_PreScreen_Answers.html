"Sure — I like to start a bit further back than most. I was a national judoka with the French team from age 14 to 22. Eight years of competing at the highest level taught me very young how to perform under pressure, balance multiple commitments simultaneously, and maintain discipline over long time horizons. From there I joined ECE Paris, where I chose to study quantitative finance and engineering because I've always been drawn to understanding markets and their behavior. I complemented that with a second Master's at a top business school, where I studied investment banking, finance, and risk from a more business and markets-oriented perspective — as opposed to the engineering side which focused on the mathematical and technological stack behind it. So I ended up with both the quantitative rigor and the market intuition, which has been a recurring theme in my career.

To conclude my academic path, I did my end-of-studies internship at BNP Paribas in Paris on the OTC commodities derivatives desk as a sales and trading assistant. It was a versatile role where I had the chance to learn from some of Europe's top sales and traders in commodities — BNP has deep expertise in that space. I worked across every commodity sub-class: oil, natural gas, power, emissions, base metals, precious metals, and ags. Day to day, I was responsible for writing the morning and evening market commentary, which went out daily to the four thousand clients of the desk. That commentary served a critical function — it provided market color that clients would come back to us with during the day, asking for trade ideas, hedging solutions, or how BNP could structure something specific. It was a key step of the desk's commercial activity. To support that workflow, I implemented a Python and VBA data pipeline pulling prices, open interest, volume, and reports from Bloomberg to standardize the data processing and deliver a more accurate, faster picture of the market each day. I was also pricing Asian and European options on refined products. That experience gave me a deep understanding of the commodities market — the mechanics, the flows, the market intuition that comes from sitting that close to traders and salespeople every day.

Fast forward — I moved to BNP Paribas in New York on the DEC and FXPB business line in what I like to call a quant swiss army knife role. The business line was about 35 people, organized into a high-touch traders team, sales traders for direct market access, corporate and institutional sales, a collateral management team, and finally the front-office risk team — which is where I primarily sat. My role was to work alongside the risk managers across all asset classes on the listed side and to build out, maintain, and improve the entire risk framework and infrastructure of the desk — both on the technical side, meaning automated reports, portfolio analysis, stress testing, and risk analytics across all asset classes, and on the process side, meaning fire drills, the liquidation framework, operational risk, and conduct risk for the front office. I was responsible for all code in production.

In that capacity, I implemented a live stress-testing model that significantly increased our efficiency and granularity — it helped us understand exactly what risk we were facing and why certain positions or portfolios were costing more in collateral. It was also used to provide delta and vega hedging color to clients on commodities portfolios. Beyond risk, I assisted the entire business line — traders, sales, collateral management — wherever quantitative work was needed. For example, I worked alongside the traders on several projects, one of which was an options skew scanner for SOFR and US Treasuries that came from a client request. The goal was to highlight day-over-day changes in open interest, delta, and gamma, and to present in a concise, trader-friendly format where the convexity sat for delta and gamma exposure and where net short gamma was most concentrated. That project had me collaborating extensively with market data providers, traders, and senior management in a high-stakes environment.

This role was exceptionally formative for me. It showed me firsthand what risk management is and made me deeply interested in it. It also clarified the kind of environment I want to work in — a hedge fund environment with the pace, the cross-team collaboration, and the high-stakes work. Risk, to me, sits at the intersection of quantitative rigor and judgment — which is exactly how I've built my professional experience. I was fortunate to work with a mentor who had over 20 years of experience at CME, ICE, and Goldman Sachs, and he told me that risk is the art of being proactively ready for when things turn sour. That resonated with me deeply, and it's how I've been working ever since.

Currently, I'm at Natixis in Paris as a cross-asset quant analyst — what I call a bridge role. After my VIE contract at BNP in New York ended, I had a cooling-off period, and my goal has always been to move back to New York and work in risk at the heart of finance — where collaboration, precision, and curiosity come together. Natixis allows me to keep working on critical skills, sharpen my market intuition, and learn from experienced individuals across all asset classes. But I call it a bridge because this Squarepoint role is exactly what I had in mind as the next step after my New York experience — building risk frameworks for a discretionary team, embedded with traders and researchers, back in the environment where I do my best work."

That means the discretionary team has access to tools, data, and quantitative rigor that most discretionary funds simply don't have. The combination of systematic infrastructure with human judgment on the discretionary side is exactly how I think about risk — intuition backed by evidence.

Third — and I'll be direct — I spent almost two years in New York and it's where I do my best work. The pace, the intensity, the proximity to the deepest capital markets in the world. Funny enough, I actually used to live across the street from your New York office — so I know the neighborhood well. I want to be back in that environment, and Squarepoint is where I want to be."

POTENTIAL CURVEBALL QUESTIONS + ANSWERS

"How do you understand the role?" / "What do you think this job involves?"

"From what I've read and how I understand it — this is a junior risk manager sitting entirely on the discretionary side of the business. Not systematic, not algorithmic — human-driven strategies where PMs and researchers formulate views and express them through positions.

The core of the job is making sure the fund makes money without blowing up. In practice that means six things:

First, evaluating and monitoring risk on every strategy — before a trade goes on and while it's live. Not just market risk either — the JD explicitly mentions operational risk and conduct risk, so process controls and model governance matter too.

Second, enforcing the Investment Committee's risk budget. The IC sets the allocation, and my job is to make sure nobody drifts beyond their mandate. That's where the hardest conversations happen — the trader wants to size up, I need to hold the line or escalate.

Third, analyzing risk and performance at the portfolio level — not just position by position, but the holistic picture. What's driving PnL, where is risk concentrating, are we inadvertently correlated across desks, what happens under stress.

Fourth, helping with portfolio construction from a risk lens. I'm not the PM — but I'm flagging concentration, hidden correlations, gaps in hedging, and where diversification is thin.

Fifth, building out the risk framework. This is an expanding team, so part of the role is building processes, controls, infrastructure, and reporting that make risk management scalable as the discretionary business grows.

And sixth, doing all of this embedded with the quants, researchers, and traders — leveraging Squarepoint's research platform, speaking their language. This isn't back-office risk that sends a report at 5pm. The value I add depends on understanding what they're doing and translating it into a risk view they respect."

"What do you know about risk management for discretionary strategies?"

"In systematic, risk is largely automated — you set hard limits, kill switches, drawdown thresholds, and the system enforces them. Discretionary is fundamentally different because the trades are thesis-driven. A PM puts on a position because they have a view, so the risk manager needs to understand the thesis to judge whether the risk is intentional and sized appropriately versus accidental.

In practice, that means monitoring exposures in real time, stress-testing against scenarios that could invalidate the thesis — not just generic VaR shocks but scenario-specific ones — identifying concentration and hidden correlation across strategies, and enforcing the IC's risk allocation. The hard part is the human element: you need to have the technical credibility to challenge a trader's sizing and the judgment to know when a risk is worth taking versus when it's excessive. You're not there to say no to everything — you're there to make sure the fund takes better risk.

The other big difference is that discretionary books can have path-dependent optionality — a PM might be right on direction but wrong on timing, and the risk manager needs to distinguish between a position that's under temporary mark-to-market pressure versus one where the thesis is actually broken."
