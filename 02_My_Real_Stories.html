BULLET 1: Cross-Asset Risk Analytics Framework (~€20bn notional)

Resume line: Architected cross-asset risk analytics framework spanning Rates, Equities, FX & Commodities (~€20bn notional); in implementation with unified Greeks aggregation across 5 desks

The Story (60-90 seconds):

"At Natixis, I'm part of the team building a cross-asset risk analytics framework covering approximately €20 billion in notional across Rates, Equities, FX, Commodities, and Credit. My primary contribution has been on the commodities side — I understand the products and risk conventions from my BNP experience, so I've been working on the normalization layer for commodities Greeks, ensuring they aggregate consistently with the other asset classes. I've also been contributing to the cross-asset stress testing scenarios, particularly around commodity-rates correlation dynamics — something I have direct experience with from my work at BNP in New York."

What the framework actually does:
- Each desk (Rates, Equities, FX, Commodities, Credit) computes Greeks differently — DV01 for rates, % Delta for equities, $/unit for commodities
- The normalization layer translates all Deltas/Vegas into a common language: P&L impact of a 1 standard deviation move in each risk factor
- Aggregation engine (Python + Polars) groups by risk factor and sums across desks to get portfolio-level Greeks
- Cross-asset stress tests capture correlation effects — "risk-off" scenario shocks all asset classes simultaneously

If probed — "What was the hardest normalization problem?":

"Reconciling how Delta is defined across asset classes. The rates desk uses DV01 in basis points, commodities uses dollar P&L per unit move. We standardized everything to P&L impact of a 1 standard deviation historical shock per risk factor, which made aggregation meaningful."

If probed — "What technology?":

"Python with Polars for the aggregation — significantly faster than Pandas at this scale. kdb+ for time-series storage of historical risk metrics."

Escape route → NYC:

"The cross-asset framework at Natixis is still in build-out, but the concept is very similar to what I was doing at BNP in New York where I built the stress testing model across IR and commodities books — except at Natixis the scope is broader, covering 5 asset classes."

BULLET 2: SABR Volatility Surface Engine (15bps RMSE improvement)

Resume line: Designed and implemented a production-ready SABR volatility surface engine (C++ calibration core, Python API) for multi-asset options; achieved 15 bps RMSE improvement validated in back-testing vs legacy system

The Story (60-90 seconds):

"At Natixis, I worked on the implementation and validation of a SABR volatility surface engine that replaced a legacy vol model. The C++ calibration core was built collaboratively — I contributed to the Python API layer and ran the full validation framework. I back-tested the SABR engine against 18 months of historical data across multiple asset classes, measuring RMSE of implied vol fits against market-observed prices. The result was a 15 basis point improvement in fit accuracy. My role was primarily on the validation, back-testing, and commodities-specific configuration — for example, choosing appropriate β values for different commodity curves and ensuring the calibration was stable for products with limited liquid strike data."

If probed — "Explain SABR":

"SABR stands for Stochastic Alpha Beta Rho. It models the forward price and its volatility as two correlated stochastic processes. Four parameters: α sets the overall vol level, β controls the CEV backbone — we fixed it at 0.5 for rates, closer to 1.0 for equities — ρ controls the skew, and ν controls the smile curvature, the vol-of-vol. We calibrate α, ρ, ν to market data using Levenberg-Marquardt optimization on each expiry slice, minimizing the squared difference between model and market implied vols."

If probed — "How was the 15bps measured?":

"We took 18 months of historical market data. For each day, calibrated both the legacy model and SABR to end-of-day market vols, computed the error at each strike, then took RMSE across all strikes and days. Legacy had about 45bps RMSE, SABR brought it to 30bps."

If probed — "What are the limitations?":

"Three main ones. First, the Hagan approximation can produce negative implied vols at very low strikes — we applied corrections for that. Second, SABR calibrates each expiry independently, so you can get calendar spread arbitrage without post-calibration smoothing. Third, there are no jumps — it's pure diffusion, so it can underestimate tail event probability."

Escape route → NYC:

"The SABR work deepened my understanding of vol surface construction, which matters for risk management because if you're running risk on an options portfolio, you need to understand where your vol model breaks down. At BNP in New York, I was on the other side — using vol surfaces daily for stress testing and Greeks computation on billion-dollar books."

BULLET 3: Cross-Asset Correlation Monitor (15% drawdown reduction)

Resume line: Built cross-asset correlation monitor (Python, Polars, kdb+); detected gold-UST yield decoupling during Q4 2025 volatility, providing analysis that supported 15% portfolio drawdown reduction

The Story (60-90 seconds):

"I built a cross-asset correlation monitor as part of the broader risk analytics framework. It computes rolling correlations — 30, 60, 90-day windows — across major asset pairs using Python and Polars, with historical data stored in kdb+. The most significant signal it flagged was the gold-US Treasury yield decoupling in Q4 2025. Historically that correlation is about negative 0.3 to negative 0.5 — yields down, gold up, both safe-haven flows. The monitor flagged it turning positive, which was a 3-sigma event versus 10-year history. I investigated the drivers — primarily central bank gold buying from China, Russia, and the Middle East as part of de-dollarization flows — and presented the analysis with portfolio impact estimates. That analysis contributed to hedging adjustments that reduced potential drawdown by approximately 15%."

If probed — "How did you measure the 15%?":

"We computed the portfolio P&L under the new correlation regime assuming the old hedge allocation versus the P&L with the adjusted allocation. The difference was approximately 15% of the expected drawdown in a rates-reversal scenario."

If probed — "What statistical tests?":

"Two main ones. A rolling Z-score — current correlation minus historical mean, divided by historical standard deviation — anything above 2 or 3 sigma gets flagged. And a Chow breakpoint test to confirm whether it's a genuine structural break versus transient noise."

If probed — "What pairs do you monitor?":

"The key ones: gold vs. US 10Y yields, oil vs. USD, equities vs. credit spreads, equities vs. VIX, commodities vs. rates. Anything where a correlation assumption underpins a hedging relationship in the portfolio."

Escape route → NYC:

"Correlation regime monitoring is something I think about constantly — at BNP in New York I saw firsthand how cross-asset correlations shift during stress events when I was running the stress testing model on our IR and commodities books."

BULLET 4: FRTB-SA / Sensitivity-Based Method (€5M capital relief)

Resume line: Led FRTB-SA quantitative workstream; recalibrated Sensitivity-Based Method (SBM) charges; analysis quantified €5M+ potential capital relief

The Story (60-90 seconds):

"I led a workstream within the FRTB-SA implementation focused on the Sensitivity-Based Method. My work was decomposing the total SBM charge by risk class — GIRR, Commodity, Equity, FX, CSR — and by component — Delta, Vega, Curvature — to identify where capital charges were higher than necessary. I found that the commodity Delta charges were over-estimated because the sensitivity computation was using bump sizes larger than required by the regulation, which was capturing Gamma effects in what should be a pure Delta charge. After recalibrating the bump methodology and optimizing the risk factor mapping, the analysis quantified approximately €5 million in potential capital relief. The findings were submitted for internal model validation review."

If probed — "Explain SBM":

"SBM computes capital in three layers. Delta charge — take Delta sensitivity per prescribed risk factor, multiply by regulatory risk weights, aggregate using prescribed correlation matrices. Vega charge — same structure but for vol sensitivities. Curvature charge — shock each factor up and down, compute P&L, strip out the Delta-explained component, and what's left is the nonlinear risk charge. Total SBM is the sum of all three, computed per risk class then summed across classes."

If probed — "Where specifically did the €5M come from?":

"The biggest saving came from the commodity space where I had the deepest domain knowledge. The existing implementation used a standard 10% bump across all commodities, but the regulation prescribes specific shock sizes per commodity type. By aligning to the exact regulatory specification, we avoided double-counting convexity in the Delta charge. There were also savings from better risk factor mapping — positions that should net within the same regulatory bucket were being split across buckets, losing diversification benefit."

If probed — "What's the difference between SA and IMA?":

"SA is prescriptive — the regulator tells you the risk weights, correlations, and formulas. IMA lets you use your own internal models, typically resulting in lower capital charges, but you need regulatory approval for each desk. Most banks use SA as the baseline and try to qualify specific desks for IMA where the capital saving justifies the compliance cost."

Escape route → Why Squarepoint:

"The FRTB work gave me a deep understanding of regulatory risk frameworks, but honestly, what excites me about Squarepoint is the buy-side framing — instead of computing capital charges for regulators, I want to compute risk allocations for an Investment Committee. The analytical framework is similar but the purpose is fundamentally different."
