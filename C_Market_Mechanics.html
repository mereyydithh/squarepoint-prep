C — MARKET MECHANICS

How markets actually work: trade lifecycle, margin, leverage, liquidity

Depth: 🟡 WELL

PART 1: TRADE LIFECYCLE

The Journey of a Trade (Equities)

1. Order placement → 2. Order routing → 3. Execution → 4. Clearing → 5. Settlement

Step 1: Order Placement

PM decides to buy 50,000 shares of XOM. Sends the order to the execution desk (or electronic algo).

Step 2: Order Routing

Order goes to the market. Multiple venues: NYSE, NASDAQ, dark pools, electronic crossing networks. Best execution obligation means the broker must seek the best available price.

Step 3: Execution

The order matches with a seller. Execution can be:
- Market order: Execute now at current best price. Guaranteed execution, not guaranteed price.
- Limit order: Execute only at my price or better. Guaranteed price, not guaranteed execution.
- Algo execution: VWAP, TWAP, iceberg — break into smaller orders to minimize market impact.

Step 4: Clearing

The clearinghouse (e.g., DTCC for US equities, LCH for swaps, CME for futures) steps between buyer and seller. It becomes:
- Buyer to the seller
- Seller to the buyer

This eliminates bilateral counterparty risk. Both sides now face the CCP, not each other.

Step 5: Settlement

Actual delivery of shares and cash. US equities: T+1 (trade date + 1 business day). Used to be T+2. Shortened in May 2024.

PART 2: MARGIN — THE MECHANICS OF LEVERAGE

Three Types of Margin

Initial Margin (IM)

The deposit required to OPEN a position. Think of it as a performance bond.
- Futures: typically 5-15% of notional.
- Options: depends on the position (long options: premium paid; short options: margin required).
- OTC derivatives: per CSA agreement.

Variation Margin (VM)

Daily mark-to-market adjustment. If the position moves against you, you OWE variation margin. If it moves for you, you RECEIVE variation margin.
- Futures: settled daily by the clearinghouse.
- OTC (bilateral): per CSA, usually daily with minimum transfer amounts.

Maintenance Margin

The minimum margin level you must maintain. If your account falls below this → margin call.
- Example: IM = $100K, maintenance = $75K. If your losses bring the account to $70K → margin call for $30K (back to IM level, not maintenance).

The Margin Call Cascade

This is how funds die:

Position moves against you
  → Account falls below maintenance margin
    → Margin call: post more cash by [deadline, usually same day]
      → You don't have cash → sell other positions to raise it
        → Selling pressure pushes those positions down
          → MORE margin calls on those positions
            → Spiral → Forced liquidation

March 2020 Treasury basis trade: this exact cascade. Leveraged 50-100x, margin requirements spiked, forced selling of Treasuries → Fed had to intervene.

Pro-Cyclical Margin

In calm markets: exchanges reduce margin requirements (lower vol = lower risk). In volatile markets: exchanges INCREASE margin requirements (higher vol = higher risk).

The problem: they increase margin exactly when you can LEAST afford it. You need more cash precisely when your positions are losing money and liquidity is drying up.

PART 3: LEVERAGE

How Hedge Funds Get Leverage

Method How it works Typical leverage
Prime broker financing Borrow cash from PB to buy more securities. Pay a financing rate (SOFR + spread). 1.5-5x for discretionary
Futures Post 5-15% margin for 100% notional exposure 7-20x on the futures position
Options (selling) Collect premium but bear full notional risk Varies widely
Total return swaps Synthetic exposure, post only margin (not full notional) 3-10x
Repo Borrow cash using securities as collateral 10-50x for government bonds

Measuring Leverage

Gross leverage = (Long notional + Short notional) / NAV
- A fund with $100M NAV, $200M longs, $150M shorts → gross leverage = 3.5x
- Tells you TOTAL market exposure.

Net leverage = (Long notional - Short notional) / NAV
- Same fund: net leverage = 0.5x (net long $50M on $100M NAV)
- Tells you DIRECTIONAL exposure.

Why both matter:
- High gross + low net = "market neutral" — lots of positions but little directional risk. But still exposed to idiosyncratic, factor, and liquidity risk.
- High gross + high net = directional AND leveraged. Maximum risk.

Why Leverage Kills

The math is simple:
- 2x leverage: a 10% move in the underlying → 20% move in your equity. A 50% decline in the underlying → 100% of your equity → wiped.
- 10x leverage: a 10% decline in the underlying → 100% of your equity → wiped.

Leverage amplifies BOTH gains and losses. But losses are more dangerous because:
1. You can always make more money. You can't un-lose your capital.
2. Drawdowns require outsized recoveries: -50% requires +100% to get back to even.
3. Margin calls force you to sell at the WORST time (when prices are lowest).

PART 4: LIQUIDITY

Two Types of Liquidity

Market Liquidity

Can you buy/sell the asset quickly without moving the price?

Measured by:
- Bid-ask spread: Tight spread = liquid. Wide spread = illiquid.
- Depth: How much size is available at the current price? (Level 2 order book)
- Market impact: How much does YOUR trade move the price?
- Average daily volume (ADV): How much normally trades. If your position is >10% of ADV, you'll move the price exiting.

Funding Liquidity

Can you access cash/credit to meet obligations (margin calls, redemptions)?

Measured by:
- Cash on hand / credit lines
- Repo availability
- PB willingness to extend margin
- LP redemption terms (gates, lock-ups)

The Liquidity Spiral (Brunnermeier)

Market liquidity and funding liquidity are connected in a doom loop:

Market becomes illiquid (bid-ask widens, depth drops)
  → Position mark-to-market drops
    → Funding pressure (margin calls, LPs redeem)
      → Forced selling
        → Selling pressure makes market MORE illiquid
          → Further price drops
            → More funding pressure → SPIRAL

This happened in 2008, March 2020, and multiple other crises. The key insight: liquidity dries up exactly when you need it most.

Liquidity Risk for a Discretionary Fund

  • Position sizing relative to ADV: If your position is 20% of ADV, it'll take ~4-5 days of 5% ADV to exit without excessive market impact. In stress, ADV drops 50%+ → your exit takes 2x longer.
  • Liquidity tiering: Classify every position: Tier 1 (exit in 1 day), Tier 2 (exit in 3-5 days), Tier 3 (exit in 1 week+). Monitor the mix.
  • Redemption mismatch: If the fund offers quarterly redemptions but holds positions that take 2 weeks to liquidate → liquidity mismatch. The fund can be forced to sell at fire-sale prices.

Dealer Positioning & Order Flow

Market makers (dealers) provide liquidity but manage their own risk:
- Dealer long gamma: Dealers who sold puts are short gamma → they need to sell as the market drops (to hedge). This AMPLIFIES downward moves.
- Dealer short gamma: Dealers who bought puts from clients are long gamma → they buy dips and sell rallies. This DAMPENS moves and provides stability.
- Gamma exposure flip level: The price at which dealers switch from long to short gamma. Below this level, market moves become larger and faster.
- End-of-month/quarter rebalancing: Pension funds, target-date funds rebalance. Large, predictable flows that move markets at MOC.

PART 5: MARKET MICROSTRUCTURE

Price Discovery

How markets find the "right" price:
- Limit order book: Bids and asks arranged by price/time priority. The highest bid and lowest ask define the spread.
- Dark pools: Orders matched privately. No pre-trade transparency. Used for large institutional orders to avoid market impact. ~40% of US equity volume.
- Auction mechanisms: Opening/closing auctions concentrate liquidity at key times.

Intraday Patterns

Time What happens Why it matters
9:30-10:00 AM Opening volatility. Overnight order flow, gap fills. Don't overreact to first 30 minutes. Wait for real flow.
10:00 AM Econ data releases (CPI, PMI at 10 AM). Can reverse the opening move entirely.
11:30-1:30 PM Lunch lull. Low volume, thin liquidity. Don't trust moves in thin markets. Susceptible to manipulation.
3:00-3:50 PM Power hour. Real institutional flow appears. More informative than morning moves.
3:50-4:00 PM MOC imbalance published. Rebalancing flow. Can create large last-minute moves. Fixed income / pension driven.

Circuit Breakers

Automatic halts when markets move too fast:
- Level 1: SPX -7% → 15-minute halt (before 3:25 PM only)
- Level 2: SPX -13% → 15-minute halt (before 3:25 PM only)
- Level 3: SPX -20% → market closes for the day
- Single stock: LULD (Limit Up/Limit Down) bands. Stock halts if price moves outside a defined % from reference price.

These exist to prevent panic selling and give participants time to reassess. They were triggered during COVID (March 2020) — 4 Level 1 halts in 2 weeks.

PART 6: SHORT SELLING MECHANICS

How Shorting Works

  1. Locate: Find shares to borrow (from the PB's inventory or securities lending desks).
  2. Borrow: Borrow shares. Pay a borrow fee (annualized, varies from 0.5% for easy-to-borrow to 50%+ for hard-to-borrow).
  3. Sell: Sell the borrowed shares in the market.
  4. Cover: Buy back shares later (hopefully at a lower price) and return them to the lender.

Short Selling Risks

  • Unlimited loss: A stock can go up infinitely. There's no natural cap on losses.
  • Short squeeze: Stock price rises → shorts forced to cover → buying pressure pushes price higher → more shorts forced to cover → cascade.
  • Recall risk: The lender can recall the shares at any time. You're forced to buy back at market price.
  • Borrow cost: High borrow costs eat into your short P&L. A stock with 30% annualized borrow costs needs to drop 30%+ just for you to break even.
  • Reg SHO / uptick rule: Regulatory restrictions on when/how you can short.

PART 7: INTERVIEW APPLICATION

If asked "How does margin work and why does it matter for risk?":

"Margin is the collateral you post to hold a position — it creates leverage. The key distinction is initial margin (required to open the position) and variation margin (daily mark-to-market settlements).

What makes margin dangerous for risk is the pro-cyclical nature: exchanges increase margin requirements when volatility spikes. So you need to post MORE cash exactly when your positions are losing money and markets are illiquid. This creates a cascade: margin call → forced selling → more price declines → more margin calls. This is exactly what happened in March 2020 with the Treasury basis trade — highly leveraged positions faced margin calls they couldn't meet, forcing liquidation into an illiquid market, until the Fed stepped in.

As an RM, I'd monitor margin utilization as a percentage of available credit — not just current margin, but projected margin under a vol spike scenario. If we're at 80% utilization in calm markets, we have no cushion for a vol event."
