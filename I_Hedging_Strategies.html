I — HEDGING STRATEGIES: THE ART OF CONTROLLED RISK TRANSFER

Puts, collars, spreads, futures, pairs, cross-asset, tail hedges, dynamic hedging, hedge Greeks, roll management, and the RM's cost-benefit framework

Depth: 🔴 DEEP

WHY HEDGING IS THE RM'S HIGHEST-VALUE SKILL

At Squarepoint, PMs are paid to take risk. The RM's job is NOT to eliminate risk — it's to ensure risk is INTENTIONAL, SIZED CORRECTLY, and SURVIVABLE. Hedging is the mechanism that converts "we're exposed and hoping" into "we're exposed and protected at defined cost."

The RM adds value in three ways:

  1. Making hidden costs explicit — "This collar is 'free' but you're giving up $3M of potential upside. Is that the trade?"
  2. Challenging false hedges — "Your SPX put covers 7% of a 15% loss on your tech-heavy book. You're 53% hedged. Is that acceptable?"
  3. Timing the hedge — "Vol is at the 15th percentile. Protection is cheap. NOW is when to buy — not after the sell-off when VIX is 35 and puts cost 3×."

PART 1: THE FIVE TRADEOFFS — EVERY HEDGE HAS A PRICE

One-liner: There is no free hedge. Every form of protection extracts payment in some currency — premium, upside, basis risk, complexity, or illiquidity. The RM's job is to make the currency explicit.

Tradeoff The tension What the PM needs to decide
Cost vs protection Better protection = more premium. Deeper OTM = cheaper but further from current price. "How much annual drag can you absorb in your Sharpe?"
Precision vs liquidity Single-name put = perfect hedge but expensive/illiquid. Index put = liquid/cheap but basis risk. "Do you need exact coverage or directional protection?"
Horizon mismatch Position has no expiry. Hedge expires. Roll costs compound. "Are you hedging an event (1 week) or a regime (6 months)?"
Upside surrender Collars and call overwriting cap gains. Zero-cost structures mean the cost IS the cap. "What maximum return would you accept in exchange for zero premium?"
Timing Cheapest when you don't need it (low vol). Most expensive when you do (crisis vol). "Are you willing to pay insurance when the sun is shining?"

The cost of NOT hedging:

If you don't hedge… What happens when the tail hits
No puts, no tail protection Full drawdown. Potential forced liquidation. Career risk.
No basis-adjusted hedge Hedge covers 50% of the loss. You thought you were protected. You weren't.
No roll plan Hedge expires unnoticed. Position runs unprotected for days/weeks.

PART 2: THE HEDGING DECISION FRAMEWORK

One-liner: Before picking an instrument, diagnose the disease. A put doesn't help if the risk is basis. A futures hedge doesn't help if the risk is convexity. Match the hedge to the SPECIFIC risk.

Five Questions Before Every Hedge

1. What SPECIFIC risk am I hedging?

Risk type The exposure Right hedge family Wrong hedge
Directional (delta) Stock goes down Puts, futures, shorts VIX calls (wrong risk)
Volatility (vega) IV drops, long options lose value Sell vol, vega-neutral structures Equity futures (wrong Greek)
Convexity (gamma) Short gamma: large moves kill you Buy options (go long gamma) Futures (linear, no convexity)
Rate risk (DV01) Yields rise, bond portfolio loses Rate futures, IRS Equity puts (wrong asset)
Credit (CS01) Spreads widen CDS, short HY ETF Treasury hedge (misses credit)
Concentration 15% of NAV in one name Single-name puts, partial exit Index hedge (basis risk)
Tail / left-tail 5σ event Deep OTM puts, VIX calls ATM puts (expensive for tail defense)
Basis Hedge and position diverge Better correlated instrument More of the same wrong hedge
Liquidity Can't exit in stress Cash buffer, fewer illiquid positions No derivative fixes this

2. What horizon?

Horizon Use case Instruments Roll frequency
1-5 days Earnings, FOMC, event Weekly options, overnight futures None — expires
1-4 weeks Near-term uncertainty Monthly puts/collars None or once
1-6 months Medium-term thesis adjustment Quarterly options, futures strips Roll quarterly
6M-permanent Structural portfolio protection LEAPs, rolling put spreads, tail program Roll semi-annually

3. How much upside am I willing to surrender?

Upside tolerance Structure Cost
Surrender nothing Outright puts Full premium (1-3% annualized for ATM)
Cap at +X% Collar (buy put, sell call at +X%) Reduced or zero net premium
Cap protection at −Y% Put spread (buy put, sell lower put) ~50% of outright put cost
Both cap upside AND cap protection Collar + put spread combo Near-zero cost, bounded up and down

4. What's the premium budget?

Rule of thumb for hedge cost relative to expected alpha:

Strategy alpha Max hedge cost (% of NAV/year) Rationale
2-4% 0.3-0.5% Hedge can't eat >15% of alpha
5-8% 0.5-1.5% Room for meaningful protection
10%+ 1.5-3.0% Can afford genuine tail protection

If premium budget = $0: The PM is choosing between (a) upside surrender (collar), (b) accepting basis risk (imperfect hedge), or (c) no hedge at all. There is no fourth option. Make this explicit.

5. What basis risk am I accepting?

Basis risk hierarchy — from best to worst hedge precision:

Hedge instrument Basis risk Cost
Single-name put on exact position Zero Most expensive
Sector ETF put (XLE for XOM) Low-moderate Less expensive
Index put (SPX for any equity) Moderate-high Cheapest, most liquid
Cross-asset (TLT for equity) High (correlation-dependent) Variable

Quantifying basis risk:

$$\text{Hedge effectiveness} = R^2 = 1 - \frac{\text{Var(hedged portfolio)}}{\text{Var(unhedged portfolio)}}$$

$$\text{Optimal hedge ratio} = h^* = \rho \times \frac{\sigma_{\text{position}}}{\sigma_{\text{hedge}}}$$

If ρ = 0.75 between XOM and XLE, optimal hedge ratio = 0.75 × (σ_XOM/σ_XLE), NOT 1:1.

PART 3: HEDGING INSTRUMENTS — THE FULL TOOLKIT

1. Outright Puts

What: Buy put options on the asset or index. Pure downside protection with defined cost.

$$\text{Put cost} = f(\text{strike, time, IV, rates, dividends})$$

Structure:

P&L
  │       ╱ Unhedged
  │      ╱
  │     ╱
──┼────╱──────────── Stock price
  │   ╱  ╱ Hedged (long stock + put)
  │  ╱  ╱
  │ ╱  ╱
  │╱__╱   ← Floor at strike − premium
  │

Worked example — SPX at 5,400:
- Buy SPX 5,300 put (1.9% OTM), 3-month expiry, IV = 16%
- Cost: ~$45 per contract (~$4,500 per contract at 100 multiplier)
- 100 contracts protecting $54M of equity → cost = $450K = 0.83% of protected notional (annualized ~3.3%)
- Protection: below 5,300, every $1 of SPX drop → $100 per contract recovered (100 contracts = $10K per point)
- Maximum loss on protected portfolio: (5,400 − 5,300) × $100 × 100 + $450K premium = $1,450K instead of unlimited

Pros Cons
Clean — defined cost, no upside surrender Expensive — theta bleeds every day
Works through gaps (overnight, weekend) Need to roll repeatedly → compound cost
Asymmetric: defined loss, unlimited gain If vol is high when you buy → overpaying
No margin requirement (you're buying) Premium is sunk — lost even if market rallies

When to use: Event protection (earnings, FOMC), known-horizon hedges, when PM refuses to cap upside.

2. Put Spreads (Bear Put Spread)

What: Buy a put at strike K₁ + sell a put at lower strike K₂. Protection between K₁ and K₂ only.

P&L
  │
  │        ╱ Unhedged
  │       ╱
──┼──────╱────────────── Stock price
  │     ╱   ╱ Hedged (put spread)
  │    ╱   ╱
  │   ╱___╱  ← Capped protection
  │  ╱       (exposed below K₂)
  │ ╱

Worked example — SPX at 5,400:
- Buy SPX 5,300 put ($45), sell SPX 5,100 put ($20). Net cost = $25 per contract ($2,500).
- Protection: 200 points between 5,300 and 5,100 = $20K per contract
- Max payout: $20K − $2.5K = $17.5K per contract (7:1 payoff ratio)
- Below 5,100: no additional protection — you're exposed again
- Cost: 55% cheaper than outright put

Pros Cons
~50% cheaper than outright put Capped protection — exposed in a crash
Still defined-cost, known max loss If SPX drops 15%, the 200-point coverage isn't enough
Good for "normal correction" hedging Not a tail hedge — doesn't protect against the worst scenarios

When to use: Budget-constrained, hedging against 3-5% corrections, when the PM believes >10% drops are unlikely or acceptable.

RM judgment call: "A put spread protects you in a correction but NOT in a crash. If SPX drops 20%, you're covered for the first 200 points and naked for the next 880. Is that what you want?"

3. Collars

What: Long stock + buy OTM put + sell OTM call. Call premium finances the put.

Payoff
  │            _____ ← Capped at call strike
  │           ╱
  │          ╱
──┼─────────╱────────── Stock price
  │        ╱
  │ ______╱  ← Floor at put strike
  │

Worked example — XOM at $113:
- Buy $108 put ($3.20), sell $120 call ($2.80). Net cost = $0.40 (nearly zero-cost)
- Below $108: hedged. Max loss = $5.40/share ($113 − $108 + $0.40 premium)
- Above $120: capped. Even if XOM → $150, you get $120
- Between $108-$120: normal exposure minus the $0.40 cost

Zero-cost collar: Adjust strikes until put cost = call premium. E.g., buy $107 put, sell $119 call → exactly zero cost. The cost is ENTIRELY in upside surrender.

Pros Cons
Near-zero or zero premium cost Caps upside — this IS the cost, just in a different currency
PM budget-friendly If the stock rallies through the call strike → PM is frustrated and blames RM
Clear bounded outcome (min and max returns) In trending markets, the collar is a drag on performance

When to use: PM says "I don't want to pay for protection." Collar makes the tradeoff explicit: protection costs upside, not premium.

RM warning: "A 'free' collar isn't free — you're paying with the gain between $120 and wherever XOM would have gone. If XOM rallies to $130, this collar cost you $10/share. That's $500K on 50,000 shares."

4. Futures Hedging

What: Sell index or sector futures to reduce portfolio delta/beta. Linear instrument — no convexity.

Beta hedge sizing:

$$N_{\text{contracts}} = \frac{V_p \times (\beta_{\text{current}} - \beta_{\text{target}})}{V_f}$$

Where $V_p$ = portfolio value, $V_f$ = futures notional per contract.

Worked example:
- Portfolio: $80M, β = 1.3. Target: β = 0.5. Reduction: 0.8
- ES futures (E-mini SPX): SPX at 5,400 → $50 × 5,400 = $270K per contract
- Contracts = ($80M × 0.8) / $270K = 237 contracts short
- Cost: margin only (~$15K per contract initial margin = $3.6M). No premium.

DV01 hedging (for rates):

$$N_{\text{contracts}} = \frac{\text{Portfolio DV01}}{\text{Futures DV01 per contract}}$$

Example: Portfolio DV01 = $50K/bp. 10Y Treasury futures DV01 = $75/bp per contract.
Contracts = $50K / $75 = 667 contracts short

Pros Cons
Most liquid instruments on earth (ES, ZN, ZB) Linear: no floor — you give up ALL downside AND upside
No premium — cost is only margin + roll You can still lose unlimited on the hedged portfolio if basis moves
Instant execution, tight spreads Margin drain — uses capital that could be deployed elsewhere
Easy to size precisely Futures have expiry → roll risk (quarterly for equity, monthly for some)

When to use: Temporary delta reduction (overnight, over weekend), beta hedging, rate duration hedging, quick risk-off.

When NOT to use: As a substitute for options when convexity matters. Futures don't protect against GAP risk — if SPX opens −5%, your delta hedge was computed at yesterday's close and you're already behind.

5. Pairs / Relative Value Hedging

What: Long one asset + short a correlated asset. Isolate the idiosyncratic (alpha) bet, hedge the systematic (factor) exposure.

Hedge ratio for a pair:

$$h^* = \frac{\text{Cov}(R_{\text{long}}, R_{\text{short}})}{\text{Var}(R_{\text{short}})} = \beta_{\text{long vs short}}$$

Worked example — Long XOM / Short CVX (oil pair):
- Long $10M XOM, β_XOM_vs_CVX = 0.85
- Hedge: Short $8.5M CVX (= $10M × 0.85)
- The pair is MARKET-NEUTRAL on oil — moves in crude affect both positions similarly
- Alpha bet: XOM will outperform CVX (better management, better assets, etc.)
- $1.5M of "unhedged" residual = the idiosyncratic bet the PM is making

Risk What goes wrong How to mitigate
Basis risk XOM and CVX diverge for non-fundamental reasons (index reconstitution, ETF flows) Monitor rolling correlation. If ρ drops below 0.6, hedge ratio is stale.
Short borrow CVX becomes hard-to-borrow → borrow cost spikes or shares recalled Check utilization rate before entering. Diversify across PBs.
Crowding Every L/S fund runs the same pair → synchronized unwind Check 13F overlap. High overlap = crowded.
Leverage L/S pairs use 2× capital (long + short) for gross exposure Size by NET risk, not gross dollars.

6. Cross-Asset Hedging

What: Hedge one asset class's risk using a DIFFERENT asset class, exploiting cross-asset correlations.

Risk being hedged Cross-asset hedge Mechanism Correlation regime
Equity sell-off Long UST (TLT, ZN, ZB) Flight to quality: stocks ↓ → bonds ↑ Usually works. FAILED in 2022 (stocks-bonds both fell).
Equity sell-off Long gold (GLD, GC futures) Safe haven — uncorrelated in moderate stress Works in geopolitical crisis. May fail if real rates dominate.
Equity crash Long VIX calls / short VIX puts VIX spikes in crashes — convex hedge Reliable in acute crashes. Expensive to carry (contango).
Inflation surge Long TIPS / commodities Direct inflation sensitivity Works for unexpected inflation. Not for expected (already priced).
Dollar weakness Long non-USD assets / gold Inverse USD correlation Generally reliable but unstable.
China/EM contagion Long put on FXI, short AUD AUD proxies China risk via trade linkage Moderate. AUD-CHN correlation can break.

CRITICAL WARNING — Correlation flips:

Regime Stocks-bonds ρ Strategy implication
Deflation fear / growth scare (1998-2021) −0.30 Bonds are a great equity hedge
Inflation regime (2022-2023) +0.40 Bonds AMPLIFY equity losses — hedge becomes additional risk
Financial stress (March 2020 briefly) +0.50 (both sold) Everything correlates to 1 — only cash protects

RM responsibility: Monitor the stocks-bonds correlation WEEKLY. If it's been positive for >3 months, any portfolio relying on Treasury duration as an equity hedge is structurally broken. Alert the CRO.

7. Tail Hedges — Existential Protection

What: Deep OTM protection designed to pay off ONLY in extreme scenarios (>3σ events). The insurance policy for the fund's survival.

Tail Hedge Structures

Structure How it works Cost (annualized) Crisis payoff Complexity
Deep OTM puts (5-10% OTM, 3M) Simple floor 1.5-3.0% 5-20× Low
Put ratio spread (buy 1 ATM, sell 2-3 OTM) Cheaper entry, magnified protection in moderate drop, but creates naked short below lower strikes 0.3-0.8% 3-8× (then reverses!) Medium — DANGEROUS if too far OTM strikes breached
Put ladder (buy at 5%, 10%, 15% OTM) Layered protection across severity levels 2.0-4.0% Covers full crash spectrum Medium
VIX call spreads (buy 30 strike, sell 50 strike) Convex payoff in panic. Cheap carry. 0.5-1.5% 3-10× Medium
Variance swap (long var) Pure vol bet. Convex: payoff scales with vol² Carry cost Enormous in crashes High
SPX/VIX call ratio Buy 2 VIX calls + sell 1 SPX put to finance ~0% 4-8× in vol spike High
Systematic tail program Rolling 3M 5% OTM puts, rebalanced monthly 1.5-2.5% Consistent 10-15× Low (if automated)

The Tail Hedge Cost-Benefit Math

Expected value framework:

$$\text{EV} = P(\text{tail event}) \times \text{Hedge payoff} - (1 - P(\text{tail event})) \times \text{Annual cost}$$

Example:
- Annual cost of tail program: $3M (1.5% of $200M fund)
- Probability of 3σ+ event per year: ~5% (historically)
- Hedge payoff in 3σ event: $60M (10× cost, protecting against $80M+ drawdown)
- EV = 5% × $60M − 95% × $3M = $3.0M − $2.85M = +$0.15M → slightly positive expected value
- PLUS: the hedge prevents forced liquidation, keeps PMs in seats, preserves investor relationships → unquantifiable survival value

The RM's pitch to the CIO: "The tail hedge costs us ~40bp of Sharpe annually. But it prevents the −30% drawdown that would trigger $100M of redemptions, cost two PMs their jobs, and take 3 years to recover from. The math is almost break-even on direct EV, but the survival value is enormous."

PART 4: DYNAMIC HEDGING — ADJUSTING AS THE MARKET MOVES

One-liner: Static hedging is "set and forget." Dynamic hedging is "adjust as conditions change." Both have costs — dynamic hedging costs execution/slippage; static hedging costs missed opportunities to optimize.

Delta Hedging — The Core Dynamic Strategy

Continuous delta hedging (Black-Scholes ideal):
- Sell $Δ of stock for every 1 long call (or vice versa)
- As spot moves → Δ changes → rebalance
- Long gamma: sell as market rises, buy as it falls → BUY LOW, SELL HIGH
- Short gamma: buy as market rises, sell as it falls → BUY HIGH, SELL LOW (adverse selection)

The gamma-theta tradeoff in delta hedging:

$$\text{P\&L}_{\text{delta-hedged}} = \frac{1}{2} \Gamma (\Delta S)^2 - \Theta \Delta t$$

If the underlying moves MORE than the gamma-theta breakeven → long gamma wins.
If it moves LESS → short gamma wins (collects theta).

Breakeven daily move:

$$\Delta S_{\text{BE}} = \sqrt{\frac{2 |\Theta|}{\Gamma}} = \sigma_{\text{implied}} \times S \times \sqrt{\Delta t}$$

This is exactly the implied vol translated to a dollar move. If you're delta-hedging a long option position, you're betting that REALIZED vol will exceed IMPLIED vol. That's the entire game of gamma scalping.

Rebalancing Triggers

Trigger type Rule When to use Pros Cons
Time-based Rebalance every N hours/days Routine, predictable Easy to implement May miss sharp intraday moves
Delta-band Rebalance when Δ drift > $X or X% Responsive to big moves
VaR trigger Rebalance when VaR exceeds Y% of limit Risk-based — responds to true risk Tighter control Requires real-time VaR computation
Event-based Rebalance before/after earnings, FOMC, data releases Proactive risk management Targeted Doesn't help with surprise events
Drawdown-based Add protection when DD > Z% of soft limit Capital-preserving Prevents spiral Reactive — may be too late in fast market

Hedge Adjustment as Greeks Change

As the underlying moves, your hedge Greeks change — especially if you're hedging with options:

Move What happens to your hedge Action needed
Stock rallies 5% Put moves further OTM → delta ↓, gamma ↓, vega ↓ Put becomes less effective. Consider rolling up the strike.
Stock drops 5% Put moves closer to ATM → delta ↑, gamma ↑ Put becomes MORE effective. Good. May want to take profit and re-establish at new lower strike.
Vol spikes 10 pts Put value increases (good for holder). Delta increases. Hedge ratio changed. May need to sell some futures delta because put now covers more.
Time passes (1 month) Theta decays. Gamma concentrates near strike. Vega ↓. Less time, less protection. Roll decision approaching. Monitoring intensifies.

Stop-Loss vs Options — Head-to-Head

Feature Stop-loss Put option
Cost Free (no premium) Premium ($)
Gap protection None — stock can open below stop Full — put pays through gaps
Whipsaw risk High — triggered then market reverses None — put is always in force until expiry
Certainty of execution Uncertain in illiquid names Guaranteed at expiry
Unlimited re-entry Yes (re-enter for free) Need to buy new put
Max loss guarantee No (gap risk) Yes (strike − spot + premium)
Best use case Liquid names, slow markets Illiquid names, event risk, overnight risk, gap risk

RM guidance: "Use stops for liquid, unlevered positions where gaps are unlikely. Use options for concentrated, leveraged, or illiquid positions where a gap could breach the drawdown limit."

PART 5: HEDGE GREEKS — HOW YOUR HEDGE CHANGES YOUR PORTFOLIO RISK

One-liner: Adding a hedge doesn't just reduce risk — it changes your ENTIRE Greek profile. A put doesn't just add delta protection — it adds gamma, theta, vega, and cross-Greeks that interact with your existing book.

Greek Impact of Common Hedges

Hedge Δ (delta) Γ (gamma) Θ (theta) ν (vega) Net effect on portfolio
Long put Negative (reduces net Δ) Positive (adds convexity) Negative (costs theta) Positive (benefits from vol spike) Safer book. Costs carry. More convex.
Put spread Negative (moderate) Mixed (long near + short far) Less negative than outright Mixed (partial vega) Cheaper. Less convex. Capped protection.
Collar Negative (put) + Positive (short call) ≈ modest net Gamma ≈ 0 (long and short cancel) ~Zero (theta of put ≈ theta of call) ~Zero Budget-neutral. Bounded outcomes. Near-zero Greek change.
Short futures Negative (reduces net Δ) Zero Zero Zero Pure delta. No convexity benefit. No cost.
VIX calls Near-zero directly Near-zero on SPX Negative (theta cost) Positive (benefits from VIX rise) Vol hedge, not delta hedge. Expensive carry.
Short equity (pair) Negative on short leg Zero (equity is linear) Zero Zero Reduces net delta. Introduces basis risk + borrow cost.

Key insight: A PM who hedges with INDEX FUTURES gets delta reduction but NO GAMMA. In a gap-down, the futures hedge responds linearly while the portfolio may gap nonlinearly (concentrated names, options). A PM who hedges with PUTS gets delta + gamma + vega — the hedge IMPROVES as the crash deepens (gamma kicks in, vega benefits from vol spike). This is why puts cost more — you're buying convexity that futures don't provide.

The Hedge P&L Under Stress

Scenario SPX −5% SPX −10% SPX −20%
No hedge −$4.0M −$8.0M −$16.0M
Futures hedge (50%) −$2.0M −$4.0M −$8.0M
ATM put −$1.5M −$1.0M +$2.0M (put in the money, vega spike helps)
5% OTM put spread (200pt wide) −$3.5M −$2.0M −$6.0M (protection capped at 200 pts)
Collar −$2.2M −$1.8M −$1.8M (floor at put strike)

The convexity payoff is visible: The ATM put MAKES money in a crash because gamma acceleration + vega spike exceed the initial theta cost. Futures just reduce exposure linearly. The deeper the crash, the greater the advantage of convex (option) hedges over linear (futures) hedges.

PART 6: ROLL MANAGEMENT — THE HIDDEN COST

One-liner: Rolling a hedge is not neutral — you're closing one position at a loss (theta decayed) and opening a new one at market price (potentially higher IV). The roll is where hedge cost compounds.

The Mechanics

Rolling: Close the expiring hedge instrument and open a new one with a later expiry.

$$\text{Roll cost} = \text{Close cost (selling decayed hedge)} + \text{Open cost (buying new hedge at current IV)} + \text{Bid-ask friction}$$

Roll Cost by Instrument

Instrument Roll frequency Typical cost per roll Annualized drag
Monthly OTM puts 12×/year 0.15-0.30% of protected notional 1.8-3.6% → EXPENSIVE
Quarterly OTM puts 4×/year 0.35-0.60% per roll 1.4-2.4%
6-month puts 2×/year 0.60-1.00% per roll 1.2-2.0% → more efficient
Equity index futures (ES) 4×/year ~1-2bp spread per roll ~0.05% → negligible
Treasury futures 4×/year (quarterly) 1-3bp per roll ~0.05%
VIX futures Monthly Contango roll cost: 3-5%/month in steep contango 30-50%+ → catastrophic for long positions
Variance swaps At maturity (3M, 6M, 1Y) Mark-to-market at close, re-enter at new IV Variable

Key insight: Monthly put rolling costs 2-3× more than quarterly. Monthly VIX rolling can destroy 30%+ of notional annually through contango. ALWAYS calculate the annualized roll cost before committing to a hedge program.

Roll Timing Optimization

Strategy When to roll Advantage Disadvantage
Calendar roll Fixed date (e.g., 3rd Friday) Predictable, auditable May roll at terrible vol levels
Vol-based roll Roll when IV dips below target Buy protection cheap May not get the opportunity — IV stays high
Delta-based roll Roll when hedge delta has decayed to <X% of initial Maintains protection level More complex to monitor
Expiry-based Roll at N days before expiry Avoids rapid theta decay near expiry Standard approach

RM best practice: Roll at ~2 weeks before expiry (for monthlies) or ~3 weeks (for quarterlies). This avoids the sharpest theta decay zone while maintaining sufficient time value.

PART 7: HEDGE EFFECTIVENESS — IS THE HEDGE ACTUALLY WORKING?

One-liner: A hedge that doesn't perform in stress is worse than no hedge — because it created FALSE CONFIDENCE that the position was protected.

Measuring Hedge Effectiveness

R² (Variance Reduction)

$$R^2 = 1 - \frac{\text{Var(hedged portfolio)}}{\text{Var(unhedged portfolio)}}$$

R² Interpretation Action
>0.90 Excellent — hedge covers >90% of risk Maintain
0.70-0.90 Good — meaningful residual basis risk Monitor basis, consider tighter instrument
0.50-0.70 Mediocre — half the risk is unhedged Evaluate: is the cost justified for partial protection?
<0.50 Poor — this is not a hedge, it's a separate position Replace instrument or remove

Conditional Hedge Effectiveness

More important than overall R²: how does the hedge perform specifically in the tail?

$$R^2_{\text{stress}} = 1 - \frac{\text{Var(hedged P\&L} \mid \text{stress days)}}{\text{Var(unhedged P\&L} \mid \text{stress days)}}$$

A hedge with R² = 0.85 overall but R²_stress = 0.40 is MISLEADING — it works in normal markets but fails when you need it most. This is the typical failure mode of cross-asset hedges (stocks-bonds in 2022).

Hit Rate and Payoff Ratio

For options hedges:
- Hit rate: What % of hedges expire with value? (For 5% OTM puts: ~20-30% historically)
- Payoff ratio: When the hedge hits, how much does it pay relative to cost? (Should be >3× to justify the cost over many cycles)

$$\text{Break-even} = \text{Hit rate must be at least} = \frac{1}{1 + \text{Payoff ratio}}$$

If payoff ratio = 5×: need hit rate > 1/6 = 17% → for most tail hedges, this is achievable.

PART 8: COMMON HEDGING MISTAKES — AND HOW THE RM PREVENTS THEM

Mistake What happens How to prevent
Hedging too late Buying puts when VIX = 35. Premium is 3× normal. Buy when vol is LOW. Build hedge program during calm. Track VIX percentile.
Over-hedging Portfolio can't make money. Theta drag > alpha. Size hedges to protect against DRAWDOWN LIMIT, not against all movement.
Wrong instrument (basis risk) SPX put on a tech-heavy book → basis = 40% of the drop isn't covered Quantify basis: R² < 0.70 → find a better instrument
Set-and-forget Put expires unnoticed. PM runs unprotected for 2 weeks during rollover. Roll calendar with automated alerts. No gap in coverage.
Ignoring roll costs Monthly put buying costs 3% annualized. Eats most of the alpha. Use quarterly rolls. Calculate annualized drag before committing.
"Free" hedges that aren't free Zero-cost collar caps $5M of upside on a $50M position. PM blames RM when stock rallies past cap. Quantify upside surrender in DOLLARS. Get PM's written acknowledgment.
Hedging the wrong Greek PM hedges delta with futures but real exposure is gamma/vega Match hedge to the SPECIFIC Greek. Futures = delta only. Puts = delta + gamma + vega.

PART 10: INTERVIEW APPLICATION

"How would you hedge a concentrated equity position?"

"I'd start with the framework: what specific risk, what horizon, what premium budget, and how much upside is the PM willing to surrender.

For a single-name concentration — say 15% of NAV in XOM — the cleanest hedge is a single-name XOM put. That's delta + gamma + vega in one instrument — the hedge IMPROVES as the crash deepens because gamma kicks in and vol spikes. But it's expensive: roughly 3-4% annualized for an ATM put.

To reduce cost: a put spread — buy the $108 put, sell the $100 put — halves the premium but caps protection at $8 per share. Or a collar — buy $108 put, sell $120 call — near-zero cost but caps upside at $120.

If the PM refuses both premium and upside surrender, we can partially beta-hedge with SPX futures, but I'd flag the basis risk: XOM-to-SPX R² is about 0.6, so 40% of XOM's risk isn't covered by the SPX hedge. In a scenario where XOM drops 15% on company-specific news while SPX drops only 2%, the futures hedge covers 2 of 15 percentage points.

The key value I add is making the tradeoff explicit: 'You can pay $400K for full protection, pay zero but cap upside at 6%, or accept 40% basis risk with a free futures hedge. Which do you prefer?' That's a conscious decision, not a hope."

"When would you NOT hedge?"

"Three situations. First, when the hedge costs more than the risk warrants — if a tail event would cost the fund $2M but protecting against it costs $1.5M annually, the expected value is negative. Second, when the hedge introduces MORE risk than it removes — a cross-asset hedge relying on a correlation that's unstable can turn a $5M loss into a $10M loss if the correlation flips. I'd rather size the position smaller than rely on a fragile hedge. Third, when the PM's thesis IS the risk — if the PM is explicitly making a directional bet and it's within risk limits, hedging the direction removes the trade itself. The RM's job isn't to eliminate risk — it's to ensure risk is intentional and survivable."

"How do you evaluate whether a hedge is working?"

"Two levels. First, statistical: I'd compute R² — the proportion of position variance explained by the hedge. If R² is below 0.70, the hedge is covering less than 70% of the risk — it might not justify its cost. Second, and more importantly, CONDITIONAL R² — how does the hedge perform specifically on stress days? A hedge with R² = 0.85 overall but R² = 0.40 on days when SPX is down >2% is a false promise. It works in normal markets and fails in crises. I'd also track cumulative hedge P&L versus cumulative position P&L over time — if the hedge is consistently LOSING without the position GAINING, the basis has drifted and the instrument needs to be reassessed."

"Explain the tradeoff between puts and futures as hedges."

"Futures are free, liquid, and precise for delta — the best instrument for temporary risk reduction. But they're LINEAR. In a gap-down, the futures hedge responds proportionally while the position might behave nonlinearly — especially if you have concentrated names or options. Futures give you no convexity benefit.

Puts cost premium, but they provide delta PLUS gamma PLUS vega. As the market drops: the put's delta increases (gamma); if vol spikes (which it virtually always does in a crash), the put gains additional value (vega). The hedge literally gets BETTER as the crash deepens. In a −20% crash, a put hedge can turn profitable — the convexity payoff exceeds the initial cost. Futures hedge will recover exactly 50% of the loss (if you hedged 50%) — never more, never less.

The decision template: use futures for temporary, measured delta reduction when you want the cheapest, most liquid solution. Use puts when you need gap protection, asymmetric payoff, or convexity — and you're willing to pay for it."
