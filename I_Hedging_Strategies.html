I — HEDGING STRATEGIES

How to protect a portfolio — puts, collars, spreads, futures, pairs, and when to use each

Depth: 🔴 DEEP

PART 1: WHY HEDGING IS HARD

Hedging sounds simple: "buy protection." In practice, every hedge involves tradeoffs:

Tradeoff Tension
Cost vs protection Better protection = more expensive. Zero-cost structures cap your upside.
Precision vs liquidity A perfect hedge (single-name put) is expensive and illiquid. A liquid hedge (SPX put) has basis risk.
Horizon mismatch Your position has no fixed expiry. Your hedge does. Roll costs compound.
Upside surrender Collars and put spreads cap your gains in exchange for cheaper protection.
Timing Hedges are cheapest when you don't need them (low vol) and most expensive when you do (high vol).

The RM's job isn't to say "hedge everything." It's to help the PM make CONSCIOUS decisions about which risks to keep and which to transfer.

PART 2: THE HEDGING DECISION FRAMEWORK

Before choosing an instrument, answer these five questions:

1. What specific risk am I hedging?

Not "the portfolio" — the SPECIFIC exposure. Is it:
- Directional equity risk (delta)?
- Volatility risk (vega)?
- Rate risk (duration/DV01)?
- Sector concentration?
- Single-name event risk (earnings)?
- Tail risk (5-sigma events)?

Different risks require different hedges.

2. What horizon?

  • 1-day to 1-week: Overnight event, earnings. → Short-dated options.
  • 1-3 months: Medium-term view adjustment. → Monthly puts/collars, futures.
  • Permanent: Structural portfolio protection. → Rolling put spreads, tail hedges.

3. How much upside am I willing to give up?

  • None: Buy puts outright. Pay premium.
  • Some: Collar (buy put, sell call). Reduce cost but cap upside.
  • Don't care about upside: Sell the position. The cheapest hedge is no position.

4. What can I afford?

  • Premium budget: how much theta bleed is acceptable?
  • Rule of thumb: tail hedges cost 0.5-2% of NAV annually. If your expected alpha is 5%, spending 2% on protection leaves 3% net.

5. What basis risk am I accepting?

  • Hedging XOM with XLE has basis risk (XOM vs sector).
  • Hedging XOM with SPX has even more basis risk.
  • Hedging with a perfect single-name put has zero basis risk but maximum cost.

PART 3: HEDGING INSTRUMENTS

1. Outright Puts

What: Buy put options on the asset or index.

When to use:
- Hedging a specific downside level
- Event protection (earnings, FOMC)
- When you want asymmetric protection (defined cost, unlimited protection)

Pros: Clean, defined cost, no upside surrender.
Cons: Expensive. Theta decay. Need to roll repeatedly for ongoing protection.

Example: Long SPX. Buy SPX 5300 put for $12.00.
- Cost: $1,200 per contract. For 100 contracts = $120K.
- Protection: below 5300, you're hedged dollar-for-dollar (minus premium). Above 5300, you keep all upside.
- Breakeven: SPX at 5288 (strike minus premium).

2. Put Spreads

What: Buy a put + sell a lower-strike put. Cheaper than outright puts but caps the protection.

When to use:
- Want downside protection but on a budget
- Believe the tail risk below a certain level is acceptable or unlikely
- Hedging against a "normal" correction, not a crash

Example: Buy SPX 5300 put, sell SPX 5100 put.
- Cost: $12.00 - $6.00 = $6.00 net. Half the cost of the outright.
- Protection: between 5300 and 5100 (200 points = $20K per contract).
- Below 5100: no additional protection. You're exposed again.
- Trade: you saved 50% on premium but gave up crash protection below 5100.

3. Collars

What: Long stock + buy put + sell call. The call premium finances (partially or fully) the put.

When to use:
- PM wants protection but refuses to pay premium
- Acceptable to cap upside in exchange for downside protection
- Earnings or event protection where the PM is happy with a bounded outcome

Zero-cost collar: choose strikes so put cost = call premium received.

Example: Long XOM at $113. Buy $108 put, sell $118 call. Net cost: ~$0.
- Below $108: hedged. Max loss = $5 per share.
- Above $118: gains capped. Even if XOM goes to $130, you get $118.
- Between $108-$118: normal exposure.

4. Futures Hedging

What: Sell futures against a long equity position to reduce delta.

When to use:
- Quick, liquid delta reduction
- Temporary risk reduction (overnight, over a weekend)
- Beta hedging: sell SPX futures to reduce portfolio beta

Pros: Very liquid, no premium, instant execution.
Cons: Linear — no protection floor, you give up ALL downside AND upside proportionally. Margin required.

Beta hedge calculation:
$$\text{Contracts} = \frac{\text{Portfolio value} \times \beta_{\text{target change}}}{\text{Futures notional per contract}}$$

Portfolio = $50M, beta = 1.2, want beta = 0.7.
Beta reduction needed = 0.5. SPX future notional = ~$270K (5400 × $50).
Contracts = ($50M × 0.5) / $270K ≈ 93 contracts short.

5. Pairs / Relative Value Hedging

What: Long one stock, short a correlated stock or ETF. Isolate the idiosyncratic bet, hedge the common factor.

When to use:
- PM has a view on XOM outperforming CVX, but not on oil direction
- Sector-neutral: long best stock, short worst stock in same sector
- Reduce beta to sector or market

Risks:
- Basis risk: the pair can diverge for non-fundamental reasons (flows, technicals).
- Short borrow cost: the short leg has borrow fees.
- Correlation can break: in stress, the pair can move in the same direction with different magnitudes.

6. Cross-Asset Hedges

What: Hedge equity risk using a different asset class.

Equity risk Cross-asset hedge How it works
Market sell-off Long TLT (long duration Treasuries) Flight to quality: stocks down → bonds up
Inflation spike Long commodity futures or TIPS Commodities and TIPS benefit from inflation
Dollar weakness Long gold or non-USD assets Gold inversely correlated with USD
Vol spike Long VIX calls VIX spikes when equities sell off

Warning: Cross-asset hedges rely on CORRELATIONS which can flip. Stocks-bonds correlation was negative 2000-2021 but flipped positive in 2022. If you hedge equities with bonds and both sell off → double loss.

7. Tail Hedges

What: Deep OTM puts or VIX calls designed to pay off only in extreme events (>3 sigma).

When to use:
- Fund-level protection against existential risk
- When you want convex payoff: small, known cost, potentially enormous payout

The cost problem: Tail hedges bleed theta constantly. Buying 5% OTM SPX puts every month costs ~1-2% of NAV annually. Most months they expire worthless. But in March 2020, they paid 20-50x.

Tail hedge structures:
- Deep OTM put: simple, expensive over time
- Put ladder: buy several strikes in the tail (5%, 10%, 15% OTM)
- 1x2 put spread: buy 1 ATM put, sell 2 OTM puts. Cheap but creates a short put position deeper OTM
- VIX call spreads: cheaper than SPX puts, convex payoff in panic, but VIX doesn't track portfolio precisely

PART 4: DYNAMIC HEDGING

What It Is

Adjusting the hedge as the market moves, rather than setting it and forgetting it.

Delta Hedging

  • Continuously adjust futures or stock position to keep portfolio delta at target.
  • If you sell as the market rises and buy as it falls → you're long gamma (spending theta).
  • If you buy as it rises and sell as it falls → you're short gamma (you're the one providing liquidity — dangerous).

Hedge Rebalancing Triggers

Don't rebalance on a schedule — rebalance on TRIGGERS:

Trigger Action
Delta drift >$5M from target Rebalance back to target
VaR breach (>90% of limit) Add protection aggressively
Drawdown >50% of soft limit Reduce gross exposure + add hedges
Correlation regime change Reassess which hedges actually work
Major event (FOMC, earnings cluster) Add short-term event protection

Stop-Loss vs Options

  • Stop-loss: Sell the position if it hits a price level. Free (no premium), but vulnerable to gaps (stock opens below your stop after overnight news) and whipsaws (stock hits stop, you sell, it reverses immediately).
  • Options: Cost premium, but protection works through gaps. An OTM put protects you even if the stock gaps down 20% overnight — unlike a stop-loss which would have been triggered at the gap.
  • Recommendation: Use stops for liquid instruments in normal markets. Use options for illiquid positions, overnight/event risk, and when gaps are a realistic concern.

PART 5: HEDGE EFFECTIVENESS — AM I ACTUALLY HEDGED?

How to Measure

Hedge Ratio

$$h = \frac{\text{Change in hedge value}}{\text{Change in position value}}$$

Perfect hedge: h = -1 (hedge gains $1 for every $1 the position loses). In practice, h is never -1.

R-squared of Hedge vs Position

Run a regression of hedge P&L against position P&L. R² tells you how much of the position's variance is captured.
- R² > 0.8: good hedge
- R² = 0.5: mediocre — half the risk is unhedged
- R² < 0.3: this isn't really a hedge

P&L in Stress

The true test: how did the hedge perform during the actual stress event? If the portfolio dropped 8% and the hedge offset 6% → effective. If the hedge offset 2% → basis risk dominated.

PART 6: COMMON HEDGING MISTAKES

Mistake What happens Fix
Hedging too late (after vol spikes) Protection is 3x more expensive than a week ago Buy protection when it's cheap (vol is low), not when you're scared
Over-hedging Portfolio can't make money; paying excessive theta Size hedges to protect against drawdown limits, not against all movement
Wrong instrument (basis risk) Hedge doesn't move with the position in stress Use the most correlated instrument, even if more expensive
Set-and-forget Hedge expires, position is unprotected Calendar reminders for expiries, rolling plan
Ignoring roll costs Monthly put buying costs 15-20% annualized Use longer-dated options to reduce roll frequency
"Free" hedges that aren't free Zero-cost collars cap upside — that IS the cost Quantify the upside surrender and make sure the PM accepts it

PART 7: INTERVIEW APPLICATION

If asked "How would you hedge a concentrated equity position?":

"It depends on the PM's preferences and constraints. I'd start with the framework: what specific risk are we hedging, what horizon, what's the premium budget, and how much upside are we willing to surrender?

For a single-name concentration like 15% of the book in XOM: the cleanest hedge is an XOM put, but that's expensive — maybe 3-4% annualized. A put spread (buy the $108 put, sell the $100 put) halves the cost but caps protection at $8 per share. A collar (buy $108 put, sell $118 call) can be near-zero cost but caps upside at $118.

If the PM refuses to pay premium or surrender upside, we can partially hedge with SPX futures to reduce beta — but that introduces basis risk because XOM doesn't move 1:1 with SPX.

The key conversation is making the PM aware of the tradeoffs: 'You can have free protection but it caps your upside at 5%. Or you can pay $400K and keep all your upside. Which do you prefer?' That's the RM adding value — making the cost explicit so the decision is conscious."
