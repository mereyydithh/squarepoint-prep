Q — CROSS-ASSET RELATIONSHIPS

Cascade chains, correlation regimes, flight to quality, risk-on/risk-off, and how everything connects

Depth: 🔴 DEEP

PART 1: WHY CROSS-ASSET THINKING MATTERS

A discretionary risk manager who watches only equities is blind. Markets are connected. A move in oil changes inflation expectations, which changes rate expectations, which changes equity valuations, which changes credit spreads, which changes EM currencies. Understanding these cascades is what separates a risk manager from a position monitor.

The fundamental question: "If X moves, what else moves — and in what order?"

PART 2: THE MAJOR TRANSMISSION CHAINS

Chain 1: Oil → Inflation → Rates → Equities → Credit

Oil spike → CPI rises → Fed holds/hikes → Bond yields up
         → Growth slows → Earnings compress → Equities de-rate
         → Credit spreads widen → Leveraged borrowers struggle
         → EM importers crushed → FX depreciation → Capital flight

Speed of transmission:
- Oil → inflation expectations: hours (TIPS breakevens move instantly)
- Oil → realized CPI: 4-8 weeks (monthly data lag)
- Higher CPI → rate expectations: same day (Fed Funds futures reprice)
- Rate expectations → equity multiples: days to weeks
- Equity weakness → credit spreads: days to weeks
- Credit tightening → real economy: 3-12 months

Real example — 2022:
1. Russia invades Ukraine (Feb 24) → Brent from $90 to $130 in 2 weeks
2. CPI prints 8.5%, 8.3%, 8.6% → Fed goes from 25bp to 75bp hikes
3. 10Y yield: 1.5% → 4.2% in 9 months
4. S&P 500: -25% peak to trough
5. HY credit spreads: 300bp → 600bp
6. Crypto collapse, EM FX sell-off, UK mini-budget pension crisis

Chain 2: Rate Shock → Duration → FX → EM Contagion

Unexpected rate move → Long-duration assets reprice (bonds, growth stocks, real estate)
                     → USD strengthens (rate differential widens)
                     → EM currencies weaken (capital exits)
                     → EM central banks forced to hike (defend currency)
                     → EM growth collapses → default risk rises → contagion

Key mechanism: The "dollar wrecking ball." When US rates rise unexpectedly, capital flows to USD assets. EM countries with USD-denominated debt face a double hit: weaker currency + higher debt service costs.

Chain 3: Risk-Off Cascade

Shock event → VIX spikes → Dealers hedge (buy protection, sell equity)
           → Margin calls → Forced selling of liquid assets
           → Correlation spikes → Diversification fails
           → Credit freezes → Funding stress → Redemptions
           → More forced selling → Death spiral

March 2020 sequence:
1. COVID spreads → VIX: 13 → 82 in 3 weeks
2. Margin calls across all asset classes
3. Even Treasuries sold (dash for cash) — BROKE the bonds-as-hedge assumption
4. Fed intervened with unlimited QE, lending facilities, corporate bond buying
5. Everything recovered — but only because of central bank backstop

Chain 4: Carry Trade Unwind

Low-rate currency (JPY, CHF) borrowed → invested in high-rate assets (EM, HY)
Rate differential narrows OR vol spikes →
     → Carry trade unprofitable → Unwind (sell HY/EM, buy JPY/CHF)
     → JPY/CHF spike → More carry trades go underwater → Accelerating unwind
     → EM assets sold → EM FX crashes → EM central banks intervene

July-August 2024 example: BOJ hiked rates + US recession fears → JPY strengthened → carry unwind → Nikkei -12% in one day → global equity sell-off → VIX spiked to 65 intraday.

Key insight: Carry trades are "picking up pennies in front of a steamroller." They earn small, steady returns then collapse violently. The RM must monitor JPY, CHF, VIX together as carry-trade risk indicators.

The Critical Regime Shift: Stocks-Bonds Correlation

This is the most important correlation for portfolio construction:

  • 2000-2021: Negative. Stocks down → bonds up. Bonds were a natural hedge. 60/40 worked.
  • 2022-present: Positive in inflation episodes. Stocks AND bonds sell off together. 60/40 fails.
  • Why? When inflation drives both asset classes, the Fed must tighten, hurting both equity multiples (higher discount rate) and bond prices (higher yields).

RM implication: If your portfolio uses bonds as a hedge and we're in an inflation regime, YOUR HEDGE DOESN'T WORK. You're doubling down on the same macro bet.

Conditional vs Unconditional Correlation

  • Unconditional: Average correlation over a long period. Useful for normal times.
  • Conditional: Correlation during STRESS. Almost always HIGHER than unconditional.

$$\rho_{\text{stress}} > \rho_{\text{normal}}$$

Why? In crises, everything becomes driven by the same factor (risk appetite), so correlations converge toward 1. This is exactly when you need diversification most — and it fails.

Practical rule: If your VaR model uses a 6-month lookback correlation of 0.3, the actual correlation during a crisis might be 0.7-0.9. Your VaR is LYINGto you about the portfolio's true risk.

PART 4: FLIGHT TO QUALITY

What Happens When Fear Takes Over

Investors sell risky assets and buy safe haven assets:

FROM (sell) TO (buy)
Equities (especially EM, small-cap) US Treasuries, German Bunds, JGBs
High-yield credit Investment-grade / government bonds
EM currencies USD, JPY, CHF
Commodity currencies (AUD, CAD, NOK) Gold
Illiquid assets Cash, money market funds

Safe Haven Hierarchy

  1. US Treasuries: THE global safe haven. Even in 2008, Treasuries rallied as everything fell.
  2. Gold: Traditional safe haven but can fail when rates rise sharply or liquidity is needed (margin calls → sell gold for cash).
  3. JPY: Safe haven due to Japan's net creditor status. Carry unwinds amplify.
  4. CHF: Swiss neutrality + current account surplus.
  5. USD: Reserve currency status. Works except when the US itself is the problem.

When Safe Havens Fail

Scenario What broke
March 2020 (Covid dash for cash) Even Treasuries sold — everyone needed CASH. Correlation went to +1.
2022 inflation Bonds sold off alongside equities. Interest rate risk overwhelmed flight-to-quality.
Gold in real rate spikes Gold falls when real rates rise sharply (opportunity cost of holding zero-yield asset).
JPY in BOJ tightening If BOJ raises rates, JPY strengthens on fundamentals, not safe-haven flow — different dynamic.

PART 5: RISK-ON / RISK-OFF (RORO)

The RORO Framework

Markets increasingly operate in binary mode: "risk on" or "risk off."

PART 7: THE NARRATIVE MATTERS MORE THAN THE DATA

The same data print can cause OPPOSITE reactions depending on the prevailing narrative:

Data "Inflation is sticky" narrative "Soft landing" narrative
Strong NFP (+300K) Equities DOWN (Fed won't cut) Equities UP (economy is fine)
Weak NFP (+100K) Equities UP (Fed can cut now) Equities DOWN (recession coming)
Hot CPI Equities DOWN hard Equities down modest (temporary)
Cool CPI Equities UP hard Equities UP modest (confirming)

RM takeaway: Don't just know the number. Know HOW THE MARKET WILL INTERPRET the number. This requires understanding:
1. What's priced in (consensus estimates vs actual)
2. What narrative is dominant right now
3. Who is positioned how (short gamma dealers amplify, long gamma dampens)

PART 8: DEALER POSITIONING & MARKET MICROSTRUCTURE

How Dealer Gamma Affects Cross-Asset

Options dealers are massively short gamma in SPX options. Their hedging creates cross-asset ripple effects:

Short gamma regime (dealers):
- Market down → dealers sell to hedge → amplifies the move down
- Market up → dealers buy to hedge → amplifies the move up
- Volatility INCREASES in both directions
- Cross-asset cascade is FASTER because dealer hedging is automated and immediate

Long gamma regime (dealers):
- Market down → dealers buy → dampens the move
- Market up → dealers sell → dampens the move
- Volatility is SUPPRESSED
- Markets are "sticky" — harder to get big cross-asset cascades

0DTE options effect: Daily expiring SPX options are now ~50% of volume. They create intraday gamma effects that didn't exist 5 years ago. Pin risk around key strikes acts as intraday magnet.

PART 10: INTERVIEW APPLICATION

If asked "Oil just spiked 8%. Walk me through what you'd check":

"First, I'd quantify our direct oil exposure — energy names long and short, any commodity-linked positions. But the bigger risk is the CASCADE. An 8% oil spike changes everything:

Starting with rates: inflation expectations reprice immediately — I'd check TIPS breakevens and fed funds futures. If the market starts pricing out rate cuts, that hits our duration-sensitive positions: tech, growth, REITs.

Then USD: oil shock usually strengthens the dollar. If we have any EM or non-USD exposure, that's an immediate headwind.

Then equities broader: I'd run a sector attribution. Energy longs are making money, but consumer discretionary, airlines, transports are probably bleeding. What's our NET effect across all these offsets?

Then correlations: in an oil shock, cross-sector correlations spike. Our 'diversified' portfolio might suddenly be 80% correlated. I'd rerun the portfolio stress test with elevated correlations.

Finally, second-order: if oil stays at this level, what does it mean for earnings next quarter? For the Fed decision? For EM economies that import oil? The direct P&L impact matters today, but the positioning implications matter for the next month.

I'd present this cascade to the PM with specific numbers: 'Our direct oil impact is +$X, but our indirect exposure through rates, FX, and correlation is -$Y. Net, we're actually vulnerable. Here are three things we could do.'"
