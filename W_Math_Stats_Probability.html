PART 1: KEY DISTRIBUTIONS — THE BUILDING BLOCKS

Normal (Gaussian) Distribution

$$f(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} \exp\left(-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)$$

Properties: Symmetric, bell-shaped, fully described by mean (μ) and variance (σ²). Sum of independent normals is normal.

Critical quantiles to MEMORIZE:

z-score Tail probability VaR use Finance meaning
1.00 15.87% one-tail — "1-sigma move"
1.28 10% one-tail 90% VaR —
1.645 5% one-tail 95% VaR "Within-day stress"
1.96 2.5% one-tail — 95% CI boundary (two-tail)
2.326 1% one-tail 99% VaR Standard regulatory VaR
2.576 0.5% one-tail 99.5% —
3.09 0.1% one-tail 99.9% "Catastrophic tail"

Sigma ranges:

Range Probability within Practical meaning
±1σ 68.27% "About two-thirds of trading days"
±2σ 95.45% "Once a month event if breached"
±3σ 99.73% "Should see ~1 per year in a daily context"
±4σ 99.994% "Normal says every 126 years; reality ≈ every 2-3 years"

Where normal works: Portfolio returns (CLT), short-horizon log returns for liquid assets, Monte Carlo inputs (as a starting point).

Where normal FAILS: Individual equity returns (fat tails), crisis returns (regime shift), options P&L (non-linear), credit losses (zero-one default events).

Log-Normal Distribution

If $\ln(P_T / P_0) \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$, then $P_T$ is log-normally distributed.

Why prices are modeled as log-normal:
- Prices can't go below zero (mostly — oil in April 2020 proved exceptions exist)
- Returns are multiplicative: $P_T = P_0 \cdot e^{r}$, where $r \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$
- Log-normal is right-skewed: large gains possible, losses bounded at -100%

Geometric Brownian Motion (GBM):

$$dS = \mu S \, dt + \sigma S \, dW$$

This is the foundation of Black-Scholes. It says price changes are proportional to price level (percentage returns, not dollar returns, are the natural unit).

Key relationship (continuous compounding):

$$S_T = S_0 \cdot \exp\left[(\mu - \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2)T + \sigma\sqrt{T} \cdot Z\right]$$

The $-\frac{1}{2}\sigma^2$ correction (the "convexity adjustment") is why the expected future price is NOT $S_0 \cdot e^{\mu T}$ — volatility drags returns down. This matters for long-horizon risk calculations.

Worked example — volatility drag:

Stock has μ = 10% annual return, σ = 30%.
- Arithmetic mean return: 10%
- Expected geometric return: 10% - ½ × (30%)² = 10% - 4.5% = 5.5%
- Over 10 years: $100 × e^(0.055 × 10) = $173 (geometric)
- Naively: $100 × e^(0.10 × 10) = $272 (arithmetic — WRONG for compounded returns)
- Volatility drag costs 37% over a decade at 30% vol

Student's t-Distribution

When to use it: Fat-tailed data or small samples where normal is inappropriate.

Degrees of freedom (ν) Tail behavior Finance application
ν = 3 Very heavy tails; no finite kurtosis Extreme asset classes (crypto, commodities)
ν = 5 Heavy tails; finite kurtosis = 9 Typical equity returns
ν = 10 Moderate tails; kurtosis = 4 FX, bond returns
ν = 30 Approaching normal Diversified portfolio returns
ν → ∞ Converges to normal Textbook (unrealistic)

Practical use: When fitting a VaR model, replace the normal with Student-t. The 99th percentile of a t(5) distribution is ~3.36σ vs. 2.33σ for normal — that's a 44% higher VaR estimate. This is often more realistic.

Exponential Distribution

$$f(x) = \lambda e^{-\lambda x}, \quad x \geq 0$$

  • Mean: $1/\lambda$
  • Memoryless property: $P(X > s + t \mid X > s) = P(X > t)$

Finance application — inter-event times:
If major market dislocations arrive at rate λ = 0.5 per year:
- Expected time between events: 1/0.5 = 2 years
- P(event in next 6 months): $1 - e^{-0.5 \times 0.5}$ = 1 - 0.779 = 22.1%
- P(no event for 3 years): $e^{-0.5 \times 3}$ = 22.3%

Poisson Distribution

$$P(X = k) = \frac{\lambda^k e^{-\lambda}}{k!}$$

Models count of events in a fixed period. Mean = variance = λ.

Finance application — VaR breach counting:

If the VaR model is correct at 99%, expected breaches in 250 days: λ = 2.5.
- P(0 breaches) = e^(-2.5) = 8.2% (model may be too conservative)
- P(exactly 3 breaches) = (2.5³ × e^(-2.5)) / 3! = 21.4% (normal)
- P(≥8 breaches) = 1 - P(X ≤ 7) = 0.9% (model probably broken)

Bernoulli and Binomial

Bernoulli: Single trial with probability p. Mean = p, Variance = p(1-p).

Binomial: n independent Bernoulli trials. $P(X = k) = \binom{n}{k} p^k (1-p)^{n-k}$

Finance application — drawdown probability:

PM has 55% win rate on daily trades. In a 20-day month:
- P(15+ winning days) = $\sum_{k=15}^{20} \binom{20}{k} (0.55)^k (0.45)^{20-k}$ = 6.7%
- P(≤7 winning days) = $\sum_{k=0}^{7} \binom{20}{k} (0.55)^k (0.45)^{20-k}$ = 5.2% (bad month)
- P(losing streak of 5+) requires geometric/Markov analysis — surprisingly common even at 55% win rate

PART 2: PROBABILITY FOUNDATIONS

Bayes' Theorem — The Most Important Formula in Risk

$$P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(B \mid A) \cdot P(A)}{P(B)}$$

In words: Posterior = (Likelihood × Prior) / Evidence

Classic Interview Problem — The Disease Test

A disease affects 1 in 10,000 people. A test is 99% accurate (sensitivity AND specificity). You test positive. Probability you have the disease?

Term Value Meaning
P(disease) = prior 0.0001 Base rate is very low
P(+|disease) = sensitivity 0.99 Test catches 99% of true cases
P(+|no disease) = 1 - specificity 0.01 Test has 1% false positive rate
P(+) = evidence 0.99 × 0.0001 + 0.01 × 0.9999 = 0.010098 Total rate of positive tests

$$P(\text{disease} \mid +) = \frac{0.99 \times 0.0001}{0.010098} = 0.0098 \approx \textbf{0.98\%}$$

Answer: ~1%, NOT 99%. The base rate dominates. A "99% accurate" test with a rare event mostly produces false positives.

Bayesian Updating in Risk

This is DIRECTLY applicable to risk: when a PM has a bad month, how much should you update your belief about their skill?

Worked example — PM evaluation:

  • Prior: You believe there's a 70% chance this PM is skilled (positive alpha).
  • A skilled PM has a bad month (≥2σ drawdown) 5% of the time.
  • An unskilled PM has a bad month 15% of the time.

After observing a bad month:
$$P(\text{skilled} \mid \text{bad month}) = \frac{0.05 \times 0.70}{0.05 \times 0.70 + 0.15 \times 0.30}$$
$$= \frac{0.035}{0.035 + 0.045} = \frac{0.035}{0.08} = \textbf{43.75\%}$$

One bad month drops your confidence from 70% to 44%. Two bad months:
$$P(\text{skilled} \mid 2\text{ bad months}) = \frac{0.05 \times 0.4375}{0.05 \times 0.4375 + 0.15 \times 0.5625} = \frac{0.0219}{0.0219 + 0.0844} = \textbf{20.6\%}$$

After two 2σ drawdowns, you're 80% confident this PM is unskilled. Act accordingly.

Bayesian Thinking in Daily RM

Situation Prior Evidence Updated belief
PM reports +$5M day "PM is average" Unusually large profit Slightly more confident PM is skilled

Stress indicator flashes "Markets are calm" Yield curve inverted Update toward recession probability

Central Limit Theorem (CLT)

Statement: The sample mean of n independent random variables approaches a normal distribution as n → ∞, regardless of the underlying distribution, with:

$$\bar{X} \sim N\left(\mu, \frac{\sigma^2}{n}\right)$$

Standard error of the mean: $\text{SE} = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}$

Why CLT matters for risk:
1. Portfolio diversification: Even if individual stock returns aren't normal, a portfolio of 50+ stocks has returns that are approximately normal (sum of many independent-ish variables). This is why VaR works better for diversified portfolios than for single names.
2. Estimation precision: To halve the standard error of your volatility estimate, you need 4x the data (because SE scales with 1/√n). 1 year of data → SE/2 requires 4 years.
3. CLT failure mode: If returns have infinite variance (some fat-tailed distributions), CLT doesn't apply. This is why extreme events can persist even in large portfolios.

Law of Large Numbers (LLN)

$$\bar{X}_n \xrightarrow{p} \mu \quad \text{as } n \to \infty$$

Finance application — PM track record evaluation:

Track record length What you can conclude Statistical power
6 months Almost nothing; noise dominates Extremely low
1 year Slight signal if Sharpe > 2 Low
3 years Meaningful if Sharpe > 1 Moderate
5+ years Genuine evaluation possible Good
10+ years High confidence Strong

The "how long to prove skill?" formula:

$$n \geq \left(\frac{z_\alpha}{\text{Sharpe}}\right)^2 \text{ (in years)}$$

At 95% confidence ($z = 1.96$):
- Sharpe 0.5: needs $(1.96/0.5)^2$ = 15.4 years
- Sharpe 1.0: needs $(1.96/1.0)^2$ = 3.8 years
- Sharpe 2.0: needs $(1.96/2.0)^2$ = 0.96 years

Insight: Most hedge fund PMs have 2-3 year track records and Sharpe ratios around 1.0. That's NOT ENOUGH to statistically prove skill at 95% confidence. We allocate on faith, judgment, and qualitative assessment — not statistical significance.

Conditional Probability & Independence

Multiplication rule: $P(A \cap B) = P(A \mid B) \cdot P(B)$

Independence: $P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B)$, which means $P(A \mid B) = P(A)$.

Critical risk insight — conditional vs. unconditional:

  • Unconditional P(SPX down >3% tomorrow) ≈ 1%
  • P(SPX down >3% tomorrow | VIX > 35 today) ≈ 8-12%
  • P(SPX down >3% tomorrow | VIX > 35 AND dollar strengthening AND credit spreads widening) ≈ 15-20%

Risk is ALWAYS conditional. The RM's job is to identify the RIGHT conditioning information.

PART 3: HYPOTHESIS TESTING — SEPARATING SIGNAL FROM NOISE

The Framework

Step What to do Example
1 State H₀ (null: no effect) and H₁ (alternative) H₀: PM alpha = 0. H₁: PM alpha > 0.
2 Choose significance level α α = 0.05 (5% false positive rate)
3 Compute test statistic t = (observed - expected) / SE
4 Compare to critical value or compute p-value p < α → reject H₀
5 Interpret IN CONTEXT "PM's alpha is statistically different from zero"

Type I and Type II Errors

H₀ true (no alpha) H₀ false (real alpha)
Reject H₀ ("PM has alpha") Type I error (false positive): you think PM is skilled but they're not Correct (true positive)
Fail to reject H₀ ("no evidence of alpha") Correct (true negative) Type II error (false negative): you miss a skilled PM

In risk management terms:
- Type I = investing in a PM who's actually just lucky → you lose money slowly
- Type II = passing on a PM who's actually skilled → opportunity cost
- Type I in model validation = concluding VaR model is broken when it's fine → costly conservatism
- Type II in model validation = concluding VaR model is fine when it's broken → blow-up

Power = 1 - β = P(reject H₀ when H₁ is true). Power increases with:
- Larger sample size (more data)
- Larger effect size (bigger alpha)
- Higher significance level (more willing to accept Type I errors)

Testing PM Alpha — The t-Test

$$t = \frac{\hat{\alpha}}{\text{SE}(\hat{\alpha})} = \frac{\hat{\alpha}}{s / \sqrt{n}}$$

where $\hat{\alpha}$ = estimated daily alpha, $s$ = daily return standard deviation, $n$ = number of trading days.

Worked example 1 — "Good" PM:

PM averages +8bps/day over 500 trading days (~2 years). Daily vol = 60bps.
$$t = \frac{0.08}{0.60 / \sqrt{500}} = \frac{0.08}{0.0268} = 2.98$$
p-value ≈ 0.001 (one-tailed). Reject H₀ at 1% level. Evidence of genuine skill.
Annualized alpha: 8bps × 252 ≈ 20%. Annualized Sharpe: $\frac{0.08}{0.60} \times \sqrt{252}$ ≈ 2.1.

Worked example 2 — "Lucky" PM:

PM averages +5bps/day over 252 trading days (1 year). Daily vol = 80bps.
$$t = \frac{0.05}{0.80 / \sqrt{252}} = \frac{0.05}{0.0504} = 0.99$$
p-value ≈ 0.16 (one-tailed). Cannot reject H₀. Not statistically significant.
Annualized alpha: 5bps × 252 ≈ 12.6%. Annualized Sharpe: $\frac{0.05}{0.80} \times \sqrt{252}$ ≈ 1.0.

Key insight: A PM with a Sharpe of 1.0 over 1 year CANNOT be distinguished from luck. You need ~4 years to prove it.

Multiple Testing Problem — The Data Mining Trap

If you test 100 strategies at α = 5%, you expect 5 false positives by chance. The strategies that "pass" are mostly noise.

Corrections:
| Method | Approach | When to use |
|---|---|---|
| Bonferroni | Divide α by number of tests: $α^ = α / m$ | Conservative; when tests are independent |
| Holm-Bonferroni | Step-down: order p-values, test sequentially | Less conservative than Bonferroni |
| Benjamini-Hochberg (FDR) | Controls false discovery rate, not family-wise error | When some false positives are acceptable |
| Haircut Sharpe | Deflate backtest Sharpe: $\text{SR}^ = \text{SR} - \sqrt{\frac{2 \ln(m)}{n}}$ | Quick adjustment for strategy screening |

Worked example — Haircut Sharpe:

Quant tested 500 strategies, found one with Sharpe = 2.5 over 5 years (n = 1260 days).
$$\text{Haircut} = \sqrt{\frac{2 \ln(500)}{1260}} = \sqrt{\frac{12.43}{1260}} = \sqrt{0.00987} = 0.099$$
$$\text{Adjusted Sharpe} = 2.5 - \sqrt{252} \times 0.099 = 2.5 - 1.57 = \textbf{0.93}$$

The 2.5 Sharpe becomes 0.93 after adjusting for data mining. Suddenly much less impressive.

PART 4: REGRESSION — THE WORKHORSE OF FACTOR ANALYSIS

Simple OLS (Ordinary Least Squares)

$$y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_i + \epsilon_i$$

Minimizing: $\sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2$

$$\hat{\beta}1 = \frac{\text{Cov}(X, Y)}{\text{Var}(X)} = \rho{X,Y} \cdot \frac{\sigma_Y}{\sigma_X}$$

$$\hat{\beta}_0 = \bar{y} - \hat{\beta}_1 \bar{x}$$

R² = fraction of variance explained:

$$R^2 = 1 - \frac{\text{SS}\text{res}}{\text{SS}\text{tot}} = 1 - \frac{\sum(y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2}{\sum(y_i - \bar{y})^2}$$

$$R^2 = \rho^2 \quad \text{(for simple OLS)}$$

Adjusted R²: Penalizes for additional variables.

$$R^2_\text{adj} = 1 - \frac{(1-R^2)(n-1)}{n-k-1}$$

where $k$ = number of predictors. This prevents overfitting: adding a useless variable increases R² but decreases adjusted R².

Multi-Factor Regression

$$r_p = \alpha + \beta_1 F_1 + \beta_2 F_2 + ... + \beta_k F_k + \epsilon$$

Full worked example — PM factor decomposition:

Regressing PM monthly returns on 4 factors over 36 months:

Factor β estimate t-stat p-value Interpretation
Intercept (α) +0.25%/month 1.85 0.07 Alpha marginally significant (not at 5%)
Market (SPX) 1.15 8.23 <0.001 Very significant; PM runs 1.15x market beta
Size (SMB) -0.42 -2.91 0.006 Significant; tilted to large cap
Momentum (WML) 0.38 2.44 0.02 Significant; positive momentum exposure
Value (HML) 0.08 0.52 0.61 NOT significant; no value tilt

R² = 0.78. Adjusted R² = 0.75.

RM interpretation:
- 78% of this PM's returns are explained by factors → only 22% is idiosyncratic
- Alpha of 0.25%/month ≈ 3%/year, but t = 1.85 → NOT statistically significant at 5%
- PM is essentially running a leveraged (β = 1.15), large-cap, momentum portfolio
- "You could replicate 78% of this PM's performance with 4 ETFs at 20bps cost vs. 2/20 fees"

Regression Diagnostics — What Can Go Wrong

Problem What happens Detection Fix
Multicollinearity Predictors correlated → β estimates unstable, signs may flip VIF > 5 is concerning; VIF > 10 = problem Remove redundant variables; use PCA
Heteroscedasticity Variance of errors not constant → wrong standard errors White's test; Breusch-Pagan test; plot residuals vs. fitted Use robust (White) standard errors
Autocorrelation Serial correlation in errors → overstated significance Durbin-Watson stat; should be ≈ 2 Newey-West HAC standard errors
Omitted variable bias Missing important factor → β estimates biased Theory says a factor matters but it's not in the model Add the factor
Endogeneity X and ε correlated → β is biased Economic reasoning (reverse causality?) IV estimation (instrumental variables)
Structural breaks Relationship changes over time → single model inappropriate Chow test; rolling regression with unstable β Rolling regression; regime-switching model

The most important regression pitfall in finance — spurious regression:

Two unrelated non-stationary series (e.g., GDP and SPX price level) will show high R² simply because both trend upward. The correlation is meaningless.

Fix: Regress on RETURNS (stationary), not levels. Or use cointegration tests.

Correlation and Its Traps

$$\rho_{X,Y} = \frac{\text{Cov}(X,Y)}{\sigma_X \cdot \sigma_Y} = \frac{E[(X-\mu_X)(Y-\mu_Y)]}{\sigma_X \cdot \sigma_Y}$$

Properties: $-1 \leq \rho \leq 1$. Symmetric: $\rho_{X,Y} = \rho_{Y,X}$.

Correlation pitfalls in finance:

Pitfall Example Why it's wrong
Correlation ≠ causation Oil up ↔ energy stocks up Causation here; but not all correlations are causal
Correlation is LINEAR only Two assets with V-shaped P&L relationship → ρ ≈ 0 Use Spearman rank correlation or copulas for non-linear
Regime-dependent Stock-bond ρ = -0.4 (normal) → +0.5 (inflation) Which ρ does your model use?
Time-varying ρ(SPX, crude) was +0.3 in 2015, +0.7 in 2020, +0.1 in 2024 Rolling correlation windows
Outlier-sensitive One extreme co-move can distort ρ for the entire sample Use robust correlation; winsorize outliers

Partial correlation — removing confounders:

$$\rho_{X,Y \cdot Z} = \frac{\rho_{X,Y} - \rho_{X,Z} \cdot \rho_{Y,Z}}{\sqrt{1 - \rho_{X,Z}^2} \cdot \sqrt{1 - \rho_{Y,Z}^2}}$$

This controls for a third variable Z. Example: what's the correlation between oil and airline stocks AFTER removing the effect of overall market (SPX)? The partial correlation is the "pure" relationship.

PART 5: TIME SERIES — THE LANGUAGE OF FINANCIAL DATA

Stationarity (Deeper Treatment)

Strict stationarity: Joint distribution of $(X_t, X_{t+1}, ..., X_{t+k})$ is the same for all $t$.

Weak stationarity (covariance stationarity): Mean and autocovariance don't depend on time.

Testing for stationarity:

Test Null hypothesis Reject means
Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) Series has unit root (NON-stationary) Series IS stationary
Phillips-Perron (PP) Same as ADF (non-parametric correction) Series IS stationary
KPSS Series IS stationary Series is NON-stationary

Best practice: Use ADF AND KPSS together. If ADF rejects and KPSS fails to reject → strong evidence of stationarity.

Typical results in finance:
| Series | ADF result | Stationary? |
|---|---|---|
| Stock prices | Fail to reject | ❌ Non-stationary |
| Stock returns | Reject | ✅ Stationary |
| VIX level | Reject (mean-reverting) | ✅ Stationary |
| Interest rates | Mixed — near unit root | ⚠️ Near-non-stationary |
| GDP | Fail to reject | ❌ Non-stationary (trending) |

Autoregressive (AR) Models

$$X_t = c + \phi_1 X_{t-1} + \phi_2 X_{t-2} + ... + \phi_p X_{t-p} + \epsilon_t$$

AR(1): $X_t = c + \phi X_{t-1} + \epsilon_t$

φ value Behavior Market example
|φ| < 1 Stationary, mean-reverting VIX, credit spreads
φ = 1 Random walk (unit root) Stock prices
|φ| > 1 Explosive (unstable) Bubble dynamics (short-lived)
φ > 0 Positive autocorrelation → momentum CTA signals
φ < 0 Negative autocorrelation → mean reversion Stat arb signals

Half-life of mean reversion:

$$t_{1/2} = \frac{-\ln(2)}{\ln(|\phi|)}$$

If φ = 0.95: half-life = -ln(2) / ln(0.95) = 0.693 / 0.0513 = 13.5 days. A deviation from the mean takes ~14 days to halve.

Cointegration — Beyond Correlation

Two non-stationary series are cointegrated if a LINEAR COMBINATION of them is stationary.

Example: SPY and IVV both track S&P 500. Each is non-stationary (prices trend), but:
$$\text{SPY}_t - \beta \cdot \text{IVV}_t \approx \text{stationary spread}$$

Why it matters for risk:
- Two correlated assets can decorrelate temporarily (ρ can drop to 0 or flip sign).
- Two COINTEGRATED assets have a LONG-RUN equilibrium relationship — the spread mean-reverts. This is the foundation of pairs/stat arb trading.
- For hedging: cointegrated hedge ratios are more stable than correlation-based ratios.

Engle-Granger test:
1. Regress Y on X ($Y_t = \alpha + \beta X_t + e_t$)
2. Test residuals ($e_t$) for stationarity using ADF
3. If residuals are stationary → X and Y are cointegrated

PART 6: MONTE CARLO SIMULATION — GENERATING THE FUTURE

The Method

  1. Specify the probability distribution of each risk factor (returns, rates, vol)
  2. Generate N random scenarios from those distributions
  3. Compute portfolio P&L for each scenario (including non-linear effects)
  4. Extract risk metrics from the P&L distribution

Single-Asset Monte Carlo VaR

Step by step:
1. Estimate μ and σ from historical returns
2. Generate N ~ 10,000+ standard normal random draws: $Z_i$
3. Compute simulated returns: $r_i = \mu + \sigma \cdot Z_i$
4. Compute simulated P&L: $\text{PnL}_i = \text{Position Value} \times r_i$
5. VaR (99%) = negative of the 1st percentile of P&L distribution

Worked example:

Portfolio value: $100M. μ = 0.03% daily. σ = 1.2% daily.
10,000 simulations: r_i = 0.0003 + 0.012 × Z_i
PnL_i = $100M × r_i
1st percentile of PnL = -$2.75M
→ 99% 1-day VaR = $2.75M

Compare to analytical: VaR = $100M × 2.326 × 1.2% = $2.79M (close — as expected when distribution is normal and payoff is linear)

Multi-Asset with Correlation — Cholesky Decomposition

If asset returns are correlated, you can't simulate them independently.

Method:
1. Build the correlation matrix $\Sigma$
2. Compute the Cholesky decomposition: $\Sigma = L L^T$
3. Generate independent standard normals: $Z$
4. Transform: $\text{Correlated returns} = L \cdot Z$

2-asset example:

Asset A: μₐ = 0.05%, σₐ = 1.0%. Asset B: μᵦ = 0.03%, σᵦ = 1.5%. ρ = 0.6.

Correlation matrix: $\Sigma = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0.6 \ 0.6 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$

Cholesky: $L = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \ 0.6 & 0.8 \end{pmatrix}$

For independent draws $Z_1, Z_2$:
- $r_A = \mu_A + \sigma_A \cdot Z_1$
- $r_B = \mu_B + \sigma_B \cdot (0.6 \cdot Z_1 + 0.8 \cdot Z_2)$

Now A and B are correlated with ρ = 0.6 in each simulation.

When Monte Carlo > Analytical

Situation Why analytical fails Why MC works
Options portfolios (non-linear) Delta-normal VaR ignores gamma MC simulates actual payoff including curvature
Path-dependent risk Margin calls, stop-losses trigger during the path MC can simulate day-by-day paths
Fat tails Normal assumption wrong MC can sample from t-distribution, GPD, etc.
Complex correlations Covariance matrix too simple MC can use copulas for tail dependence
Stress testing Need specific scenario combinations MC can condition on stress events

Variance Reduction Techniques

Technique How it works Improvement
Antithetic variates For each Z, also simulate -Z Reduces variance by ~30-50%
Control variates Use a variable with known expected value to adjust Reduces variance proportional to correlation
Importance sampling Oversample the tail region of interest Critical for rare event estimation
Stratified sampling Divide distribution into strata, sample from each Ensures tail representation

PART 7: MOMENT ANALYSIS — BEYOND MEAN AND VARIANCE

The Four Moments

Moment What it measures Formula (excess) Normal value
1st: Mean (μ) Central tendency $E[X]$ Any
2nd: Variance (σ²) Dispersion / risk $E[(X-\mu)^2]$ Any
3rd: Skewness (γ₁) Asymmetry $E\left[\left(\frac{X-\mu}{\sigma}\right)^3\right]$ 0
4th: Kurtosis (κ) Tail thickness $E\left[\left(\frac{X-\mu}{\sigma}\right)^4\right] - 3$ 0 (excess)

Skewness in Finance

Skew Distribution shape Typical strategy Risk character
γ₁ < -1 Heavy left tail Short vol, sell puts, carry Frequent small gains, rare large losses
γ₁ ≈ 0 Symmetric Market beta, balanced Symmetric risk
γ₁ > +1 Heavy right tail Long vol, trend following Frequent small losses, rare large gains

The Sharpe-Skew deception:
A short-vol strategy with Sharpe = 1.5 and skew = -3.0 LOOKS great until the tail event arrives. The Sharpe ratio treats upside and downside volatility equally — it REWARDS negative skew because the denominator (total vol) is inflated by rare large losses that haven't happened yet in the sample.

Risk-adjusted metrics that account for skew:

$$\text{Sortino} = \frac{R_p - R_f}{\sigma_\text{downside}}$$

$$\text{Omega}(\theta) = \frac{\int_\theta^\infty [1 - F(x)] \, dx}{\int_{-\infty}^\theta F(x) \, dx} = \frac{\text{Expected gains above threshold}}{\text{Expected losses below threshold}}$$

$$\text{Adjusted Sharpe} = \text{SR} \times \left[1 + \frac{\gamma_1}{6} \text{SR} - \frac{(\kappa - 3)}{24} \text{SR}^2\right]$$

Kurtosis — The Tail Risk Metric

Excess kurtosis Tail behavior Example
κ = 0 Normal tails Theoretical baseline
κ = 1-3 Moderately fat tails Large-cap equity indices
κ = 3-10 Significantly fat tails Individual equities, FX
κ = 10-30 Very fat tails Commodities (energy, ags)
κ > 30 Extremely fat tails Crypto, emerging markets

Practical test: If kurtosis is high (>3), then:
- VaR at 99% is understated by roughly $(1 + \kappa/8) \times$ the normal estimate (rough approximation)
- Expected Shortfall diverges even more from the normal estimate
- The Jarque-Bera test will reject normality

$$\text{JB} = \frac{n}{6}\left(\gamma_1^2 + \frac{(\kappa)^2}{4}\right) \sim \chi^2_2$$

PART 9: INFORMATION THEORY & ENTROPY

Shannon Entropy

$$H(X) = -\sum_{i} p_i \log_2(p_i)$$

Measures uncertainty. Higher entropy = more uncertainty = more disorder.

Distribution Entropy Interpretation
Certain outcome (p = 1) 0 bits No uncertainty at all
Fair coin (p = 0.5, 0.5) 1 bit Maximum uncertainty for binary
Biased coin (p = 0.9, 0.1) 0.47 bits Less uncertain — you can predict heads
Uniform over 8 outcomes 3 bits Maximum uncertainty for 8 states

Finance application — market concentration:

$$H_\text{portfolio} = -\sum_{i=1}^n w_i \log_2(w_i)$$

A portfolio with equal weights (1/n each) has maximum entropy. As concentration increases (one position dominates), entropy decreases. Entropy is an alternative to HHI for measuring portfolio diversification.

KL Divergence — Distance Between Distributions

$$D_\text{KL}(P | Q) = \sum_i P(i) \log\frac{P(i)}{Q(i)}$$

Measures how different distribution P is from distribution Q. NOT symmetric: $D_\text{KL}(P|Q) \neq D_\text{KL}(Q|P)$.

Risk application: How different is today's return distribution from the historical distribution the model was calibrated on? Large KL divergence → regime has shifted → model is stale.

PART 10: STOCHASTIC CALCULUS ESSENTIALS — JUST ENOUGH

Why You Need This (Briefly)

Options pricing, GBM, and most continuous-time finance models require stochastic calculus. You need to know the CONCEPTS, not the proofs.

Brownian Motion (Wiener Process)

$W_t$ is Brownian motion if:
1. $W_0 = 0$
2. Increments are independent: $W_t - W_s$ is independent of $W_u$ for $u \leq s$
3. Increments are normal: $W_t - W_s \sim N(0, t - s)$
4. Paths are continuous

Key properties:
- $E[W_t] = 0$ (no drift)
- $\text{Var}(W_t) = t$ (variance grows linearly with time → vol grows with √t)
- $dW_t \sim N(0, dt)$
- $(dW_t)^2 = dt$ (this is the KEY identity for Itô's lemma)

Itô's Lemma — The Chain Rule of Stochastic Calculus

If $X_t$ follows $dX_t = \mu \, dt + \sigma \, dW_t$ and $f$ is a twice-differentiable function:

$$df(X_t) = f'(X_t) \, dX_t + \frac{1}{2} f''(X_t) \, \sigma^2 \, dt$$

$$= \left[f'(X_t) \mu + \frac{1}{2} f''(X_t) \sigma^2\right] dt + f'(X_t) \sigma \, dW_t$$

The extra term $\frac{1}{2} f''(X_t) \sigma^2 dt$ is the "Itô correction" — it's what makes stochastic calculus different from regular calculus. It comes from $(dW)^2 = dt$.

Why it matters for you: This extra term is WHY log returns differ from simple returns (the $-\frac{1}{2}\sigma^2$ convexity adjustment in GBM). It's also the mathematical foundation of gamma P&L:

$$d(\text{Option value}) \approx \Delta \, dS + \frac{1}{2} \Gamma \, (dS)^2 + \Theta \, dt$$

The $\frac{1}{2} \Gamma (dS)^2$ term IS the Itô correction applied to options.

Risk-Neutral Pricing (One Paragraph)

Under the risk-neutral measure $\mathbb{Q}$, the expected return on ALL assets equals the risk-free rate $r$. This allows us to price derivatives by discounting expected payoffs at $r$. The stock follows $dS = rS \, dt + \sigma S \, dW^{\mathbb{Q}}_t$ under $\mathbb{Q}$ (not $dS = \mu S \, dt + ...$). Black-Scholes comes directly from this. You don't need to derive it — just know that the real-world drift μ doesn't appear in option prices; only σ and r matter.

PART 11: PROBABILITY PUZZLES & MENTAL MATH

Classic Interview Puzzles

Puzzle 1: Conditional Coin

Flip a coin twice. Given that at least one flip was heads, what's P(both heads)?

Sample space of two flips: {HH, HT, TH, TT}. Conditioning on "at least one heads" removes TT → remaining: {HH, HT, TH}.
$$P(\text{both heads} \mid \text{at least one heads}) = \frac{1}{3}$$

Twist: If told "the FIRST flip was heads," then: {HH, HT} → P(both) = 1/2. The conditioning information matters.

Puzzle 2: The Three Cards (Monty Hall Variant)

3 cards: red/red, blue/blue, red/blue. You draw a card, see a red side. P(other side is also red)?

There are 3 red faces in total. 2 belong to the red/red card, 1 to the red/blue. Given you see a red face, each face is equally likely to be the one observed.
$$P(\text{other red}) = \frac{2}{3}$$

Puzzle 3: Dice Sum

Two dice thrown. P(sum = 7)?

Favorable: (1,6), (2,5), (3,4), (4,3), (5,2), (6,1) = 6 outcomes.
Total: 36 outcomes. P = 6/36 = 1/6.

Bonus: P(sum = 7) > P(sum = any other number). Seven is the most likely sum.

Puzzle 4: Geometric Distribution — Expected Flips

Expected coin flips to get first heads?

$$E[X] = \frac{1}{p} = \frac{1}{0.5} = 2$$

Expected flips for two heads IN A ROW?

Let E = expected flips. After each flip:
- Tails (prob 0.5): wasted 1 flip, restart → contributed $0.5 \times (1 + E)$
- Heads then tails (prob 0.25): wasted 2 flips, restart → contributed $0.25 \times (2 + E)$
- Heads then heads (prob 0.25): done in 2 flips → contributed $0.25 \times 2$

$$E = 0.5(1 + E) + 0.25(2 + E) + 0.25 \times 2$$
$$E = 0.5 + 0.5E + 0.5 + 0.25E + 0.5$$
$$E = 1.5 + 0.75E$$
$$0.25E = 1.5$$
$$E = 6$$

Puzzle 5: The Birthday Problem

How many people needed in a room for a >50% chance that two share a birthday?

$$P(\text{no shared birthday among } n) = \prod_{i=0}^{n-1} \frac{365-i}{365}$$

For n = 23: P(at least one match) ≈ 50.7%. Answer: 23 people.

Finance analog: In a portfolio of 23+ positions, there's a >50% chance two have highly correlated (>0.8) drawdowns on the same day. Diversification is harder than it looks.

Puzzle 6: The Sock Drawer (Pigeonhole Principle)

Drawer has red and blue socks. How many must you pull to guarantee a matching pair?

3 socks. With 2 colors, by the pigeonhole principle, 3 draws guarantee at least 2 are the same color.

Generalization: With k colors, you need k + 1 draws to guarantee a match.

Puzzle 7: Expected Value of Max of Two Dice

$$E[\max(D_1, D_2)] = \sum_{k=1}^{6} k \cdot P(\max = k)$$

$P(\max = k) = P(\text{both} \leq k) - P(\text{both} \leq k-1) = (k/6)^2 - ((k-1)/6)^2$

= $\frac{1}{36}(1 + 3 + 5 + 7 + 9 + 11) \times (1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6)$ weighted

$$E[\max] = \frac{1}{36}(1 \times 1 + 2 \times 3 + 3 \times 5 + 4 \times 7 + 5 \times 9 + 6 \times 11) = \frac{161}{36} = \textbf{4.47}$$

Finance analog: If two PMs independently generate returns, the expected max return is higher than either individual expected return. This is why multi-PM structures outperform single-PM structures (option value of selection).

PART 12: INTERVIEW ANSWERS — MATH & STATS

Q: "Walk me through how you'd determine if a PM's performance is due to skill or luck."

"I'd compute the t-statistic on their excess returns — daily alpha divided by its standard error. The formula is alpha-hat divided by daily vol over square root of N trading days.

The key insight is how much data you need. A PM with a Sharpe of 1.0 — which is quite good — needs nearly 4 years of data to be statistically significant at the 5% level. At Sharpe 0.5, you need over 15 years. Most PM track records are 2-3 years, which means we almost never have ENOUGH data to definitively prove skill.

In practice, I'd supplement the t-test with two other checks. First, factor decomposition: if 80% of the PM's return is explained by market, momentum, and size factors, the 'alpha' is mostly factor beta that could be replicated cheaply. Second, I'd look at the DISTRIBUTION of returns, not just the average. Negative skew with high kurtosis suggests the PM is harvesting carry or selling vol — the Sharpe looks good because the blow-up hasn't happened yet in-sample. The adjusted Sharpe ratio, which penalizes for negative skew and fat tails, often tells a very different story."

Q: "Explain Bayes' theorem and give a risk management application."

"Bayes says your updated belief equals your prior belief multiplied by the likelihood of the evidence, divided by the overall probability of that evidence. In plain English: how much should new information change your mind?

In risk management, I'd use it for PM evaluation. Say I start with a 70% belief that a PM is skilled. A skilled PM has a 2-sigma drawdown month about 5% of the time; an unskilled PM has one about 15% of the time. After observing a bad month, Bayes updates my belief to about 44%. After two bad months, I'm down to about 21% confidence in their skill.

The power of this is that it's CALIBRATED updating. Without Bayes, people either overreact — fire a PM after one bad month — or underreact — keep a PM for years because 'they'll come back.' Bayes tells you the mathematically correct amount to update, which depends on your prior and the relative probabilities of the evidence under each hypothesis."

Q: "Why can't you just use the normal distribution for risk management?"

"Three reasons, all of which cause the normal distribution to UNDERSTATE tail risk.

First, fat tails. Real return distributions have excess kurtosis of 3 to 10 for equities. A 4-sigma daily move should happen once every 126 years under normal assumptions — in reality, it happens every 2 to 3 years. So 99% VaR is consistently too low.

Second, skewness. Most equity and credit strategies have negative skew — small frequent gains and rare large losses. The normal distribution is symmetric and doesn't capture this. A short-vol strategy with Sharpe 1.5 can look great for 3 years and then lose 50% in a week.

Third, regime dependence. The normal distribution uses one mean and one variance, but markets switch between low-vol and high-vol regimes. A single normal calibrated to 'average' conditions understates risk in stress AND overstates risk in calm. Mixture distributions or regime-switching models are more appropriate.

In practice, I'd use the normal as a baseline but supplement with Expected Shortfall — which captures the tail shape — and stress tests using historical crisis returns, which don't assume ANY distribution."
