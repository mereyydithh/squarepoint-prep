PART 1: KEY DISTRIBUTIONS

Normal (Gaussian)

$$f(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}}$$

Range % within Tail probability
±1σ 68.27% 15.87% per tail
±1.645σ 90% 5% per tail (95% VaR)
±2σ 95.45% 2.28% per tail
±2.326σ 98% 1% per tail (99% VaR)
±3σ 99.73% 0.13% per tail

Log-Normal

  • Asset prices follow log-normal: prices can't go below zero, returns are multiplicative.
  • If $\ln(P)$ is normal → $P$ is log-normal.
  • Right-skewed: most outcomes clustered left, long right tail (big gains possible, max loss capped at -100%).

Student's t-Distribution

  • Fatter tails than normal, controlled by degrees of freedom $\nu$.
  • $\nu = 3$: very fat tails. $\nu \to \infty$: converges to normal.
  • Used for small samples and when data exhibits fat tails.

Fat Tails — The Most Important Concept

Financial returns have excess kurtosis (kurtosis > 3). Events that should happen once every 10,000 years under normal assumptions occur every 10-20 years in reality.

Why this matters:
- VaR under normal assumption UNDERSTATES true tail risk.
- This is WHY Expected Shortfall (average loss beyond VaR) is preferred.
- FRTB replaced VaR with ES for regulatory capital.

PART 2: PROBABILITY FOUNDATIONS

Bayes' Theorem

$$P(A|B) = \frac{P(B|A) \cdot P(A)}{P(B)}$$

Classic interview example: A disease affects 1 in 10,000 people. A test is 99% accurate (both sensitivity and specificity). You test positive. What's the probability you actually have the disease?

  • $P(\text{disease}) = 0.0001$
  • $P(\text{positive | disease}) = 0.99$
  • $P(\text{positive | no disease}) = 0.01$
  • $P(\text{positive}) = 0.99 \times 0.0001 + 0.01 \times 0.9999 = 0.010098$

$$P(\text{disease | positive}) = \frac{0.99 \times 0.0001}{0.010098} = 0.0098 \approx 0.98\%$$

Answer: ~1%, NOT 99%. The base rate dominates. This is crucial for risk: a "99% accurate" model doesn't mean a 99% chance you're right — it depends on how rare the event is.

Central Limit Theorem (CLT)

The average of a large number of independent random variables is approximately normally distributed, regardless of the underlying distribution.

Why it matters: This is why portfolio returns tend toward normal (many positions averaging out) and why sample means are easier to work with than individual observations.

Law of Large Numbers

Sample mean converges to population mean as $n \to \infty$.

Application: Why PMs need long track records. A PM with 6 months of returns can't be distinguished from luck. Years of returns → skill signal emerges.

PART 3: HYPOTHESIS TESTING

Framework

  1. State $H_0$ (null: "no effect") vs $H_1$ (alternative: "there is an effect").
  2. Choose significance level $\alpha$ (0.05 or 0.01).
  3. Compute test statistic.
  4. If p-value < $\alpha$ → reject $H_0$.

Errors

$H_0$ true $H_0$ false
Reject $H_0$ Type I (false positive) Correct
Fail to reject $H_0$ Correct Type II (false negative)

PM Alpha Test — Is This Skill or Luck?

$$t = \frac{\bar{r} - 0}{s / \sqrt{n}}$$

Example: PM averages +5bps/day over 252 trading days. Daily vol = 80bps.
$$t = \frac{0.05}{0.80 / \sqrt{252}} = \frac{0.05}{0.0504} = 0.99$$

Need $t > 2.0$ for significance at 5% level. Result: NOT statistically significant. 252 days of 5bps daily return with 80bps volatility is indistinguishable from luck.

Insight for interview: "When evaluating a PM's track record, I'd compute the t-statistic on their excess returns. A Sharpe of 0.5 needs ~16 years of data to be statistically significant at the 5% level. Most PM track records are too short to prove skill."

PART 4: REGRESSION & FACTOR MODELS

Simple OLS

$$y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_i + \epsilon_i$$

  • $\beta_1$: sensitivity of $y$ to $x$ (e.g., portfolio return to market return = beta)
  • $R^2$: fraction of variance explained
  • Adjusted $R^2$: penalizes for adding more variables (prevents overfitting)

Multi-Factor Regression

$$r_{\text{portfolio}} = \alpha + \beta_1 r_{\text{market}} + \beta_2 r_{\text{rates}} + \beta_3 r_{\text{oil}} + \epsilon$$

Decomposes portfolio return into:
- $\alpha$: genuine skill (the residual after accounting for factor exposure)
- $\beta_i$: sensitivity to each factor (exposures the PM is taking)
- $\epsilon$: unexplained noise

RM use: "This PM has an alpha of 3%, but their beta to the market is 1.4 and their beta to oil is 0.8. In a rising market with strong oil, most of their return is factor exposure, not skill."

Common Pitfalls

Pitfall Problem Fix
Multicollinearity Correlated predictors → unstable $\beta$ estimates Check VIF, remove redundant variables
Heteroscedasticity Variance not constant → standard errors wrong Newey-West robust errors
Autocorrelation Serial correlation in residuals → overstated significance Durbin-Watson test, HAC errors
Spurious correlation Two unrelated series both trending → high $R^2$ First-difference or de-trend the data

Correlation vs Causation

$$\rho_{X,Y} = \frac{\text{Cov}(X,Y)}{\sigma_X \cdot \sigma_Y}$$

Correlation measures LINEAR co-movement. It does NOT imply causation. In finance:
- Ice cream sales and drowning deaths correlate (both driven by summer heat).
- Oil prices and airline losses correlate (one causes the other).
- In crisis, EVERYTHING correlates (common factor: risk appetite).

PART 5: MONTE CARLO SIMULATION

How It Works

  1. Define the probability distribution of returns (or risk factors).
  2. Generate thousands of random scenarios from that distribution.
  3. Compute portfolio P&L for each scenario.
  4. Extract risk metrics (VaR = percentile, ES = average beyond VaR).

Single-Asset Monte Carlo VaR

mu = returns.mean()
sigma = returns.std()
simulated = np.random.normal(mu, sigma, 100_000)
simulated_pnl = portfolio_value * simulated
var_99 = -np.percentile(simulated_pnl, 1)

Multi-Asset with Correlation (Cholesky)

When assets are correlated, you can't simulate them independently. Use Cholesky decomposition:

$$\text{correlated_returns} = L \cdot z$$

Where $L$ = Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix, $z$ = vector of independent standard normals.

This preserves the correlation structure across assets in simulation.

Limitations

  • Output is only as good as the input distribution (garbage in = garbage out).
  • Normal assumptions understate tails.
  • Correlations assumed constant (they're not — they spike in crisis).
  • Computationally expensive for large portfolios.

PART 6: ANNUALIZATION

The most common mental math in risk:

Conversion Formula
Daily → annual return × 252
Daily → annual volatility × $\sqrt{252}$ ≈ × 15.87
Monthly → annual vol × $\sqrt{12}$ ≈ × 3.46

Example: Daily vol = 1.2% → Annual vol = 1.2% × 15.87 ≈ 19%.

Why square root? Variance scales linearly with time (under independence). Standard deviation = square root of variance, so vol scales with $\sqrt{T}$.

PART 7: PROBABILITY PUZZLES (INTERVIEW GOLD)

Problem Answer Logic
Flip coin twice, given ≥1 heads, P(both heads)? 1/3 Sample space: {HH, HT, TH}. Only HH out of 3.
Sum of two dice = 7? 1/6 6 favorable out of 36: (1,6)(2,5)(3,4)(4,3)(5,2)(6,1).
Pull socks in dark to guarantee a pair? 3 socks 2 colors → pigeonhole principle: 3rd guarantees a match.
3 cards (red/red, blue/blue, red/blue). See red side. P(other side red)? 2/3 2 red faces on RR card vs 1 on RB card. 2 out of 3 red faces belong to RR.
Expected flips to get first heads? 2 Geometric: $E[X] = 1/p = 1/0.5 = 2$.
Expected flips for two heads in a row? 6 Recursive: $E = 2 + 0.5 \times E_1 + 0.5 \times E$, where $E_1 = 1$. $E = 6$.
