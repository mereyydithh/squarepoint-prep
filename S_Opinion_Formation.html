S — OPINION FORMATION

Having a risk-informed view — not trading, not being useless

Depth: 🔴 DEEP

WHY THIS MATTERS

Every interviewer at Squarepoint will test whether you can think, not just calculate. The trader asks: "What do you think the Fed does next?" The senior RM asks: "Where's the biggest risk to the book?" Your future desk head asks: "What would YOU focus on day one?"

  • "I don't have a view" → you're a calculator — replaceable by a spreadsheet.
  • "I think the Fed should cut because..." → you're playing trader — not your job.
  • "From a risk perspective, the asymmetry is..." → you're a risk manager with a brain.

This file teaches you the complete system for forming, calibrating, defending, and expressing risk-informed opinions — the skill that separates junior RM candidates who get offers from those who don't.

PART 1: THE RISK MANAGER'S VIEW vs. THE TRADER'S VIEW

Fundamental Distinction

Dimension Trader Risk Manager
Core question "What WILL happen?" "What COULD happen that the market under-prices?"
Objective Directional P&L Portfolio resilience
Success metric Right more than wrong Identified the risk BEFORE it materialized
Time horizon Trade-specific (days-weeks) Regime-level (weeks-months)
Confidence req'd High (they're sizing on it) Moderate (they're protecting against it)
Expression Position Recommendation

The 3 Things You're Really Identifying

  1. Consensus complacency — the market is pricing X but Y is plausible
  2. Payoff asymmetry — the loss from being wrong exceeds the gain from being right
  3. Portfolio vulnerability — where OUR book is specifically exposed

Key insight: You don't need to be RIGHT about what will happen. You need to be right about what MATTERS — i.e., where the uncompensated risk sits.

Why This Matters at Squarepoint Specifically

Squarepoint runs discretionary strategies inside a systematic infrastructure. PMs have quantitative tools but make human judgment calls. The RM who can engage with market views through a risk lens — rather than just policing limits — becomes an intellectual partner, not a bureaucrat.

PART 2: THE 6-STEP OPINION FORMATION FRAMEWORK

Step 1: Know What The Market Is Pricing

Before you can have a view, map the consensus. These are your "implied assumptions":

Instrument What It Tells You How To Read
Fed funds futures Cuts/hikes priced, by when CME FedWatch → prob-weighted path
VIX / VSTOXX Expected vol (annualized σ) VIX 20 ≈ 1.26% daily moves
VIX term structure Contango = calm, backwardation = stressed Front/2nd month ratio
Put/call skew (25Δ) Demand for downside protection Elevated skew = fear is specific to downside
Credit spreads (CDX IG/HY) Default risk priced by market IG 100bp ≈ benign; HY 500bp+ = stress
TIPS breakevens Inflation expectations 5Y BEI vs 10Y BEI; slope = forward inflation shift
Oil term structure Supply/demand balance Backwardation = tight now; deep contango = glut
USD (DXY) Risk appetite + rate differentials Strong USD = tightening financial conditions globally
Gold Real yield + tail hedging demand Gold up while USD flat = fear bid

Worked example — reading the dashboard:

VIX at 24, skew at 8 vol points, HY spreads 450bp, 2s10s at -40bp, oil in backwardation, gold at $2,300.
Translation: Market expects elevated but not crisis-level volatility. Skew says downside hedging demand is firm. Credit is stressed but not distressed. Yield curve inverted = recession priced. Oil tight now but not pricing prolonged disruption. Gold elevated = some tail fear.
Gap: VIX at 24 implies ±1.5% daily. Recent realized has been ±2.5%. Realized > implied → vol is underpriced.

Step 2: Identify Where Consensus Could Break

The market prices the modal outcome (most likely). It consistently under-prices:
- Binary events where probability × impact doesn't match the implied price
- Regime shifts that invalidate the last 6-12 months of correlation patterns
- Second-order effects (oil up → inflation up → rates up → credit stress → contagion)
- Positioning-driven amplification (crowded trades that create forced selling cascades)

The "What If I'm Wrong" Test

For any market consensus, ask:
1. What assumption needs to hold for consensus to be right?
2. What would falsify that assumption?
3. What's the magnitude of the move if it fails?
4. How quickly would our book feel it?

Consensus Key assumption Falsification trigger Impact if wrong
"Fed will cut in June" Inflation continues cooling Core CPI MoM > 0.4% Rates +30bp, equities -3-5%
"Oil spike is temporary" Geopolitical resolution in weeks Hormuz blocked through summer Brent $130-150, gasoline $6+
"Credit is fine" Defaults stay below 3% Leveraged sector blow-up HY spreads +200bp, equity -8%
"China stimulus works" Property sector stabilizes New developer default EM equities -10%, copper -15%
"AI trade continues" Earnings growth justifies multiples Revenue miss at mega-cap Nasdaq -12%, sector rotation

Step 3: Quantify The Asymmetry

This is where you differentiate yourself. Don't just say "there's a risk." Put numbers on it.

Asymmetry Score Formula:

$$\text{Asymmetry} = \frac{P_{\text{tail}} \times \text{Loss}{\text{tail}}}{P{\text{base}} \times \text{Gain}_{\text{base}}}$$

If asymmetry > 1.0, the risk-weighted outcome is against the current position.

Worked example:

Market pricing: 70% chance Fed cuts (equities +2%), 30% chance hold + hawkish (equities -5%)
Asymmetry = (0.30 × 5%) / (0.70 × 2%) = 1.5 / 1.4 = 1.07
Conclusion: Even though a cut is more likely, the expected value of the equity position is slightly negative risk-weighted. The portfolio needs tail protection.

Another way to express this to a PM:

"We make $2M if the base case plays out, but lose $5M in the tail. Spending $200K on protection gives us 25:1 payoff in the tail while only reducing the base case by 10%."

Step 4: Map To Portfolio Exposure

Your view must connect to this book, today. Generic macro commentary is useless.

For each risk you identify:
1. Which positions are exposed? (name + notional)
2. Through which channel? (delta, gamma, vega, correlation, liquidity)
3. What's the estimated P&L impact under the scenario?
4. What would change the thesis?

Template:

RISK: [description]
EXPOSED POSITIONS: [name, notional, channel]
SCENARIO P&L: [base case → $X, tail case → -$Y]
ASYMMETRY: [ratio]
RECOMMENDATION: [specific action + cost]

Step 5: Formulate The Recommendation

Risk managers who just identify problems are annoying. Risk managers who solve them are invaluable.

The Recommendation Stack (always present these 3):

Option Description Cost VaR reduction
Option A Reduce exposure (trim position by X%) Opportunity cost -30% VaR
Option B Hedge with derivatives (specific structure) Premium $ -50% tail VaR
Option C Accept risk, tighten stops $0 direct cost -0% (but bounded losses)

Critical: You RECOMMEND one option, but you present all three. "I'd lean toward Option B because cost is low relative to the tail risk, but it's your call."

Step 6: Set Kill Conditions

Every view needs a falsification trigger — a specific data point or price level that would make you change your mind.

  • "I'll drop this concern if core CPI MoM comes in below 0.3%"
  • "If VIX sustains below 18 for 5 days, the vol regime has normalized"
  • "If Hormuz shipping resumes above 80% of normal capacity, the oil premium unwinds"

Why this matters: It prevents anchoring bias. It shows the interviewer you think probabilistically, not ideologically.

PART 3: THE OPINION QUALITY SPECTRUM

Level 1 — Headline Parrot (FAIL)

"I think the market is risky because of tariffs and geopolitical tensions."
- No specifics, no numbers, no portfolio connection. Sounds like a news headline.

Level 2 — Informed Observer (WEAK)

"VIX is at 24, credit spreads are widening, and the Fed faces a difficult decision."
- Cites data but doesn't interpret it. No view, no recommendation.

Level 3 — Analyst (ACCEPTABLE)

"VIX at 24 implies ±1.5% daily moves, but realized vol is running at 2.5%. The market is underpricing volatility ahead of CPI."
- Interprets data, identifies a gap. But no portfolio implication.

Level 4 — Risk Manager (GOOD)

"Realized vol exceeding implied by 60% means our delta-hedging is more expensive than our models assume. With CPI Friday, I'd recommend the book moves to shorter gamma rebalancing intervals and we stress-test the hedging P&L for realized vol at 30."
- Identifies risk → quantifies → connects to book → recommends action.

Level 5 — Strategic Risk Partner (OUTSTANDING)

"We're in a positive stock-bond correlation regime with realized vol exceeding implied. This combination means our portfolio's Sharpe ratio math is broken — diversification assumptions overstate our risk-adjusted returns by ~20%. Before CPI, I'd recommend: (1) shrink gross by 10% to match the true diversification benefit, (2) move from correlation-based hedges to explicit puts, and (3) widen the house stress scenarios to include 'hot CPI + bond sell-off' coincidence. Cost: $400K in premium + opportunity cost. Benefit: eliminates the uncompensated tail that's sitting in four PM books."
- Regime-aware, quantified, specific, cost-benefit framed, actionable. This is the standard.

PART 4: BUILDING VIEWS ON 8 KEY RISK THEMES

For each theme, here's the complete thinking structure:

Theme 1: Central Bank Policy

What to track: Fed funds futures, dot plot, OIS curve, CB communication tone
Where consensus breaks: When the data forces a policy choice that conflicts with forward guidance

Scenario Signal it's coming Book impact RM action
Dovish surprise (cut) NFP < 100K, core CPI < 0.2% MoM Rates rally, equity up, USD down, gold up Check for short-duration positions that get hurt; reduce hedges that assumed hawkish
Hawkish surprise (hold/hike) Core CPI > 0.4%, wage growth > 5% Rates sell off, growth stocks hammered, USD up Check for levered long-duration; add rate hedge pre-event
Communication gaffe Fed member contradicts consensus Vol spike, whipsaw Warn PMs to reduce event-day gamma; widen stops

Framework phrase for interview: "I don't predict what the Fed does. I map the scenarios, assess which one the book is most vulnerable to, and make sure we're either compensated for that risk or protected against it."

Theme 2: Energy / Commodity Supply

What to track: Oil term structure, shipping data, inventories (EIA/API), geopolitical news
Where consensus breaks: Supply disruption duration — markets consistently underestimate how long conflicts last

Key transmission chain:
Oil up → input cost inflation → margin compression (airlines, transport, chemicals) → consumer spending down → earnings revisions → equity sell-off BUT energy/defense sectors rally → cross-asset correlation spikes → diversification fails

Theme 3: Credit Cycle

What to track: CDX IG/HY spreads, leveraged loan prices, fallen angel count, bank lending standards
Where consensus breaks: Credit cycles turn suddenly — spreads are calm until they aren't

Red flags:
- HY-IG spread ratio widening → lowest quality getting hit first
- CCC-rated paper underperforming B-rated → bottom of the barrel cracking
- Leveraged loan repricing → private credit stress
- Bank loan officer survey tightening → upstream supply of credit shrinking

Theme 4: Volatility Regime

What to track: VIX level, VIX term structure shape, realized vs implied spread, VVIX (vol of vol)
Where consensus breaks: VIX can be low for months then explode — it mean-reverts but from catastrophic levels, not from low

VIX regime Implication RM posture
< 14 Complacent. Vol is cheap. Recommend buying tail protection (cheap insurance)
14-20 Normal. Markets pricing moderate uncertainty. Standard monitoring
20-30 Elevated. Hedging is expensive but may be necessary. Tighten gross, shorten gamma rebal frequency
30-40 Stressed. Consider if positions can withstand this. Active de-risking, margin monitoring
> 40 Crisis. Liquidity dries up. Emergency mode: see R playbooks

Theme 5: Positioning / Crowding

What to track: CFTC COT data, prime broker positioning reports, ETF flows, short interest
Where consensus breaks: When everyone is on the same side, the exit is too small

Crowding indicators:
- Record net long/short in futures → reversal risk
- Concentrated short interest → squeeze risk
- ETF inflow streak → momentum fragility
- Low dispersion + high correlation → herding

RM application: "Our PM is long the same trade that every macro fund is long according to COT data. If this reverses, liquidity will evaporate because everyone is hitting the same exit. I'd recommend reducing size by 20% or adding explicit stop-losses, even though the fundamental thesis is intact."

Theme 6: FX / Dollar Dynamics

What to track: DXY, rate differentials, FX vol (CVIX), EM FX basket, risk reversals
Where consensus breaks: Dollar moves affect everything — it's the most underrated cross-asset driver

Transmission: Strong USD → EM stress → commodity prices down → carry trade losses → vol spike → risk-off cascade

Theme 7: Liquidity Conditions

What to track: Fed balance sheet, bank reserves, repo rates, bid-ask spreads, market depth
Where consensus breaks: Liquidity is invisible until it's gone

Warning signals:
- ON RRP dropping toward zero → reserve scarcity approaching
- Repo rate spikes → funding stress
- Bid-ask widening in treasuries → market-maker withdrawal
- Flash-crash frequency increasing → microstructure fragility

Theme 8: Earnings & Valuation

What to track: Forward EPS revisions, sector earnings breadth, multiple compression/expansion
Where consensus breaks: Earnings estimates are always wrong — the question is by how much and in which direction

RM lens on earnings: "I don't care if earnings beat by 5%. I care about the REVISION TREND. If forward estimates are being cut, the market is pricing hope. If our book has beta-1 to equities, every 1% earnings revision down = ~1.5% equity downside via multiple compression."

PART 5: CALIBRATING CONVICTION — THE CONFIDENCE FRAMEWORK

The Bayesian Risk Manager

Your views should be held probabilistically, not dogmatically. Assign a confidence level and tie it to evidence quality:

Confidence Evidence quality How to express Position sizing analog
90%+ Multiple confirming data, consistent across instruments "I'm highly confident that..." Full recommended hedge
70-90% Strong data but some ambiguity, conflicting signals exist "The balance of evidence suggests..." Partial hedge, contingency plan
50-70% Mixed signals, limited data, high uncertainty "I lean toward X but could easily be wrong..." Monitor, prepare but don't act
< 50% Too many unknowns "I don't have a strong view — here's the framework..." No directional view, focus on risk bounds

Updating Your Views (Bayesian Updating)

When new data arrives:
1. What was your prior? (what you believed before)
2. What does the data say? (the signal)
3. How much should you update? (depends on signal quality vs prior confidence)

Rules of thumb:
- One data point rarely justifies a view change → "noisy signal"
- Three consecutive data points in the same direction → trend → update
- One outlier data point → investigate, don't react
- Data that contradicts your highest-confidence view → take it seriously; that's the most informative signal

Worked example:

Prior: 70% probability inflation is cooling (based on 3 months of declining core CPI)
New data: Core CPI MoM prints 0.5% (consensus was 0.3%)
Update: This is a 2σ miss. One month doesn't prove a trend, but it breaks the declining pattern. New estimate: 50% inflation cooling, 50% re-acceleration.
Portfolio action: "My cooling view was 70/30. After this print, it's closer to 50/50. The asymmetry has shifted — I'd recommend adding rate protection for the re-acceleration scenario. Cost: $150K in swaption premium."

PART 7: DEFENDING YOUR VIEW UNDER PRESSURE

The Push-Back Playbook

PMs and interviewers will challenge you. They're testing if you crumble, get defensive, or hold your ground with data.

Challenge 1: "You're being too bearish"

Bad response: "No, I'm right, look at the data."
Good response: "I might be. The base case is constructive and I agree with that. My concern is specific: the tail scenario carries 3x the P&L impact of the base case, and it's only 30% priced. I'm not saying we de-risk — I'm saying we should understand what we lose if consensus is wrong, and whether the cost of hedging that tail is worth paying."

Challenge 2: "VIX is already elevated, protection is expensive"

Good response: "VIX at 24 implies daily moves of ±1.5%, but we've realized ±2.5% three times this week. Implied is actually CHEAP relative to what's happening. And if you're worried about cost, we can structure a put spread — gives us downside coverage for about half the price of a naked put. The marginal cost vs the tail protection is favorable."

Challenge 3: "But the fundamental thesis is intact"

Good response: "Your thesis may be 100% right on direction and still lose money due to path. If vol stays at 30, your stop-loss gets triggered before your thesis plays out. Sizing is a risk management input, not a conviction input. I'd suggest reducing size to survive the vol, keeping the thesis."

Challenge 4: "Why should I listen to a risk manager about the market?"

Good response: "You shouldn't listen to me about direction — that's your edge. Listen to me about the DISTRIBUTION of outcomes. I see the aggregate book every day. I know where our exposures are correlated across PMs. I know when positioning is crowded. And I know what the worst day looks like. That's information you don't have in isolation."

Challenge 5: "I've seen this pattern before, it always resolves"

Good response: "Survivorship bias. We remember the times it resolved because those are boring. We forget the times it didn't because the people who stayed levered into those aren't around to tell the story. What's the cost of being protected if you're right? It's small. What's the cost of being unprotected if you're wrong?"

The 5 Rules of Defending Views

  1. Acknowledge first. "You might be right that..." → then pivot to asymmetry.
  2. Data, not emotion. Numbers, scenarios, historical precedent — never "I feel like..."
  3. Frame as risk geometry, not direction. "I'm not saying it goes down. I'm saying the down scenario isn't priced."
  4. Always offer solutions. The view without a recommendation is just complaining.
  5. Know when to step back. PM heard you, accepts the risk, it's within budget → document and move on. You did your job.

PART 8: COMMON OPINION TRAPS AND HOW TO AVOID THEM

Trap 1: Anchoring to Your First View

What happens: You form a view at 7am. Data changes by 10am. You ignore the new data.
Fix: Set specific data thresholds that trigger view updates (kill conditions). Review your view after every material data point. "Was I right for the right reasons?"

Trap 2: Narrative Creep

What happens: You build a compelling story and start fitting all new data into it, even contradictory data.
Fix: For every view, write down in advance what data would DISPROVE it. If that data arrives, update.

Trap 3: Complexity as a Crutch

What happens: You make your view so nuanced that it can never be wrong because it covers every scenario.
Fix: Force yourself to state your view in ONE sentence with a specific number. "I think realized vol will exceed implied by at least 30% over the next two weeks." Measurable. Falsifiable.

Trap 4: Confusing Consensus With Stupid

What happens: You automatically assume the market is wrong because you want to look contrarian.
Fix: Consensus is right most of the time. The value is in identifying the SPECIFIC SPOTS where it's wrong. Start from "the market is probably right" and work from there.

Trap 5: Risk Theater

What happens: You flag 15 risks every morning. None are actionable. PMs tune you out.
Fix: Flag 1-2 things that ACTUALLY MATTER today. Specificity beats comprehensiveness.

Trap 6: Overconfidence After a Good Call

What happens: You predicted the last sell-off correctly. Now you think you can predict markets.
Fix: You didn't predict it — you identified the asymmetry. Stay humble. Risk management isn't about being right; it's about being prepared.

  4. Maintain a running "What changed?" list. By end of day, you should be able to say: "Three things changed today: [A], [B], [C]. Here's what it means for our book."

PART 10: FROM VIEW TO ACTION — THE COMPLETE WORKFLOW

Morning Routine: The 3-Question Drill (90 seconds)

Before you open Bloomberg, answer from MEMORY:

  1. What's the biggest risk to the book TODAY?
  2. Must be specific: "CPI print in 4 hours could show re-acceleration, and we're net long 5Y duration"

  3. Not vague: "markets are risky"

  4. Is the market pricing it?

  5. Check: VIX, skew, relevant futures, credit spreads
  6. If YES → no edge in protection, but validate our sizing

  7. If NO → asymmetry exists → proceed to recommendation

  8. What would I recommend IF it materializes?

  9. Must be specific: "Reduce duration by $10M DV01, buy 1-month 25Δ SPX puts"
  10. Not vague: "we should de-risk"

The View Journal

Keep a running log. Accountability mechanism + pattern recognition:

Date View Confidence Kill condition Outcome What I learned
Apr 7 RV > IV for 2 more weeks 75% VIX term structure normalizes (contango > 5%) Pending -
Apr 3 HY-IG spread ratio signals credit stress 60% CDX HY back below 400bp Wrong — HY tightened Spread widening was technical (ETF rebalance), not fundamental
Mar 28 Oil backwardation deepens through April 80% EIA inventory build > 5M bbl Right Geo premium persists longer than consensus expects

Why this matters in interviews: "I keep a view journal. It forces me to be specific, accountable, and honest about when I'm wrong. Over the last month, I've been right about [X] because [evidence], wrong about [Y] because [reason I missed]. The mistake taught me [specific lesson]."

When To Stay Silent

Having a view is important. Knowing when NOT to express one is equally important.

Stay silent when:
- You're less informed than the PM on their specific sector
- The risk is within normal operating parameters and they clearly see it
- You just flagged this yesterday and nothing has changed
- The meeting already has 5 agenda items and yours is #7 in importance

Speak up when:
- You see cross-PM correlation risk that no individual PM can see
- The risk has migrated from "within tolerance" to "approaching threshold"
- New data materially changes the asymmetry
- The PM is in a behavioral pattern you've seen before (adding to losers, anchoring)

PART 11: OPINION FORMATION UNDER UNCERTAINTY — REGIME AMBIGUITY

What To Do When You Genuinely Don't Know

The most honest and dangerous answer in risk management is: "I don't know." Make it useful:

The Uncertainty Template:

"I don't have a strong view on [X]. Here's what I do know:
- The range of outcomes is [wide/narrow]
- The market is pricing [consensus]
- Our book's exposure to the tails is [quantified]
- Regardless of direction, the risk management action is [specific]"

Example:

"I don't know if the Fed cuts or holds. The market is pricing 55/45. What I do know:
- If they hold, our duration position loses $3M. If they cut, it gains $1M.
- The asymmetry says we should have less duration going in, regardless of view.
- Cost of reducing duration exposure: ~$50K in transaction costs.
- The risk-adjusted case for protection is clear even without a directional opinion."

Scenario-Based Thinking as an Alternative to Conviction

When you can't form a view, form a decision tree:

          Market
         /      \
     Cut (55%)   Hold (45%)
     /    \       /     \
  + Dovish  Neutral  + Hawkish  Neutral
  guidance           guidance
   (30%)    (25%)    (25%)     (20%)
  Eq +3%   Eq +1%   Eq -5%    Eq -2%

Expected = 0.30(+3) + 0.25(+1) + 0.25(-5) + 0.20(-2)
         = 0.90 + 0.25 - 1.25 - 0.40 = -0.50%

→ Expected equity return is NEGATIVE despite the cut being more likely.
→ This justifies protective positioning.

PART 12: INTERVIEW ANSWERS — OPINION FORMATION

Q: "What's a market risk you'd be watching right now?"

"The risk I find most interesting right now is the divergence between credit and equity signals. CDX High Yield has widened about 60 basis points over the past three weeks while the S&P is roughly flat. Historically, when credit leads equities by more than 2 weeks, equities tend to converge to credit's signal about 70% of the time.

For a discretionary equity book, this means one of two things: either credit is seeing a real fundamental deterioration — in which case equities will catch down 5-8% — or credit is mispricing idiosyncratic stress as systematic. The data I'd watch to distinguish: if the widening is concentrated in CCC/B names and not BB, it's likely idiosyncratic and less contagious.

My recommendation to a PM would be: reduce net long exposure by 10-15% until the divergence resolves, and buy 1-month SPX put spreads. Cost is modest relative to the insurance value. If credit tightens back and I'm wrong, we gave up a few basis points. If credit is right, we're protected."

Q: "How do you form a market view differently from a trader?"

"A trader needs to be right directionally — they're betting capital on 'this goes up.' My job is different. I need to identify where the DISTRIBUTION of outcomes is mispriced, especially in the tails.

My process has six steps: I start by mapping what the market is pricing across instruments — not just equities but rates, credit, vol, and FX because they often disagree. Then I identify where consensus could break, quantify the asymmetry of outcomes, map it to our specific portfolio exposures, formulate a recommendation with cost-benefit, and set a kill condition for when I'd change my mind.

For example, right now the VIX term structure is in backwardation for the third straight day while realized vol is running 60% above implied. That tells me the market expects near-term trouble but thinks it's short-lived. But historically, sustained backwardation precedes corrections. My view isn't 'markets crash' — it's 'the distribution has a fatter left tail than the market is pricing, and at current vol levels, protection is actually cheap relative to what's being realized.'"

Q: "Give me an example of when you'd push back on a PM vs. when you'd stay silent?"

"I'd push back when I see something the PM can't see from their individual book — specifically, cross-PM correlation risk.

If three PMs all independently added energy exposure this week, each position might look fine in isolation. But in aggregate, the house is now 2x our sector concentration limit in energy, and all three positions lose in the same scenario: oil reversal. That's invisible to any individual PM and it's my job to surface it. I'd go to each PM with the data: 'Your position alone is fine. But the house is concentrated. We need one of you to hedge or reduce by $X to stay within aggregate limits.'

I'd stay silent when a PM has a position I personally disagree with directionally — say they're long a stock I think is overvalued — but the position is within risk limits, properly sized, and they clearly have a thesis. That's not my call. My job is risk geometry, not stock-picking. I'd document that I reviewed it, note the key risk factors, and move on."
