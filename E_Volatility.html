E — VOLATILITY: CONCEPT & PRODUCT

Implied vs realized, the vol surface, vol as a tradeable, and dealer dynamics

Depth: 🟡 WELL

PART 1: WHAT VOLATILITY ACTUALLY IS

Realized (Historical) Volatility

The standard deviation of actual returns over a lookback period. "How much did this asset actually move?"

  • Daily realized vol: Standard deviation of daily returns × √252 (to annualize). 252 trading days/year.
  • Example: If daily returns have a standard deviation of 1.2%, annualized realized vol = 1.2% × √252 ≈ 19%.
  • Lookback matters: 10-day realized vol captures the recent regime. 60-day is smoother. 1-year is a longer trend. They can diverge significantly.

Implied Volatility

The market's FORECAST of future volatility, as embedded in option prices. "How much does the market THINK this asset will move?"

  • You can't observe implied vol directly — it's backed out of option prices using Black-Scholes (or another model).
  • Give Black-Scholes the market price, spot, strike, time, rate → solve for σ. That σ is implied vol.
  • Implied vol is FORWARD-LOOKING. Realized vol is BACKWARD-LOOKING.

The Volatility Risk Premium (VRP)

Implied vol is almost always HIGHER than subsequent realized vol.

Why? Because options are insurance. People pay a premium for protection, just like car insurance costs more than the expected value of claims.

  • Long-run average: implied vol is ~2-4 vol points above realized vol for SPX.
  • This is why selling options is profitable on average — you collect the VRP.
  • BUT: when vol spikes, realized can EXCEED implied temporarily (the insurance underpays). This is when short vol strategies blow up.

VRP trade: Sell implied, buy realized. Systematically profitable but has HUGE left-tail risk. See: Volmageddon (Feb 2018), XIV ETN → zero.

PART 2: THE VOLATILITY SURFACE

Why One Number Isn't Enough

Black-Scholes assumes constant volatility across all strikes and maturities. Reality disagrees. Implied vol varies by BOTH strike and expiry. This creates a 2D surface.

Skew (the Smile along Strike)

Equity skew: OTM puts have HIGHER implied vol than ATM options, which have higher vol than OTM calls. The surface "smirks" to the left.

Why?
1. Demand: Everyone buys downside protection (puts) → demand pushes up put prices → higher implied vol for low strikes.
2. Fat tails: Markets crash more suddenly than they rally. The probability of a -20% move is higher than what a normal distribution predicts → puts need to be more expensive.
3. Leverage effect: When stocks drop, companies become more leveraged (debt/equity ratio rises) → riskier → vol should be higher at lower prices.

Measuring skew:
- 25-delta risk reversal: IV of 25-delta put minus IV of 25-delta call. More negative = steeper skew.
- 90-100% skew: IV at 90% moneyness minus IV at 100% (ATM). Tells you how much more expensive puts are.

Skew tells you something: Steep skew = market is nervous about downside. Flat skew = market is complacent. Skew STEEPENS in sell-offs and FLATTENS in rallies.

Term Structure (along Time)

Normal (upward sloping): Longer-dated options have higher implied vol than shorter-dated. Makes sense — more uncertainty over longer horizons.

Inverted (downward sloping): Short-dated options have HIGHER implied vol than long-dated. This happens when there's a near-term event (earnings, FOMC, geopolitical) that the market expects to resolve. After the event → vol should normalize.

Contango vs backwardation in vol language: Normal term structure = vol contango. Inverted = vol backwardation. VIX futures are typically in contango (longer-dated VIX > shorter-dated).

PART 3: VOLATILITY MODELS

SABR (Stochastic Alpha Beta Rho)

What it does: Models the vol surface by making vol itself stochastic (vol of vol). Four parameters:

Parameter What it controls
α (alpha) Level of vol at current forward
β (beta) Backbone — how vol moves with the underlying (0 = normal, 1 = lognormal)
ρ (rho) Correlation between spot and vol. Negative ρ → skew (as spot drops, vol rises)
ν (nu) Vol of vol. Higher ν → fatter tails, steeper wings

Why you know this: You worked with SABR at Natixis. It's the industry standard for swaption and FX vol surfaces. The Hagan analytical approximation makes it fast to calibrate.

Limitations of SABR:
- Single expiry — calibrated per expiry slice, doesn't enforce consistency across the term structure.
- Can produce negative probability densities for extreme strikes (arbitrage in the wings).
- No jumps — continuous dynamics. Can't handle overnight gaps.

Local Vol (Dupire)

  • Extracts a unique local volatility function from the full vol surface such that all option prices are replicated.
  • "What vol would a particle need at each (spot, time) point to match market prices?"
  • Problem: the dynamics are wrong — it predicts that vol moves inversely with spot (which IS true for equities but by the wrong amount). Forward skew is too flat.

Stochastic Vol (Heston)

  • Vol follows its own mean-reverting stochastic process: $dv = \kappa(\theta - v)dt + \xi\sqrt{v}dW$
  • Five parameters: initial vol, long-run vol (θ), mean reversion speed (κ), vol of vol (ξ), correlation (ρ).
  • Better dynamics than local vol but harder to calibrate.
  • Used for exotic options where path matters (barriers, Asian).

PART 4: VOLATILITY AS A TRADEABLE

VIX — The "Fear Index"

What it is: The 30-day expected volatility of the S&P 500, annualized. Calculated from SPX option prices across strikes.

VIX levels and what they mean:

VIX Market state
10-14 Extreme complacency. "Picking up pennies." Short vol is very crowded.
15-20 Normal. Healthy uncertainty.
20-30 Elevated. Market is nervous. Hedging demand is up.
30-40 Fear. Significant stress event underway.
40+ Panic. March 2020 hit 82.

VIX behavior:
- VIX is negatively correlated with SPX (~-0.75 to -0.85). Market drops → VIX spikes.
- The relationship is asymmetric: VIX spikes much harder on market drops than it falls on market rallies.
- VIX is mean-reverting: extreme spikes compress back over days/weeks.
- You CANNOT trade VIX directly. You trade VIX futures, VIX options, or VIX ETPs.

VIX Futures

  • Trade on CBOE Futures Exchange. Monthly expiry.
  • Usually in contango: Longer-dated VIX futures > shorter-dated > VIX spot. Why? Roll cost + insurance premium.
  • VIX futures don't track VIX spot well. VIX spot can spike 50% in a day; the 2-month VIX future might move 15%.
  • Roll cost crushes VIX ETPs: Long VIX ETPs (like UVXY) lose money systematically due to contango roll. This is by design — the contango IS the vol risk premium.

Variance Swaps

  • Pay or receive the difference between realized variance and a fixed strike variance.
  • Long variance swap: you profit if realized vol > strike. You're buying vol.
  • Short variance swap: you profit if realized vol < strike. You're selling vol.
  • Key difference from options: Variance swaps are linear in VARIANCE (not vol). Since variance = vol², large moves are amplified quadratically. A 40-vol realized against a 20-vol strike isn't 2x payout — it's 4x (1600 variance vs 400 variance).
  • Short variance swap risk: unlimited. This is what killed XIV / short vol funds in Feb 2018.

Straddles as Vol Trades

  • Buy ATM straddle = buy volatility. You profit if the stock moves MORE than the straddle implies.
  • Breakeven move = straddle price / stock price. If a $100 stock's ATM straddle costs $5, the implied move is 5%. Stock needs to move >5% in either direction for you to profit at expiry.
  • Pre-earnings straddle pricing: "The straddle implies a 7% move on earnings. Historical average earnings move is 5%. Straddle is expensive."

PART 5: DEALER GAMMA & MARKET DYNAMICS

Who Is the Market Maker?

Options dealers (banks, market makers) sell options to clients (hedge funds, pension funds, corporates) and delta-hedge their inventory. Their gamma position determines how they impact the underlying market.

Dealer Long Gamma (Normal State)

  • Dealers have sold puts to clients (who want protection) and delta-hedged.
  • As the market drops → put deltas increase → dealers need to BUY stock to stay hedged.
  • As the market rises → put deltas decrease → dealers need to SELL stock.
  • Effect on market: Dealers BUY dips and SELL rallies → DAMPENS volatility. Acts as a stabilizer.

Dealer Short Gamma (Danger Zone)

  • In certain conditions (after a sell-off, or around large put strikes), dealers can flip SHORT gamma.
  • Now: market drops → dealers need to SELL to stay hedged. Market rises → dealers need to BUY.
  • Effect on market: Dealers SELL into sell-offs and BUY into rallies → AMPLIFIES volatility. Acts as a destabilizer.

The Gamma Flip Level

  • The SPX price at which dealers switch from long to short gamma.
  • Below the flip: Moves are larger, faster, and more violent. Vol realized is higher.
  • Above the flip: Moves are contained. Vol is suppressed.
  • Firms like SpotGamma, SqueezeMetrics publish estimated dealer gamma positioning. It's an input, not a crystal ball.

OPEX (Options Expiration) Effects

  • On monthly expiry (3rd Friday) and especially quarterly expiry ("quad witch"):
  • Large open interest expires or is rolled.
  • Gamma exposure drops sharply (options expire → no more gamma to hedge).
  • Market can "unpin" and move sharply after expiry.
  • Pin risk: Stock gravitates toward strikes with large open interest as dealers hedge → reduces realized vol going into expiry → then potentially releases after expiry.

PART 6: VOLATILITY AS A RISK INDICATOR

What Vol Tells the RM

Signal Interpretation Action
Implied < realized Market is complacent — options are cheap relative to actual moves Buy protection while it's cheap
Implied >> realized Market is scared — options are expensive Protection is costly. Consider selling premium (with limits).
VIX term structure inverted Near-term fear is extreme (event risk) Near-term hedging is expensive. Consider longer-dated alternatives.
Skew steepening rapidly Strong demand for puts — someone is buying protection aggressively Watch for large position liquidation or event risk
Correlation spike (implied) All stocks moving together — systematic risk dominating Reduce net delta. Diversification isn't working.
VIX > 30 and rising Full risk-off mode. Liquidity drying up. Tighten all limits. Active communication with PMs. Daily IC updates.

PART 7: INTERVIEW APPLICATION

If asked "Explain implied vs realized volatility and why the difference matters":

"Realized vol is what actually happened — the standard deviation of past returns. Implied vol is what the market expects to happen — it's extracted from option prices using a pricing model.

The key relationship is: implied is almost always higher than subsequent realized. This is the volatility risk premium — the cost of insurance. It's why selling options is profitable on average.

For risk management, I care about three things: First, the level of implied vol — is protection cheap or expensive right now? Second, the ratio of implied to realized — if realized is running above implied, the market is underestimating risk and I'd want to add protection. Third, the structure — is skew steep (fear of downside), is the term structure inverted (near-term event fear)? Each of these tells me something different about the risk environment."

If asked about SABR:

"SABR is a stochastic volatility model I worked with at Natixis for calibrating volatility surfaces. It has four parameters — alpha controls the overall vol level, beta controls how vol moves with the underlying, rho captures the skew through spot-vol correlation, and nu is the vol of vol which drives the wings.

Its main advantage is that it's analytically tractable — the Hagan approximation makes it fast to calibrate per expiry. The main limitation is that it's a single-expiry model — it doesn't enforce consistency across the term structure, so you calibrate each expiry independently and can get arbitrage between expiries if you're not careful."
