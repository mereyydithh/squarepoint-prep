E — VOLATILITY: THE ASSET CLASS BEHIND EVERY OPTION

Realized vs implied, the vol surface, vol regimes, vol products, dealer dynamics, and the RM's vol toolkit

Depth: 🔴 DEEP

WHY THIS IS CORE AT SQUAREPOINT

At a systematic-at-its-core fund running discretionary strategies, volatility isn't just a parameter in a model — it's a tradeable asset, a risk indicator, and a regime signal all at once. Every PM's book has implicit or explicit vol exposure. Every straddle, every protective put, every variance swap, every gamma scalp is a vol position. The risk manager's job is to:

  1. Decompose — what vol level, surface shape, and dynamics are priced into the current book?
  2. Monitor — is the implied-realized spread compressing or expanding? Is skew telling us something?
  3. Anticipate — what happens to the portfolio if we shift from a 15-vol regime to a 35-vol regime overnight?
  4. Challenge — is the PM paying too much for vol? Selling it too cheaply? Ignoring the term structure?

Vol is the connective tissue between Greeks, P&L attribution, hedging, and regime analysis. If you don't understand vol deeply, you can't do the job.

PART 1: WHAT VOLATILITY ACTUALLY IS

Realized (Historical) Volatility

One-liner: Realized vol measures how much the asset ACTUALLY moved — the backward-looking truth.

The annualized standard deviation of log returns over a lookback window:

$$\sigma_{\text{realized}} = \sqrt{\frac{252}{n-1} \sum_{i=1}^{n} (r_i - \bar{r})^2}$$

Where $r_i = \ln(S_i / S_{i-1})$ and 252 = trading days per year.

Worked example: SPX daily returns over 10 days: +0.8%, −1.2%, +0.3%, −0.5%, +1.5%, −2.0%, +0.4%, +0.1%, −0.7%, +0.6%
- Standard deviation of these returns ≈ 1.01%
- Annualized: 1.01% × √252 ≈ 16.0%

Lookback Window Matters — A LOT

Window What it captures Use case
5-day Very recent micro-regime. Noisy. Intraday/gamma scalping reference
10-day Short-term regime. Responsive to events. Near-term vol comparison vs 10d IV
20-day (~1 month) Standard "recent" realized vol Main comparison vs 30-day IV
60-day (~3 months) Medium-term trend Structural regime assessment
252-day (1 year) Long-term baseline Vol regime classification (historically low/high?)

The divergence problem: In January 2018, 252-day realized vol was ~7% (historic lows). 10-day realized was ~4%. VIX was 11. Then Feb 5 hit — VIX tripled in one day. ALL lookback windows were backward-looking by definition. None warned you.

Alternative Realized Vol Estimators

Estimator Formula concept Why it exists
Close-to-close Standard deviation of log returns Simple, standard. Misses intraday.
Parkinson (High-Low) Uses daily range (H−L) ~5x more efficient than close-to-close. Captures intraday moves.
Garman-Klass Uses O, H, L, C Most efficient unbiased estimator for continuous processes.
Yang-Zhang O, H, L, C + overnight jumps Handles overnight gaps AND intraday — best all-around for equities.
Realized variance (tick) Sum of squared intraday returns Highest frequency. Used in academic research. Microstructure noise issues.

At Squarepoint's level: You don't just compute "realized vol" — you choose the estimator that matches the question. Close-to-close for comparison with BS-implied vol (which is a close-to-close concept). Parkinson for true intraday activity. Yang-Zhang if overnight gaps matter (single stocks, EM).

Implied Volatility

One-liner: Implied vol is the market's consensus forecast of future volatility — backed out of option prices. It's the weather forecast, not the weather.

You observe an option's market price. You have all Black-Scholes inputs except σ. Solve for σ:

$$C_{\text{market}} = \text{BS}(S, K, T, r, q, \sigma_{\text{implied}})$$

Newton-Raphson or bisection → you get σ_implied. This single number tells you what vol the market is collectively pricing.

Critical distinction:
- Implied vol is FORWARD-LOOKING. It reflects expectations + risk premium for the FUTURE period until expiry.
- Realized vol is BACKWARD-LOOKING. It tells you what ALREADY happened.
- They answer different questions. Comparing them tells you whether options are "cheap" or "expensive."

Implied Vol ≠ Expected Vol

Implied vol contains TWO components:
1. Expected future realized vol — the market's best guess of actual movements
2. Volatility risk premium (VRP) — the extra charge for bearing vol risk (insurance premium)

$$\sigma_{\text{implied}} = \sigma_{\text{expected realized}} + \text{VRP}$$

You CANNOT assume IV = expected vol. The VRP means options are systematically "overpriced" relative to what actually realizes. This is a feature, not a bug — it compensates sellers for bearing tail risk.

The Volatility Risk Premium (VRP)

One-liner: Implied vol is almost always higher than subsequent realized vol. The difference is the insurance premium — and it's what makes selling vol profitable on average, and catastrophic in the tail.

Empirical facts for SPX:
- Long-run average VRP: ~2-4 vol points (implied > realized)
- VRP is present ~85% of months
- VRP is LARGER in low-vol regimes (complacency → sellers tolerate less premium)
- VRP collapses or INVERTS in crises (realized > implied → options were too cheap → sellers get destroyed)

Worked example — the VRP trade:
- Sell 1-month ATM SPX straddle at 18% IV, delta-hedge daily
- Over the month, SPX realizes 14% vol
- Your P&L ≈ vega × (IV − RV) ≈ $300K × 4 = +$1.2M (simplified)
- You "harvested" the VRP

When it inverts (the blowup):
- February 5, 2018 ("Volmageddon"): VIX went from 17 to 50 in one day. XIV ETN lost 96% of value OVERNIGHT and was terminated. Realized vol hit ~100% annualized for that day. Anyone short vol at 17 IV ate a 5σ+ loss.
- March 2020: VIX from 14 to 82. Realized vol hit 130%+ (annualized). Short vol positions destroyed across the industry.
- The VRP trade is picking up pennies in front of a steamroller — positive expected value on most days, existential risk on the worst day.

VRP by Asset Class

Asset Typical VRP (IV − RV) Notes
SPX options +2 to +4 pts Most studied. Reliable positive VRP.
Single stocks +3 to +8 pts Higher VRP (more demand for hedging + earnings events)
FX options +1 to +2 pts Smaller — FX vol is lower overall
Commodity options Varies widely Oil: large VRP. Gold: smaller. Seasonals distort.
VIX options Large positive VIX options on VIX futures — double layer of premium

PART 2: THE VOLATILITY SURFACE

Why One Number Isn't Enough

Black-Scholes assumes ONE constant σ for all strikes and maturities. Reality: implied vol varies by strike (skew/smile) and expiry (term structure), creating a 2D surface.

                    THE VOL SURFACE

    IV              ← OTM Puts (high IV)
    │
    │  ╲                          ╱  ← OTM Calls (smile in some assets)
    │    ╲                      ╱
    │      ╲                  ╱
    │        ╲──────────────╱     ← ATM (lowest for equities)
    │
    └──────────────────────────── Strike (K)
         80%    90%   100%   110%   120%

    For EQUITIES: "smirk" — left side elevated, right side modestly lower or flat
    For FX/commodities: "smile" — both wings elevated, more symmetric

Skew — The Strike Dimension

One-liner: Skew measures how much more expensive downside protection is relative to ATM options — it's the market's fear of crashes priced in real-time.

Why Equity Skew Exists (Three Reinforcing Reasons)

  1. Demand/supply imbalance: The entire institutional world buys puts (pension funds, endowments, insurance companies). Nobody systematically buys OTM calls at scale. This demand asymmetry inflates put IV.

  2. Fat-tailed returns: Equity returns are negatively skewed with excess kurtosis. The probability of SPX −20% in a month is MUCH higher than a normal distribution predicts. Options need to be priced for real-world tail risk, not Gaussian tails. Post-1987 crash, the market permanently repriced this — skew didn't exist before Black Monday.

  3. Leverage effect (Black, 1976): When stock prices fall, the debt/equity ratio rises mechanically → the firm becomes riskier → vol should be higher at lower prices. Self-reinforcing feedback loop.

Measuring Skew — The Numbers You'll Use

Metric Definition Typical SPX value What it tells you
25Δ risk reversal (RR) IV(25Δ put) − IV(25Δ call) −5 to −8 pts (negative = put premium) Direction of skew. More negative = steeper.
25Δ butterfly (BF) ½ × [IV(25Δ put) + IV(25Δ call)] − IV(ATM) +2 to +5 pts (positive = wings expensive) Curvature / kurtosis pricing. Higher = fatter tails priced.
90-100 skew IV(90% moneyness) − IV(ATM) +4 to +8 pts How much more puts cost than ATM. Simple hedge cost indicator.
Skew index (CBOE SKEW) Derived from SPX OTM option prices 100-150 (higher = more tail risk priced) Aggregate tail risk indicator.

Skew Dynamics — How It Moves

Market condition Skew behavior Why
Sell-off (SPX −3%+) Skew steepens sharply Put demand surges, call IV compressed. Fear spike.
Grinding rally Skew flattens Put demand fades, upside calls get bid. Complacency.
Vol spike (VIX +10) Skew can flatten RELATIVELY When ATM vol explodes, relative skew may compress even as absolute put IV rises
Pre-event (FOMC/CPI) Short-dated skew steepens Hedging demand concentrates in near-dated puts
Post-event resolution Skew normalizes rapidly Uncertainty resolved → hedging demand drops

Sticky-strike vs sticky-delta:
- Sticky-strike: Implied vol at a given strike stays fixed as spot moves. Implies: if spot drops 5%, ATM vol rises (because you're now ATM at a lower strike which had higher vol). This is the empirical behavior for equities — vol rises when spot drops.
- Sticky-delta: The vol at a given delta stays fixed. ATM vol doesn't change when spot moves. Less realistic for equities, more applicable to FX in calm markets.
- Why it matters: The assumption determines how you delta-hedge. Sticky-strike delta-hedging uses a different delta than sticky-delta — the difference can be 0.02-0.05 for OTM options, which on 10,000 contracts is material.

Term Structure — The Time Dimension

One-liner: The vol term structure tells you whether the market expects current vol conditions to persist or mean-revert.

Shape Name What it means When you see it
Upward sloping Contango Longer = higher IV. Normal state. Calm markets — uncertainty increases with horizon.
Flat Flat Vol expectations similar across tenors. Transition periods.
Inverted Backwardation Short-dated IV > long-dated. Near-term fear. Event risk (earnings, election, crisis). Market expects the event to resolve and vol to drop.
Humped Humped Peak at intermediate tenor (e.g., 3-month). Specific event at a known future date (e.g., 3 months out = election).

Worked example — VIX term structure in crisis:
- Normal: VIX = 15, 2-month VIX futures = 17, 4-month = 18 (contango)
- Crisis: VIX = 45, 2-month = 35, 4-month = 28 (steep backwardation — market expects vol to normalize)
- The SLOPE encodes mean-reversion expectations. Steep backwardation = "this crisis is temporary."

Term Structure as a Trading Signal

Signal Interpretation Trade
Steep contango Short vol is expensive short-dated, cheap long-dated Calendar spreads: sell near-dated vol, buy far-dated
Steep backwardation Near-term panic. Vol likely to mean-revert. Sell near-dated vol (IF you can survive the mark-to-market)
Contango flattening Front vol catching up — early warning of trouble Reduce short vol. Add near-dated protection.

The Full Surface — Strike × Time Together

Every point on the vol surface $\sigma(K, T)$ is an implied vol. The surface must satisfy no-arbitrage conditions:

  1. Calendar spread arbitrage: Total implied variance must be non-decreasing in time: $\sigma^2(K, T_2) \times T_2 \geq \sigma^2(K, T_1) \times T_1$ for $T_2 > T_1$
  2. Butterfly arbitrage: The risk-neutral density must be non-negative everywhere → constrains how fast vol can change across strikes
  3. Put-call parity: Calls and puts at same strike/expiry must imply the same vol (otherwise direct arbitrage)

Surface interpolation matters: Between observed strikes and expiries, how do you interpolate? SVI (Stochastic Volatility Inspired) parameterization is standard for equities. SABR for rates/FX. Getting this wrong → model produces arbitrageable prices → your Greeks are wrong → your hedges leak.

PART 3: VOLATILITY MODELS — HOW YOU GENERATE THE SURFACE

At Squarepoint, you won't run these models yourself as a risk manager. But you need to understand what the quants are doing, why the model matters, and where it breaks.

Model Taxonomy

Model class Key idea Strengths Weaknesses Used for
Black-Scholes Constant vol Simple, closed-form Greeks Wrong (vol isn't constant) Quick pricing, reference point
Local vol (Dupire) σ = σ(S,t), deterministic Fits any surface exactly. Unique. Wrong dynamics — forward skew too flat. Vol moves wrong with spot. Exotic pricing with surface-consistent calibration.
Stochastic vol (Heston) Vol follows its own stochastic process Better dynamics. Mean-reverting vol. Hard to calibrate. Can't fit short-dated skew well. Exotics. Multi-asset. Research.
SABR Stochastic vol, analytical approx. Fast, closed-form. Industry standard for rates/FX. Single-expiry. Wing issues. No jumps. Swaptions, FX vanillas, caps/floors.
Local-stochastic vol (LSV) Combines local + stochastic. Lever function. Best of both: fits surface AND has good dynamics. Complex. Calibration is expensive. Front-office exotic pricing at top banks.
Jump-diffusion (Merton) Adds Poisson jumps to GBM Captures overnight gaps. Hard to hedge jumps. Incomplete market. Equity options around events (earnings, M&A).
Rough vol Vol is driven by fractional Brownian motion (H ≈ 0.1) Matches empirical vol dynamics perfectly. Fast timescale clustering. Computationally expensive. New. Research frontier. Not yet standard in production.

SABR — In Depth (Because You Used It at Natixis)

$$dF = \alpha F^\beta dW_1$$
$$d\alpha = \nu \alpha dW_2$$
$$dW_1 dW_2 = \rho \, dt$$

Parameter What it controls Calibration intuition
α (alpha) ATM vol level Set from ATM market vol
β (beta) Backbone — how vol moves with spot. β=0: normal model. β=1: lognormal. β=0.5: CIR-like. Usually FIXED (not calibrated). Equities: β≈0.5. Rates: β=0 or 0.5.
ρ (rho) Spot-vol correlation → controls SKEW Negative ρ → downside skew (equities: ρ ≈ −0.3 to −0.7)
ν (nu) Vol of vol → controls CURVATURE/WINGS Higher ν → steeper smile/fatter tails

Hagan approximation provides closed-form σ_implied(K) given (α, β, ρ, ν, F, K, T). Makes calibration fast — Newton-Raphson on 3 parameters (α, ρ, ν with β fixed) per expiry slice.

Limitations you should be able to discuss:
- Arbitrage in the wings: for extreme strikes, the Hagan formula can produce negative probability densities. Various fixes exist (Oblój correction, full numerical PDE).
- No term structure consistency: calibrated independently per expiry, so no guarantee that calendar spread arbitrage is excluded.
- No jumps: cannot capture overnight gaps, earnings moves, flash crashes.

Local Volatility (Dupire)

$$\sigma_{\text{local}}^2(K,T) = \frac{\frac{\partial C}{\partial T} + (r-q)K\frac{\partial C}{\partial K} + qC}{\frac{1}{2}K^2 \frac{\partial^2 C}{\partial K^2}}$$

  • Requires a smooth, arbitrage-free call price surface C(K,T) — interpolation quality is critical.
  • Produces a UNIQUE local vol function that reprices all vanillas exactly.
  • The fatal flaw: Local vol predicts that when spot drops 10%, the vol smile SHIFTS to the left but FLATTENS. In reality, when spot drops 10%, the smile shifts left AND steepens. This means local vol underestimates the cost of OTM puts in a crisis — dangerous for risk management.

Heston Stochastic Vol

$$dS = \mu S \, dt + \sqrt{v} S \, dW_S$$
$$dv = \kappa(\theta - v) dt + \xi \sqrt{v} \, dW_v$$
$$dW_S dW_v = \rho \, dt$$

Parameter Meaning Typical equity values
v₀ Current instantaneous variance (ATM IV)²
θ Long-run variance (mean-reversion target) 0.04 (= 20% long-run vol)
κ Mean-reversion speed 1-5 (faster = snaps back quicker)
ξ Vol of vol 0.3-1.0
ρ Spot-vol correlation −0.5 to −0.8 (equities)

Feller condition: $2\kappa\theta > \xi^2$ guarantees variance stays positive. If violated, variance can hit zero — numerically problematic.

Semi-analytic pricing: Heston has a characteristic function solution → Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) pricing → much faster than Monte Carlo for vanillas. Exotics still need MC.

PART 4: VOLATILITY REGIMES — THE RISK MANAGER'S FRAMEWORK

One-liner: Vol doesn't just have a level — it has a REGIME. And regime shifts are the most dangerous events in finance because everything correlated changes simultaneously.

The Four Vol Regimes

Regime VIX range Realized vol Term structure Skew Correlation Market behavior
1. Low vol 10-15 5-12% Steep contango Moderate Low (15-25%) Grinding rally. Carry trades work. Short vol prints money. Complacency building.
2. Normal vol 15-22 12-18% Mild contango Normal Moderate (25-40%) Healthy market. Two-way flow. Risk management is standard.
3. Elevated vol 22-35 18-30% Flat to mild backwardation Steep High (40-60%) Corrections, event clusters. Hedging costs up.
4. Crisis vol 35-80+ 30-100%+ Sharp backwardation Extremely steep Very high (60-80%+) Panic. Liquidity evaporates. Correlations → 1. Models break.

Regime Transitions — What Kills Portfolios

Transition Speed Damage Historical examples
Low → Crisis Hours to days Catastrophic for short vol Feb 2018 (Volmageddon), Mar 2020 (COVID)
Crisis → Elevated Days to weeks Vol sellers rush in too early, get burned by aftershocks Mar→Apr 2020 multiple VIX re-spikes
Elevated → Normal Weeks to months Gradual. Short vol finally profitable again. 2020 H2 normalization
Normal → Low Months to years Slow grind. Short vol becomes more and more crowded. 2016-2017 (VIX spent months at 10-12)

The asymmetry: Vol regimes shift UP in days/hours but shift DOWN over weeks/months. This is the fundamental reason why being short vol has negative skew — you earn slowly and lose fast.

Vol Clustering

Empirical fact: Large moves cluster. A 3σ day is much more likely to be followed by another 3σ day than the unconditional probability suggests. This is called volatility clustering — formalized by GARCH/EWMA models:

$$\sigma^2_{t+1} = \lambda \sigma^2_t + (1-\lambda) r_t^2 \quad \text{(EWMA, } \lambda \approx 0.94 \text{)}$$

For the RM: If you see one 3σ day, don't relax. Expect elevated vol to persist for days to weeks. Reduce gross exposure. Tighten stops. The worst thing you can do after a big down day is assume "it's over."

PART 5: VOLATILITY AS A TRADEABLE ASSET

VIX — The "Fear Index"

One-liner: VIX is the 30-day expected volatility of SPX, derived from option prices. You CAN'T trade it directly — only through derivatives on VIX.

How VIX Is Calculated

VIX uses a model-free approach — it's NOT derived from Black-Scholes. It aggregates prices of OTM SPX options across all strikes:

$$\text{VIX}^2 = \frac{2}{T} \sum_i \frac{\Delta K_i}{K_i^2} e^{rT} Q(K_i) - \frac{1}{T}\left(\frac{F}{K_0} - 1\right)^2$$

Where Q(Kᵢ) is the midpoint price of the OTM option at strike Kᵢ. This is a weighted portfolio of OTM options that replicates expected variance.

Key implication: VIX is sensitive to OTM put prices → tail risk pricing affects VIX directly. This is why VIX can spike even if ATM vol doesn't move much — if panic buying inflates OTM puts, VIX rises.

VIX Levels — Calibrate Your Intuition

15-20 ±0.9-1.3% Long-run median ~18 Normal. Standard risk limits apply.
20-30 ±1.3-1.9% Corrections, policy uncertainty Elevated. Tighten limits. Review hedges.
30-40 ±1.9-2.5% Dec 2018, Aug 2015, Oct 2022 Fear. Active hedge adjustments. Daily IC calls.
40-60 ±2.5-3.8% Feb 2018 peak, Q4 2008, 2011 Severe stress. Consider reducing gross.

The product wasn't just short vol — it was convexly short vol. This is the textbook illustration of why short gamma + auto-rebalancing = existential risk.

Variance Swaps

One-liner: Variance swaps are pure vol bets — linear in VARIANCE (= vol²), which makes them convex in vol. Long variance is a crash bet on steroids.

Mechanics:
- Two legs at expiry: floating leg pays realized variance, fixed leg pays strike variance
- Payoff = Notional × (σ²_realized − K²_var)
- Quote convention: strike in vol terms (e.g., "strike 20 var swap" → K²_var = 400)

Worked example:
- Buy $100K vega notional variance swap struck at 20 vol
- Scenario A: Market realizes 25% vol → Payoff = $100K × (25² − 20²) / (2 × 20) = $100K × 225/40 = +$562,500
- Scenario B: Market realizes 40% vol → Payoff = $100K × (40² − 20²) / (2 × 20) = $100K × 1200/40 = +$3,000,000
- Scenario C: Market realizes 15% vol → Payoff = $100K × (15² − 20²) / (2 × 20) = $100K × −175/40 = −$437,500

Key insight — nonlinearity: Going from 20→25 realized (5pts higher) = +$562K. Going from 20→40 (20pts higher) = +$3M (not 4×$562K = $2.25M, but $3M). The squaring amplifies moves. This is why long variance swaps are MUCH more profitable in true crises than straddles — and why short variance swaps can blow up spectacularly.

Variance vs volatility swap payoff comparison:

Instrument Payoff depends on Big move behavior Short position risk
Straddle ~Linear in vol (approximately) Linear gains/losses Capped-ish (if delta-hedged, losses ∝ vol)
Vol swap Linear in vol Linear P&L Limited (vol can't go below 0)
Variance swap Linear in variance (= vol²) Convex in vol → accelerating gains Unlimited → tail losses are quadratic

Other Vol Products

Product What it is Who uses it
Vol swap Pays realized vol − strike vol (linear in vol, not variance) Cleaner than variance swap. Less tail exposure.
Corridor variance swap Variance accrues only when spot is within a range Targeted vol bets. Cheaper than outright var swap.
Conditional variance swap Variance accrues only on up-days or down-days "Upside variance" vs "downside variance" — isolate skew
VIX options Options on VIX (which are options on expected vol) Tail hedging. Vol of vol trading.
Gamma swaps Like variance swaps but weighted by spot/spot₀ Reduces blowup risk vs var swap — weight goes to zero as spot drops

PART 6: DEALER DYNAMICS & VOL ECOSYSTEM

The Options Market Ecosystem

Understanding WHO buys and sells options, and WHY, is critical for interpreting vol signals.

Participant Typical position Why Vol impact
Pension funds / Insurance Buy OTM puts (systematic hedging programs) Mandate requires downside protection Pushes put IV up → steepens skew
Covered call funds Sell OTM calls monthly (BuyWrite, QYLD) Generate yield for retail investors Pushes call IV down → flattens skew
Hedge funds (vol trading) Both sides — dispersion, RV, cross-asset Alpha generation Two-way flow — can amplify or dampen
Retail (post-2020) Buy OTM calls (meme stocks, 0DTE) Speculation Can push call IV higher than put IV (rare "positive skew")
Corporates Buy puts / collars on FX, commodity exposures Hedging operating risk Consistent demand for OTM puts
Dealers / Market makers Take the other side of ALL of the above. Dynamically delta-hedge. Intermediation Their aggregate gamma determines market impact

Dealer Gamma — The Invisible Hand

Long Gamma (Normal State)

When dealers are net long gamma (the typical state — they've BOUGHT options that clients have sold, or they're net long from hedging put sales):

Market drops → dealer delta becomes more negative → dealer BUYS stock to re-hedge
Market rises → dealer delta becomes more positive → dealer SELLS stock to re-hedge

NET EFFECT: Buy low, sell high. DAMPENS volatility.
Realized vol < implied vol (the VRP in action).
Market feels "sticky" — dips get bought, rallies get faded.

Short Gamma (Danger Zone)

When dealers are net short gamma (typically after selling puts that crossed below strike, or in certain skew environments):

Market drops → dealer delta becomes more positive → dealer SELLS stock to re-hedge
Market rises → dealer delta becomes more negative → dealer BUYS stock to re-hedge

NET EFFECT: Sell low, buy high. AMPLIFIES volatility.
Realized vol > implied vol.
Market feels "slippery" — dips cascade, rallies squeeze.

The Gamma Flip Level (GEX)

The spot price at which aggregate dealer gamma crosses from positive to negative:

Spot position relative to GEX flip Market behavior Realized vol RM action
Well above Suppressed, mean-reverting Low → short vol works Normal monitoring
Near the flip Transitioning, unstable Moderate, increasing Heightened attention
Below the flip Amplified, trending High → long vol works Reduce exposure, add hedges

Data sources: SpotGamma, SqueezeMetrics (GEX/DIX), Goldman/JPM flow desks publish gamma estimates. These aren't precise — but the direction matters.

Vanna & Charm Flows — The Second-Order Dealers

Beyond gamma hedging, dealers also hedge second-order Greeks:

Vanna flows:
- Dealers short OTM puts → short vanna → when vol drops, their delta shifts → they SELL → adds selling pressure
- When vol rises, delta shifts → they BUY → supportive
- Net effect: Vol changes → delta changes → forced hedging flows. These are systematic and predictable. Billions of dollars daily.

Charm flows:
- Dealers' delta decays overnight (charm). Near expiry, this is large.
- If dealers are short near-expiry puts → charm increases their delta → they need to SELL at open → selling pressure
- This creates systematic morning selling around OpEx weeks

0DTE — The New Vol Regime (2022–Present)

Since CBOE launched daily SPX option expiries in 2022, 0DTE options now account for ~50% of SPX option volume.

Feature Impact
Massive gamma at strikes Concentrated at round SPX levels (100s). Creates pin/de-pin intraday.
Intraday vol spikes A large 0DTE trade → dealers hedge → magnifies move → more 0DTE trading. Feedback loop.
Traditional vol metrics less useful VIX measures 30-day vol. 0DTE gamma operates on a 1-day or intraday horizon. VIX can be low while intraday vols are extreme.
Gamma drops to zero at 4pm Options expire → gamma exposure vanishes → next day starts fresh. Unlike monthly options where gamma builds.
The "reset" effect Daily expiry means dealers don't accumulate gamma over weeks. Each day's positioning is quasi-independent.

RM concern: Traditional overnight VaR models don't capture intraday vol spikes from 0DTE gamma. A fund with concentrated SPX options positions near round strikes needs an intraday gamma monitoring framework — not just end-of-day risk reports.

PART 7: VOLATILITY FORECASTING — WHAT THE QUANTS USE

Time-Series Models

Model Key idea Parameters Pros Cons
EWMA σ²ₜ₊₁ = λσ²ₜ + (1−λ)r²ₜ λ ≈ 0.94 (RiskMetrics) Simple, fast, no mean reversion assumed No long-run level. λ choice matters.
GARCH(1,1) σ²ₜ₊₁ = ω + αr²ₜ + βσ²ₜ ω, α, β (α+β < 1) Mean-reverting. Academically standard. Symmetric — up and down moves have same vol impact.
GJR-GARCH Adds asymmetric term for negative returns ω, α, β, γ Captures leverage effect (down moves → more vol). Better for equities. More parameters → overfitting risk.
EGARCH Log variance is modeled (ensures v > 0) Similar to GJR Guaranteed positive variance. Handles asymmetry. Harder to interpret.
HAR-RV RVₜ₊₁ = α + β₁RV_daily + β₂RV_weekly + β₃RV_monthly Three lag coefficients Captures multi-horizon vol persistence. Simple linear regression. Best forecast performance in practice. Requires high-frequency data.

For the interview: "GARCH captures volatility clustering — the tendency for large moves to follow large moves. The key parameters are α (sensitivity to recent shocks), β (persistence of current vol), and their sum (α+β) tells you how long it takes vol to mean-revert. Close to 1 means vol shocks are very persistent."

Practical comparison — which model to cite:
- EWMA: "We use EWMA at λ=0.94 for daily VaR." → Standard, RiskMetrics legacy.
- GARCH(1,1): "Our vol forecasts use GARCH for mean-reverting estimates." → Academic credibility.
- HAR-RV: "For vol forecasting, HAR-RV using multi-horizon realized vol inputs outperforms GARCH out-of-sample." → Shows you know the frontier.

PART 8: CORRELATION — THE OTHER DIMENSION OF VOL

One-liner: Vol tells you how much individual assets move. Correlation tells you whether they move TOGETHER — and in a crisis, they all do.

Implied Correlation

$$\sigma_{\text{index}}^2 = \sum_i w_i^2 \sigma_i^2 + 2\sum_{i<j} w_i w_j \sigma_i \sigma_j \rho_{ij}$$

If you know index vol (from index options) and individual stock vols (from stock options), you can back out the average implied correlation.

CBOE ICJ / implied correlation indices track this.

Correlation Behavior

Market condition Implied correlation What it means
Calm market 20-35% Stocks moving independently. Sector rotation. Stock-picking works.
Correction 40-55% Macro factors dominating. Sector correlations rising.
Crisis 60-80%+ "All correlations go to 1." Systematic risk only. No diversification. Everything dumps together.

The diversification illusion: A portfolio of 30 uncorrelated stocks at 25% individual vol has portfolio vol ≈ 4.6% (1/√30 effect). At 80% correlation (crisis), the SAME portfolio has vol ≈ 22%. Diversification "vanishes" when you need it most.

Dispersion Trading

The most important vol trade at funds like Squarepoint:

The trade: Sell index vol (sell SPX straddles or var swaps) + buy single-stock vol (buy straddles/var swaps on constituents).

Why it works:
- Index vol ≈ avg stock vol × √correlation
- If realized correlation < implied correlation → index vol is "too expensive" relative to single-stock vol
- You profit from the correlation risk premium — the market systematically overprices correlation

P&L decomposition:
- Profit when stocks move INDEPENDENTLY (low realized correlation → index moves less than sum of parts)
- Lose when everything correlates (crisis → index and stocks move together)

Risk: Short correlation. A correlation spike (crisis) destroys the trade. This is a premium-collection strategy with left-tail correlation risk.

PART 10: INTERVIEW APPLICATION

"Explain implied vol versus realized vol and why the gap matters":

"Realized vol is the standard deviation of actual returns — it tells you what happened. Implied vol is backed out of option prices — it tells you what the market expects, PLUS an insurance premium.

Implied is almost always higher than realized. That gap is the volatility risk premium — it's ~2-4 points for SPX on average. It explains why selling options is profitable on average: you're collecting insurance premium. But the premium exists for a reason — when realized vol spikes above implied (Feb 2018, Mar 2020), the losses are catastrophic and nonlinear, especially for variance swaps where payoffs are quadratic in vol.

For risk management, I monitor three things: the IV/RV ratio (options cheap or expensive?), the slope of the vol term structure (is near-term fear elevated?), and skew (is the market pricing tail risk?). Together they tell me whether the portfolio's vol exposure is well-positioned for the current regime."

"Walk me through the vol surface — what is it and why does it matter?":

"The vol surface maps implied volatility across two dimensions: strike and expiry. Along the strike axis you get skew — for equities, OTM puts have higher IV than ATM, which creates a characteristic smirk. This reflects three things: demand for downside protection, fat-tailed returns, and the leverage effect where lower prices mean higher leverage and therefore higher vol.

Along the time axis you get the term structure — normally upward-sloping (more uncertainty over longer horizons) but it inverts when there's near-term event risk.

Why it matters operationally: the surface determines all option prices, which means it determines all Greeks. A PM who sells an OTM put at 25% IV in a 20% ATM vol environment isn't selling at '5 points of edge' — the skew IS the fair price for that strike. Understanding the surface is understanding whether a trade has genuine edge or is just harvesting a risk premium."

"How does dealer gamma affect markets?":

"Options dealers take the other side of client flow and delta-hedge dynamically. When they're long gamma — the normal state — they buy dips and sell rallies because their delta moves against the market. This SUPPRESSES realized vol — it's why realized vol is often below implied.

When they're short gamma — typically after a selloff pushes spot through large put strikes — the dynamics flip. They SELL into selloffs and BUY into rallies, amplifying moves. This is the mechanism behind cascading sell-offs.

As a risk manager, I'd monitor estimated dealer gamma positioning. Below the gamma flip level, the market loses its natural shock absorber. Realized vol will likely exceed implied vol, meaning any short-vol positions in the book face compounding losses. That's when I'd flag for hedge adjustments."

"Explain the VIX and how you'd use it in risk management":

"VIX is the 30-day expected SPX volatility derived from OTM option prices. It's model-free — not Black-Scholes-derived. A VIX of 20 implies roughly 1.26% daily SPX moves.

I use VIX structurally, not reactively. First, the level: below 15 means protection is cheap and I'd want to ensure hedges are in place before complacency ends. Above 30 means we're in a stress regime and I'd tighten all limits. Second, the term structure: if VIX futures are in backwardation, the market is saying 'this crisis is now' — and that's when near-dated hedging is most expensive so I'd hedge in the back of the curve. Third, VIX relative to VVIX: high VVIX means the market doesn't even know what vol will be — maximum uncertainty."
