G — PORTFOLIO RISK: THE ARCHITECTURE OF FUND SURVIVAL

Correlation dynamics, diversification math, factor models, concentration, basis risk, crowding, tail dependence, liquidity spirals, portfolio construction, and the RM's aggregation framework

Depth: 🔴 DEEP

WHY THIS IS THE SPINE OF YOUR JOB

At Squarepoint, individual position risk is table stakes. Every PM manages their own book. Your job as Junior Risk Manager is the PORTFOLIO level — aggregating across desks, detecting hidden commonalities, and ensuring the whole fund survives scenarios no single PM is thinking about. This file covers the intellectual framework for doing that.

The core reality: risk is not additive. A fund with four $3M-VaR desks doesn't have $12M of total VaR. It could be $6M (diversification) or $11M (hidden correlation). Understanding WHY — and monitoring the transition between those states — is the entire job.

PART 1: CORRELATION — THE MOST DANGEROUS NUMBER IN FINANCE

One-liner: Correlation is a chameleon — it looks stable in calm markets and shape-shifts violently the moment you need it to stay put.

The Math

$$\rho_{X,Y} = \frac{\text{Cov}(X,Y)}{\sigma_X \cdot \sigma_Y} = \frac{E[(X - \mu_X)(Y - \mu_Y)]}{\sigma_X \cdot \sigma_Y}$$

Ranges from −1 (perfect inverse) to +1 (perfect co-movement). Zero = no LINEAR relationship (but non-linear dependence can still exist — see copulas).

Why Correlation Is Treacherous

Five properties that make correlation dangerous:

Property What it means Consequence
Regime-dependent ρ changes with market state (risk-on/off, vol level, macro regime) The ρ you estimated from 2017-2019 is useless in March 2020
Asymmetric ρ is typically HIGHER in sell-offs than rallies Diversification vanishes when you need it most
Non-stationary Even within a regime, ρ drifts over time as fundamentals change Last year's correlation matrix is already stale
Only captures linear dependence ρ=0 does NOT mean independent. Assets can have strong tail dependence with zero linear correlation Copula methods needed for tail co-movement
Estimation error For N assets: N(N−1)/2 pairwise correlations. 500 assets → 124,750 parameters → massive estimation noise Covariance matrix is unreliable without shrinkage or factor structure

Unconditional vs Conditional Correlation

Unconditional (average): ρ computed over the whole sample. This is what most risk models use. It's an average of calm and crisis, which means it's wrong in BOTH states.

Conditional (stress-specific): ρ computed only when the market is under stress (e.g., SPX down >2%). This is what actually drives tail P&L.

Pair Unconditional ρ Conditional ρ (stress) What flipped
SPX vs single stocks 0.30 0.70+ Idiosyncratic washes out; beta dominates
SPX vs HY credit 0.50 0.85+ Both driven by risk appetite
SPX vs UST 10Y −0.30 −0.50 (usually) BUT +0.40 in 2022 Inflation regime flips the sign
SPX vs gold −0.10 −0.30 (usually) BUT +0.20 if real rates dominate Real rate sensitivity overrides safe-haven
Cross-PM portfolios 0.10-0.20 0.50-0.80 Hidden common macro factors emerge
Momentum vs value −0.60 −0.90 (reversal) or +0.40 (both crash) Depends on whether it's rotation or deleveraging

The 2022 case study: Stocks-bonds correlation flipped from −0.30 to +0.40. Every "60/40" portfolio, every equity-bonds hedge, every duration overlay ASSUMED negative correlation. When it flipped positive, hedges became additional losses. This regime change affected trillions in AUM globally. The RM who saw it coming was invaluable; the one who didn't was running a model that made things WORSE.

Correlation Models the Quant Desk Uses

Model How it works Strengths Weaknesses
Rolling window ρ = last N-day sample correlation Simple, intuitive Jumpy, equal-weights old data, ghost effects
EWMA Exponentially weight recent observations more heavily (λ=0.94 RiskMetrics) Responds to regime shifts faster Still backward-looking, one decay parameter for everything
DCC-GARCH (Dynamic Conditional Correlation) Two-stage: GARCH each series individually, then model conditional correlation dynamics Captures time-varying ρ with momentum and mean-reversion Computationally heavy for 500+ assets
Factor model implied ρ_ij = β_i·β_j·σ²_market / (σ_i·σ_j) + residual Shrinks estimation error. Structured. Misses idiosyncratic co-movement
Copula Models JOINT distribution separately from marginals Captures tail dependence even when linear ρ=0 Hard to calibrate, model-selection risk

What Squarepoint likely uses: Factor-model-implied correlation matrix (Barra or internal PCA factors) with DCC overlays for liquid assets and stressed-correlation overrides for tail scenarios. The single-number ρ from a spreadsheet is not what drives the risk system.

Eigenvalue Decomposition of the Correlation Matrix

A powerful diagnostic from linear algebra. Decompose the N×N correlation matrix into eigenvalues and eigenvectors:

$$\mathbf{C} = \mathbf{Q} \boldsymbol{\Lambda} \mathbf{Q}^T$$

What the eigenvalues tell you:

Eigenvalue pattern Diagnosis Action
One dominant eigenvalue (explains >60% of variance) Portfolio dominated by a single factor (usually market beta) PM is running a directional bet, not a diversified book
Top 3 eigenvalues explain >85% Portfolio has 3 principal risk drivers Identify them — likely market, sector, style
Many similar eigenvalues Genuinely diversified — many independent bets Healthy. Diversification is REAL.
Smallest eigenvalue near zero Near-singular matrix — multicollinearity Risk system outputs can be numerically unstable. Requires regularization.

Worked example — 4 desks:
- Eigenvalue decomposition of desk-level P&L correlation matrix: λ = [2.8, 0.6, 0.4, 0.2]
- First eigenvalue explains 2.8/4 = 70% of total variance
- Finding: all four desks are driven by the same factor (turns out: implicit SPX beta)
- The fund THINKS it has 4 independent desks. It actually has 1.3 effective bets.

What the RM Needs to Do About Correlation

  1. Never trust the trailing ρ at face value. Ask: "Which regime produced this estimate? Would it hold in stress?"
  2. Run stressed-correlation scenarios. Re-compute VaR with ρ = 0.7 and ρ = 0.9 for all risk-on pairs. How much does VaR increase?
  3. Monitor stocks-bonds ρ daily. In an inflation regime, positive ρ changes everything about hedging.
  4. Aggregate across PMs. The most dangerous risk is when the CRO sees four "independent" desks but eigenvalue decomposition shows one factor.
  5. Use implied correlation from options markets as a forward-looking estimate alongside backward-looking statistical estimates.

PART 2: DIVERSIFICATION — THE MATH AND ITS LIMITS

One-liner: Diversification is the only free lunch in finance — until the restaurant closes during a crisis and you discover everyone was eating at the same table.

The Diversification Formula

For a portfolio of N assets with equal weights:

$$\sigma_p = \sigma \sqrt{\frac{1}{N} + \frac{N-1}{N} \bar{\rho}}$$

Where σ = average individual asset vol, $\bar{\rho}$ = average pairwise correlation.

Key insight from this formula:
- As $N \to \infty$: $\sigma_p \to \sigma \sqrt{\bar{\rho}}$
- You can NEVER diversify below $\sigma \sqrt{\bar{\rho}}$ — this is the systematic risk floor
- If $\bar{\rho} = 0.30$ and σ = 25%: floor = 25% × √0.30 = 13.7% — no matter how many stocks you hold

Diversification benefit table (assuming σ = 25%, equal weight):

N positions ρ = 0.0 ρ = 0.20 ρ = 0.40 ρ = 0.60 ρ = 0.80
1 25.0% 25.0% 25.0% 25.0% 25.0%
5 11.2% 13.4% 16.0% 18.7% 21.5%
10 7.9% 12.1% 15.0% 18.2% 21.1%
30 4.6% 11.5% 14.4% 17.8% 20.8%
100 2.5% 11.2% 14.1% 17.7% 20.7%
∞ 0% 11.2% 15.8% 19.4% 22.4%

Reading the table: At ρ=0.40 (realistic for equities in the same market), going from 10 to 100 stocks only reduces vol from 15.0% to 14.1% — diminishing returns are extreme. Most diversification benefit is captured by 15-20 uncorrelated positions. Beyond that, you need to reduce CORRELATION, not add positions.

The Diversification Ratio

$$\text{DR} = \frac{\sum_i w_i \sigma_i}{\sigma_p} = \frac{\text{Weighted average vol}}{\text{Portfolio vol}}$$

DR Interpretation
1.0 No diversification (all assets perfectly correlated or single asset)
1.2-1.5 Moderate diversification
1.5-2.0 Good diversification
2.0+ Strong diversification — portfolio vol much less than weighted-average vol

RM use: Track DR daily. If it DROPS (toward 1.0), correlations are rising and diversification is evaporating — typically in stress. DR dropping from 1.8 to 1.3 is an early warning.

Effective Number of Bets (ENB)

$$\text{ENB} = \frac{(\text{DR})^2}{1} = \left(\frac{\sum w_i \sigma_i}{\sigma_p}\right)^2$$

Alternative (from PCA): $\text{ENB} = \exp\left(-\sum_i p_i \ln p_i\right)$ where $p_i = \lambda_i / \sum \lambda_j$ (Shannon entropy of eigenvalues).

A fund with 50 positions but ENB = 3 has three effective bets. This number should be tracked and compared to what the PM claims.

When Diversification Fails

Mechanism: In tail events, correlations spike → diversification benefit collapses → portfolio vol jumps → margin calls → forced selling → more correlation → feedback loop.

Crisis Pre-crisis ρ_avg Crisis ρ_avg Diversification loss
LTCM 1998 0.25 0.70 ~60% of diversification benefit gone
GFC 2008 0.30 0.80 ~70% gone
COVID 2020 0.25 0.75 ~65% gone (recovered within weeks)
2022 inflation stocks-bonds −0.30 stocks-bonds +0.40 Sign reversal — hedge became loss

RM implication: Your VaR model uses calm-period correlations showing portfolio vol of 10%. In a crisis, actual correlations spike and real portfolio vol is 18%. Your VaR was understated by 80%. This is precisely the scenario you must stress-test for.

PART 3: FACTOR MODELS — X-RAYING THE PORTFOLIO

One-liner: Factor models look through the 500 individual positions and tell you the 8 real risks driving the fund — because 500 positions with the same factor exposure is not diversification, it's a leveraged bet on that factor.

What Factor Models Do

A factor model decomposes every asset's return into systematic (explainable) and idiosyncratic (residual) components:

$$r_i = \alpha_i + \sum_{k=1}^K \beta_{ik} f_k + \epsilon_i$$

Where $f_k$ = factor return, $\beta_{ik}$ = asset i's loading on factor k, $\epsilon_i$ = idiosyncratic return.

Portfolio-level: The portfolio's risk is then:

$$\sigma_p^2 = \mathbf{b}p^T \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_f \mathbf{b}_p + \sum_i w_i^2 \sigma{\epsilon_i}^2$$

Where the first term = systematic (factor) risk and the second = specific (idiosyncratic) risk.

In a well-diversified portfolio: Specific risk → 0 as N increases. Factor risk dominates. This means understanding factor exposures IS understanding portfolio risk.

The Three Factor Model Families

1. Fundamental Factor Models (Barra / Axioma)

Pre-defined factors based on company characteristics.

Barra equity model example (70+ factors):

Category Factors What they capture
Country US, UK, JP, DE, CN, ... Geographic return drivers
Industry Energy, Banks, Tech, Healthcare, ... (30+) Sector-specific returns
Style Size, Value, Momentum, Volatility, Growth, Quality, Leverage, Yield, Liquidity Cross-sectional attributes
Currency USD, EUR, GBP, JPY, ... FX exposure within equity returns

How Barra works: Each stock gets factor loadings updated monthly (e.g., AAPL: Size = −1.2 [large], Momentum = +0.8, Value = −0.5 [growth]). The model estimates factor returns daily. Portfolio risk = f(aggregate factor exposures × factor covariance matrix + specific risk).

Squarepoint context: Almost certainly runs a multi-factor risk model (Barra, Axioma, or proprietary PCA-based). Daily reports show: "Your top risk contributors are: Market factor (40% of variance), Momentum (25%), Tech sector (15%), Specific (20%)."

2. Macroeconomic Factor Models

Factors are observable macro variables.

Factor Proxy What it captures
Equity market SPX return Broad market risk premium
Interest rates ΔUS10Y Rate sensitivity
Credit ΔIG spread Credit cycle
Inflation ΔTIPS breakeven Inflation expectations
Commodity ΔBCOM / ΔWTI Real asset sensitivity
Currency ΔDXY Dollar strength
Volatility ΔVIX Risk appetite

Use case: For a discretionary macro fund, decompose: "How much of our P&L came from our rate view vs our equity view vs our commodity view?"

3. Statistical Factor Models (PCA)

No economic interpretation — purely statistical. Extract principal components from the return covariance matrix.

$$\text{PC}_1 = \text{direction of maximum variance}$$
$$\text{PC}_2 = \text{direction of maximum remaining variance, orthogonal to PC}_1$$

Typical equity PCA results:
- PC1 ≈ market factor (explains 40-60% of variance)
- PC2 ≈ growth vs value rotation (~10-15%)
- PC3 ≈ small vs large (~5-8%)
- PC4+ ≈ sector rotations, less interpretable

Strengths over fundamental: No assumptions about what the factors ARE. Purely data-driven. Captures hidden factors the fundamental model misses.

Weaknesses: Hard to interpret ("PC3 is a long banks/short utilities factor… why?"). Unstable — the PCs rotate over time. Factor loadings change every recalibration.

Factor Risk Decomposition — The Power Tool

Worked example — Fund with 3 desks:

Factor Desk A exposure Desk B exposure Desk C exposure Fund aggregate
Market β +0.30 +0.15 −0.10 +0.35
Momentum +0.50 +0.30 +0.20 +1.00 ← DANGER
Value −0.20 +0.10 +0.05 −0.05
Size (small) +0.10 +0.30 +0.10 +0.50
Rates (DV01) $0K −$5K +$3K −$2K

Diagnosis: Each desk looks individually reasonable. But aggregate momentum exposure = +1.00 — that's a MASSIVE hidden concentration. If momentum reverses (as it did violently in Q4 2018 or Sep 2020), all three desks lose simultaneously. No single PM sees this. Only the RM aggregating across desks can detect it.

Factor risk contribution:

$$\text{Factor risk}k = \frac{\beta{p,k}^2 \sigma_k^2}{\sigma_p^2} \times 100\%$$

Factor Contribution to portfolio variance
Momentum 52% ← majority of risk is ONE factor
Market 22%
Size 12%
Rates 8%
Specific 6%

Action: Momentum is >50% of risk. Either (a) the CIO consciously wants this as the house view, or (b) it's unintentional and needs to be reduced. The RM's job is to surface this and have the conversation.

Alpha vs Factor Return Decomposition

$$R_p = \underbrace{\alpha}{\text{skill}} + \underbrace{\sum_k \beta_k f_k}{\text{factor exposure}} + \underbrace{\epsilon}_{\text{residual}}$$

Component What the RM checks
α > 0 (significant) PM is generating genuine alpha. Justify the fees.
α ≈ 0, R² high PM's return is ALL factor — no skill, just exposure. "You're charging 2-and-20 for levered beta."
α < 0, R² high PM is DESTROYING value relative to factor exposure. Worse than an ETF.
R² low Model doesn't explain the returns. Need better factors or PM is truly idiosyncratic.

PART 4: CONCENTRATION RISK — THE SILENT KILLER

One-liner: A portfolio with 100 names can be more concentrated than a portfolio with 10 — if those 100 names are all loading on the same factor, you don't own 100 bets, you own one.

Types of Concentration

1. Single-Name Concentration

Metric — Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI):

$$\text{HHI} = \sum_{i=1}^N w_i^2$$

Where $w_i$ = weight of position i.

HHI Effective N (= 1/HHI) Concentration level
0.01 100 Very diversified
0.05 20 Moderate
0.10 10 Concentrated
0.20 5 Highly concentrated
0.50+ 2 or fewer Single-bet portfolio

Worked example:
- Portfolio: 5 positions at weights 40%, 25%, 15%, 12%, 8%
- HHI = 0.16 + 0.0625 + 0.0225 + 0.0144 + 0.0064 = 0.266
- Effective N = 1/0.266 = 3.8
- Despite "5 positions," the effective diversification = ~4 independent bets (because the 40% position dominates)

Limit framework:
| Measure | Typical limit (discretionary HF) | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Largest single position | <5% of NAV | Single-stock gap risk |
| Top 10 positions | <40% of NAV | Concentration in top holdings |
| Effective N (1/HHI) | >10 | Minimum diversification threshold |

2. Sector Concentration

Even 50 stocks can be ONE bet if they're all in the same sector.

Example: A PM holds 20 semiconductor names across the US, Taiwan, and Korea. They claim geographic diversification. But semis are globally correlated (same cycle, same end-demand). When the CHIPS Act or AI capex cycle reverses, ALL 20 positions move together. The PM had sector concentration disguised as geographic diversification.

3. Factor Concentration (The Hidden Kind)

This is what kills multi-PM funds. Each PM picks stocks independently but they all end up loading on the same systematic factors.

How it hides:
- PM A is long tech (growth exposure)
- PM B is long banks and short utilities (rate-sensitivity exposure)
- PM C is long NVIDIA and short defensive names (AI momentum exposure)
- All three are implicitly: long growth, long risk-on, short safety → ONE factor bet across three "independent" desks

Detection: Factor regression on aggregate portfolio, eigenvalue decomposition of cross-PM correlation matrix, track effective number of bets.

4. Counterparty Concentration

"We trade with 3 prime brokers. Our biggest PB holds 60% of our margin balance." If that PB pulls lines (as Bear Stearns and Lehman clients learned), 60% of the fund's positions face forced liquidation.

Limits: No single PB > 40% of margin. Diversify clearing across at least 3 CCPs.

5. Crowding — Concentration ACROSS the Industry

Your fund is diversified. But every other hedge fund holds the same positions. In aggregate, the trade is crowded. When one fund de-leverages, everyone must follow → amplified crash.

Detection tools:
- 13F overlap: Count how many large funds hold each position
- Short interest: Positions with >25% SI are crowded shorts → short-squeeze risk (GameStop Jan 2021)
- Factor-crowding score: Measure aggregate HF positioning in momentum, value, etc.
- Hedge Fund Research (HFR) crowding indices

Historical crowding blow-ups:

Event Date What was crowded Loss
Quant quake Aug 2007 Statistical arbitrage positions (momentum, mean-reversion) Multi-billion across quant funds
Short vol Feb 2018 VIX ETPs (XIV et al.), short variance swaps XIV lost 96% in one day
Momentum reversal Sep 2020 Tech/growth vs value rotation 10-20% drawdowns for momentum funds
Japan carry unwind Aug 2024 Short JPY / long risk assets Nikkei −12%, multi-strat funds hit

PART 5: BASIS RISK — THE GAP BETWEEN YOUR HEDGE AND YOUR POSITION

One-liner: Basis risk is the risk that your insurance policy pays out on a DIFFERENT house than the one that burned down.

The Definition

Basis = price of the hedged asset − price of the hedging instrument.

If basis is stable, the hedge works. If basis moves, you're exposed — you can lose on BOTH the position AND the hedge simultaneously.

Common Basis Risks in Practice

Position Hedge Basis = What can go wrong
Long XOM stock Short XLE ETF XOM − (β × XLE) XOM-specific news (earnings, M&A) creates idiosyncratic move ETF doesn't cover
Long Brent crude Short WTI futures Brent − WTI spread Spread blew to $25 in 2011 (Libya). Normally $2-3.
Long IG corp bond Short Treasury futures Credit spread Rate hedge works; credit spread widens → still lose money
Long single-stock Short index put Stock − index Stock drops 20% on fraud; index drops 2%. Put covers 2 of 20.
Long physical commodity Short front-month future Spot − future (calendar basis) COVID: oil futures went NEGATIVE while physical was still $20+
Long EM equity Short EM FX Equity × FX cross-rate FX hedge tenor ≠ equity holding period

Measuring Basis Risk

Hedge effectiveness (R²):

$$R^2 = 1 - \frac{\text{Var}(\text{hedged portfolio})}{\text{Var}(\text{unhedged portfolio})}$$

R² Interpretation
>0.95 Excellent hedge — basis is tight
0.80-0.95 Decent but meaningful basis risk remains
0.50-0.80 Hedge is directional only — basis will cause P&L
<0.50 Not really a hedge — you're trading TWO positions, not hedging one

Optimal hedge ratio:

$$h^* = \rho \times \frac{\sigma_{\text{position}}}{\sigma_{\text{hedge}}}$$

If the position and hedge aren't perfectly correlated or don't have the same volatility, the hedge ratio ≠ 1.0. A naive 1:1 hedge leaves basis risk.

Why Basis Risk Can Be Worse Than No Hedge

Scenario: PM is long $50M of corporate bonds, hedges with $50M short in Treasury futures.
- Rates rise 50bp: Treasury hedge gains $1.6M ✓
- BUT credit spreads also widen 80bp: corporate bond loses ADDITIONAL $2.0M from credit
- Net: hedge gained $1.6M, position lost $1.6M (rate) + $2.0M (credit) = net loss $2.0M
- PM thought they were hedged. They were hedged against RATES but not CREDIT. The hedge created false confidence.

RM lesson: Always ask: "What is this hedge ACTUALLY hedging? What risks remain?"

PART 6: TAIL RISK & FAT TAILS — PREPARING FOR "IMPOSSIBLE" EVENTS

One-liner: Normal distributions say a 5-sigma daily move happens once every 14,000 years. Markets produce them every few years. Any risk framework built on normality is a framework for losing money in the tail.

Fat Tails — The Numbers

Sigma event Normal: frequency Market reality Underestimation factor
3σ (e.g., SPX −4.5%) 1 in 370 days 1 in 50-70 days ~6×
4σ (e.g., SPX −6%) 1 in 31,560 days (126 years) 1 in 500-700 days (2-3 years) ~50×
5σ (e.g., SPX −7.5%) 1 in 3.5 million days 1 in 5,000-8,000 days (20-30 years) ~500×
6σ+ (e.g., SPX −10%+) "Never" Oct 1987, Oct 2008, Mar 2020 ∞

Excess kurtosis = Kurtosis − 3 (above the normal benchmark):
- Normal distribution: 0
- SPX daily returns: ~20-25 (massively fat-tailed)
- Individual stocks: 5-50+
- Credit spreads: 30+

Extreme Value Theory (EVT)

Rather than modeling the ENTIRE distribution, EVT focuses specifically on the tail.

Generalized Pareto Distribution (GPD): For losses exceeding a high threshold u:

$$P(\text{Loss} > u + y \mid \text{Loss} > u) = \left(1 + \frac{\xi y}{\beta}\right)^{-1/\xi}$$

Where ξ (xi) = shape parameter (tail index):
| ξ value | Tail type | Distribution family |
|---|---|---|
| ξ = 0 | Exponential (thin) tail | Normal-like |
| ξ > 0 | Power-law (fat) tail — extreme events more likely | Pareto, Student-t. THIS IS FINANCE. |
| ξ < 0 | Bounded tail | Physical limits (temperatures, etc.) |

For equities, ξ ≈ 0.2-0.4 → moderately fat tails. For credit, ξ can be >0.5 → very fat tails.

Why EVT matters for RM: Standard VaR at 99% uses the whole distribution. But the 99.9th or 99.99th percentile (the "fund-killing" event) requires EVT because you're extrapolating into the tail where you have almost no data.

Tail Dependence — When Everything Crashes Together

Linear correlation misses tail dependence — the tendency of extreme moves to occur simultaneously.

Copula language:
- Upper tail dependence (λ_U): Probability of joint extreme GAIN
- Lower tail dependence (λ_L): Probability of joint extreme LOSS

Copula family λ_L (lower tail) λ_U (upper tail) Financial relevance
Gaussian 0 0 No tail dependence → UNDERESTIMATES joint crashes
Student-t >0 >0 Symmetric tail dependence → better for crashes
Clayton >0 0 Lower-tail only → models crashes without upside linkage
Gumbel 0 >0 Upper-tail only → less relevant for risk

RM implication: If your risk model uses a Gaussian copula (or equivalently, normal joint distribution), it SYSTEMATICALLY underrates the probability of multiple assets crashing simultaneously. This was a key failure in 2008 CDO pricing → Gaussian copula said joint default probability was negligible → it wasn't.

Tail Hedging — The Cost-Benefit Framework

Instrument Mechanism Annual cost (approx) Crisis payoff Rebalance
OTM index puts (5% OTM, 3M) Direct floor on portfolio 1.5-3.0% of NAV 5-20× payout in −20% crash Roll quarterly
Put spreads (buy 5% OTM, sell 15% OTM) Cheaper floor, capped payout 0.5-1.5% 2-5× payout, capped Roll quarterly
VIX calls (strike 30-40) Vol convexity 1.0-2.5% 3-10× in vol spikes Roll monthly
Variance swap (buy var) Pure vol exposure, convex payout Carry cost in low-vol Massive payout if rv >> iv Maturity-based
Dynamic delta hedging Increase short delta as market drops (portfolio insurance) Execution costs + whipsaw Scales with move Continuous
Cash buffer Hold 5-15% cash for margin calls Opportunity cost Prevents forced liquidation Continuous

The RM's tail-hedge conversation:
- "The tail hedge costs $3M/year in premium decay."
- "The scenario it protects against: SPX −25%, all desks hit simultaneously, forced liquidation → $80M loss."
- "Is $3M/year good insurance against an $80M loss with, say, 3-5% annual probability?"
- Expected tail loss = $80M × 4% = $3.2M → break-even. The hedge is WORTH it on expected value alone, before considering survival value.

PART 7: LIQUIDITY RISK — THE RISK OF NOT BEING ABLE TO EXIT

One-liner: A position isn't risky because it can lose value — every position can lose value. It's risky because you might not be able to EXIT when you desperately need to.

Two Dimensions of Liquidity Risk

Market Liquidity Risk (Can you SELL?)

Market impact model (Almgren-Chriss framework):

$$\text{Cost} = \underbrace{\frac{1}{2} s}{\text{spread}} + \underbrace{\eta \cdot v}{\text{temporary impact}} + \underbrace{\gamma \cdot \frac{Q}{\text{ADV}}}_{\text{permanent impact}}$$

Where s = bid-ask spread, η = temporary impact coefficient, v = execution speed, γ = permanent impact, Q = total shares.

Key insight: Market impact is NON-LINEAR. Trading 1% of ADV costs X. Trading 5% of ADV doesn't cost 5X — it costs 10-15X because you're consuming liquidity depth levels.

ADV concentration:

Position / ADV Classification RM action
<1% Liquid No concern
1-5% Manageable Monitor
5-10% Illiquid Require liquidity plan
10-25% You ARE the market Hard limit — must reduce or justify
>25% Trapped Forced to accept fire-sale prices or side-pocket

Funding Liquidity Risk (Can you meet CASH obligations?)

Obligation Urgency What drives it
Margin calls (daily) Immediate (T+1) P&L losses, vol spike, correlation spike
Redemptions Monthly/quarterly (with notice) Investor sentiment, relative performance
Collateral calls (CSA) Daily Mark-to-market on OTC derivatives
Margin requirement increases Same-day to T+1 Exchange or PB increases margin rates

The liquidity spiral (Brunnermeier & Pedersen):

Losses → Margin calls → Forced selling → Prices drop further →
More margin calls → More selling → Prices drop more → ...
SPIRAL: Funding liquidity ↔ Market liquidity FEEDBACK LOOP

This is the mechanism that kills leveraged funds. It's why LTCM failed (1998), why Bear Stearns' hedge funds failed (2007), and why every multi-strategy fund has a kill switch.

Liquidity Tiering — The RM's Classification

Tier Characteristics DTL (days to liquidate at 10% ADV) VaR treatment
1 Listed, ADV >$100M, spread <5bp <1 day Standard 1-day VaR
2 Listed, ADV $10-100M, spread 5-20bp 1-5 days LVaR = VaR × √DTL
3 listed/OTC, ADV $1-10M, spread 20-100bp 5-20 days LVaR + stress spread + concentration haircut
4 OTC, bespoke, ADV <$1M or no market 20+ days Full notional at risk in stress. May need side-pocket.

Redemption wave Need to liquidate 10-20% of AUM in a week. But most liquid positions are sold first → remaining portfolio becomes MORE illiquid. Pre-plan the liquidation waterfall: sell Tier 1 first, Tier 2 next. Never sell Tier 4 in stress.
PB credit pullback Prime broker reduces line or increases haircuts. Margin utilization jumps. Maintain relationships with 3+ PBs. Keep 15%+ margin headroom.
Correlated forced selling Every fund is selling the same crowded positions simultaneously → market impact explodes Crowding analysis: if you hold the same names as 50 other hedge funds, your "liquid" positions become illiquid in a crowded unwind

PART 8: PORTFOLIO CONSTRUCTION FOR RISK

One-liner: A PM constructs portfolios for return. The RM constructs CONSTRAINTS for survival. The intersection — portfolios that maximize return subject to risk constraints — is where the real edge lives.

Position Sizing Frameworks

1. Equal Risk Contribution (Risk Parity)

$$w_i \cdot \text{MCR}_i = \frac{\text{Total Risk}}{N} \quad \forall i$$

Every position contributes the same amount of risk. High-vol positions get smaller weights; low-vol positions get larger.

Worked example:
- 3 assets: Equities (σ=15%), Bonds (σ=5%), Commodities (σ=20%)
- Equal-weight (33/33/33): Equities contribute 55% of risk, commodities 35%, bonds 10%
- Risk parity weights (approx): Equities 20%, Bonds 60%, Commodities 15%
- Now each contributes ~33% of total risk

Pro: Truly diversified at the RISK level, not the dollar level. Con: Requires leverage (lots of bonds to match equity vol). Low return per unit of capital without leverage.

2. Kelly Criterion — Sizing by Edge

$$f^* = \frac{p(b+1) - 1}{b} = \frac{\text{Edge}}{\text{Odds}}$$

Or for continuous returns: $f^* = \frac{\mu - r_f}{\sigma^2}$

Where $\mu$ = expected return, $r_f$ = risk-free rate, $\sigma^2$ = variance.

Practical Kelly:
- Full Kelly is too aggressive (assumes perfect parameter estimates, no tail risk)
- Most practitioners use Half-Kelly or Quarter-Kelly
- Over-betting (above full Kelly) is WORSE than under-betting — expected growth rate DECREASES

Fraction of Kelly Risk profile Use case
Full Kelly Maximizes long-run geometric growth — extremely volatile Theoretical benchmark only
Half Kelly 75% of growth rate, ~50% of drawdown Aggressive but practical
Quarter Kelly 50% of growth rate, ~25% of drawdown Conservative — most institutional

3. Volatility Targeting

Set a target portfolio volatility (e.g., 10%) and scale position sizes to achieve it:

$$\text{Leverage} = \frac{\sigma_{\text{target}}}{\sigma_{\text{realized}}}$$

When realized vol is low → lever UP. When realized vol spikes → lever DOWN. This automatically de-risks in crises.

RM consideration: Vol targeting creates PROCYCLICAL behavior in the short term (selling into drops, buying into rallies) but STRUCTURAL risk management (lower leverage in high-vol regimes where tail events are more likely).

PART 10: INTERVIEW APPLICATION

"How would you think about portfolio risk beyond VaR?"

"VaR gives me one number — the 99th percentile loss under normal conditions — but it's the START of risk analysis, not the end. I'd layer on five additional views.

First, Expected Shortfall — what's the average loss in that 1% worst case? And critically, the ES/VaR ratio: if it's above 3, there's a concave payoff hiding in the book — likely short gamma or short tail risk — and VaR is dangerously misleading.

Second, factor decomposition — what's actually DRIVING the risk? Two portfolios can have the same VaR but very different factor profiles. One might be diversified across 10 uncorrelated bets. The other might be one momentum bet disguised as 30 positions. The effective number of bets is the real diversification metric.

Third, stress testing — how does the portfolio perform under 2008, COVID, or a hypothetical Taiwan scenario? This tests what VaR misses: regime changes, correlation spikes, non-linear payoffs.

Fourth, liquidity analysis — can we actually EXIT these positions if we need to? A portfolio that takes 3 weeks to liquidate but faces daily margin calls has a fundamental mismatch.

Fifth, and most critically at a multi-PM fund: cross-desk aggregation. Each PM's risk might look fine individually, but if all four desks are loading on the same macro factor, eigenvalue decomposition of the cross-desk correlation matrix will show it. That's the hidden concentration that kills multi-strategy funds."

"What's the most underappreciated risk in a multi-PM fund?"

"Hidden factor correlation across PMs. Each PM manages their own book and genuinely believes their risk is independent. But when I run factor attribution across ALL desks simultaneously, I often find they're loading on the same two or three macro factors — rates, momentum, risk appetite — without realizing it.

In calm markets, this looks like diversification because the idiosyncratic component masks the common factor. In stress, correlations spike and you discover that 'four independent strategies' are actually one big bet. If I do eigenvalue decomposition of the inter-desk correlation matrix and the first eigenvalue explains 60%+ of variance, that fund has one and a half effective bets, not four.

The fix is systematic: daily cross-desk factor aggregation, limits on aggregate factor exposure (not just per-desk), and an effective-number-of-bets metric that the CRO monitors. At a fund like Squarepoint with many desks, this aggregation is probably the single highest-value-add function of the risk team."

"How would you evaluate whether a PM is generating alpha vs just running factor exposure?"

"Multi-factor regression: regress PM returns against market, size, value, momentum, quality, and relevant macro factors. Three things I'm looking for.

First, the R² — how much of the return is EXPLAINED by known factors? If R² is 0.85, only 15% of the return is truly idiosyncratic. The PM is mostly delivering factor exposure, which could be replicated with a cheap ETF portfolio.

Second, the alpha — is the intercept statistically positive after accounting for ALL factor exposures? A positive alpha with t-stat >2.0 is evidence of genuine skill. But I'd want at least 3-4 years of data — two good quarters proves nothing. The standard error on Sharpe is roughly 1/√n, and distinguishing Sharpe 1.0 from zero at 95% confidence requires ~4 years of monthly data.

Third, the factor loadings themselves — are they what the PM claims? A PM who says 'I'm a stock picker' but has β_momentum = 0.7 is not picking stocks. They're running a momentum strategy and charging stock-picking fees. That's not necessarily bad — but the risk budget they consume should be priced against a momentum benchmark, not against cash."

"Walk me through how you'd construct a risk dashboard from scratch."

"I'd organize it in six layers, from most granular to most strategic.

First, exposure metrics — net and gross notional, leverage, long/short ratio. These tell me the SIZE of the bet. Second, concentration metrics — HHI, effective N, single-name and sector limits. These tell me if the bet is SPREAD or STACKED. Third, factor decomposition — style and macro factor exposures, effective number of bets, cross-desk factor aggregation. This tells me the NATURE of the bet. Fourth, risk measures — VaR, ES, ES/VaR ratio, stress test P&L across historical and hypothetical scenarios. This tells me HOW MUCH we can lose. Fifth, liquidity metrics — DTL distribution, margin headroom, percent of book liquidable in 1/3/5 days. This tells me CAN WE GET OUT. Sixth, drawdown tracking — current drawdown from HWM versus soft/hard/kill limits. This tells me WHERE WE ARE relative to survival thresholds.

I'd review all six layers every morning BEFORE markets open, with automated alerts for any limit that crosses 80% utilization. The most important single metric? Probably the effective number of bets from eigenvalue decomposition — because it integrates correlation, concentration, and factor exposure into one number that tells me whether we're genuinely diversified or running a disguised single bet."
