G — PORTFOLIO RISK CONCEPTS

Correlation, concentration, basis risk, liquidity risk, factor exposure, tail risk

Depth: 🔴 DEEP

PART 1: CORRELATION

What It Is

Correlation measures the degree to which two assets move together. Ranges from -1 (perfect opposite movement) to +1 (perfect co-movement). Zero = no linear relationship.

$$\rho_{X,Y} = \frac{\text{Cov}(X,Y)}{\sigma_X \times \sigma_Y}$$

Why Correlation Is the Most Dangerous Number in Risk Management

Correlation is NOT constant. It changes with:
- Market regime (risk-on vs risk-off)
- Volatility level (correlations spike when vol spikes)
- Macro environment (inflation vs deflation regime)
- Crisis vs calm

The critical asymmetry: When you need diversification most (in a crisis), correlation goes to 1. Assets that were "uncorrelated" in calm markets all sell off together.

Unconditional vs Conditional Correlation

Unconditional: The average correlation across all market environments. "SPX and oil have a 0.35 correlation over the past 5 years." This is what most risk models use.

Conditional (on stress): The correlation specifically during sell-offs. "SPX and oil have a 0.78 correlation when SPX is down >2% in a day." This is what actually matters for portfolio risk.

Pair Unconditional ρ Conditional ρ (in stress)
SPX vs single stocks 0.30 0.70+
SPX vs HY credit 0.50 0.85+
SPX vs US Treasury -0.30 -0.50 (usually) BUT +0.40 in 2022 (inflation regime)
Gold vs SPX -0.10 -0.30 (usually) BUT +0.20 if real rates dominate
Cross-PM portfolios at same fund 0.10-0.20 0.50-0.80 (hidden common factors)

What the RM Needs to Do About Correlation

  1. Don't trust trailing correlations. They're backward-looking and regime-dependent.
  2. Stress test with elevated correlations. Run scenarios where ρ = 0.7 or 0.9 instead of the historical 0.3.
  3. Monitor correlation regime changes. If stocks-bonds correlation flips from -0.3 to +0.3 → the entire hedging framework changes.
  4. Aggregate across PMs. Scenario Analysis Scenario 5 — the most dangerous risk is when all PMs are loading on the same hidden factor.

PART 2: CONCENTRATION RISK

What It Is

Too much risk in a single position, sector, factor, or counterparty.

Types of Concentration

Single-Name Concentration

"20% of the book is in one stock." If that stock gaps down 15% on earnings, you're down 3% on the whole fund. That's a week's return gone.

Sector Concentration

"40% of the book is in energy." Even with 10 different names, they'll all move together when oil moves. You don't have 10 positions — you have one oil bet dressed up as 10 positions.

Factor Concentration

The most dangerous kind because it's HIDDEN. Multiple positions across different sectors can all load on the same factor:
- Long tech + short utilities + long growth + short value = massive momentum factor bet
- Long energy + long EM = oil beta bet in disguise
- Long banks + short Treasuries = rate bet in disguise

How to detect: Factor regression. Regress portfolio returns against standard factors (SPX, value, momentum, size, oil, rates, DXY). If R² against one factor is >0.6 → that factor IS your portfolio.

Crowding Concentration

Even if YOUR fund is diversified, if every other hedge fund holds the same positions, the aggregate is concentrated. When one fund de-risks, they all need to → amplified selling → crash.

How to detect:
- 13F overlap analysis (how many funds hold the same stocks?)
- Short interest concentration (high short interest = crowded short)
- Factor-weighted positioning (is the "momentum long" crowded across the industry?)

PART 3: BASIS RISK

What It Is

The risk that a hedge doesn't move 1:1 with the position it's supposed to hedge. The "basis" = price of the hedged asset minus price of the hedging instrument.

Examples

Position Hedge Basis risk
Long XOM stock Short XLE (energy ETF) XOM might outperform XLE → hedge doesn't fully offset
Long Brent crude Short WTI crude Brent-WTI spread can widen/narrow independently
Long corporate bonds Short Treasury futures Credit spread can move independent of rate moves
Long foreign stocks Short FX FX hedge has a different tenor/maturity than equity position

Why Basis Risk Matters

A PM says: "I'm hedged — I have a put on SPX against my portfolio." But the portfolio is 60% tech, 20% healthcare, 20% energy. If tech sells off 15% but SPX only drops 7% (because banks and energy hold up), the hedge covers 7% of a 15% loss. That's basis risk.

How to Manage

  • Hedge with the MOST CORRELATED instrument to the actual exposure.
  • Monitor the basis continuously — it's not static.
  • Understand that basis risk is the risk of being "right on the direction, wrong on the hedge."
  • If basis is unstable → reduce position size or find a better hedge.

PART 4: LIQUIDITY RISK

Two Dimensions (recap from Market Mechanics)

Market Liquidity Risk

Can you exit the position without moving the price?

Measures:
- ADV ratio: Position size / Average Daily Volume. >5% = illiquid. >20% = you ARE the market.
- Bid-ask spread: Wider spread = more illiquid. Cost of immediate execution.
- Market impact: How much your trade moves the price. Non-linear — 2x the size doesn't just cost 2x; it costs more.
- Days to liquidate: Position / (30% of ADV) = approximate days needed to exit without excessive impact.

Funding Liquidity Risk

Can you meet cash obligations (margin calls, redemptions)?

Liquidity mismatch: If the fund allows monthly redemptions but holds positions that take 3 weeks to liquidate → mismatch. When redemptions hit during a crisis → fire-sale liquidation.

Liquidity Risk Hierarchy

Instrument Liquidity tier Notes
US Treasuries 1 (most liquid) Usually — BUT March 2020 proved even Treasuries can become illiquid
S&P 500 E-mini futures 1 Most liquid equity product in the world
Large cap equities (AAPL, MSFT) 1-2 Very liquid in normal markets
Mid/small cap equities 2-3 Can take days to exit in size
Investment grade bonds 2-3 OTC, dealer-intermediated
High yield bonds 3-4 Illiquid in stress, wide bid-ask
OTC derivatives (IRS, CDS) 3-4 Custom terms, dealer dependent
Exotic OTC derivatives 4-5 May have ONE counterparty
Private credit / loans 5 (least liquid) No secondary market in stress

Liquidity Monitoring for the RM

Daily checklist:
1. What % of the portfolio can be liquidated in 1 day? 3 days? 1 week?
2. What is margin utilization as % of available credit?
3. What's the cash buffer? (How many days of negative P&L before margin calls?)
4. Any position >5% of ADV? Flag it.
5. In a vol spike, how much does margin increase? Do we have the cash?

PART 5: FACTOR EXPOSURE

What Factors Are

Systematic drivers of returns that explain WHY a portfolio moves. The main families:

Equity Style Factors (Fama-French & extensions)

Factor Long Short What drives it
Market (MKT) Equities Risk-free Equity risk premium
Size (SMB) Small caps Large caps Liquidity premium, information asymmetry
Value (HML) Low P/B stocks High P/B stocks Mean reversion, behavioral
Momentum (MOM) Past winners Past losers Behavioral (herding, underreaction)
Quality (QMJ) High profit, low leverage Low profit, high leverage Fundamental risk
Low Vol (BAB) Low-beta stocks High-beta stocks Leverage aversion, benchmark hugging

Macro Factors

Factor What it captures
Rates (DV01) Sensitivity to interest rate moves
Credit (CS01) Sensitivity to credit spread moves
Oil Sensitivity to energy prices
Dollar (DXY) Sensitivity to USD strength/weakness
VIX / Vol Sensitivity to volatility changes
Inflation breakeven Sensitivity to inflation expectations

Why Factor Exposure Matters for Risk

1. "Alpha" is often disguised factor exposure

A PM claims they're generating alpha through stock selection. But factor decomposition shows 80% of their returns are explained by long momentum + long growth. They're not picking stocks — they're selling momentum exposure at a 2-and-20 fee.

2. Diversification is only real at the factor level

A fund with 50 equity positions looks diversified. But if all 50 stocks load heavily on momentum → one factor bet. Diversification at the NAME level is meaningless if factor concentration exists.

3. Factor crowding creates systemic risk

When every fund owns the same factor (e.g., momentum 2015-2020), a factor rotation (momentum crash Q4 2018, or value-momentum rotation 2020) causes coordinated losses across the industry.

How to Monitor Factor Exposure

  • Daily factor attribution: decompose portfolio returns into factor contributions
  • Factor P&L: how much did each factor contribute to today's P&L?
  • Factor concentration: is >50% of risk in a single factor?
  • Factor tilts vs mandate: if the PM says "stock picker" but 70% of risk is factor → conversation

PART 6: TAIL RISK & FAT TAILS

What Fat Tails Are

Real financial returns have more extreme moves than a normal distribution predicts. The "tails" of the distribution are "fatter."

Quantifying:
- Kurtosis: Normal distribution has kurtosis = 3. Financial returns typically have kurtosis = 5-10+. Higher = fatter tails.
- Practical: A 4-sigma daily move should happen once in ~126 years under normal distribution. In equities, it happens roughly every 2-3 years. The models undercount extreme events by a factor of ~50x.

Tail Risk = The Risk That Matters Most

Normal days are boring. The RM's value is in TAIL events. Your job is to ensure the fund survives:
- A day that VaR said was "impossible"
- A move that "hasn't happened in 30 years"
- A correlation that "doesn't exist"

Tail Risk Management Tools

Tool What it does Cost
OTM put options Direct tail protection Theta decay (0.5-2% annualized)
Put spreads Cheaper tail protection (caps the payout) Less theta but limited payout
VIX calls Convex payoff in vol spikes Expensive, roll cost in contango
Dynamic hedging Increase hedges as market drops Requires discipline, can be whipsawed
Position limits Prevent catastrophic concentration Limits PM flexibility
Diversification Spread risk across uncorrelated assets Only works if correlations hold
Cash buffer Reserve for margin calls Opportunity cost

The Paradox of Tail Protection

  • Tail protection COSTS money. Buying puts every month at 1.5% annualized drag = 7.5% over 5 years.
  • But when the tail hits, it SAVES the fund. March 2020: funds with tail hedges survived. Funds without were forced to liquidate.
  • The RM's job: articulate this tradeoff. "The tail hedge costs $2M/year. The tail event it protects against could cost $50M. Is $2M good insurance?"

PART 7: PUTTING IT ALL TOGETHER — PORTFOLIO RISK DECOMPOSITION

The Complete View

When you look at a portfolio's risk, you need all of these layers:

Layer 1: WHAT do we own?
  → Position-level review (names, sizes, Greeks)

Layer 2: WHY is it risky?
  → Factor decomposition (what's driving our risk?)

Layer 3: HOW MUCH can we lose?
  → VaR, ES, stress test results

Layer 4: WHAT IF things get worse?
  → Correlation stress, liquidity stress, margin stress

Layer 5: CAN WE GET OUT?
  → Liquidity analysis (days to liquidate, concentration vs ADV)

PART 8: INTERVIEW APPLICATION

If asked "How would you think about portfolio risk beyond VaR?":

"VaR gives me one number — the 99th percentile loss under normal conditions. I'd layer on four additional views.

First, Expected Shortfall — what's the average loss in that 1% worst case? This captures tail severity.

Second, factor decomposition — what's actually DRIVING the portfolio's risk? Two portfolios can have the same VaR but very different factor profiles. One might be diversified across 10 uncorrelated bets. The other might be a single momentum bet disguised as 30 positions.

Third, stress testing — how does the portfolio perform under specific scenarios like March 2020, or a hypothetical Hormuz closure? This tests what VaR misses: regime changes, correlation spikes, non-linear payoffs.

Fourth, liquidity analysis — can we actually exit these positions if we need to? A portfolio that takes 3 weeks to liquidate but has daily margin calls has a fundamental mismatch."

If asked "What's the most underappreciated risk in a multi-PM fund?":

"Correlation across PMs. Each PM runs their own book and thinks their risk is independent. But in practice, they often load on the same macro factor — rates, oil, momentum — without realizing it. In calm markets, the aggregate looks diversified. In stress, correlations spike and you discover that your 'four independent strategies' are actually one big bet.

This is exactly what killed multi-strategy funds in past crises. The RM's job is to run factor attribution across ALL PMs — not just each one individually — and flag when the aggregate factor exposure exceeds what any single PM would be allowed to hold."
