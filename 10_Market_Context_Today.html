Why This Is the #1 Risk Right Now

The Strait of Hormuz handles ~20% of global oil and natural gas supply. Its closure is not a localized event — it's a global supply shock that cascades through every asset class:

Strait closed → Oil $110+ → Gasoline/diesel/jet fuel soar
    │
    ├── Inflation spikes → Central banks can't cut rates → Bonds sell off
    │
    ├── Consumer spending squeezed → Growth slows → Stagflation risk
    │
    ├── Energy-importing nations (Japan, India, Europe) hit hardest → FX weakness
    │
    ├── Energy exporters (Saudi, UAE, Norway, Canada) benefit → FX strength
    │
    └── Shipping/logistics disrupted → Supply chains stressed → Broader inflation

IEA chief said today: "The current oil and gas crisis is worse than 1973, 1979, and 2022 together."

Gold — A Paradox

Gold at $4,649 is down despite a major war. Why?
1. Higher-for-longer rate expectations → opportunity cost of holding gold rises
2. Dollar strength → gold priced in USD becomes expensive for non-US buyers
3. Margin calls → some investors selling gold to cover losses elsewhere
4. Gold had its worst month in 17+ years in March despite the war

But: China's central bank has been buying gold for 17 consecutive months (74.38M fine troy ounces as of March). Central bank gold buying provides a floor.

Commodities Risk Manager's Read

  • Second-order effects already visible:
  • China raising domestic gasoline/diesel prices again (today's headline)
  • Singapore introduced $780M relief package for war impact
  • Pakistan switching to electric motorbikes due to fuel costs
  • South Asian energy crisis developing
  • Fertilizer disruption threatening food supply chain
  • Risk monitoring: Any discretionary book with energy exposure needs real-time Hormuz transit data, Iranian military movements, and diplomatic channel signals. This is not a "check once a day" risk — it moves in minutes.

PART 5: RISK THEMES — What a Discretionary Risk Manager Would Focus On

Theme 1: Stagflation Risk (THE Macro Risk)

What it is: High inflation + slow/negative growth simultaneously.

Evidence it's happening:
- US ISM services: growth slowing, input prices highest in 13+ years
- Eurozone PMI: growth at 9-month low on "surging costs"
- UK 10Y at 4.85%: market pricing sticky inflation
- Oil above $110: direct inflation input
- Italy's central bank: cut growth forecasts AND raised inflation estimates

Why it matters for a discretionary book:
- Stagflation invalidates most macro models (which assume either "growth up + inflation up" or "growth down + inflation down")
- Long equity / short rates positions get killed (equities fall on growth fears, rates rise on inflation fears)
- Relative value trades break down as correlations spike

Risk manager action: Stress test the book for a stagflation scenario — assume 3% GDP growth drops to 0.5% while CPI goes from 3% to 5%+. Which positions survive?

Theme 2: Central Bank Policy Divergence

Setup:
- Fed at 3.75% — unclear if it cuts (conflicting signals)
- ECB at 2.15% — needs to cut for growth but inflation argues against it
- BOJ at 0.75% — wants to normalize but yen weakness forces patience
- RBI at 5.25% — India's oil import bill is exploding

Why it matters:
- Policy divergence creates FX volatility (carries unwind)
- If Fed holds while ECB cuts → EUR/USD drops → European equity returns for USD-based investors change
- If BOJ signals any hawkishness → yen strengthens → JPY carry trades unwind violently

Risk manager action: Map the portfolio's sensitivity to each central bank's next move. The April 28-30 window has BOJ, Fed, ECB, BOE all deciding — correlated policy shocks are possible.

Theme 3: Geopolitical Binary Events

Tonight's deadline is the archetype of what discretionary risk management faces.

The market is pricing "extend deadline" as base case. If that's wrong:

If Deal → If Escalation →
Oil -25% to $80-85 Oil +20% to $130-140
VIX from 24 to 15 VIX from 24 to 35+
S&P +3-5% S&P -5-10%
USD weakens, EUR strengthens USD spikes, EM FX collapses
Gold flat to down Gold uncertain (rate fears vs safe haven)
Energy stocks sell off Energy stocks surge, everything else sells

Risk manager action:
1. Pre-compute P&L impact under each scenario
2. Identify asymmetric exposures (positions that lose much more in one scenario than they gain in the other)
3. Consider options structures that hedge the tail (e.g., out-of-money puts on equity, calls on oil)
4. Set pre-agreed stop-loss levels before the event, not during

Theme 4: Liquidity Risk Is Elevated

Signs of liquidity stress:
- VIX at 24 → market makers widening bid-ask spreads
- European markets were closed Friday-Monday (Easter) → pent-up order flow hitting today
- Private credit turmoil — Bloomberg reports "retail investors eyeing the exits" from private credit funds
- India bank options hedging costs climbing (Bloomberg headline today)

Risk manager action:
- Wider stop-loss buffers around illiquid positions
- Monitor repo and funding markets for stress signals
- Be wary of positions that are "liquid in theory but illiquid in crisis" (e.g., corporate bonds, structured products, small-cap equities)

Theme 5: Private Credit Contagion

Bloomberg Markets Magazine feature: "Private Credit Turmoil Has Retail Investors Eyeing the Exits"

  • The private credit market has exploded in size since 2020
  • Rising rates + oil shock = stress on leveraged borrowers
  • Fund redemptions are accelerating
  • Yale's endowment model (heavy private equity/illiquid alternatives) is underperforming plain stocks and bonds

Why it matters for Squarepoint:
- If private credit stress spills into public markets (via bank exposure, CLO haircuts, or forced selling), it creates correlated sell-offs
- Discretionary funds that trade credit (Squarepoint's Arini partnership is credit-focused) face direct mark-to-market risk
- Contagion from private to public markets happened before (2008, 2020) — early warning signals should be on the dashboard
