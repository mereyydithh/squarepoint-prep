F — RISK MEASURES

VaR, ES, stress testing, DV01, performance metrics — the quant toolkit

Depth: 🔴 DEEP

PART 1: VALUE AT RISK (VaR)

What It Is

"What is the maximum loss, at a given confidence level, over a given time horizon, under normal market conditions?"

Example: "1-day 99% VaR = $5M" means: there is a 99% chance that losses will not exceed $5M over the next trading day. Equivalently: on 1 day in 100, losses WILL exceed $5M — and VaR says nothing about how much.

Three Methods

1. Parametric (Variance-Covariance)

How it works: Assume returns are normally distributed. VaR = portfolio value × σ × z-score.

$$\text{VaR}{99\%} = \mu - z{0.99} \times \sigma = \mu - 2.326 \times \sigma$$

For 95% confidence: z = 1.645. For 99%: z = 2.326.

Pros Cons
Fast — analytical formula Assumes normal distribution (misses fat tails)
Easy to compute for large portfolios Doesn't capture non-linear positions (options) well
Works for linear instruments (stocks, futures, bonds) Correlation matrix can be unstable

2. Historical Simulation

How it works: Take actual historical returns (say, last 500 days). Apply each day's returns to current positions. Sort the resulting P&L distribution. The 99th percentile loss IS the VaR.

Pros Cons
No distribution assumption — uses actual data Limited by history: if your window doesn't include a crisis, you miss it
Captures fat tails if they're in the data All days weighted equally (yesterday = 2 years ago)
Captures non-linear positions naturally Look-back window choice is subjective

3. Monte Carlo Simulation

How it works: Simulate thousands of random scenarios using specified distributions and correlations for each risk factor. Price the portfolio under each scenario. Build a P&L distribution. Take the percentile.

Pros Cons
Most flexible — any distribution, any payoff Computationally expensive
Handles non-linear instruments well Only as good as the model assumptions
Can model complex correlations Need to specify the simulation parameters (model risk)

VaR Limitations — KNOW THESE COLD

  1. VaR is not worst case. It tells you the threshold, not what happens BEYOND it. "99% VaR = $5M" means on that 1-in-100 day, the loss could be $5M or $50M or $500M. VaR is silent on the magnitude of tail losses.

  2. VaR assumes normal conditions. It doesn't capture regime changes, liquidity dry-ups, or correlation spikes. The loss distribution in crisis is different from the loss distribution used to calculate VaR.

  3. VaR is backward-looking. Historical VaR uses past data. If the past was calm, VaR will be low — just when risk is building (complacency).

  4. VaR is not subadditive. The VaR of a combined portfolio can be GREATER than the sum of individual VaRs. This violates a basic property of a "coherent" risk measure. (ES/CVaR is subadditive.)

  5. VaR can be gamed. A trader can structure a position that has low VaR but massive tail risk — sell deep OTM options, collect premium, VaR looks clean. Until it doesn't.

PART 2: EXPECTED SHORTFALL (ES / CVaR)

What It Is

"What is the AVERAGE loss when losses exceed VaR?"

ES answers the question VaR refuses to: "how bad is it when it's bad?"

$$\text{ES}{99\%} = E[\text{Loss} | \text{Loss} > \text{VaR}{99\%}]$$

Why ES Is Better Than VaR

Property VaR ES
Tells you tail severity? No — only the cut-off point Yes — average of all losses beyond VaR
Subadditive? No (can violate for non-normal distributions) Yes — always
Responds to tail shape? No — blind to what's beyond the threshold Yes — fatter tails → higher ES
FRTB standard? Old regime (VaR-based) New regime (ES-based) — banks moving to ES

Example

Portfolio with 99% VaR = $5M.

If the 1% worst scenarios average -$12M → ES = $12M.
If the 1% worst scenarios average -$50M → ES = $50M.

Both portfolios have the SAME VaR. But the second one has 4x worse tail risk. Only ES captures this.

PART 3: STRESS TESTING

What It Is

"What happens to our portfolio under extreme but plausible scenarios?"

Stress testing complements VaR/ES by not relying on historical distributions. You DEFINE the scenario and measure the impact.

Three Types

1. Historical Stress Tests

Replay actual crisis events. "What if March 2020 happened again with our current portfolio?"

Event SPX VIX 10Y Credit Oil
GFC 2008 (Sep-Nov) -40% 80+ -200bps (flight to quality) +400bps HY -55%
Volmageddon Feb 2018 -10% 14 → 50 +30bps +50bps -5%
COVID crash Mar 2020 -34% 14 → 82 -100bps then +50bps +600bps HY -65%
Russia/Ukraine 2022 -10% 36 +100bps (inflation) +100bps +50%
SVB crisis Mar 2023 -5% 26 -50bps (flight to quality) +80bps -10%

Apply these moves to your current positions and aggregate the P&L.

2. Hypothetical Stress Tests

Create plausible scenarios that haven't happened. Often MORE useful than historical because the next crisis won't look exactly like the last one.

Examples:
- "Iran closes Hormuz → Oil +30%, equities -15%, vol +40pts"
- "Fed emergency rate hike 50bps → 2Y +80bps, equities -10%"
- "US Treasury downgrade → Treasuries sell off AND equities sell off simultaneously"

3. Sensitivity Tests (Factor Shocks)

Bump one factor at a time. "What happens if SPX moves -5%? -10%? -20%?"

Build a grid:

Scenario SPX -5% SPX -10% SPX -15%
Vol unchanged -$XM -$YM -$ZM
Vol +5pts -$X'M -$Y'M -$Z'M
Vol +15pts -$X''M -$Y''M -$Z''M

This creates a "heat map" of portfolio P&L across scenarios. The RM can see at a glance where the danger zones are.

PART 4: FIXED INCOME RISK MEASURES

DV01 (Dollar Value of a Basis Point)

"How much does my position gain/lose for a 1bp move in yield?"

$$\text{DV01} = \text{Duration} \times \text{Price} \times 0.0001$$

A bond portfolio with DV01 = $200K loses $200K per basis point of yield increase. A 50bp move → -$10M.

CS01 (Credit Spread 01)

Same concept but for credit spread changes. "How much does my position gain/lose for a 1bp widening in credit spreads?"

Key Rate Duration (KRD)

Duration broken down by tenor point. Instead of "the portfolio has duration 5," you get: "2Y bucket: 0.8, 5Y: 1.5, 10Y: 1.8, 30Y: 0.9."

This matters because the yield curve doesn't move in parallel. If you have zero net duration but +3 at the short end and -3 at the long end → you're making a steepener bet. Net duration hides this.

PART 5: PERFORMANCE & RISK-ADJUSTED METRICS

Sharpe Ratio

$$\text{Sharpe} = \frac{R_p - R_f}{\sigma_p}$$

Return above risk-free rate, divided by volatility. How much return per unit of risk.

Sharpe What it means
< 0.5 Poor risk-adjusted return
0.5 - 1.0 Decent
1.0 - 2.0 Very good. Most successful hedge funds are here.
> 2.0 Exceptional or the track record is too short to be reliable

Sharpe limitations:
- Treats upside and downside vol equally. A fund with high upside vol looks "worse" than it should.
- Can be gamed by selling tail risk (high Sharpe until the tail event arrives).
- Time-period dependent. A 1-year Sharpe can look great; a 10-year Sharpe is more meaningful.

Sortino Ratio

$$\text{Sortino} = \frac{R_p - R_f}{\sigma_{\text{downside}}}$$

Like Sharpe but only penalizes DOWNSIDE volatility. Better for strategies with asymmetric returns.

Maximum Drawdown

The largest peak-to-trough decline in the portfolio. Not a distributional metric — it's the observed worst case.

  • Max drawdown = -15% means at some point, the fund lost 15% from its peak before recovering.
  • Recovery time matters too: -15% drawdown recovered in 3 months is very different from -15% recovered in 18 months.

Calmar Ratio

$$\text{Calmar} = \frac{\text{Annualized return}}{\text{Max drawdown}}$$

Return per unit of worst loss. Particularly useful for evaluating funds that had a bad drawdown: "Yes they drew down 20%, but they returned 30% annualized."

Information Ratio

$$\text{IR} = \frac{R_p - R_b}{\text{Tracking error}}$$

Alpha (excess return above benchmark) divided by tracking error (volatility of that excess return). Measures skill in beating the benchmark, risk-adjusted.

Alpha & Beta Decomposition

$$R_p = \alpha + \beta \times R_m + \epsilon$$

  • β = how much the fund moves with the market. β = 0.5 means 50% of returns are explained by market moves.
  • α = the excess return after removing market exposure. THIS is what the fund charges 2-and-20 for.
  • If a fund has high returns but β = 1.2, most of the return is just leveraged market exposure — not alpha.

Your job as RM: If a PM claims they're generating alpha but their β is 0.9 and Sharpe is 0.7, they're basically a leveraged index fund. The PM should deliver alpha with controlled beta. Monitor the decomposition.

PART 6: DRAWDOWN MANAGEMENT

The Math of Recovery

Drawdown Recovery needed
-10% +11.1%
-20% +25.0%
-30% +42.9%
-50% +100%
-75% +300%

This is why drawdown limits exist. A -50% drawdown requires doubling your capital to recover. It's NOT symmetric.

Risk Budget Framework

Total fund risk is allocated to PMs:
- PM A: $2M daily VaR budget (40% of fund risk)
- PM B: $1.5M daily VaR budget (30%)
- PM C: $1M daily VaR budget (20%)
- Reserve: $0.5M (10%)

Each PM must operate within their budget. If they exceed, they must reduce positions or get IC approval.

What the RM monitors:
- Current VaR vs budget (utilization %)
- Drawdown vs soft/hard limits
- Marginal contribution to risk (does adding PM A's positions make the fund riskier or provide diversification?)

PART 7: INTERVIEW APPLICATION

If asked "What are the limitations of VaR?":

"VaR has four key limitations. First, it tells you nothing about losses beyond the threshold — a 99% VaR of $5M could mean the 1% scenarios average $6M or $60M. That's why Expected Shortfall is a better measure of tail risk.

Second, it assumes relatively normal market conditions. In a crisis, correlations spike, liquidity dries up, and the loss distribution changes — VaR calculated from calm periods systematically underestimates crisis losses.

Third, it can be gamed — a trader can sell deep OTM options, which generates premium income and keeps VaR low, while taking on massive tail risk that VaR doesn't capture.

Fourth, it's not subadditive — combining two portfolios can produce a VaR higher than the sum of individual VaRs, which is counterintuitive and technically violates the definition of a coherent risk measure.

That's why good risk management never relies on VaR alone. You need ES for tail risk, stress testing for extreme scenarios, and qualitative judgment for risks that no model captures."

If asked "How would you set up a stress testing framework?":

"I'd run three types. Historical stress tests — replay actual crises like 2008, March 2020 — to see if our current portfolio survives past shocks. Hypothetical scenarios — things that haven't happened but could, like Hormuz closure or a Fed policy reversal — because the next crisis won't look exactly like the last one. And sensitivity tests — systematic factor shocks across a grid of scenarios — to map out where our P&L breaks.

The most important output isn't a single number but the heat map: what combination of moves hurts us most? If a -10% equity move with a +20pt vol spike creates a $25M loss, and our drawdown limit is $30M, that's a conversation worth having before it happens, not after."
