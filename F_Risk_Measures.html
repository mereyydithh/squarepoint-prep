F — RISK MEASURES: THE QUANTITATIVE LANGUAGE OF RISK

VaR, ES, stress testing, factor sensitivities, performance metrics, backtesting, and the RM's measurement toolkit

Depth: 🔴 DEEP

WHY THIS IS YOUR OPERATING SYSTEM

At Squarepoint, everything is measured. Every PM has a VaR budget, a drawdown limit, a set of stress scenarios they must survive, and a decomposition of their P&L into risk factors. As a Junior Risk Manager, your daily job involves:

  1. Reading risk reports — "Desk A has 95% VaR of $3.2M, ES/VaR ratio of 1.8, 65% utilization of risk budget"
  2. Flagging anomalies — "Desk B's parametric VaR dropped 20% but their gamma exposure doubled — VaR is misleading because of nonlinearity"
  3. Running stress tests — "What happens to the fund under SPX −15%, VIX +30, UST 10Y +100bp?"
  4. Challenging PMs — "Your Sharpe is 1.8 but your Sortino is 0.6 — your return is coming from selling tail risk, not alpha"

Risk measures are not abstract math. They are the WORDS you use to communicate risk, set limits, and protect capital. Get them wrong, or use them naively, and you miss what kills the fund.

PART 1: VALUE AT RISK (VaR)

One-liner: VaR is the maximum loss at a given confidence level over a given horizon under NORMAL conditions — and it says absolutely nothing about what happens when conditions stop being normal.

The Definition

$$\text{VaR}_\alpha = \inf { x : P(\text{Loss} > x) \leq 1 - \alpha }$$

In English: "99% 1-day VaR = $5M" means: "On 99 out of 100 days, the loss will NOT exceed $5M." Equivalently: "On 1 in 100 days, losses WILL exceed $5M — and VaR is completely silent on HOW FAR beyond $5M."

Key parameters:
| Parameter | Common choices | Impact |
|---|---|---|
| Confidence level | 95%, 97.5%, 99%, 99.5% | Higher confidence → larger VaR number → more tail captured |
| Time horizon | 1 day, 10 days | 10-day VaR ≈ 1-day VaR × √10 (scaling assumption — crude but standard) |

Regulatory context:
| Framework | VaR spec | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Basel II/III (banks) | 99% 10-day VaR | Plus multiplier (3× or higher if backtesting fails) |
| FRTB (new) | 97.5% ES replacing VaR | Moving to ES — more tail-sensitive |
| Hedge fund (internal) | 95% or 99% 1-day VaR | Fund-specific. Squarepoint uses internal risk budgets. |

Three Methods of Computing VaR

1. Parametric (Variance-Covariance / Delta-Normal)

Assumption: Portfolio returns are normally distributed.

$$\text{VaR}{99\%} = z{0.99} \times \sigma_p \times \text{Portfolio Value}$$

Where $z_{0.99} = 2.326$, $z_{0.975} = 1.96$, $z_{0.95} = 1.645$.

For a portfolio of N assets:

$$\sigma_p^2 = \mathbf{w}^T \boldsymbol{\Sigma} \mathbf{w}$$

Where w = weight vector, Σ = covariance matrix.

Worked example — Two-asset portfolio:
- $60M in SPX (σ = 18%), $40M in UST 10Y (σ = 8%), correlation ρ = −0.30
- Portfolio variance = (0.6)²(0.18)² + (0.4)²(0.08)² + 2(0.6)(0.4)(0.18)(0.08)(−0.30)
= 0.011664 + 0.001024 − 0.003456 = 0.009232
- Portfolio σ = √0.009232 = 9.61%
- 99% 1-day VaR = 2.326 × 9.61%/√252 × $100M = 2.326 × 0.605% × $100M = $1.41M
- Note: without correlation benefit (ρ=0), VaR would be $1.53M. The −0.30 correlation saves $120K of daily VaR.

Strengths Weaknesses
Fast — closed-form, scales to 10,000+ positions Assumes normality — misses fat tails, skewness
Transparent — can decompose into factor contributions Fails for non-linear positions (options). Delta-normal only.
Easy to communicate Covariance matrix can be unstable — 500 assets need 125,000 pairwise correlations
Component VaR and marginal VaR derivable analytically Correlation breakdown in crisis — the matrix estimated from calm times doesn't apply in stress

Option-unfriendly: Parametric VaR uses delta (linear) approximation. An option P&L is nonlinear (gamma). Parametric VaR UNDERESTIMATES risk for short gamma positions and OVERESTIMATES risk for long gamma positions. At a fund running options, this is dangerous.

2. Historical Simulation

Process:
1. Collect N days of historical returns for all risk factors (typically 250-1000 days)
2. Apply each day's actual returns to today's portfolio positions
3. Generate N simulated P&L outcomes
4. Sort them. The loss at the (1−α)×N-th percentile = VaR.

Worked example:
- 500 trading days of history
- Apply each day's returns to current $100M portfolio
- Sort the 500 simulated P&Ls
- 99% VaR: the 5th worst day (500 × 1% = 5) → say that's −$4.8M
- 95% VaR: the 25th worst day → say that's −$2.1M

Strengths Weaknesses
No distribution assumption — captures actual fat tails, skewness Hostage to history — if the window doesn't include a crisis, VaR misses it
Naturally handles non-linear positions (options repriced each scenario) Equal weighting — a return from 3 years ago counts the same as yesterday (unless you use EWMA weighting)
Captures cross-asset correlation as it actually occurred Look-back window dilemma: long window = smoother but includes stale data; short window = responsive but noisier
Easy to explain to non-quants Ghost effects: a single extreme day drops OUT of the window → VaR drops suddenly, even though nothing changed in the portfolio

Improvement — BHS (Bootstrapped Historical Simulation): Resample with replacement from the historical window to generate more scenarios. Reduces dependence on a single ordering. Also allows confidence intervals around VaR itself.

Improvement — Filtered Historical Simulation (FHS): Use GARCH to standardize returns (remove time-varying vol), then resample. Combines historical tail shape with current vol estimate. Best of both worlds.

3. Monte Carlo VaR

Process:
1. Specify return distributions and correlation structure for all risk factors
2. Generate 10,000-100,000+ random scenarios
3. For each scenario, fully reprice the portfolio (including options, exotics)
4. Build the P&L distribution. Take the percentile.

Strengths Weaknesses
Most flexible — any distribution, any payoff, any correlation structure Computationally expensive — tens of thousands of full portfolio repricings
Handles non-linear instruments properly Model risk — only as good as your distributional assumptions
Can incorporate fat tails (t-distribution), jumps, stochastic vol Convergence — need enough simulations for stable tail estimates
Can model regime changes, dynamic correlations False precision risk — fancy simulation doesn't mean accurate if the model is wrong

When MC is essential: Portfolios heavy in exotic options (barriers, Asian, lookbacks), structured products, or complex multi-asset derivatives. Parametric and historical VaR can't handle these — only full repricing captures the nonlinearity.

VaR Decomposition — Where the Risk Lives

At Squarepoint, a single aggregate VaR number is useless. You need to know WHERE the risk is.

Component VaR

$$\text{Component VaR}i = \rho{i,p} \times \sigma_i \times w_i \times z \times V$$

The contribution of position i to total portfolio VaR. Component VaRs SUM to total VaR (they account for diversification).

The power: "Your total VaR is $5M. Desk A contributes $3.2M. Desk B contributes $2.8M. Desk C contributes −$1.0M." Desk C is a diversifier — its risk REDUCES total VaR because it's negatively correlated with the rest.

Marginal VaR

$$\text{Marginal VaR}_i = \frac{\partial \text{VaR}}{\partial w_i}$$

How much does VaR change if you add $1 more to position i? Tells you which positions are most efficient to add or trim from a risk perspective.

Incremental VaR

$$\text{Incremental VaR}i = \text{VaR}{\text{with position}} - \text{VaR}_{\text{without position}}$$

Unlike marginal (infinitesimal change), incremental measures the FULL effect of adding/removing a position. Critical for new trade approval: "This new position adds $400K of incremental VaR. Do we have risk budget?"

VaR Limitations — THE Critical Section

Every interviewer at Squarepoint will test whether you blindly trust VaR or understand its failures.

Limitation Why it matters Real-world consequence
Not a worst case VaR = threshold, not a range beyond it. The 1% tail could be $5M or $500M. LTCM, Bear Stearns — losses far beyond VaR
Assumes normal conditions Crisis behavior (correlation spike, liquidity dry-up, gap moves) ≠ normal behavior Every major blowup violates the conditions VaR assumed
Backward-looking Computed from recent history. If recent history was calm → VaR is low → just when risk is building 2008: VaR was LOW entering the crisis because 2005-2007 was calm
Not subadditive VaR(A+B) can > VaR(A) + VaR(B) — violates coherence Portfolio combination can look RISKIER than sum of parts — nonsensical for allocation
Gameable Sell deep OTM options → premium income, VaR looks clean, massive hidden tail risk Every "low-VaR, high-Sharpe" strategy should be checked for hidden concavity
Point-in-time Computed at close. Positions change intraday. VaR at 4pm may not reflect 2pm risk. Intraday blowups not captured by EOD VaR
Correlation instability Calm-period correlations used in VaR → wrong in crisis (correlations spike to 0.8+) Diversification assumed by VaR vanishes in the tail

The interview line: "VaR is necessary but far from sufficient. I think of it as a speedometer — useful for daily monitoring, but it doesn't tell you there's a cliff ahead. For that you need stress testing, ES, and judgment."

PART 2: EXPECTED SHORTFALL (ES / CVaR / Tail VaR)

One-liner: ES answers the question VaR refuses to ask — "When we breach VaR, how bad does it actually get?"

The Definition

$$\text{ES}\alpha = E[\text{Loss} \mid \text{Loss} > \text{VaR}\alpha] = \frac{1}{1-\alpha} \int_\alpha^1 \text{VaR}_u \, du$$

In English: The average of ALL losses in the tail beyond VaR. If 99% VaR = $5M, ES averages the losses on the 1% worst days.

Why ES > VaR

Property VaR ES
Tail severity Blind — only the threshold Measures the whole tail
Subadditivity Violates (not coherent) Always satisfies (coherent)
Sensitivity to tail shape None — two portfolios with same VaR but different tail sizes look identical Higher for fatter tails
Regulatory trend Old regime (Basel II/III) New regime (FRTB) — banks moving to 97.5% ES
Gameability Easy to manipulate (sell tail) Much harder to game

Worked Example — Why ES Matters

Portfolio A (selling OTM puts):
- 99% VaR = $5M
- On the 1% worst days: losses are $10M, $15M, $25M, $40M, $80M
- ES = average($10M, $15M, $25M, $40M, $80M) = $34M
- ES/VaR ratio = 34/5 = 6.8 ← FAT tail. Concave payoff. Hidden risk.

Portfolio B (diversified long equity):
- 99% VaR = $5M
- On the 1% worst days: losses are $5.5M, $6M, $6.5M, $7M, $7.5M
- ES = average = $6.5M
- ES/VaR ratio = 6.5/5 = 1.3 ← thin tail. Linear payoff. What you see is what you get.

Both have IDENTICAL VaR. Only ES reveals that Portfolio A has 5× worse tail risk.

ES/VaR Ratio — The Tail Shape Indicator

ES/VaR ratio Tail shape What it means Typical position type
1.0-1.5 Thin / normal VaR captures most of the risk Diversified long equity, futures
1.5-2.5 Moderate Some tail concentration Mixed books, moderate option overlay
2.5-4.0 Fat Significant hidden tail risk Concentrated trades, short gamma positions
4.0+ Very fat / cliff Extreme concavity — DANGER Short deep OTM options, short var swaps, illiquid credit

RM rule of thumb: If a desk's ES/VaR ratio exceeds 3.0, investigate. They're likely running a concave P&L profile — earning steady premium but exposed to catastrophic tail loss.

ES Under Different Distributions

For normal distribution: $\text{ES}{99\%} = \sigma \times \frac{\phi(z{0.99})}{0.01} = \sigma \times 2.665$

Compare to $\text{VaR}_{99\%} = 2.326 \sigma$ → ES/VaR = 2.665/2.326 = 1.15 (thin tail for normal).

For Student-t (ν=5): ES/VaR ≈ 1.83. Fatter tails → bigger gap between VaR and ES.

For actual equity markets (empirical): ES/VaR typically 1.4-1.6 for diversified portfolios, 2.0-5.0+ for option-heavy books.

PART 3: STRESS TESTING — THE COMPLEMENT TO VaR

One-liner: VaR tells you what happens on a normal bad day. Stress tests tell you what happens on the WORST day — the one that actually threatens the fund's survival.

Why Stress Testing Exists

VaR and ES are statistical — they rely on historical distributions or model assumptions. Stress testing is scenario-based — you DEFINE the scenario and measure the impact. This makes it:

  • Not backward-looking — you can test events that haven't happened yet
  • Not model-dependent — you specify the shocks directly
  • Intuitive — "If SPX drops 20% and VIX hits 60, we lose $X" is understood by everyone

Three Types of Stress Tests

Replay actual crises with current positions.

Reverse Stress Testing

Start from the loss. Work backward to find the scenario that causes it.

"What scenario causes a $30M loss (our drawdown limit)?" → solve for the combination of factor moves. This is arguably the most important stress test because it directly answers: "What kills us?"

Process:
1. Define the loss threshold (e.g., max drawdown limit, fund-breaking loss)
2. Optimize over scenarios: find the minimum-severity scenario that hits the threshold
3. Ask: "Is this scenario plausible?" If yes → you have a problem.
4. The scenarios that produce fund-death with PLAUSIBLE moves are the ones to hedge against.

PART 4: FIXED INCOME & CREDIT RISK MEASURES

DV01 (Dollar Value of a Basis Point)

One-liner: How much money you make or lose for a 1bp move in yield. THE unit of rates risk.

$$\text{DV01} \approx \frac{\text{ModDur} \times \text{Price} \times \text{Notional}}{10{,}000}$$

Worked example:
- $50M par of 10Y UST, modified duration = 8.5, price = 95
- DV01 = 8.5 × 95/100 × $50M / 10,000 = $40,375 per bp
- If yields rise 25bp: loss = 25 × $40,375 = −$1,009,375
- If yields fall 50bp: gain = 50 × $40,375 = +$2,018,750

DV01 ladder — decomposition by maturity:

Tenor DV01 What it means
2Y bucket +$15K/bp Long duration at the short end (benefits from rate cuts)
5Y bucket −$8K/bp Short duration at 5Y (hedging?)
10Y bucket +$40K/bp Main risk — long 10Y
30Y bucket +$5K/bp Small long-end exposure
Net +$52K/bp Net long duration — loses if yields rise

Why the ladder matters more than total DV01: A portfolio can have DV01 = $0 (duration-neutral) but have +$50K/bp at 2Y and −$50K/bp at 30Y — that's a massive curve steepener bet that nets to zero DV01. The RM must see the LADDER, not just the total.

Key Rate Duration (KRD)

Duration decomposed by tenor point. Instead of one number, you get a vector:

$$\text{KRD}_i = -\frac{1}{P} \frac{\partial P}{\partial y_i}$$

Where $y_i$ is the yield at tenor i. This captures non-parallel yield curve moves — flattening, steepening, butterfly — that parallel duration completely misses.

CS01 (Credit Spread 01)

P&L for a 1bp widening in credit spreads. The credit equivalent of DV01.

$$\text{CS01} \approx \text{Spread Duration} \times \text{MV} / 10{,}000$$

Worked example:
- $20M corporate bond, spread duration = 4.5
- CS01 = 4.5 × $20M / 10,000 = $9,000 per bp of spread widening
- If credit spreads widen 100bp (stress): loss = 100 × $9,000 = −$900,000

Portfolio CS01 by rating bucket:

Rating CS01 Risk contribution
IG (A/BBB) $25K/bp Main exposure — investment-grade credit
HY (BB) $8K/bp Higher-yielding, more volatile
HY (B/CCC) $3K/bp Small but spreads can widen 500bp+ in crisis

CR01 (Credit Rho 01) and JTD (Jump to Default)

Measure Definition When it matters
CR01 P&L for a 1bp parallel shift in the credit curve Standard credit sensitivity
JTD Loss if a single name defaults immediately (recovery assumed at ~40%) Concentration risk — what if our biggest credit position defaults tomorrow?

JTD worked example: $10M notional in Company X CDS (protection buyer). If X defaults: you receive (1 − Recovery) × Notional = 0.60 × $10M = $6M gain. If you're the protection SELLER: you PAY $6M. JTD monitors this single-name concentration.

PART 5: PERFORMANCE & RISK-ADJUSTED METRICS

These metrics evaluate WHETHER the risk being taken is generating adequate return. A PM with high return but higher risk is not necessarily good — and a PM with low return but well-controlled risk might be excellent.

Sharpe Ratio

$$\text{Sharpe} = \frac{R_p - R_f}{\sigma_p}$$

Where:
- $R_p$ = annualized portfolio return
- $R_f$ = risk-free rate (typically the T-bill or OIS rate)
- $\sigma_p$ = annualized portfolio volatility (standard deviation of returns)

The numerator ($R_p - R_f$) is the excess return — what you earned above the risk-free alternative. The denominator is how much risk you took to get it.

Sharpe Assessment Reality check
< 0.3 Poor — not justifying the risk Why is the fund open?
0.3-0.7 Below average Typical for beta-heavy strategies
0.7-1.0 Good Solid risk-adjusted performance
1.0-2.0 Very good Most successful hedge funds live here
2.0-3.0 Exceptional Either genuine edge, short track record, or illiquidity premium
> 3.0 Suspicious Too good — likely short vol/tail risk, illiquidity marks, or data mining

Sharpe Limitations — Why You Can't Trust It Alone

Issue Explanation Example
Symmetric vol penalty Penalizes upside vol equally with downside A fund that makes 50% in one month and 2% in 11 months has "high vol" but spectacular returns
Short-vol gaming Selling OTM options generates steady premium + low vol → high Sharpe — until the blowup Many "Sharpe 2.0+" funds blew up in Feb 2018. Pre-blowup Sharpe was excellent.
Time dependence Sharpe from the last 6 months ≠ Sharpe over 5 years Short-period Sharpe is noisy: √(1/n) standard error
Non-normal returns Sharpe assumes return/risk = mean/stdev, which assumes normality. Skew/kurtosis ignored. A fund with negative skew has worse TRUE risk-adjusted performance than Sharpe suggests
Benchmark-blind Sharpe doesn't tell you if returns come from skill (alpha) or market exposure (beta) A PM with β=1.2 in a bull market has high Sharpe but zero alpha

Statistical significance of Sharpe: To distinguish Sharpe = 1.0 from Sharpe = 0 at 95% confidence, you need:

$$n \geq \left(\frac{1.96}{\text{Sharpe}}\right)^2 = \left(\frac{1.96}{1.0}\right)^2 \approx 4 \text{ years of monthly data}$$

Two years of good performance is NOT enough to conclude skill.

Sortino Ratio

$$\text{Sortino} = \frac{R_p - R_f}{\sigma_{\text{downside}}}$$

Same $R_p$ and $R_f$ as Sharpe, but the denominator is downside deviation — standard deviation computed only from returns BELOW a target (usually 0 or $R_f$). Upside volatility is ignored entirely.

Why Sortino > Sharpe for hedge funds: A fund that generates its return through positively skewed bets (long gamma, event-driven, distressed credit) has LOW downside vol and HIGH upside vol. Sharpe penalizes both. Sortino correctly rewards the upside skew.

RM insight: Compare Sharpe to Sortino. If Sortino >> Sharpe → positive skew (good). If Sortino << Sharpe → NEGATIVE skew (P&L distribution has a fat left tail — investigate).

Maximum Drawdown (MDD)

The largest peak-to-trough decline before a new peak.

$$\text{MDD} = \max_{t \in [0,T]} \left( \max_{s \leq t} V_s - V_t \right) / \max_{s \leq t} V_s$$

Why drawdown matters more than vol for survival:
- A fund with 12% annualized vol can survive indefinitely.
- A fund with a −40% drawdown might not survive — investors redeem, margin calls hit, the PM is fired.
- Drawdowns are path-dependent and NOT captured by VaR/σ. A fund can have low vol and still hit a massive drawdown through a slow grind lower.

The Math of Recovery — Non-Symmetric

Drawdown Return required to recover Time at 10% annual return
−5% +5.3% ~6 months
−10% +11.1% ~1.1 years
−20% +25.0% ~2.3 years
−30% +42.9% ~3.6 years
−50% +100% ~7.3 years
−75% +300% >15 years (fund is dead)

RM implication: This is why drawdown limits are HARD limits. Once you're past −20%, recovery becomes a multi-year project. Past −50%, the fund is effectively done — even if it could recover mathematically, investors will have redeemed.

Calmar Ratio

$$\text{Calmar} = \frac{\text{Annualized return}}{|\text{Max drawdown}|}$$

Return per unit of worst loss. Typical for CTAs and macro funds.

Calmar Assessment
< 0.5 Poor — the drawdown is too large relative to returns
0.5-1.0 Decent
1.0-2.0 Very good
> 2.0 Excellent or too-short track record

Information Ratio (IR)

$$\text{IR} = \frac{R_p - R_b}{\text{Tracking Error}}$$

Alpha divided by tracking error. Measures the CONSISTENCY of outperformance relative to benchmark.

IR Assessment
< 0.3 No distinguishable skill. Could be noise.
0.3-0.5 Some evidence of skill
0.5-1.0 Good — consistent outperformance
> 1.0 Rare. Very high skill or very concentrated bets that happened to work.

Alpha & Beta Decomposition

$$R_p = \alpha + \beta \times R_m + \epsilon$$

Component What it is What to check
β (beta) Market exposure — how much of the return is just riding the index β > 0.3 → significant market dependency. Not pure alpha.
α (alpha) Excess return after removing market exposure This is what justifies management fees. Should be positive and significant.
ε (residual) Idiosyncratic — unexplained by market factor High residual vol = concentrated bets. Check factor exposures.

Multi-factor decomposition (Fama-French, Barra):

$$R_p = \alpha + \beta_1 R_{\text{market}} + \beta_2 R_{\text{size}} + \beta_3 R_{\text{value}} + \beta_4 R_{\text{momentum}} + \epsilon$$

A PM who claims alpha but has β_momentum = 0.8 is just running momentum — the "alpha" is factor exposure, not skill. Real alpha should be the residual after ALL known factor exposures are removed.

Omega Ratio

$$\Omega = \frac{\int_r^\infty (1-F(x)) \, dx}{\int_{-\infty}^r F(x) \, dx}$$

The ratio of gains above a threshold to losses below it. Captures the ENTIRE distribution — no normality assumption. Higher is better. Unlike Sharpe, properly accounts for skew and kurtosis.

PART 6: VaR BACKTESTING — VERIFYING THE RISK MODEL

One-liner: A VaR model that's never tested is just an expensive guess. Backtesting asks: "How many times did actual losses exceed VaR?" and if the answer is too many (or too few), the model is broken.

The Framework

Process: Compare daily realized P&L against the previous day's VaR prediction. Count "exceptions" (days where loss > VaR).

For a 99% VaR model over 250 trading days: expected exceptions = 250 × 0.01 = 2.5 per year.

Kupiec Test (Proportion of Failures)

Tests whether the number of exceptions is statistically consistent with the stated confidence level.

$$LR_{\text{POF}} = -2 \ln \left[ \frac{(1-p)^{n-x} p^x}{(1-x/n)^{n-x} (x/n)^x} \right] \sim \chi^2(1)$$

Where p = expected failure rate (1% for 99% VaR), x = actual exceptions, n = total observations.

Traffic light system (Basel):

Exceptions (250 days, 99% VaR) Zone Consequence
0-4 🟢 Green Model is acceptable
5-9 🟡 Yellow Model under review. Multiplier increases.
10+ 🔴 Red Model rejected. Major multiplier increase or model replacement.

Christoffersen Test (Independence)

Kupiec tests whether exceptions are the RIGHT NUMBER. Christoffersen tests whether they're INDEPENDENT (not clustered).

Why independence matters: If your model has 3 exceptions per year but they ALL occur in the same week, that's worse than 3 exceptions spread across the year — it means your model fails exactly when risk is highest (volatility clustering → model is too slow to adapt).

Conditional Coverage Test

Combines both: correct number AND independent. The joint test is the gold standard for VaR backtesting.

What Backtesting Results Tell You

Result Diagnosis Action
Too FEW exceptions (0 in 2 years) VaR is too conservative — overstating risk Capital inefficiency. PM is being constrained unnecessarily. Recalibrate(lower vol estimates or update correlation).
Correct exceptions (evenly spaced) Model is well-calibrated Continue monitoring.
Too MANY exceptions (10+ per year) VaR underestimates risk — dangerous Investigate: stale correlations? Missing risk factors? Nonlinearity not captured? Increase multiplier or switch methods.
Exceptions clustered Model doesn't adapt to regime changes Switch from equal-weight historical to EWMA/GARCH-weighted. Or use Filtered Historical Simulation.

PART 7: RISK BUDGETING & LIMIT FRAMEWORK

At a fund like Squarepoint, risk measures aren't academic — they're the LIMITS that constrain PMs and protect capital.

The Hierarchy of Limits

Level Limit type Set by Examples
Fund level Total fund VaR, gross/net exposure, drawdown CRO / Board Fund VaR < $15M (99% 1d). Max DD = −15%.
Desk / PM level VaR budget, Greeks limits, concentration Risk team Desk A: VaR ≤ $3M, short gamma < $1M/1%, max single-name = 5% of NAV
Position level Size limits, liquidity thresholds Risk team + compliance No single equity > 3% of ADV. No illiquid bond > $5M notional.
Factor level Factor exposure limits Risk team Beta < ±0.3. Sector concentration < 20%. Duration < ±2Y.

Risk Budget Allocation

$$\text{Fund VaR budget} = \sum_i \text{Desk}_i \text{ VaR allocation} + \text{Reserve}$$

Example:
- Fund 99% 1-day VaR budget: $10M
- Desk A (equity vol): $4M (40%)
- Desk B (macro rates): $3M (30%)
- Desk C (credit): $2M (20%)
- Reserve / unallocated: $1M (10%)

Key concepts:
- Utilization: Desk A running $3.2M actual VaR on $4M budget = 80% utilization. Normal.
- Over-utilization: Running $4.5M on $4M budget = 112%. Requires risk approval or position reduction.
- Correlation benefit: If desks are uncorrelated, total fund VaR < sum of desk VaRs. This is the diversification benefit — the reserve accounts for the possibility that correlations spike.

Drawdown Limits — Hard and Soft

Limit Level Action
Soft drawdown (−5%) Warning PM briefs IC. Risk reviews positions. No forced action yet.
Hard drawdown (−10%) Action required Risk budget cut by 50%. PM must present recovery plan. IC approval for any new trades.
Kill switch (−15%) Book liquidation Systematic position reduction. PM may be suspended. Book handed to risk desk for orderly unwind.

Drawdown measurement: Typically measured from high-water mark on a rolling 12-month basis. Intraday marks may apply for systematic strategies.

Marginal Contribution to Risk — Capital Allocation

$$\text{MCR}i = \frac{\partial \text{VaR}{\text{fund}}}{\partial w_i}$$

Tells you: "If Desk A gets $1M more capital, how much does fund VaR increase?" Used for:
- Capital allocation decisions — allocate more to desks with low MCR (they add return efficiently relative to risk)
- Performance evaluation — RAROC (Risk-Adjusted Return On Capital) = Return / Economic Capital consumed

$$\text{RAROC}_i = \frac{\text{Desk}_i \text{ P\&L}}{\text{Desk}_i \text{ VaR allocation}}$$

PART 8: LIQUIDITY RISK MEASUREMENT

One-liner: A position isn't just risky because it can lose value — it's risky because you might not be able to EXIT when you need to.

Liquidity-Adjusted VaR (LVaR)

Standard VaR assumes you can close positions at mid-price. Reality: you close at bid (selling) or ask (buying), and the spread widens in stress.

$$\text{LVaR} = \text{VaR} + \text{Liquidation Cost}$$

Where:
$$\text{Liquidation Cost} = \frac{1}{2} \sum_i \text{Spread}_i \times \text{Position Size}_i \times \text{Stress Multiplier}_i$$

Worked example:
- Standard VaR = $5M
- Portfolio has $200M notional with average normal spread of 5bp, stress spread 25bp
- Normal liquidation cost = ½ × 0.0005 × $200M = $50K (negligible)
- Stress liquidation cost = ½ × 0.0025 × $200M = $250K
- LVaR = $5M + $250K = $5.25M

For ILLIQUID positions (HY bonds, small-cap, EM): stress spreads can be 200bp+ → liquidation cost dominates.

Days to Liquidate

$$\text{DTL} = \frac{\text{Position Size}}{\text{ADV} \times \text{Participation Rate}}$$

Participation rate Meaning Typical
10% of ADV Conservative — minimal market impact Standard assumption
25% of ADV Moderate Acceptable for liquid names
50%+ of ADV Aggressive — significant market impact Emergency only

Worked example:
- Hold $20M of stock X, ADV = $5M, 10% participation
- DTL = $20M / ($5M × 0.10) = 40 days
- Your 1-day VaR assumes you can exit in 1 day. You can't. Liquidity-adjusted horizon should be 40 days. VaR should be scaled: VaR_40d ≈ VaR_1d × √40 = 6.3× larger.

Liquidity Tiering

Tier Liquidity profile DTL VaR treatment
1 (Liquid) ADV > $50M, tight spreads, deep book < 3 days Standard 1-day VaR
2 (Semi-liquid) ADV $5-50M, moderate spreads 3-10 days Apply √DTL scaling
3 (Illiquid) ADV < $5M, wide spreads, thin book 10-30 days Apply √DTL + stress spread + redemption gate consideration
4 (Very illiquid) Bespoke OTC, no secondary market 30+ days or impossible Mark uncertainty. Full position size at risk in stress. May need side-pocket.

PART 9: MODEL RISK & META-RISK

One-liner: Every risk measure is the output of a model. If the model is wrong, the risk measure is worse than useless — it creates false confidence.

Sources of Model Risk

Source What goes wrong Impact on risk
Wrong distribution Assumed normal, actual is fat-tailed VaR underestimates tail risk by 2-5×
Stale parameters Correlations estimated from calm period used in crisis Diversification benefit evaporates — actual risk much higher
Missing risk factors Model has 5 factors, but the 6th (liquidity, correlation, convexity) is the one that kills you "Unknown unknowns" — risk model shows green while the portfolio bleeds
Wrong pricing model Options priced with BS (constant vol), actual dynamics are stochastic vol Greeks are wrong → hedges are wrong → P&L leaks
Overfitting Model calibrated to fit history perfectly, fails on new data Backtests look perfect, live performance is terrible
Implementation errors Bugs in risk system, data feed errors, stale prices "Garbage in, garbage out" — most common source of risk model failure

Model Validation Framework

Check What you do Frequency
Backtesting Compare VaR predictions to realized P&L Daily (automated)
Benchmarking Compare internal model to external/vendor models Quarterly
Sensitivity analysis Perturb model inputs — does output change reasonably? Quarterly or on model change
Challenger model Run a completely different model in parallel Continuous
P&L explain Compare model-predicted P&L (from Greeks) to actual P&L Daily
Expert review Quants review model assumptions, limitations, edge cases Annual or on model change

P&L explain as model validation: If your model predicts today's P&L should be −$2.3M (from delta + gamma + vega + theta) but actual is −$4.1M, the −$1.8M "unexplained" is model error. Persistent unexplained P&L = model is wrong. Investigate.

PART 10: INTERVIEW APPLICATION

"What are the limitations of VaR?"

"VaR is a useful speedometer but a terrible crash detector. Five key limitations: First, it tells you nothing about losses BEYOND the threshold — a $5M VaR could be hiding $50M of tail risk, and you'd only know from Expected Shortfall or stress testing. Second, it's computed from historical data under normal conditions — but the distribution changes in a crisis: correlations spike, liquidity vanishes, and your calm-period covariance matrix is wrong. Third, it's not subadditive — combining portfolios can produce higher VaR than the sum, which violates basic intuition and makes it unreliable for capital allocation. Fourth, it's gameable — selling deep OTM options generates premium income with low measured VaR while embedding massive tail risk. Finally, it's a point-in-time snapshot at market close that misses intraday risk.

That's why I'd always supplement VaR with ES for tail severity, stress tests for extreme scenarios, and the ES/VaR ratio as a quick diagnostic — if that ratio exceeds 3, there's a concave payoff hiding in the book."

"How would you set up a stress testing framework?"

"Three layers. Historical stress tests — replay 2008, COVID, Volmageddon with current positions — because you need to know if the portfolio survives past crises. Hypothetical scenarios — events that haven't happened, like a Taiwan conflict or US Treasury auction failure — because the next crisis won't look like the last one. And sensitivity grids — systematic factor bumps across equity × vol × rates dimensions — to map out the P&L heat map and find exactly where the portfolio breaks.

I'd add a fourth: reverse stress testing. Start from the loss threshold — say our drawdown limit is $30M — and work backward to find the minimum-severity scenario that causes it. If that scenario is plausible, we have an actionable problem to address TODAY, not during the crisis."

"Walk me through the Sharpe ratio and its problems":

"Sharpe is excess return over risk-free divided by vol. It's the standard risk-adjusted performance metric — most successful hedge funds are in the 0.7-2.0 range. But it has three fatal flaws for evaluating hedge fund strategies.

First, it treats upside and downside vol equally, so a positively skewed fund is penalized for its best feature. The Sortino ratio fixes this by only measuring downside deviation. Second, it can be gamed — any strategy that sells tail risk generates steady premium with low measured vol, producing Sharpe 2.0+ until the tail event arrives and it goes to zero. I'd check Sortino/Sharpe ratio: if Sortino is much lower than Sharpe, the fund has hidden negative skew. Third, short-period Sharpe is statistically noisy — you need ~4 years of monthly data to distinguish Sharpe 1.0 from zero at 95% confidence. Two quarters of good performance proves nothing."

"Explain the difference between VaR, ES, and stress testing":

"These three form a risk measurement pyramid. VaR tells you the boundary of normal losses — 'on 99 out of 100 days, you won't lose more than $5M.' It's the daily speedometer. ES tells you what happens when you crash — 'when you DO breach VaR, the average loss is $12M.' It captures tail severity and is what FRTB is moving toward for banks. Stress testing asks 'what if the road disappears entirely?' — you define specific extreme scenarios and measure the impact directly, without relying on historical distributions.

A complete risk framework uses all three: VaR for daily monitoring and limit-setting, ES for tail adequacy and identifying concave payoffs, and stress tests for the extreme scenarios that the statistical models can't capture. If I had to choose one, I'd choose stress testing — it's the only one that works in a true crisis, because it doesn't depend on the past looking like the future."
