D — GREEKS: THE LANGUAGE OF DERIVATIVES RISK

First-order, second-order, cross-Greeks, portfolio Greeks — everything that drives options P&L

Depth: 🔴 DEEP

WHY YOU NEED THIS COLD

At a systematic-at-its-core fund like Squarepoint, Greeks aren't abstract theory — they're the operating system. Every risk report, every morning call, every P&L decomposition, every limit breach speaks in Greeks. When a PM says "I'm long 500 gamma at the 6400 strike with $150K vega and bleeding $40K theta/day," you need to IMMEDIATELY understand:
- The dollar P&L for a 2% move in either direction
- How the profile changes if vol spikes 5 points
- Whether the gamma-theta ratio is efficient or they're overpaying
- Where the tail risk lives

Greeks ARE the risk. Delta tells you WHERE you are. Gamma tells you WHERE YOU'LL BE. Vega tells you WHAT HAPPENS IF FEAR ARRIVES. Theta tells you THE DAILY COST. Learn them like your own reflexes.

PART 1: DELTA (Δ) — DIRECTIONAL EXPOSURE

One-liner: Delta is how many dollars you make or lose for each $1 the underlying moves — it's your effective stock-equivalent position.

Analogy: Delta is a volume knob. It controls how loud the stock's moves play through to your P&L. Deep ITM (Δ=0.95) = cranked to 10, you hear everything — the option moves nearly dollar-for-dollar. ATM (Δ=0.50) = halfway, you catch half the signal. Deep OTM (Δ=0.05) = turned almost off, the stock is screaming but your option barely whispers. And the knob isn't fixed — gamma is the hand that keeps turning it.

What Delta Measures

The first derivative of option price with respect to the underlying:

$$\Delta = \frac{\partial V}{\partial S}$$

Option Delta range ATM Deep ITM Deep OTM
Call 0 to +1 ≈ +0.50 → +1.00 (behaves like stock) → 0 (nearly worthless)
Put −1 to 0 ≈ −0.50 → −1.00 (behaves like short stock) → 0 (nearly worthless)

Delta in Practice — Dollar Delta

The number that matters is dollar delta, not option delta.

$$\text{Dollar delta} = \Delta \times \text{contracts} \times \text{multiplier} \times S$$

Worked example: You own 200 SPX calls, delta = 0.45, multiplier = 100, SPX = 5,400
- Dollar delta = 0.45 × 200 × 100 × $5,400 = $48.6M
- That's your effective equity exposure — you're behaving as if you own $48.6M of SPX

Portfolio delta = Σ of all position dollar deltas. A book with +$60M call delta and −$35M put delta = +$25M net delta (net long, gains if market rises).

Delta as Probability (Rough Approximation)

Delta ≈ risk-neutral probability the option expires ITM. A 0.25 delta call ≈ 25% chance of finishing ITM. NOT a real-world probability (it's risk-neutral), but useful intuition for sizing and discussion.

Delta Hedging

One-liner: Delta hedging removes directional risk, leaving your P&L driven by gamma, vega, and theta — the "pure Greeks" that options traders actually trade.

Process: If you're long $25M dollar delta → short $25M of the underlying (sell stock or sell futures). Now you're "delta-neutral" — a small move up or down has near-zero P&L. But:
- Your delta changes after the move (because of gamma)
- You need to re-hedge → "dynamic hedging"
- How often you re-hedge determines your realized gamma P&L vs theta cost

Hedging frequency tradeoff:
- Hedge every tick → perfectly capture gamma, but massive transaction costs (bid-ask × volume)
- Hedge once a day → lower cost, but miss intraday moves and leave delta drift
- Optimal frequency depends on: gamma size, spread costs, realized vol, how much variance you need to capture

What Moves Delta (Delta Sensitivity)

Factor Effect on call delta Effect on put delta
Spot rises Delta increases (→ 1) Delta increases (→ 0, less negative)
Spot falls Delta decreases (→ 0) Delta decreases (→ −1, more negative)
Vol rises OTM deltas increase, ITM deltas decrease (smiling toward 0.50) Same — deltas compress toward ±0.50
Vol falls OTM deltas decrease, ITM deltas increase (polarize toward 0 or 1) Same — deltas polarize
Time passes OTM deltas decrease, ITM deltas increase (same polarization as lower vol) Same

Key insight: High vol and long time to expiry make delta LESS extreme (everything gravitates toward 0.50). Low vol and near expiry make delta BINARY (near 0 or near 1). This is why near-expiry ATM options have explosive delta moves — they can swing from 0.50 to 0.95 in an hour.

PART 2: GAMMA (Γ) — THE MOST IMPORTANT GREEK FOR RISK

One-liner: Gamma is how fast your delta changes — it's the acceleration of your P&L, and it determines whether big moves help or destroy you.

What Gamma Measures

The second derivative of option price with respect to the underlying:

$$\Gamma = \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2} = \frac{\partial \Delta}{\partial S}$$

Analogy — the airbag: Gamma is the airbag in your car. When you're long gamma, the airbag deploys on impact — it cushions you, absorbs the shock, and can even turn crashes into profit (P&L ∝ move²). When you're short gamma, the airbag has been ripped out — any collision accelerates the damage, and the harder you crash, the worse it gets. That's why gamma is THE #1 Greek for risk managers: it's the cushion that either protects you or exposes you.

Second analogy — speed vs acceleration: Delta is your speed. Gamma is your acceleration. Long gamma = you accelerate in the right direction (speeding up when markets move). Short gamma = you accelerate in the WRONG direction (losses get faster and faster).

Dollar Gamma — The Number You Actually Use

Dollar gamma = P&L for a 1% move in the underlying (attributed to gamma alone):

$$\text{Dollar gamma P\&L} = \frac{1}{2} \times \Gamma_\$ \times (\%\Delta S)^2$$

Where:
$$\Gamma_\$ = \Gamma \times S^2 \times \text{contracts} \times \text{multiplier} / 100$$

Worked example: You own 100 ATM SPX calls.
- Γ (per option) = 0.0004, S = 5,400, multiplier = 100
- Dollar gamma = 0.0004 × 5,400² × 100 × 100 / 100 = $1,166,400 per 1% move
- If SPX drops 3%: Gamma P&L = ½ × $1.17M × 3² = +$5.25M (long gamma → you gain from big moves, either direction)
- If SPX drops 3% and you were SHORT those same options: −$5.25M (loss ACCELERATES)

The asymmetry that kills:
- Long gamma: P&L ∝ move² → ALWAYS positive for big moves regardless of direction. Small moves → small gains. Big moves → big gains. Convex.
- Short gamma: P&L ∝ −move² → ALWAYS negative for big moves. Small moves → small losses (offset by theta). Big moves → catastrophic. Concave.

Where Gamma Is Highest

Factor Gamma effect Why
ATM options Maximum gamma Delta is most uncertain (0.50). Tiny move determines ITM/OTM.
Near expiry Very high gamma Less time → delta must resolve to 0 or 1 FAST. ATM + near expiry = explosive.
Low vol Higher gamma (ATM) Lower vol concentrates the probability around current price → delta is more sensitive.
OTM/ITM options Low gamma Delta is already near 0 or 1 — not much room to change.

0DTE (zero-days-to-expiry) gamma: SPX 0DTE options now account for ~50% of SPX options volume. Their gamma is ENORMOUS — delta can flip from 0.10 to 0.90 in 30 minutes. This creates intraday volatility that didn't exist before 2022. Dealers hedging 0DTE gamma create violent intraday swings.

Gamma Scalping — How You Monetize Long Gamma

Start: Delta-neutral, long gamma

Market rises 1%:
  → Delta increases (long gamma → you're now net LONG)
  → SELL to re-hedge back to delta-neutral
  → Lock in profit on the move

Market falls 1%:
  → Delta decreases (you're now net SHORT)
  → BUY to re-hedge back to delta-neutral
  → Lock in profit on the move

Net result: BUY LOW, SELL HIGH automatically
But: paying theta every day for this privilege

Break-even realized vol: For a gamma scalper:

$$\text{Daily gamma P\&L} = \frac{1}{2} \times \Gamma_\$ \times (\text{daily realized return})^2$$

$$\text{Break-even: when } \sigma_{\text{realized}} = \sigma_{\text{implied}}$$

If you bought options at 20% implied vol, you need the market to actually realize 20% vol to break even. If it only realizes 15%, your theta costs exceed your gamma gains → net loss.

Pin Risk — Where Gamma Becomes Dangerous

One-liner: Pin risk is the nightmare scenario where the underlying closes RIGHT at the strike on expiry day — you don't know if your options will be exercised or not.

At expiry, an ATM option's gamma goes to infinity theoretically. Delta oscillates between 0 and 1 with tiny price moves. If you're short 10,000 ATM calls expiring today and the stock is at $100.01:
- Are they exercised? → You're short 1M shares at the close
- Stock drops to $99.99 → NOT exercised → You have zero position
- The difference between $100.01 and $99.99 is $0.02 on the stock, but the difference in your position is $100M of risk

This is why risk managers watch OpEx dates closely and require reduced positions into expiry.

PART 3: THETA (Θ) — THE COST OF OPTIONALITY

One-liner: Theta is the daily rent you pay to hold an option — every day the clock ticks, your option loses time value.

Analogy: Theta is a parking meter running. Every day costs you coins, and you can't pause it. Far from expiry, the meter ticks slowly — a few cents a day. Near expiry, it's eating quarters every hour. The critical question: are you feeding the meter (long options — paying rent and hoping the move comes before time runs out) or do you OWN the meter (short options — collecting coins from everyone else's ticking clock)?

What Theta Measures

The first derivative of option price with respect to time:

$$\Theta = \frac{\partial V}{\partial t}$$

  • Long options: Theta is NEGATIVE. You PAY rent. The option decays.
  • Short options: Theta is POSITIVE. You EARN rent. Time decay works for you.

Theta in Dollars

$$\text{Daily theta} = \Theta \times \text{contracts} \times \text{multiplier}$$

Worked example: You own 150 SPX puts with θ = −$3.20/day per option, multiplier = 100.
- Daily theta cost = −$3.20 × 150 × 100 = −$48,000/day
- Monthly cost = −$1.04M (22 trading days)
- Your puts need to gain $1M+ from delta/gamma/vega just to break even each month

Theta Decay Is NOT Linear

$$\Theta \propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{T}}$$

Days to expiry Relative daily decay Behavior
90 days 1x (slow) Gentle bleed — barely noticeable day-to-day
30 days 1.7x Accelerating — weekly check-ins matter
7 days 3.6x Fast — meaningful daily P&L from time alone
1 day 9.5x Extreme — ATM options can lose 30-50% of remaining value in the last day

The square root rule: An option with 64 days has roughly double the time value of an option with 16 days (√64/√16 = 2). Not 4x, even though it has 4x the time.

Weekend and Holiday Theta

Options lose value over weekends and holidays (2-3 calendar days of theta for a 1-day hold over weekend). This means:
- Friday close: Long options lose extra theta. Short option sellers receive it.
- Holiday weeks: 3-day weekends → accelerated decay. PMs who are long gamma sometimes reduce before long weekends to avoid paying 3 days of theta for 1 day of possible movement.
- IV adjustment: Some of this is priced in — Friday IV may be slightly lower than Monday IV, meaning the market partially prices the weekend decay into vol.

The Gamma-Theta Tradeoff — THE Fundamental Relationship

There is NO free lunch. You CANNOT have positive gamma AND positive theta simultaneously with vanilla options.

Profile Gamma Theta P&L shape Who does this
Long gamma, short theta + − Many small losses, occasional big wins Vol buyers, hedgers, gamma scalpers
Short gamma, long theta − + Many small wins, occasional BIG losses Market makers, premium sellers, income strategies

The efficiency metric — Gamma/Theta ratio:

$$\text{Gamma/Theta} = \frac{\Gamma_\$}{\Theta_\$}$$

  • High ratio: You're getting a lot of gamma per dollar of theta paid. Good deal — cheap optionality.
  • Low ratio: You're overpaying for gamma. Time decay will eat you alive before you can scalp enough.

When is gamma cheap? Low implied vol (market complacent). When is it expensive? High implied vol (post-crash, panic). The paradox: gamma is cheapest when you don't feel like you need it and most expensive when you're desperate for it.

Interview line: "Long gamma means your delta automatically adjusts in your favor — you're protected against big moves — but you pay theta every day for that protection. Short gamma is the opposite: you earn rent daily but you're writing insurance against tail events. The RM's job is to make sure the PM is aware of which regime they're in and whether the gamma/theta tradeoff is efficient — are we paying a fair price for the optionality, or are we overpaying because vol is expensive?"

PART 4: VEGA (ν) — EXPOSURE TO VOLATILITY ITSELF

One-liner: Vega is how much your option's price changes when implied vol moves by one point — it's your bet on the market's fear level.

Analogy: Vega is your bet on the weather forecast, not the weather itself. You don't need the storm to actually hit — you just need the forecast to get scarier (IV up) and your options gain value. Long vega profits from increasing uncertainty, not from the event itself. Short vega is betting the forecast stays calm. That's why you can buy straddles before CPI and LOSE even if CPI moves the market — if the forecast (IV) was already pricing a hurricane and the actual storm was a drizzle, the forecast improves (IV crushes) and you're underwater.

What Vega Measures

$$\nu = \frac{\partial V}{\partial \sigma}$$

  • Long options (bought): Positive vega. Vol up → you gain. Vol down → you lose.
  • Short options (sold): Negative vega. Vol up → you lose. Vol down → you gain.

Dollar Vega

$$\text{Dollar vega} = \nu \times \text{contracts} \times \text{multiplier}$$

Worked example: Portfolio has $300K vega (sum of all positions).
- Vol rises 3 points: P&L = +$300K × 3 = +$900K
- Vol drops 2 points: P&L = −$300K × 2 = −$600K
- VIX spike from 15 to 40 (25 pts): P&L = +$300K × 25 = +$7.5M (if you're long vega, this is your payday)

Where Vega Lives

Factor Vega effect Why
ATM options Maximum vega Highest uncertainty → most sensitive to vol changes
Long-dated options Much higher vega More time for vol to compound → vol matters more
Near-expiry options Very low vega So little time left, vol changes barely matter
OTM/ITM options Lower vega Less uncertainty about ITM/OTM outcome

Key relationship: Vega ∝ √T. A 1-year option has ~2x the vega of a 3-month option (√12/√3 ≈ 2). This means:
- Long-dated options are your main vega exposure
- Near-dated options contribute gamma but little vega
- A portfolio with long near-dated + short far-dated can be long gamma but short vega → different risk profiles at different horizons

Vega Bucketing — What Sophisticated Desks Do

At Squarepoint's level, "total vega" is not enough. You bucket vega by tenor:

Bucket What's in it What drives it
0-30 day vega Near-dated options Event risk, realized vol, gamma scalping P&L
30-90 day vega Medium-dated Earnings cycles, vol term structure rolls
90-365 day vega Far-dated Structural vol regime, macro uncertainty
1Y+ vega LEAPS, long-dated structures Monetary policy regime, secular vol trends

Why it matters: You can be net FLAT total vega but LONG 1-month vega and SHORT 6-month vega. That's a trade on the vol term structure — you're betting near-term vol is cheap relative to far-term vol. Very different risk from being outright long or short vol.

Vol-Spot Correlation — Why Vega and Delta Compound in Crashes

One-liner: Vol spikes when markets drop. If you're short vega AND long delta, a sell-off hits you on BOTH dimensions simultaneously.

Empirically:
- SPX drops 1% → VIX rises ~2-4 points (on average)
- SPX drops 5% → VIX rises ~15-25 points
- This is NOT symmetric: SPX rallies 5% → VIX drops ~5-8 points

Consequence: Short vega is correlated with long delta. Both hurt in a sell-off. A portfolio that's "+$20M delta, −$300K vega" has compounding downside: the 5% drop costs you $1M on delta, and the associated 20-point vol spike costs you $6M on vega. Total loss = $7M, not $1M.

PART 5: RHO (ρ) — INTEREST RATE SENSITIVITY

One-liner: Rho measures how much an option's price changes for a 1% shift in rates — usually minor for short-dated equity options, critical for rates derivatives and LEAPS.

Analogy: Rho is the current in a river. On a short kayak trip (weekly options), you barely notice it — paddle a few strokes and you're done. On a multi-day expedition (LEAPS, swaptions), that same current carries you miles off course. In the zero-rate era (2010–2021), the river was a dead canal — rho was irrelevant. At 4%+ rates (2023–2026), there's a real current, and if you're floating long-dated positions, you'd better know which way it's pulling.

$$\rho = \frac{\partial V}{\partial r}$$

Option Rho sign Why When it matters
Long call Positive Higher rates → higher forward → calls worth more LEAPS, FX options, swaptions
Long put Negative Higher rates → higher forward → puts worth less LEAPS, FX options
Short tenors Negligible Rate change × short time = tiny impact Not a concern for weekly/monthly options

Current environment (2026): With rates at 4%+, rho is more material than during the zero-rate era. A 1-year LEAPS call with $10K rho loses $10K if rates fall 1% — not negligible for large positions.

PART 6: GREEKS BY POSITION TYPE — THE REFERENCE TABLE

Know this cold. For EVERY option position, know every Greek sign and behavior:

Gamma (Γ) + (always) − (always) + (always) − (always)
Theta (Θ) − (pay rent) + (earn rent) − (pay rent) + (earn rent)
Vega (ν) + (want vol up) − (want vol down) + (want vol up) − (want vol down)

The universal pattern:
- LONG any option → always +Γ, +ν, −Θ
- SHORT any option → always −Γ, −ν, +Θ
- The ONLY difference between calls and puts: sign of delta and rho

PART 7: SECOND-ORDER GREEKS — THE DERIVATIVES OF DERIVATIVES

These measure how first-order Greeks change. You won't price with these, but you need to know what they do because they explain why your risk SHIFTS in a crisis.

Vanna — How Delta Changes with Vol

One-liner: Vanna is how your directional exposure shape-shifts when fear arrives — it's why your hedges get stronger (or weaker) exactly when it matters most.

$$\text{Vanna} = \frac{\partial \Delta}{\partial \sigma} = \frac{\partial \nu}{\partial S}$$

In plain English: When vol spikes, how does your delta move?

Why it matters in a crisis:
- You hold OTM puts as hedges (delta = −0.20). Vol spikes 15 points → vanna causes delta to shift toward −0.55. Your puts now hedge 2.5x more of your portfolio.
- This is GOOD — it's built-in tail protection. Your hedges become more effective exactly when you need them.
- But if you're long OTM calls, a vol spike pushes their delta TOWARD 0.50 — your calls become "less OTM" in delta terms, even if the stock hasn't moved.

Vanna flows (dealer impact): Dealers short OTM puts → they have negative vanna exposure. Vol spikes → their delta shifts (they become shorter delta) → they need to BUY to re-hedge → supportive for the market. Vol drops → their delta shifts the other way → they SELL → adds selling pressure. These systematic "vanna flows" are a major driver of daily market mechanics.

Volga (Vomma) — How Vega Changes with Vol

One-liner: Volga is the convexity of your vol bet — it tells you whether a vol spike makes things progressively better or progressively worse for your book.

$$\text{Volga} = \frac{\partial \nu}{\partial \sigma} = \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial \sigma^2}$$

In plain English: When vol goes up, does your vega increase or decrease?

Position Volga What it means
OTM options (wings) Positive As vol rises, your vega INCREASES → you become MORE long vol in a crisis. Convexity of vol. Tail protection.
ATM options Near zero Vega doesn't change much with vol. Linear vol exposure.

For the RM: A portfolio that's long volga has built-in vol tail protection — it becomes increasingly hedged as vol rises. Short volga is the opposite: your vega exposure grows against you as conditions deteriorate. This is why selling OTM puts (wings) is particularly dangerous — you're short volga, so as vol spikes, your vega loss ACCELERATES.

Charm (Delta Bleed) — How Delta Changes with Time

One-liner: Charm is the silent thief — it steals your delta hedge overnight, so you wake up unhedged even though nothing moved.

$$\text{Charm} = \frac{\partial \Delta}{\partial t} = \frac{\partial \Theta}{\partial S}$$

In plain English: How much does your delta drift overnight just from one day passing?

Why it matters:
- A PM delta-hedges perfectly on Friday. Over the weekend, 2 days pass. Charm shifts delta. Monday morning, they're no longer hedged — even though the stock opened unchanged.
- Near-expiry OTM options: charm pulls delta toward 0 (losing delta). Near-expiry ITM options: charm pushes delta toward ±1. This delta drift is systematic and needs to be accounted for.
- 0DTE positions: Charm is enormous. Delta changes continuously just from the clock ticking.

Speed — How Gamma Changes with Spot

One-liner: Speed tells you whether your gamma itself is stable or about to shift — it's the gamma of gamma.

$$\text{Speed} = \frac{\partial \Gamma}{\partial S}$$

Tells you: if the market moves, does your gamma exposure increase or decrease? Important for:
- Barrier options (gamma can jump discontinuously near the barrier)
- Large gamma positions where the gamma itself is unstable
- Understanding whether your risk is linear in gamma or gamma itself is changing

Color — How Gamma Changes with Time

One-liner: Color is gamma's clock — it measures how fast your gamma concentrates as expiry approaches.

$$\text{Color} = \frac{\partial \Gamma}{\partial t}$$

Gamma increases as options approach expiry (for ATM options). Color tells you the rate. Important for understanding how your gamma exposure evolves day-to-day, especially in the final week.

Summary of Second-Order Greeks

Greek What it measures Key insight
Vanna ∂Δ/∂σ How your delta shifts when vol moves. Drives dealer hedging flows.
Volga ∂ν/∂σ How your vega changes when vol moves. Long volga = tail protection.
Charm ∂Δ/∂t How delta drifts overnight. Critical near expiry and over weekends.
Speed ∂Γ/∂S How gamma changes with spot. Important near barriers.
Color ∂Γ/∂t How gamma evolves with time. Gamma spikes into expiry.

PART 8: PORTFOLIO GREEKS — THE RISK MANAGER'S VIEW

Aggregation

All Greeks are additive across positions:

$$\text{Portfolio } \Delta = \sum_{i} \Delta_i \times n_i \times m_i$$

Same for Γ, Θ, ν. Sum them across every position to get the book-level Greeks.

The Greeks Dashboard — What You Monitor Daily

Greek What you're checking How it's reported Red flag
Net delta ($) Directional exposure $M equivalent Exceeds mandate/risk budget (e.g., ±$30M limit)
Dollar gamma (1%) P&L for a 1% move (from gamma alone) $K or $M per 1% Large short gamma near current spot → danger zone
Theta ($/day) Daily time decay cost or income $/day Theta cost exceeding realistic gamma capture expectations
Vega ($/vol pt) Exposure to IV change $K per 1 vol point Large short vega in low-vol environment (complacency trap)
Vega by tenor Bucketed: 0-30d, 30-90d, 90d+ $K per bucket Mismatches between tenor buckets (long front, short back = vol curve bet)
Gamma/theta Efficiency of optionality spend Ratio Low ratio = overpaying for gamma. PM bleeding unnecessarily.
Vanna exposure Delta sensitivity to vol $M delta shift per vol pt Large vanna → delta will shift violently in a vol spike. Need to anticipate.

Greek Limits — How an RM Sets Them

At a fund like Squarepoint, each PM desk has Greek limits:

Limit Typical constraint Why
Delta limit ±$50M per desk Caps directional exposure. Prevents a single PM from betting the fund.
Gamma limit Short gamma < $X per 1% Caps convexity risk. Limits how much losses can accelerate.
Vega limit ±$Y per vol point Caps vol exposure. Prevents one vol spike from wiping a desk.
Theta limit −$Z/day max spend Prevents excessive bleed from buying overpriced optionality.
Concentration limit Max % of Greeks from a single name/strike Prevents pin risk and single-name gamma concentration.
Expiry limit Max gamma exposure within 5 days of expiry Controls pin risk and explosive near-expiry gamma.

The aggregation challenge: PM A is long gamma, PM B is short gamma. At the PORTFOLIO level, they may offset. But that offset only works if both positions are on the SAME underlying at the SAME strike. A systematic RM system needs to calculate Greeks at the portfolio level AND flag concentrated risks at the position level.

Long Gamma vs Short Gamma — The Risk Manager's Decision Framework

Dimension Long Gamma Book Short Gamma Book
Daily P&L Volatile — swings with realized vol Smooth — steady theta income
Tail risk Protected — gains from big moves Exposed — large moves cause accelerating losses
P&L distribution Negative skew: many small losses, occasional big wins Positive skew: many small wins, occasional BIG losses
Regime preference High realized vol Low realized vol
What can kill it Prolonged low vol → theta bleed exhausts capital Single large move → losses exceed all prior theta income
RM monitoring Is theta cost sustainable? How long can we bleed? What's worst-case on a 3σ/5σ day? Can the fund survive it?

PART 9: GREEKS IN ACTION — SCENARIO ANALYSIS

Scenario 1: Market Drops 5%, Vol Spikes 12 Points

Portfolio:
- Net delta: +$30M (long equities)
- Dollar gamma: −$800K per 1% (short gamma — sold puts for income)
- Vega: −$250K per vol point (short vega from the same sold puts)
- Theta: +$45K/day (earning from the short premium)

Decomposition:

Component Calculation P&L
Delta +$30M × −5% −$1,500,000
Gamma ½ × (−$800K) × 5² −$10,000,000
Vega −$250K × 12 pts −$3,000,000
Theta +$45K × 1 day +$45,000
TOTAL −$14,455,000

The PM thought: "I'm only $30M long delta. A 5% drop costs me $1.5M. Manageable."
Reality: Short gamma turned a $1.5M delta loss into $11.5M. Short vega added another $3M. Total: $14.5M — nearly 10x the naïve delta-only estimate.

What the RM should have flagged BEFORE:
- "Your gamma profile means a 5% move costs $10M+ beyond your delta loss"
- "Your vega is correlated with your delta — a sell-off creates compounding losses"
- "Your P&L profile is CONCAVE — you lose more than linearly in tail events"
- "Stress scenario: SPX −5%, VIX +12 → $14.5M loss. Is that within your book's risk budget?"

Scenario 2: Market Grinds Sideways for 30 Days

Same portfolio as above:

Component Calculation P&L
Delta SPX ±0% $0
Gamma No moves → no gamma P&L $0
Vega Vol drops 2 pts (complacency) +$500,000
Theta +$45K × 22 trading days +$990,000
TOTAL +$1,490,000

This is the seduction of short gamma. In calm months, you earn $1.5M for "doing nothing." It FEELS like free money. But one bad month (Scenario 1) takes away 10 months of income. This is the classic premium-seller distribution — steady, steady, steady, CRASH.

Scenario 3: The Vol Crush After an Event

PM bought straddles ahead of CPI:
- Gamma: +$500K per 1%
- Vega: +$400K per vol point
- Theta: −$80K/day

CPI comes in line with expectations. Market moves 0.3%. Vol drops 4 points.

Component Calculation P&L
Delta Straddle is delta-neutral → $0 $0
Gamma ½ × $500K × 0.3² = tiny +$225
Vega +$400K × (−4) −$1,600,000
Theta −$80K × 1 day −$80,000
TOTAL −$1,679,775

The PM was right about CPI creating a move — but the move was too small to cover the vol crush. The pre-event vol premium collapsed, and the gamma gain on a 0.3% move was negligible. This is the "vol event" trap: you need the event to produce a move large enough to overcome the IV crush. If IV was 25% pre-CPI and realized vol on the day was 10%, you overpaid.

PART 10: THE GREEKS SURFACE — HOW GREEKS CHANGE ACROSS STRIKE AND EXPIRY

Understanding WHERE Greeks are highest helps with position construction:

                    GAMMA SURFACE

       Short expiry  ┃  Long expiry
                     ┃
  OTM    ▪           ┃    ·            OTM
         ▪▪          ┃    ··
  ATM    ▪▪▪▪▪▪      ┃    ···          ATM     ← Gamma peaks ATM, spikes near expiry
         ▪▪          ┃    ··
  OTM    ▪           ┃    ·            OTM
                     ┃
         CONCENTRATED      SPREAD OUT
         (explosive)       (gentle)

                    VEGA SURFACE

       Short expiry  ┃  Long expiry
                     ┃
  OTM    ·           ┃    ▪            OTM
         ·           ┃    ▪▪
  ATM    ··          ┃    ▪▪▪▪▪▪       ATM     ← Vega peaks ATM, grows with time
         ·           ┃    ▪▪
  OTM    ·           ┃    ▪            OTM
                     ┃
         LOW VEGA          HIGH VEGA
         (time too short)  (vol compounds)

The key insight for position construction:
- Want gamma (movement exposure)? → Buy near-dated ATM
- Want vega (vol exposure)? → Buy far-dated ATM
- Want gamma but NOT vega? → Buy near-dated, hedge vega by selling far-dated (calendar spread)
- Want vega but NOT gamma? → Buy far-dated OTM wings (low gamma, meaningful vega + volga)

PART 11: INTERVIEW APPLICATION

If asked "Explain gamma and why it matters for risk management":

"Gamma measures the rate of change of delta — it's the acceleration of P&L. If you're long gamma, big moves help you: your delta automatically adjusts in your favor, and you profit proportionally to the SQUARE of the move. Short gamma is the opposite: losses accelerate as markets move against you.

For risk management at a systematic-leaning fund, gamma is more important than delta because delta tells you where you are NOW but gamma tells you where you'll be AFTER a move. A desk that's earning $45K/day in theta from sold premium looks great on a calm day — but if they have $800K of short gamma per 1% and the market drops 5%, that theta income is wiped out by a $10M gamma loss in a single session. My job is to quantify that BEFORE it happens — stress the portfolio under 3σ and 5σ moves and make sure the tail loss is within the desk's risk budget."

If asked "Walk me through the gamma-theta tradeoff":

"There's no free lunch. Long gamma means you profit from realized volatility — your position naturally buys low and sells high through dynamic hedging — but you pay theta every day for that privilege. The break-even point is when realized vol equals implied vol. If you buy options at 20% IV and the market only realizes 15%, you bleed. Short gamma is the mirror: you earn theta daily, but you're writing insurance against tail events and your losses go as the square of the move.

At the portfolio level, I'd track gamma/theta ratio as an efficiency metric. If a PM is paying $80K/day in theta but only has $300K/1% gamma, their break-even daily move is large — they need big, frequent moves to justify the cost. Conversely, a PM earning $45K/day in theta with $800K/1% short gamma has a Sharpe-killer hiding in the tail. I'd run the historical distribution of daily moves and show them: 'On 95% of days, you earn. On the other 5%, you lose everything from the past 3 months.'"

If asked "What are second-order Greeks and why should I care?":

"Second-order Greeks measure how first-order Greeks change — and in a crisis, that's what determines whether your hedges hold. Vanna tells you how delta shifts when vol moves. In a sell-off, vol spikes and vanna causes your put hedge deltas to become MORE negative — so your hedges automatically become more effective. That's a feature. Volga tells you how vega changes with vol — if you're short OTM puts and vol spikes, volga makes your vega loss ACCELERATE, compounding the damage.

At the portfolio level, these second-order effects also drive systematic flows. Dealers who are short OTM puts have negative vanna exposure — when vol drops, their delta shifts and they sell, adding to downward pressure. When vol spikes, they buy, adding support. These 'vanna flows' move billions of dollars daily and are one reason markets behave differently around option-heavy environments like monthly expiry or around the gamma flip level."
