D — GREEKS: FIRST & SECOND ORDER

The language of risk for every derivatives position

Depth: 🔴 DEEP

WHY YOU NEED THIS COLD

Every conversation with a trader about options positions uses Greeks. If a PM says "I'm long 500 gamma at the 6400 strike," you need to immediately understand:
- What that means in dollar terms
- How their P&L changes if the market moves
- Where the danger is

Greeks ARE the risk of a derivatives position. Know them like you know your own name.

PART 1: FIRST-ORDER GREEKS

DELTA (Δ) — Directional Exposure

What it measures: How much the option price changes for a $1 move in the underlying.

Range:
- Calls: 0 to +1. Deep ITM call → delta ≈ 1 (moves 1:1 with stock). ATM call → delta ≈ 0.5. OTM call → delta → 0.
- Puts: -1 to 0. Deep ITM put → delta ≈ -1. ATM put → delta ≈ -0.5. OTM put → delta → 0.

Dollar delta: Delta × number of contracts × multiplier × spot price.
If you own 100 ATM calls on SPX (delta = 0.50, multiplier = 100): dollar delta = 0.50 × 100 × 100 × $5,400 = $27M equivalent. That's how much equity exposure you actually have.

Delta as probability (rough approximation): Delta ≈ probability the option expires ITM. A 0.30 delta call ≈ 30% chance of finishing ITM. Not a formal probability, but useful intuition.

Net delta / portfolio delta: Sum all position deltas. A book with +$50M call delta and -$30M put delta = +$20M net delta. Net long = gains if market rises, loses if market falls.

Delta hedging: To be delta-neutral, sell/short the underlying equal to your dollar delta. If you're long $20M delta → short $20M of stock. Now you don't care about small moves — your P&L is driven by gamma, vega, and theta instead.

GAMMA (Γ) — Acceleration of Risk

What it measures: How much delta changes for a $1 move in the underlying. It's the RATE OF CHANGE of delta. The second derivative.

Why it's the most important Greek for risk:
- Gamma tells you how your EXPOSURE changes as the market moves.
- Long gamma: your delta moves IN YOUR FAVOR. Market up → you get longer. Market down → you get shorter. You automatically "buy low, sell high."
- Short gamma: your delta moves AGAINST YOU. Market up → you get shorter (missing the rally). Market down → you get longer (accumulating exposure into the drop). You "buy high, sell low."

Gamma P&L:
$$\text{Gamma P\&L} = \frac{1}{2} \times \Gamma \times (\Delta S)^2$$

This is always positive for long gamma (regardless of direction). Big move up? Win. Big move down? Also win. That's the beauty of long gamma.

For short gamma: always negative for big moves. Small moves are fine (you collect theta). But large moves are catastrophic and the losses ACCELERATE.

Where gamma is highest: ATM options near expiry. A 1-day-to-expiry ATM option has massive gamma — its delta can swing from 0.5 to 1.0 or 0.0 in minutes. This is "pin risk" — the stock is right at the strike near expiry.

Gamma scalping: Delta-hedge a long gamma position repeatedly. As the market moves up, you sell (because delta increased); as it moves down, you buy (delta decreased). You're harvesting volatility. But you're paying theta every day for this privilege.

THETA (Θ) — Time Decay

What it measures: How much the option loses per day just from time passing.

Always negative for long options. You own an option → it loses value every day. Think of it as "rent" for the optionality.

Always positive for short options. You sold an option → you EARN theta every day. But you bear gamma risk.

The gamma-theta tradeoff:
- Long gamma, negative theta: You profit from big moves but bleed every calm day.
- Short gamma, positive theta: You earn money every calm day but get killed on big moves.

This is the fundamental tradeoff in options. There's no free lunch — you can't have positive gamma AND positive theta.

Theta acceleration: Time decay is NOT linear. An option loses more theta per day as it approaches expiry. A 30-day option might lose $50/day; at 5 days to expiry, it's losing $200/day.

$$\theta \propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{T}}$$

Weekend theta: Options lose time value over the weekend (2 calendar days). Friday night → Monday morning, you lose theta. As an RM, track whether PMs are using expiry selection to manage theta costs.

VEGA (ν) — Volatility Sensitivity

What it measures: How much the option price changes for a 1 percentage point change in implied volatility.

Always positive for long options. Higher vol → higher option prices (more chance of a big move → option is worth more).

Always negative for short options. Higher vol → your short option liability increases.

Where vega is highest: ATM options with long time to expiry. A 1-year ATM option has much more vega than a 1-week ATM option (more time for vol to matter).

Vega in dollars: If your portfolio has $500K vega, it means: a 1 vol point increase in implied volatility → +$500K P&L. A 1 vol point decrease → -$500K.

Why vega matters for risk:
- Vol can spike very fast. VIX went from 14 to 82 in March 2020. That's 68 vol points. If you were short vega, you got destroyed.
- Vol tends to spike DOWN the market (fear → vol up) and compress UP the market (complacency → vol down). So short vega is correlated to long delta — both hurt in a sell-off.

RHO (ρ) — Interest Rate Sensitivity

What it measures: How much the option price changes for a 1% change in interest rates.

Usually small for short-dated equity options. But material for:
- Long-dated options (LEAPS)
- Currency options (rate differential drives pricing)
- Bond options / swaptions

Call rho is positive (higher rates → calls worth more, because the present value of the strike is lower).
Put rho is negative (higher rates → puts worth less).

In the current high-rate environment, rho matters more than usual for longer-dated positions.

PART 2: SECOND-ORDER GREEKS

These measure how the first-order Greeks change. You won't derive these, but you need to know what they do.

VANNA — Delta sensitivity to vol

$$\text{Vanna} = \frac{\partial \Delta}{\partial \sigma} = \frac{\partial \nu}{\partial S}$$

What it means: How much delta changes when implied vol changes.

Why it matters: In a sell-off, vol spikes. If your options have negative vanna, your delta shifts against you BECAUSE vol went up. Double whammy: market down AND your hedges shifted.

Practical example: You hold OTM puts as hedges (negative delta). Vol spikes → their delta becomes MORE negative (toward -1). Your hedge suddenly covers more of your portfolio. This is good — it's why vanna is a feature of put protection, not a bug.

VOLGA (VOMMA) — Vega sensitivity to vol

$$\text{Volga} = \frac{\partial \nu}{\partial \sigma}$$

What it means: How much vega changes when implied vol changes.

Why it matters: If you're long OTM options (wings), they have positive volga — as vol rises, your vega INCREASES, which means the next vol point up is worth even more. Your wings become increasingly valuable in a vol spike. This is the "convexity of vol."

For the RM: A portfolio that's long volga has tail protection — it becomes more protected as conditions deteriorate. Short volga is the opposite — increasingly exposed as vol rises.

CHARM — Delta sensitivity to time

$$\text{Charm} = \frac{\partial \Delta}{\partial T}$$

What it means: How much delta changes just from one day passing.

Why it matters: Over a weekend, delta can drift significantly for near-expiry options. A PM who delta-hedged on Friday may find themselves unhedged by Monday — not because the stock moved, but because time passed and delta changed.

SPEED — Gamma sensitivity to underlying

$$\text{Speed} = \frac{\partial \Gamma}{\partial S}$$

How gamma changes as the underlying moves. Important near barriers and for very large gamma positions.

PART 3: PORTFOLIO GREEKS

Aggregation

Portfolio Greeks = sum of all position Greeks:
- Portfolio delta = Σ (position delta × quantity)
- Portfolio gamma = Σ (position gamma × quantity)
- Same for vega, theta.

The Greek Dashboard (What You Monitor)

Greek What you're checking Red flag
Net delta Directional exposure — are we net long or short the market? Delta exceeds mandate / risk budget
Gamma Are we long or short gamma? How convex is our P&L? Large short gamma near current spot → danger zone
Theta How much are we paying (or earning) per day? Theta costs exceeding realistic expectations for gamma capture
Vega How much do we gain/lose if vol moves 1pt? Large short vega in a low-vol environment (complacency)
Gamma per theta How much gamma are we buying per dollar of theta spent? Low ratio = overpaying for optionality

Long Gamma vs Short Gamma — The Risk Manager's View

Long Gamma Short Gamma
Pay or earn theta? PAY EARN
Big moves help or hurt? HELP HURT
P&L pattern Many small losses, occasional big wins Many small wins, occasional big losses
Risk manager's concern Theta bleed → are we spending too much? Tail event → can we survive a 5-sigma day?
Who does this? Hedge funds buying protection, vol arbitrageurs Market makers, sellers of premium (income strategies)

PART 4: GREEK INTERACTIONS IN PRACTICE

Scenario: Market drops 5%, vol spikes 8 points

A PM has a portfolio with:
- Net delta: +$20M (long equities)
- Gamma: -$500K per 1% move (short gamma from sold puts)
- Vega: -$200K per vol point
- Theta: +$30K/day

What happens:

  1. Delta loss: +$20M delta × -5% = -$1.0M
  2. Gamma acceleration: Being short gamma, losses accelerate. Gamma P&L = ½ × (-$500K) × 5² = -$6.25M (approximate, since gamma changes with spot)
  3. Vega loss: Vol spikes 8pts, short vega: 8 × (-$200K) = -$1.6M
  4. Theta gain: Still earning: +$30K (negligible in this context)

Total estimated loss: ~$8.8M

The PM thought they were "only" $20M delta. But short gamma and short vega turned a $1M delta loss into an $8.8M portfolio loss. That's why Greeks beyond delta matter.

What the RM should have flagged BEFORE:
- "You're short gamma — a 5% move costs you $6M+ on top of your delta loss."
- "Your short vega adds another layer — a vol spike compounds the sell-off damage."
- "Your net risk profile is concave — you lose more than linearly in a down move."

PART 5: INTERVIEW APPLICATION

If asked "Explain gamma and why it matters for risk management":

"Gamma measures how fast delta changes — it's the acceleration of exposure. If you're long gamma, big market moves help you: your delta automatically adjusts in your favor. If you're short gamma, big moves hurt you: your exposure grows in the wrong direction as the market moves against you.

For risk management, gamma is arguably more important than delta because delta tells you where you are RIGHT NOW but gamma tells you WHERE YOU'LL BE after a move. A portfolio that's short gamma and short vega has a concave P&L profile — it underperforms in tail events on multiple dimensions simultaneously. That's what I'd flag: not just the current delta, but the shape of the P&L curve and how it changes in stress."

If asked "Walk me through the gamma-theta tradeoff":

"There's no free lunch in options. Long gamma means you profit from big moves — but you pay theta every day for that privilege. Short gamma means you earn theta daily — but you're exposed to large, accelerating losses on big moves.

The RM's job is to check that the tradeoff is conscious and fair. If a PM is paying $50K/day in theta, I'd ask: 'What realized vol level do you need to break even on this position?' If they're paying for 25% vol but the market is realizing 18%, they're bleeding. Conversely, if they're earning theta by selling premium, I'd check: 'What's your worst-case P&L on a 3-sigma day? Can the fund survive that?'"
