PART 3: THE COMPLETE HISTORY

The Origin Story (2000-2008): Inside Lehman Brothers

2000 — Olivier Durantel and Gregoire Schneider found nQuant inside Lehman Brothers Tokyo. Initial strategy: long/short equities using quantitative methods. They were prop traders — trading the bank's own money, not client funds.

2003-2004 — The nQuant team moves to Lehman Brothers New York. Antoine Fillet (2003) and Maxime Fortin (2004) join as graduates straight out of Polytechnique. The team is now four founders strong.

2005-2008 — nQuant expands to Lehman Brothers London, becoming a truly global operation. They diversify beyond equities into low-latency (high-frequency) trading strategies — a move that would prove crucial to their future success.

Surviving Lehman's Collapse (2008-2014): The Barclays Years

September 15, 2008 — Lehman Brothers files for bankruptcy. Barclays swoops in and acquires Lehman's North American operations for $1.75 billion. nQuant is part of the package.

2008-2014 — The nQuant team operates under Barclays for six years. They continue building and refining their quantitative strategies. Importantly, they're operating as a prop desk inside a bank — trading the bank's capital, generating returns for Barclays, but not managing external money.

May 2014 — Barclays announces plans to cut non-core businesses as part of a restructuring. nQuant is classified as non-core. Meanwhile, the Volcker Rule (from Dodd-Frank, effective April 2014) prohibits commercial banks from proprietary trading. nQuant's days inside Barclays are numbered.

August 2014 — Bloomberg reports that nQuant will spin off as an independent firm, taking approximately 60 employees with it.

Independence (2014-Present): Squarepoint Capital

December 16, 2014 — nQuant formally spins off from Barclays and renames to Squarepoint Capital. For the first time in 14 years, the team is independent.

January 2, 2015 — Squarepoint receives FCA (Financial Conduct Authority) approval in the UK to manage investments on behalf of third parties. This is the first time the team manages external capital — previously they only traded Barclays' (and before that, Lehman's) money.

2015-2017 — Rapid growth phase. Within two years, Squarepoint doubles its headcount from the ~60 employees at launch. They aggressively hire quant researchers, developers, and traders to expand capacity.

2020 — Squarepoint Ops (US entity) files Form ADV showing:
- ~440 employees in the US operations alone
- $34.6 billion in regulatory AUM
- 80% non-US investors
- San Francisco Employees' Retirement System approves a $200M allocation to Squarepoint funds

October 2021 — Squarepoint enters a strategic partnership with Arini Capital, a European credit-focused hedge fund founded by Hamza Lemssouguer (ex-Credit Suisse). Squarepoint provides operational/infrastructure support to Arini. This is significant — it shows Squarepoint is willing to support discretionary-adjacent strategies.

March 2022 — WSJ reports that Squarepoint has been trading bitcoin futures on CME. However, the fund has been "hesitant on crypto" — cautious, systematic approach rather than speculative.

2024-2025 — AUM explodes from ~$127bn to ~$182bn. Whether this is organic growth (more leverage, more strategies) or net inflows is unclear, but likely a combination.

2026 — The discretionary team expansion is underway. They're hiring risk managers for this new business line — that's you.

PART 6: LAWSUITS & IP PROTECTION (KNOW THIS)

Squarepoint has been involved in two notable legal disputes. These reveal important things about the firm's culture.

The Sesum Case (2018) — Protecting Trade Secrets

In April 2018, Squarepoint sued former researcher Vojislav Sesum, alleging he:
1. Developed a trading strategy while employed at Squarepoint
2. Left the firm
3. Offered the same strategy to Millennium Management

The arbitrator ordered Sesum to pay:
- $188,137 in damages
- $919,053 in disgorged earnings from Millennium (money Millennium paid him for the stolen strategy)

Key takeaway: Squarepoint is extremely aggressive about protecting intellectual property. They enforce non-competes and IP agreements. This is standard at top quant funds — your strategies, code, and ideas belong to the firm.

The Footnoted.com Case (2017) — Data Acquisition Controversy

A researcher named Nicolas Elbaze (based in Switzerland) obtained a trial subscription to Footnoted.com, a financial research site. Footnoted alleged that Squarepoint:
- Used the trial to access 16,000+ pages of content
- Potentially used an automated webcrawler (4-8 second intervals between page requests)
- Refused to pay for a subscription after the trial

Squarepoint denied wrongdoing, and the case was never formally litigated (Footnoted couldn't afford the legal costs). However, the incident was extensively covered by Institutional Investor in October 2020.

Key takeaway: Squarepoint is voraciously data-hungry ("leverages large historical data sets, either compiled in-house or licensed/purchased from third parties" — SEC filing). They aggressively seek out new data sources. The firm is also willing to threaten legal action against those who publicly criticize them. This is a hardball culture.

What This Means for You

  • IP protection is serious. If you join, everything you build belongs to Squarepoint. Non-competes are likely strict (garden leave period).
  • Data is core to their identity. The more data sources and analytical tools you can bring to your risk work, the more valued you'll be.
  • Don't mention these lawsuits in the interview. But know them so you understand the culture.

PART 11: INTERVIEW ANGLES — How to Impress

Do's

  1. The French connection — All 4 founders are French (Polytechnique). You're French. Don't overplay it, but it's a cultural fit signal. If it comes up naturally, acknowledge the shared background.

  2. The systematic-to-discretionary evolution — Show you understand WHY they're expanding:

    "What attracted me is that Squarepoint has this world-class systematic platform that took 25 years to build. The discretionary team gets to leverage that infrastructure — data, execution, risk models — while adding human judgment. That's a rare combination."

  3. Builder mentality — The discretionary team is new. The JD says "build out the risk framework." Connect this to your NYC experience building tools from scratch.

  4. Platform knowledge — Know the tech stack and show you can hit the ground running: Python, kdb+, Bloomberg, SQL = their daily tools.

  5. Market awareness — Reference current events (Middle East, Q1 hedge fund performance, rate expectations). Shows you're plugged in.

  6. The Arini connection — Mentioning the Arini partnership (without going deep) shows you've researched beyond the front page.

  7. Use their language — Mirror the specific words from their website: "collaborative," "pragmatic," "inquisitive," "ownership," "passionate about the details."

Don'ts

  • Don't pretend to know their performance or returns — Private, unpublished. Saying "I heard you returned 15%" is a red flag.
  • Don't compare negatively to Millennium/Citadel — Different model. Don't say "you're better than..." Say "what differentiates you is..."
  • Don't ask Solomon about specific strategy details — He's a recruiter. He won't know and you'll seem tone-deaf.
  • Don't say "I saw on Wikipedia" — Say "in my research" or "from what I understand"
  • Don't mention the lawsuits — Footnoted or Sesum. You know them for context, not for conversation.
  • Don't quote exact AUM numbers unprompted — It sounds like you're trying too hard. But if asked "what do you know about us?", having $182bn ready shows diligence.
  • Don't ask about work-life balance first — This is a hedge fund, not a bank. Ask about the team structure, the build-out, the tech stack first.

Questions to Ask Solomon

  1. "Can you tell me more about how the discretionary team is structured — how many PMs, what asset classes they focus on?"
