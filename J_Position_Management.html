J — POSITION & PORTFOLIO MANAGEMENT

Sizing, conviction, drawdown budgets, broken-thesis exits, liquidity-adjusted sizing, scaling, risk budgeting, and the RM's position monitoring framework

Depth: 🔴 DEEP

WHY POSITION MANAGEMENT IS THE RM'S DAILY OPERATING SYSTEM

Stock selection gets the headlines. Position management determines the actual returns.

A PM who picks 60% winners but sizes them poorly — overweight busted trades, underweight winners, concentrated in correlated names — will UNDERPERFORM a PM who picks 50% winners but sizes them intelligently, cuts losers fast, and maintains diversification.

The RM's role in position management:

Function What you do What you DON'T do
Sizing Flag when position size exceeds conviction/liquidity/risk budget Tell the PM WHICH stocks to buy
Monitoring Track every position's risk contribution, P&L, thesis status Trade for the PM
Escalation Surface drawdown protocols, force the broken-thesis conversation Override the PM's investment judgment
Architecture Ensure portfolio construction rules are applied consistently Design the PM's trading strategy

At Squarepoint, the discretionary desk is embedded in a systematic-at-core firm. The CRO expects the same RIGOR in position management from the discretionary PM that the quant models apply automatically. You are the bridge — translating discretionary judgment into systematic discipline.

PART 1: POSITION SIZING — THE MOST IMPORTANT DECISION

One-liner: The PM decides WHAT to trade. The RM helps decide HOW MUCH. Sizing is the largest single driver of portfolio P&L variance.

Why Sizing Matters More Than Selection

The math is stark:
- A fund with 60% win rate and EQUAL sizing across positions has a Sharpe of ~0.5
- The SAME fund with CONVICTION-WEIGHTED sizing (larger in high-conviction winners) has Sharpe of ~0.8
- The SAME fund with POOR sizing (overweight losers due to averaging down) has Sharpe of ~0.2

Sizing explains 40-60% of P&L variance. Selection explains 20-30%. The remainder is timing and luck.

Five Sizing Frameworks

1. Equal Weight

Every position gets the same notional allocation.

$$w_i = \frac{1}{N}$$

Pros Cons
Simple, no overconfidence bias Ignores conviction — best idea = worst idea
Mechanically diversified Ignores volatility — 50-vol biotech = 12-vol utility
Easy to implement and monitor Rebalancing on every new position entry

When it's right: High-turnover statistical strategies, initial deployment of new desk, when PM track record is unproven.

2. Conviction-Based Sizing

Size proportional to PM confidence level, within a structured tier system.

Conviction tier Size (% NAV) # of positions typical Total allocation
Tier 1 — Anchor (top 3-5 ideas) 5-8% each 3-5 15-40%
Tier 2 — Core 2-4% each 8-12 20-40%
Tier 3 — Satellite 0.5-2% each 10-20 10-30%
Tier 4 — Exploratory 0.25-0.5% each 5-10 2-5%

RM checks on conviction sizing:
- Does the PM have enough Tier 1 positions to justify concentration?
- Are Tier 4 positions consuming disproportionate attention relative to their P&L contribution?
- Is there "conviction creep" — positions that started as Tier 3 growing to Tier 1 through price appreciation without the PM consciously deciding to increase conviction?

3. Risk-Based Sizing (Vol-Weighted)

Size so each position contributes EQUAL RISK (volatility contribution), not equal dollars.

$$\text{Notional}_i = \frac{\text{Target risk budget per position}}{\sigma_i}$$

Where $\sigma_i$ = annualized volatility of asset $i$.

Worked example:

Stock Annualized vol (σ) Target risk $/position = $200K Notional allocation
JNJ 15% $200K / 0.15 $1.33M
AAPL 25% $200K / 0.25 $800K
TSLA 50% $200K / 0.50 $400K
MSTR 80% $200K / 0.80 $250K

Each contributes ~$200K of annual risk to the portfolio despite wildly different notionals. Without vol-weighting, equal notional in TSLA vs JNJ means TSLA dominates portfolio risk 3:1.

4. Kelly Criterion and Practical Variants

Full Kelly:

$$f^* = \frac{p \cdot b - q}{b} = \frac{\text{Edge}}{\text{Odds}}$$

Where:
- $f^*$ = fraction of capital to bet
- $p$ = probability of winning
- $b$ = win/loss ratio (amount won per dollar risked if correct)
- $q = 1 - p$ = probability of losing

Worked example:
- Trade has 55% win rate ($p = 0.55$), with 2:1 payoff ($b = 2$)
- $f^* = (0.55 \times 2 - 0.45) / 2 = 0.65 / 2 = 0.325$ = 32.5% of capital

That's insane. Full Kelly implies enormous bets and drawdowns of 50%+ routinely. In practice:

Kelly fraction Max drawdown (approximate) Use case
Full Kelly 50%+ periodic Never used in discretionary
Half Kelly ~25% Aggressive quant strategies
Quarter Kelly ~12% Discretionary with good track record
Eighth Kelly ~6% New PM, untested strategy

RM insight: Kelly is a CEILING, not a target. The edge estimate ($p$ and $b$) is always uncertain — if your edge estimate is wrong by even 20%, full Kelly turns into overbet → ruin. The PM's confidence in the edge should determine how far below Kelly they size.

5. Maximum Loss-Based Sizing

Size so the defined stop-loss represents an acceptable portfolio hit:

$$\text{Notional} = \frac{\text{Acceptable loss per position}}{\text{Stop-loss distance (%)}}$$

Worked example:
- Portfolio: $100M. Max loss per position: 0.5% of NAV = $500K
- Stop-loss distance: 8% (PM will exit if the stock drops 8%)
- Max notional = $500K / 0.08 = $6.25M (6.25% of NAV)

If the stop is tighter (3%): max notional = $500K / 0.03 = $16.7M — but this risks whipsaw exits.
If the stop is wider (15%): max notional = $500K / 0.15 = $3.3M — smaller position, more room.

The tradeoff: Wide stop × small position = less whipsaw, lower aggregate risk per position. Tight stop × large position = more whipsaw but higher individual position conviction.

PART 2: LIQUIDITY-ADJUSTED SIZING — THE CONSTRAINT MOST PMs IGNORE

One-liner: You can't size a position larger than you can EXIT. Liquidity is not a soft constraint — it's a hard wall.

ADV (Average Daily Volume) Constraint

The core rule: Never own more than N days of ADV in a single position, where N represents the maximum acceptable liquidation timeline.

$$\text{Max notional} = \text{ADV}(\$) \times \text{Participation rate} \times \text{Max exit days}$$

Where:
- $\text{ADV}$ = average daily dollar volume (20-day average)
- $\text{Participation rate}$ = % of daily volume you can trade without moving the market (typically 10-25%)
- $\text{Max exit days}$ = how many days to fully exit (depends on urgency and market conditions)

Worked example — normal markets:

Stock ADV ($) 15% participation 5-day exit Max position
AAPL $12B $1.8B/day 5 days $9.0B (unconstrained)
XOM $2.5B $375M/day 5 days $1.9B (unconstrained)
Mid-cap stock $50M $7.5M/day 5 days $37.5M
Small-cap stock $5M $750K/day 5 days $3.75M
Micro-cap $500K $75K/day 5 days $375K

Stress-adjusted ADV: In a crisis, volumes can DROP 30-50% (bid disappears) or SPIKE 300%+ (panic selling floods the market but at dislocated prices). The RM should size to the STRESS ADV, not the calm-weather ADV.

$$\text{Stress ADV} = \text{Normal ADV} \times \text{Stress factor}$$

Market condition Stress factor Effect on max position
Normal 1.0 Full position
Moderate stress 0.6-0.7 40% smaller max position
Crisis 0.3-0.5 50-70% smaller max position
Flash crash / circuit breaker 0.1-0.2 80%+ reduction — may not be able to exit at all

Market Impact Cost

$$\text{Impact cost} \approx \sigma \times \sqrt{\frac{Q}{V}} \times k$$

Where:
- $\sigma$ = daily volatility
- $Q$ = quantity being traded
- $V$ = daily volume
- $k$ = impact coefficient (typically 0.5-1.5 depending on asset class)

Example: Selling $5M of a $50M ADV stock:
- σ = 2%/day, Q/V = 0.10, k = 1.0
- Impact = 2% × √0.10 × 1.0 = 2% × 0.316 = 0.63% per execution
- On $5M: slippage = $31.5K — manageable

Example: Selling $5M of a $5M ADV stock:
- σ = 3.5%/day, Q/V = 1.0, k = 1.0
- Impact = 3.5% × √1.0 × 1.0 = 3.5% per execution
- On $5M: slippage = $175K — THIS is the liquidity tax

The RM's Liquidity Sizing Check

Before EVERY new position, the RM should verify:

Check Formula Threshold
Days to exit (normal) Position / (ADV × 15%) < 5 days for most; < 3 for volatile names
Days to exit (stress) Position / (ADV × stress factor × 15%) < 10 days
Position as % of ADV Position / ADV < 100% for most; < 50% for small/mid
Estimated impact Almgren-Chriss or simple model < 1% one-way; < 0.5% preferred
Crowding check Ownership % of float < 5% of free float

PART 3: CONVICTION AND THE BROKEN-THESIS FRAMEWORK

One-liner: Conviction isn't "I feel strongly." It's a structured assessment of variant perception, catalyst, downside, and edge durability — each of which can break independently.

The Four Pillars of Conviction

Pillar Question the PM must answer Red flag if absent
Variant perception What do I see that the market doesn't? "I just like the stock" → no edge
Catalyst identification What event will make the market agree with me — and WHEN? No catalyst = a dead-money trade
Downside quantification If I'm wrong, how much do I lose? Where's the floor? "I don't know" → position is unsized
Edge durability Why won't this edge get arbitraged away before my catalyst? Edge is common knowledge → already priced

Conviction scoring (the RM's internal framework):

Score Each pillar Size implication
4/4 — All four solid Clear edge, known catalyst, defined downside, durable edge Tier 1: 5-8% of NAV
3/4 — One pillar weak One area of uncertainty Tier 2: 2-4%
2/4 — Two pillars weak Significant uncertainty Tier 3: 0.5-2%
1/4 — Only one pillar Speculative Tier 4: 0.25-0.5% max
0/4 — None articulated Gambling Reject or challenge

The Three Broken-Thesis Tests

When a position is losing money, the PM (and RM) must answer three sequential questions:

Test 1: Is the original thesis intact?

"We bought XOM at $110 because we expected Brent above $85 through Q3 and XOM's breakeven at $55 gives 55% margin of safety."

  • Brent is now at $78. XOM is at $102. The PM is down $800K.
  • But: XOM breakeven is STILL $55. Brent at $78 is STILL profitable. The thesis thesis might be intact — the market is just disagreeing temporarily.
  • If thesis is intact → HOLD or ADD (if risk budget allows). The loss is unrealized and the fundamental setup hasn't changed.

Test 2: Has new information arrived that wasn't in the original analysis?

New information Impact Action
OPEC signals production increase Supply thesis broken Cut or reduce significantly
New regulation on carbon emissions Long-term fundamental shift Reassess from scratch
XOM management change / strategy pivot Company-specific thesis changed May need to cut
Competitor discovers major new field Market share thesis weakened Reduce position
Brent just drifted lower on no news No new information — just price Thesis may still be intact

Critical distinction: Price movement is NOT new information. Price moving against you doesn't break the thesis — only NEW FUNDAMENTAL INFORMATION breaks the thesis. The RM's job is to separate the two.

Test 3: Would you put this position on TODAY at the current price?

  • Forget your entry. You have NO position. Would you buy XOM here at $102 with Brent at $78?
  • If YES → hold. Loss aversion isn't driving the decision.
  • If NO → the position is alive only because of anchoring bias. Cut it.
  • If MAYBE → the conviction has degraded. Trim to Tier 3 or Tier 4 size.

This is the most powerful test because it strips out:
- Anchoring to entry price
- Sunk cost fallacy ("I've already lost $800K, I need to make it back")
- Loss aversion ("I'll just wait for it to come back to even")
- Ego ("I can't be wrong about this")

RM role: When a PM is underwater and anchoring, don't argue about the stock. Simply ask: "Would you put this on today?" If the PM hesitates, the position is dead. The PM already knows — they just need someone to say it out loud.

PART 4: DRAWDOWN PROTOCOLS & RISK BUDGETS

One-liner: A risk budget is a CONCESSION of trust from the firm. Breach it and you lose trust. Manage it well and you earn more.

Risk Budget Architecture

Each PM operates within a multi-dimensional risk budget allocated by the CRO:

Dimension Metric Soft limit Hard limit Breach consequence
Daily P&L Worst daily loss −$500K −$1M Soft: RM conversation. Hard: mandatory de-risk to 50% gross.
Weekly P&L Cumulative week loss −$1M −$2M Hard: position review + gross cap reduction
Monthly P&L Cumulative month loss −$2M −$4M Hard: forced liquidation of worst risk/reward positions
YTD drawdown Peak-to-trough −$5M −$8M Hard: desk shutdown discussion with CRO
VaR (1-day 99%) Parametric or historical $3M $5M Hard: no new risk-adding trades
Gross exposure |L| + |S| $80M $100M Hard: reduce positions to limit
Net exposure L − S ±$30M ±$50M Hard: bring within band
Single name % of NAV 5% 8% Hard: trim immediately
Sector % of gross 25% 35% Hard: diversify or hedge
Top 5 concentration Top 5 as % of gross 40% 55% Hard: spread risk across more positions

The Drawdown Escalation Ladder

As the PM consumes more of their loss budget, risk limits TIGHTEN progressively:

Zone Loss budget consumed RM response PM restrictions Escalation
🟢 Green 0-25% Normal monitoring None None
🟡 Yellow 25-50% Daily position review with PM Review all Tier 1 positions. Run broken-thesis tests. RM flags to CRO in morning report
🟠 Orange 50-75% Twice-daily check-ins Gross capped at 80% of limit. No new Tier 1 positions without RM approval. CRO briefed directly. Recovery plan requested.
🔴 Red 75-100% Continuous monitoring Gross capped at 60%. Only risk-reducing trades. All new trades require RM + CRO approval. PM presents recovery plan to CRO within 24 hours.
⚫ Stop-out 100% Full de-risk All positions closed or hedged to delta-flat. PM sits out until reset period. Formal review with CRO + management.

Squarepoint context: The discretionary desk is NEW. Initial loss budgets will be TIGHT:
- Monthly: probably $2-3M soft, $4-5M hard
- YTD: probably $6-8M before mandatory review
- VaR: $3-5M 1-day 99%

As the PM builds track record, budgets expand. The RM is the credibility bridge — documenting disciplined risk-taking is how the desk earns more capital.

PART 5: PORTFOLIO CONSTRUCTION MONITORING

One-liner: The PM builds the portfolio. The RM monitors whether the portfolio the PM ACTUALLY has matches the portfolio they MEANT to have.

Exposure Metrics

Metric Formula Interpretation
Gross exposure $\lvert L \rvert + \lvert S \rvert$ Total capital deployed. Higher = more leverage/activity.
Net exposure $L - S$ Directional bias. +$20M = bullish. −$10M = bearish.
Net/Gross ratio $\text{Net} / \text{Gross}$ 0.0 = market-neutral. 0.5 = moderately long. 1.0 = fully long (no shorts).
Beta-adjusted net $\sum w_i \beta_i$ True market exposure accounting for betas. A portfolio can be $0 net exposure but net long beta if longs have higher β than shorts.
Effective leverage Gross / NAV How much capital is at work relative to equity. >2× is aggressive for discretionary.

Worked example:

Longs Shorts Total
Notional $65M $35M
Gross $100M
Net $30M (long biased)
Net/Gross 0.30
Avg beta (long) 1.15
Avg beta (short) 0.90
Beta-adjusted net $65M × 1.15 − $35M × 0.90 $43.3M (MORE long than net $ suggests)

RM insight: The PM says "I'm only modestly net long at $30M." But beta-adjusted net is $43.3M — the PM's longs are higher-beta than the shorts. In a sell-off, the portfolio behaves like a $43M long portfolio, not a $30M one. This is a common surprise that the RM must surface.

PART 6: POSITION LIFECYCLE — FROM ENTRY TO EXIT

One-liner: Every position has a birth, a life, and a death. The RM tracks all three stages with a different lens at each.

Entry — The Pre-Trade Check

Before EVERY new position, the RM runs a structured check:

Check What the RM verifies Pass/Fail criteria
Risk budget Does adding this breach any limit? All limits remain below soft threshold
Marginal VaR What's the INCREMENTAL VaR of this position? Marginal VaR < position's pro-rata share
Correlation How does this position correlate with existing book? If ρ > 0.7 with any existing position → flag
Factor exposure Does this add unwanted factor tilt? Factor loads within ±0.5 of target
Liquidity Can we exit in <5 days at <1% impact? Days to exit (normal) < 5
Conviction tier Does size match conviction? Size within tier band
Crowding Are we entering a crowded position? Ownership < 5% of float; 13F overlap < 30%
Hedge plan If this goes wrong, what's the exit plan? PM articulates stop-loss level and/or hedge

The RM doesn't have VETO power (at most firms). But they can:
- Flag concerns in writing
- Escalate to CRO if risk budget is threatened
- Require the PM to explicitly acknowledge risks
- Document the conversation (CYA is real)

Holding — Ongoing Monitoring

Frequency What the RM checks
Daily P&L, VaR contribution, drawdown status, any limit breach
Weekly Thesis status for top 10 positions, factor drift, correlation changes, concentration metrics
Monthly Full portfolio review with PM: every position discussed, broken-thesis tests on losers, winner management
Quarterly Performance attribution: are we making money from selection, sizing, or luck?

Red flags during holding period:

Signal What it means RM response
Position grows from Tier 3 to Tier 1 size via appreciation, no PM action "Conviction creep" — PM didn't consciously decide to increase size "This position has grown to 7% of NAV. Do you want to be Tier 1 conviction, or should we trim to target weight?"
High P&L volatility relative to conviction Position dominates book risk despite being a Tier 2 idea Re-examine sizing. Is the vol-adjusted size appropriate?
Correlation with existing positions increasing Portfolio becoming less diversified over time Run stress scenario: "What if all your correlated positions drop together?"
PM stops talking about a position Often a sign it's not working and PM is avoiding the conversation Force the conversation: "Let's review all positions that haven't been discussed in 2+ weeks."

Exit — Four Types

1. Profit Exit — Thesis Realized

Signal Action
Catalyst hit: stock re-rated as thesis predicted Close or trim to Tier 4 "monitoring" size
Valuation target reached Close and reallocate capital to next-best idea
Risk/reward no longer attractive at new price Trim. Don't hold winners forever without thesis.

Disposition effect warning: PMs cut winners too early ("let me lock in profit") and hold losers too long ("it'll come back"). The RM should challenge BOTH impulses.

2. Loss Exit — Thesis Broken

Run the three tests (Part 3). If thesis is broken:
- Cut immediately. Do NOT wait for a bounce.
- The entry price is IRRELEVANT. The only question is: would you buy this today?
- If position is large enough that exit moves the market, use an execution algo (VWAP/TWAP) over 1-3 days.

3. Thesis Intact Exit — Risk Budget Drives Exit

Sometimes the thesis is fine but the PORTFOLIO situation forces the exit:
- Drawdown protocol at 🔴 Red → all new risk frozen, may need to trim existing positions
- VaR limit breach → must reduce risk contribution
- Correlation event → position is no longer diversifying

Hardest conversation in RM: "The thesis is right, but we need to sell because we're out of risk budget." The PM hates this. But the alternative is breaching the hard limit and losing 100% of risk authority.

4. Forced Exit — Exogenous

Trigger Action
Regulatory (e.g., sanctions, compliance) Exit immediately regardless of P&L
Short borrow recall Cover short immediately
Liquidity event (stock halts, ADV collapses) Reduce as fast as market allows
Fund-level redemption Pro-rata reduction across all positions

PART 7: POSITION SCALING — WHEN AND HOW TO ADD OR TRIM

One-liner: Scaling in and out of positions is the PM's primary expression of changing conviction. The RM ensures scaling decisions are CONSCIOUS, not reactive.

Scaling In (Adding to Position)

When to add When NOT to add
Thesis confirmed by new data (earnings beat expectation, catalyst approaching) Averaging down on a broken thesis ("it's cheaper now!")
Stock at better entry point with thesis INTACT Adding because of loss aversion ("I need it to recover")
Risk budget has capacity and correlations support it Risk budget is at Orange or Red zone
PM has explicit pre-planned scaling schedule Impulse reaction to a price drop

Pre-planned scaling approach:

"I want to build a 5% position in XOM. I'll enter 2% initially, add 1.5% if Q2 earnings confirm thesis, and add the final 1.5% if Brent stays above $80 through July."

This is GOOD because:
- Each tranche has explicit conditions
- The position grows WITH evidence, not against it
- If conditions aren't met, the position stays small

RM check: "You planned to add at 2 specific trigger points. Earnings MISSED. Why are you adding now?"

Scaling Out (Trimming)

When to trim When NOT to trim
Valuation target partially reached Panic — price dropped and you're scared
Position has grown too large relative to conviction tier Disposition effect — taking profit too early on a working thesis
Concentration risk emerging (>5% NAV single name) Forced by irrelevant noise
Risk budget constraints (approaching Orange/Red) To "be doing something" — activity is not progress
Approaching known risk event (earnings) where PM wants to reduce exposure

+3 months $118 $4.6M (appreciated) 4.6% Hold — thesis intact Brent at $87, everything tracking
+5 months $128 $5.0M (appreciated) 5.0% Trim $1.0M → $4.0M Position hit 5% NAV → concentration risk. Trim to original dollar amount.

PART 8: RISK BUDGETING — DECOMPOSING PORTFOLIO RISK

One-liner: A 5% position in a high-beta, high-correlation stock might contribute 20% of portfolio VaR. The RM's job is to make RISK CONTRIBUTION visible, not just dollar weight.

Marginal Risk Contribution

$$\text{Marginal VaR}i = \frac{\partial \text{VaR}_p}{\partial w_i} = \beta{i,p} \times \text{VaR}_p$$

Where $\beta_{i,p}$ = beta of position $i$ to the portfolio.

Component Risk Contribution:

$$\text{Component VaR}_i = w_i \times \text{Marginal VaR}_i$$

$$\sum_{i=1}^{N} \text{Component VaR}_i = \text{VaR}_p$$

This decomposes total portfolio VaR into each position's contribution. The sum of all component VaRs equals the total portfolio VaR.

Worked Example — Risk Decomposition

Portfolio: $80M, 5 positions

Position Weight ($) Weight (%) Daily vol Correlation w/ port Component VaR ($K) Risk contribution (%)
TSLA $12M 15% 3.5% 0.85 $380K 33%
XOM $20M 25% 1.8% 0.70 $280K 24%
AAPL $18M 22.5% 2.0% 0.75 $270K 23%
JNJ $15M 18.75% 1.0% 0.50 $90K 8%
TLT (short) $15M 18.75% 1.2% −0.30 −$68K −6% (reduces risk)
TOTAL $80M 100% $952K 82% (short TLT diversifies)

RM observations:
1. TSLA is 15% of dollars but 33% of risk. This is the classic vol-weight vs risk-weight divergence.
2. JNJ is 18.75% of dollars but only 8% of risk. It's barely contributing to portfolio volatility.
3. TLT short has NEGATIVE risk contribution — it's diversifying. This is a functioning hedge.
4. "PM, TSLA is your smallest dollar position but your biggest risk. If TSLA drops 10%, you lose ~$120K MORE than if any other single position drops 10%. Is that intentional?"

Risk Parity — Equal Risk Contribution

Target each position to contribute EQUAL risk:

$$\text{Risk Contribution}_i = \frac{1}{N} \times \text{Total VaR}$$

With 5 positions: each should contribute 20% of total VaR. Currently:
- TSLA: 33% → OVERWEIGHT in risk terms (need to trim from 15% to ~9% of capital)
- JNJ: 8% → UNDERWEIGHT in risk terms (could increase or accept as intentional)

RM doesn't enforce risk parity — discretionary PMs deliberately concentrate risk on their best ideas. But the RM SHOWS the risk decomposition so the PM's concentration is INTENTIONAL, not accidental.

PART 10: INTERVIEW APPLICATION

"A PM wants to add a new position but is already at 85% of their risk budget. What do you do?"

"First, I'd look at the MARGINAL risk contribution. If the new position is diversifying — low correlation to the existing book — it might barely change portfolio VaR. A negatively correlated position could actually REDUCE VaR even though it adds gross exposure. In that case, 85% might only go to 86%.

But if it's correlated — same sector, same factor exposure — it could push to 95%+. That's one bad day from breaching the hard limit and triggering mandatory de-risking.

My response isn't 'no.' It's: 'You're at 85%. This position takes you to 94% because it's 0.75 correlated with your two largest holdings. If any of those three names drops 3%, you'll breach the hard limit and I'll have to reduce your gross by 30%. Do you want to trim an existing position to make room, or is this idea worth that risk?'

I'm making the tradeoff transparent. The PM makes the investment decision — I make sure it's a CONSCIOUS one."

"How do you know when a position is too large?"

"Three lenses. First, RISK CONTRIBUTION: if one position contributes more than 25% of portfolio VaR, it's the portfolio — everything else is noise. That concentration better be the PM's highest-conviction idea with a clear catalyst.

Second, LIQUIDITY: if the position takes more than 5 days to exit at 15% participation, it's too large for the strategy's risk profile. In stress, that 5-day exit becomes 15 days when volumes dry up.

Third, LOSS IMPACT: if the defined stop-loss distance on this position would consume more than 30% of the monthly loss budget in a single trade, the position is too large relative to the risk budget. You can't afford to be right on 4 positions and wrong on 1 if that 1 takes out half your budget."

"Walk me through how you'd manage a drawdown."

"It's a structured escalation. At 25% of monthly budget consumed, I have a conversation. We review the top losers and run broken-thesis tests: is the thesis intact? Has new information arrived? Would you put this on today?

At 50%, restrictions kick in — gross exposure capped, no new positions without my review. This isn't punishment; it's capital preservation. The worst outcome is a PM losing 75% of budget in the first two weeks and having to sit out the remaining two weeks of the month.

At 75%, we're in active defense. Only risk-reducing trades. The PM presents a recovery plan: 'Here's what I'm keeping, here's what I'm cutting, and here's why I expect to recover X% by month end.'

And at 100% — stop-out. Positions closed or hedged flat. Reset at the next period. This sounds harsh, but it protects the PM's career. A systematic fund would have cut risk automatically at −5%. The discretionary desk gets more rope, but the rope has a defined length."

"How would you handle a PM who keeps averaging down on losing positions?"

"That's one of the most common and dangerous patterns. Averaging down is rational IF the thesis is intact, the PM planned to scale in, and the risk budget allows it. But in practice, most averaging down is loss aversion disguised as conviction.

I'd approach it with data, not judgment. I'd show the PM three things: First, this position was originally sized as Tier 2 at $3M. Through averaging down, it's now $6.5M and Tier 1 by size — but did your conviction actually increase to Tier 1, or did the price drop force your hand? Second, here's your risk contribution: this position went from 12% of portfolio VaR to 28%. It's dominating the book. Third, here's the asymmetry: you need a 15% recovery just to get back to breakeven — is that realistic in your time horizon?

If the PM can pass all three broken-thesis tests and articulate why today — at this price — they would put on a Tier 1 position, I'll support it. If they hesitate, the position needs to be cut to its original Tier 2 size at minimum."
