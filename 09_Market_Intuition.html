MARKET INTUITION & PRACTICAL MARKET KNOWLEDGE

How Instruments Actually Work, What Moves Markets, and What You Need to Feel in Your Bones

PART 1: HOW INSTRUMENTS ACTUALLY WORK — MECHANICS

This is the stuff textbooks skip. It's how things work on the actual plumbing level.

1.1 FUTURES — Lifecycle from Trade to Expiry

What Happens When You Buy a Futures Contract

  1. Trade execution: Your order hits the exchange (CME, ICE, Eurex). Matched against a seller. You don't know who the seller is — the exchange's clearinghouse stands in the middle.
  2. Initial margin posted: The clearinghouse demands collateral. For ES (S&P 500 futures), this is ~$12,000-15,000 per contract (the notional is ~$250,000). You post cash or treasuries.
  3. Daily mark-to-market: Every day at settlement (usually 4:00 PM ET for US), the clearinghouse calculates your P&L. If ES went up 10 points and you're long 1 contract, you made $500 ($50/point). That $500 is credited to your margin account. If you lost, it's debited.
  4. Margin call: If your account drops below the maintenance margin (lower than initial), you get a margin call. You must post additional cash by the next morning. If you don't, the clearinghouse liquidates your position. This is non-negotiable.
  5. Rolling: Before the contract expires, you close the near-month and open the next month. This is "rolling." The cost/gain depends on the calendar spread.
  6. Expiry:
  7. Cash-settled (ES, NQ, FESX, Brent ICE): On expiry, the final settlement price is calculated (usually from the opening prices of index constituents), and cash is transferred. No physical delivery.
  8. Physically-settled (WTI NYMEX, corn, soybeans, gold): If you hold to expiry, you're obligated to take/make delivery of the physical commodity. Financial participants NEVER want this. WTI delivers to Cushing, Oklahoma. Gold delivers to COMEX-approved vaults. Delivery logistics are complex and expensive.

The April 2020 WTI Disaster — Why Physical Delivery Matters

WTI May 2020 futures went to -$37.63/barrel because:
- Nobody could take physical delivery (storage was full at Cushing)
- Financial players holding long positions panicked as expiry approached
- There were no buyers for the physical crude
- Forced sellers at any price → negative price (you PAY someone to take the oil)

Lesson: Always know if your contract is physically or cash settled. Always monitor positions approaching expiry.

Settlement Prices — Why They Matter

  • Daily settlement determines your margin P&L. It's calculated by the exchange — usually a volume-weighted average of trades in the final minutes.
  • Final settlement on expiry determines your ultimate P&L:
  • Index futures (ES): Special Opening Quotation (SOQ) — the opening prices of all 500 stocks on expiry morning. This is why "triple witching" days (3rd Friday of Mar/Jun/Sep/Dec) have huge volume at the open.
  • Commodity futures: varies by contract. Some use closing price, some use a fixing window.
  • Settlement risk: If the settlement price is manipulated or anomalous (flash crash during the settlement window), your P&L is distorted.

Futures Basis — Why Futures ≠ Spot

$$\text{Basis} = F - S = S(e^{(r-q)T} - 1) \approx (r - q) \times S \times T$$

Where $r$ = funding rate, $q$ = dividend/convenience yield.

  • Positive basis (contango / futures > spot): Normal when $r > q$. You pay a premium for future delivery.
  • Negative basis (backwardation / spot > futures): When $q > r$. Supply is tight NOW. Immediate delivery is worth more.
  • Basis convergence: As expiry approaches, $T \to 0$, so basis → 0. Futures price converges to spot. This MUST happen by definition (at expiry, futures = spot).
  • Basis trade (Treasury): Buy the cash treasury bond, sell the treasury future, earn the basis. This was a massive trade before COVID — when repo markets froze in March 2020, the basis blew out from a few basis points to 50bp+ and forced liquidation of $1 trillion+ in positions.

1.2 OPTIONS — What Actually Happens From Trade to Expiry

The Life of an Option Position

Day 1 — You buy a call option:
- You pay the premium upfront. That's your maximum loss.
- The market maker who sold it to you now has a problem: they're short Delta. They need to hedge.
- The market maker buys Δ shares of the underlying to be Delta-neutral. Now they're hedged... for this instant.

Day 2 — The stock moves up 2%:
- Your call is worth more. You have a profit.
- The market maker's Delta has increased (Gamma). Their hedge is now too small.
- The market maker buys MORE shares to re-hedge. This is "Delta hedging."
- Key insight: When dealers are short Gamma (which is typical — they sell options to customers), they MUST buy when the market goes up and sell when it goes down. This amplifies moves. When dealers are long Gamma, they do the opposite (sell into strength, buy weakness) which dampens moves.

Day 5 — Nothing happens. Stock flat.
- Your option loses value from time decay (Theta). You're paying ~$X/day to hold this position.
- The market maker collects that Theta as income. This is their compensation for taking Gamma risk.

Approaching expiry — 3 days left:
- If the option is ATM, Gamma explodes. Small price moves cause big changes in Delta.
- If the stock is near the strike, you don't know if the option will expire in or out of the money.
- Delta flips between 0 and 1 rapidly (for the market maker, this means constant re-hedging with huge position swings).
- Pin risk: If the stock closes exactly at the strike, you don't know if you'll be assigned. This creates an overnight position of unknown size.

Expiry day:
- European options (SPX, European equity index options): Exercise only on expiry. Settlement is usually based on the opening price of the settlement day (AM settlement) or closing price (PM settlement). No early exercise complications.
- American options (single stock options in US): Can be exercised ANY TIME before expiry.
- Early exercise on calls: Happens almost exclusively the day before an ex-dividend date. If the dividend > remaining time value of the option, it's rational to exercise early to capture the dividend.
- Early exercise on puts: Happens when the put is deep in-the-money and the time value is less than the interest earned on the cash from selling the stock.
- Auto-exercise: Options that are $0.01+ in the money at expiry are automatically exercised by the OCC (Options Clearing Corporation). If you DON'T want to exercise, you must submit a "Do Not Exercise" notice.

Option Expiry Cycles — What Expires When

US Equity Options:
- Monthly: 3rd Friday of each month (traditional)
- Weekly (Weeklys): Every Friday. Huge for short-term trading/hedging.
- Daily (0DTE — Zero Days to Expiry): SPX options now expire EVERY TRADING DAY. This is massive — 0DTE volume is ~50% of total SPX option volume. The Gamma effects from daily expiries are reshaping market microstructure.

Index Options (SPX):
- AM-settled: Cash-settled based on Special Opening Quotation. The option doesn't trade on expiry morning — you just get the settlement.
- PM-settled: Cash-settled based on closing SPX price. Most Weeklys and dailies are PM-settled.

Futures Options:
- Options on futures (ES options, CL options, GC options)
- Exercise gives you a futures position, not the underlying
- Expiry is typically a few days before the underlying futures' expiry

The Greeks in Practice — What They FEEL Like

Textbooks define the Greeks mathematically. Here's what they feel like when you're watching them:

Delta in practice:
- You look at your book and see "total Delta: +$5M." That means if the market goes up 1%, you make ~$50K. If it drops 1%, you lose ~$50K. It's your directional bet.
- A PM says "I'm flat Delta." That means they THINK they have no directional exposure. But Delta is a snapshot — it changes as the market moves (Gamma).

Gamma in practice:
- Gamma tells you how STABLE your Delta is.
- If you have high positive Gamma, your P&L accelerates in your favor as the market moves. You feel like you're "locked in" — the further it goes, the more you make.
- If you have high negative Gamma, every move hurts more and more. You feel like you're on a treadmill — constantly re-hedging and losing ground.
- The worst feeling: Short Gamma into a sharp move. The market drops 3%, your Delta suddenly doubled, you're scrambling to sell to re-hedge, but selling pushes the market lower, which makes your Delta even bigger. Feedback loop.

Vega in practice:
- You're long Vega and vol is running. It feels great — your options are worth more and more.
- Then vol suddenly collapses (a geopolitical situation resolves, earnings pass without surprise). Your positions lose value even though the underlying didn't move much. That's a "vol crush."
- Vega risk is sneaky because most people focus on direction (Delta). A PM can be "right" on direction but lose money because vol collapsed.

Theta in practice:
- Every morning at 9:31 AM, before the market even ticks, your long options book is worth slightly less than yesterday. That's Theta burn.
- If you're a net seller of options (positive Theta), you wake up every morning slightly richer. Except when you don't (Black Monday, Volmageddon, COVID crash).
- Theta accelerates near expiry. An ATM option with 1 day left might lose 50% of its remaining value overnight. With 30 days left, it loses ~3%.

1.3 SWAPS — How They Actually Work

Interest Rate Swap (IRS) — Step by Step

The setup:
- Two parties: Party A (pays fixed, receives floating) and Party B (pays floating, receives fixed)
- Notional: $100M
- Tenor: 5 years
- Fixed rate: 4.00% (annual payment)
- Floating rate: SOFR (paid quarterly)

What happens on each payment date (quarterly):

The floating leg resets based on the SOFR rate at the beginning of the period.

Quarter SOFR Rate Party A Pays (Fixed) Party A Receives (Float) Net to A
Q1 3.80% $1,000,000 (= 4.00% × $100M × 0.25) $950,000 (= 3.80% × $100M × 0.25) -$50,000
Q2 4.20% $1,000,000 $1,050,000 +$50,000
Q3 4.50% $1,000,000 $1,125,000 +$125,000
Q4 3.50% $1,000,000 $875,000 -$125,000
  • No principal is exchanged. Only the net difference is paid on each date.
  • At inception, the swap has zero PV (the fixed rate is set so both legs are worth the same)
  • After inception, it gains/loses value as rates move. If rates rise, the fixed payer (Party A) benefits (they locked in a lower rate). If rates fall, the fixed receiver (Party B) benefits.

Mark-to-market:
- The swap's value = PV(remaining floating payments) − PV(remaining fixed payments)
- Discounted at the current OIS (SOFR) curve
- This is your daily P&L for a swap position

Termination before maturity:
- You can't "sell" a swap like a bond. You unwind by entering an equal and opposite swap (or agreeing with the counterparty to cancel for a cash payment equal to the current mark-to-market).

Total Return Swap (TRS) — Equity

The setup:
- Party A (total return payer): pays the total return (price change + dividends) of a stock/index
- Party B (total return receiver): pays a financing rate (SOFR + spread)

What happens:
- If the stock goes up 5%, Party A pays 5% to Party B
- If the stock goes down 3%, Party B pays 3% to Party A
- Party B also pays the financing cost (e.g., SOFR + 50bp) on the notional
- NET: Party B has synthetic long exposure to the stock without owning it

Why use a TRS instead of just buying the stock?
- Leverage: No need to buy the full amount — just post margin on the swap
- Avoids settlement, custody, stamp duty (UK stamp duty = 0.5%)
- Avoids disclosure: (Pre-Archegos) You could build a massive position without triggering 5% ownership disclosure. Post-Archegos, regulators now require TRS position reporting.
- Short selling: Easier to get short exposure via TRS than borrowing shares

Commodity Swap — OTC

The setup (what you priced at BNP):
- Corporate airline client wants to lock in jet fuel price for the next 12 months
- Airline pays: fixed price of $2.50/gallon × 1,000,000 gallons/month = $2.5M/month
- Bank (BNP) pays: floating market price of jet fuel × 1,000,000 gallons/month
- If jet fuel is at $3.00: bank pays $3M, airline pays $2.5M, net: bank pays airline $500K
- If jet fuel is at $2.00: bank pays $2M, airline pays $2.5M, net: airline pays bank $500K
- The airline has locked in $2.50/gallon regardless of market moves

Basis risk in practice:
- Jet fuel doesn't have a liquid futures contract. The swap is usually written on Brent crude or heating oil (ULSD).
- The airline has basis risk: jet fuel can diverge from Brent (the crack spread widens)
- Sometimes the swap uses a jet fuel index (Platts Singapore jet) which reduces basis risk but is less liquid

Credit Default Swap (CDS) — Step by Step

The setup:
- Protection buyer pays 200bp/year on $10M notional = $200,000/year (paid quarterly at $50,000)
- Protection seller collects the premium and agrees to pay out if the reference entity defaults
- If no default: Protection buyer pays premium for nothing. Like insurance.
- If default occurs:
- Physical settlement: Protection buyer delivers the defaulted bonds, protection seller pays par ($10M). Buyer gets par for bonds that might be worth 30 cents on the dollar.
- Cash settlement: An auction determines the recovery rate (say 40%). Seller pays buyer $(1 - 0.40) × $10M = $6M$.

Mark-to-market during the life:
- If the credit spread widens (credit deteriorates), the protection buyer profits (their insurance is now worth more)
- If the credit spread tightens (credit improves), the protection seller profits
- CDS DV01 (or CS01): The P&L for a 1bp move in the CDS spread

The CDS Big Bang (2009):
- Standardized CDS coupons to either 100bp or 500bp (regardless of the actual fair spread)
- At trade inception, an upfront payment is made to compensate for the difference between the standard coupon and the fair spread
- This made CDS easier to trade and clear centrally

1.4 BOND MECHANICS — What Happens Between Coupon Dates

Accrued Interest

When you buy a bond between coupon dates, you pay the seller the accrued interest (the interest earned since the last coupon).

$$\text{Accrued} = \frac{\text{Days since last coupon}}{\text{Days in coupon period}} \times \text{Coupon payment}$$

  • Clean price = quoted price (without accrued interest)
  • Dirty price = clean price + accrued = what you actually pay
  • Bloomberg quotes clean, you settle dirty. This is a source of confusion.

Day Count Conventions (This WILL Come Up)

Convention Calculation Where Used
ACT/360 Actual days / 360 Money markets, SOFR, EURIBOR, USD swaps float leg
ACT/365 Actual days / 365 GBP swaps, some USD instruments
ACT/ACT Actual days / actual days in year US Treasuries, French OATs
30/360 Assumes 30-day months / 360-day year US corporate bonds, USD swap fixed leg
30E/360 European version of 30/360 (subtle differences at month-end) European bonds, EUR swap fixed leg

Why it matters: A 1-day difference in accrued interest on a $100M position = real money. Get the day count wrong, and your P&L reconciliation breaks.

Coupon Frequency

  • US Treasuries: Semi-annual coupon
  • European govies (Bunds, OATs, BTPs): Annual coupon
  • US corporate bonds: Semi-annual
  • This affects duration calculation. Semi-annual coupon bonds have slightly shorter duration than annual coupon bonds of the same maturity (more frequent cash flows).

PART 2: WHAT MOVES MARKETS — THE MACRO CALENDAR

2.1 THE FED — Most Important Market Mover on Earth

FOMC (Federal Open Market Committee)

  • Meets 8 times per year (roughly every 6 weeks). Schedule published a year in advance.
  • Decision at 2:00 PM ET: Rate decision + statement released. Markets react INSTANTLY.
  • Press conference at 2:30 PM ET: Fed Chair takes questions. This is where the NUANCE happens — a single word change can move markets violently.
  • Dot plot (quarterly): Each FOMC member's projection for future rates. Released at the Mar/Jun/Sep/Dec meetings (the ones with the SEP — Summary of Economic Projections).

What to Watch at Every FOMC

  1. The rate decision itself: Usually priced in (Fed Funds futures show the market's expectation with ~95% accuracy). A SURPRISE hike/cut is a 50-100bp move in rates and 3-5% in equities.

  2. The statement — word changes:

  3. "Inflation remains elevated" vs "inflation has eased" → dovish shift, rates trade lower, equities bid
  4. "Labor market remains tight" vs "labor market is coming into balance" → dovish
  5. "Further tightening" vs "maintaining" vs "adjustment" → signals next move
  6. Adding or removing "at an appropriate pace" signals urgency

  7. Every single word is dissected. Markets move on the DIFFERENCE from the prior statement.

  8. Powell's press conference:

  9. Tone matters more than words. Is he confident? Hedging? Nervous?
  10. Keywords that move markets: "data-dependent" (neutral), "restrictive" (still hawkish), "normalization" (going back to neutral), "accommodation" (dovish), "appropriate" (status quo), "elevated" (concerned), "patient" (no rush to act)

  11. The Q&A is where slip-ups happen. A reporter asks "would you rule out a rate cut in March?" — Powell says "it's not our base case" vs "it's certainly possible" — that 4-word difference = $billions in market moves

  12. The dot plot (quarterly):

  13. Each dot = one FOMC member's year-end rate forecast
  14. Market cares about the median dot for the current year and next year
  15. If the median dot for 2026 moves from 4.00% to 3.50% → market now expects more cuts → rates fall, equities rally, USD weakens
  16. Dot plot vs market pricing: If the dots show 2 cuts but the market has priced 4, correction coming

The Fed Funds Futures Complex

  • Fed Funds futures (CME): The market's probabilistic bet on where the Fed Funds rate will be at each meeting
  • CME FedWatch tool: Gives a percentage probability of hike/hold/cut at each meeting
  • This is your real-time gauge of market expectations. If Fed Funds futures price a 90% chance of a cut and the Fed holds, that's a shock.
  • SOFR futures (3-month SOFR): More liquid, give you the full rate path expectation for the next 2-3 years

How FOMC Moves Translate Across Asset Classes

2.2 OTHER CENTRAL BANKS

ECB (European Central Bank)

  • Meets every 6 weeks. Decision + press conference.
  • Key difference from Fed: ECB also does "TLTROs" (Targeted Longer-Term Refinancing Operations) — cheap loans to banks. When these expire/aren't renewed, it's effectively tightening.
  • Fragmentation risk: Unlike the Fed, the ECB must worry about spread between member state bonds (Italy BTP vs Germany Bund). If the spread blows out (>250bp), the ECB may (reluctantly) intervene with TPI (Transmission Protection Instrument).
  • Watch: Lagarde tends to be less precise than Powell. Markets sometimes struggle to read ECB guidance.

BOJ (Bank of Japan)

  • The most important central bank for carry trades.
  • Yield Curve Control (YCC): BOJ targeted a 0% yield on the 10Y JGB. When they adjusted this (July 2023, March 2024), global bond markets shook.
  • Rate hikes: BOJ raised rates from negative to 0.25% in 2024. Any further hikes → JPY strengthens violently → carry trades unwind → risk-off globally (Aug 2024 unwind).
  • Watch: BOJ communication is opaque. Policy changes often come as "surprises" even when telegraphed. Impact is global because of the JPY carry trade.

BOE (Bank of England)

  • MPC (Monetary Policy Committee) meets 8 times/year
  • Less globally impactful than Fed/ECB/BOJ, but critical for GBP positions
  • Watch: UK inflation tends to be stickier than US/Eurozone. BOE often behind the curve.

PBOC (People's Bank of China)

  • Not independent — acts as arm of Chinese government policy
  • Sets MLF (Medium-Term Lending Facility) rate and LPR (Loan Prime Rate)
  • FX intervention: Actively manages USD/CNY. Sets daily fixing. If the fixing is weaker than expected → signal of devaluation tolerance → EM sell-off, commodity pressure
  • Watch: PBOC doesn't hold press conferences. You read policy through actions, state media leaks, and the daily fixing.

SNB (Swiss National Bank)

  • Small but can create massive moves. Jan 15, 2015: removed the EUR/CHF 1.20 floor. CHF appreciated 30% in minutes. Multiple hedge funds and brokerages went bankrupt (FXCM, Alpari).
  • Watch: SNB is willing to intervene aggressively. They also hold massive equity portfolios (they own US tech stocks!).

How Data Surprises Move Markets

The market doesn't react to the number — it reacts to the number vs expectations.

$$\text{Market reaction} \propto \text{Actual} - \text{Consensus expectation}$$

The Narrative Matters More Than the Number

The SAME data print can cause OPPOSITE market reactions depending on the prevailing narrative.

Example 1 — Strong jobs in a "soft landing" narrative:
- Market interpretation: "Economy is healthy, no recession" → Equities rally, moderate rate response

Example 2 — Strong jobs in an "inflation is sticky" narrative:
- Market interpretation: "Fed can't cut, will stay higher for longer" → Equities sell off, yields spike

Example 3 — Weak jobs in a "soft landing" narrative:
- Market interpretation: "Fed can cut, smooth transition" → Equities rally, rates fall

Example 4 — Weak jobs in a "hard landing" narrative:
- Market interpretation: "Recession is here, earnings will collapse" → Equities crash, rates fall, risk-off

Your job as risk manager: You need to understand not just WHAT the data says, but HOW THE MARKET WILL INTERPRET IT given the current narrative. This is market intuition.

2.4 GEOPOLITICAL HEADLINES — How They Flow Through Markets

The Transmission Mechanism

A geopolitical headline hits → markets react in a chain:

HEADLINE: "Iran attacks Israeli military base"
     │
     ▼
[1] OIL: Brent spikes 5-8% (supply disruption fear, Strait of Hormuz)
     │
     ▼
[2] SAFE HAVENS: Gold +2-3%, UST rally (yields fall 10-15bp), CHF/JPY strengthen
     │
     ▼
[3] RISK-OFF: Equities gap down 1-3%. VIX spikes 20-40%.
     │
     ▼
[4] CREDIT: Spreads widen 10-20bp (general risk aversion)
     │
     ▼
[5] EM: EM FX/equities sell off (flight to safety, USD funding stress)
     │
     ▼
[6] SECOND ORDER: Airlines/transports crash, defense stocks rally,
    energy stocks rally. Inflation expectations rise → rate expectations adjust.

Speed of Transmission

  • Oil reaction: Seconds. Algorithmic.
  • Index futures: Seconds. Correlated algo trading.
  • Individual stocks: Minutes. Humans process sector implications.
  • Credit/bonds: Minutes to hours. Less liquid.
  • EM FX: Minutes (electronic). Hours for NDFs/illiquid EM.
  • Central bank expectations: Hours to days. Market rethinks the full path.

4:00 PM US equity market closes. Settlement prices set. Risk team calculates EOD VaR, P&L, limits
4:00-8:00 PM Extended hours. Low liquidity but earnings are released here. A company reporting after the close can gap 10-20%. Monitor any positions with after-hours earnings
5:00 PM (CT) CME daily settlement for futures. Futures P&L finalized for the day
6:00 PM (ET) Globex futures session opens (following day). Asian trading begins on US futures

2.6 EARNINGS SEASON — How It Works

The Calendar

  • Reporting window: ~2 weeks after quarter end
  • Q1 earnings: mid-April to mid-May
  • Q2: mid-July to mid-August
  • Q3: mid-October to mid-November
  • Q4: mid-January to mid-February
  • Blackout period: Companies can't buy back stock ~2 weeks before earnings. Removes a buyer from the market.

What Moves the Stock

$$\text{Stock reaction} \approx f(\text{EPS surprise}, \text{Revenue surprise}, \text{Guidance}, \text{Positioning})$$

Factor Weight Details
EPS vs consensus 30% Beat by how much? Miss by how much? The bar is pre-managed — whisper numbers matter more than published consensus
Revenue vs consensus 20% Revenue is harder to "manage" than EPS (which can be adjusted with buybacks, one-time items)
Forward guidance 40% THIS IS THE MOST IMPORTANT. A company can beat on EPS but guide down for next quarter → stock crashes. Or miss on EPS but guide up → stock rallies
Positioning / expectations 10% If the stock already rallied 20% into earnings (high expectations), even a beat might not be enough. If expectations are low, a modest beat sends it flying

Implied Move (Straddle Pricing)

Before earnings, the options market prices an expected move:

$$\text{Implied move} \approx \frac{\text{ATM straddle price}}{\text{Stock price}}$$

If the stock is $100 and the weekly straddle (expiring after earnings) costs $8 → implied move = 8%.

  • If the ACTUAL move is > 8%: long straddle holders win (vol was underpriced)
  • If the ACTUAL move is < 8%: short straddle holders win (vol was overpriced)
  • Historically, options overestimate earnings moves ~60% of the time — selling earnings straddles has a slight positive edge, but the losses when you're wrong are large (short Gamma blowup)

Risk Manager During Earnings

  • Pre-earnings: Identify all positions with earnings upcoming. Calculate the implied move × position size = potential P&L shock. Present to PM: "If AAPL moves the implied 6%, your P&L swings ±$X"
  • During: Monitor after-hours reports. If a major holding reports badly, calculate immediate next-day impact on the PM's book
  • Post-earnings: Update risk metrics with new prices. Review any limit breaches.

PART 3: CROSS-MARKET RELATIONSHIPS — The Intermarket Playbook

Understanding how markets relate to each other is what separates a risk monitor from a risk MANAGER.

3.1 The Core Relationships

Stocks vs Bonds (The Most Important Relationship in Finance)

Low/stable inflation regime (pre-2020):
- Stocks up, bonds down (yields up) — "risk-on, growth is good"
- Stocks down, bonds up (yields down) — "risk-off, flight to quality"
- Correlation: NEGATIVE. Bonds hedge equities. This is the basis for 60/40 portfolios.

High/uncertain inflation regime (2022-?):
- Stocks down AND bonds down (yields up) — "inflation shock, Fed tightening"
- Correlation: POSITIVE. Bonds no longer hedge equities. 60/40 breaks.

How to know which regime you're in:
- If CPI is above 3% and rising → positive correlation likely
- If CPI is below 2.5% and stable → negative correlation likely
- The transition period is the most dangerous — models calibrated on one regime fail in the other

Dollar vs Everything

Dollar strengthening (DXY up):
- EM assets pressured (USD-denominated debt gets more expensive)
- Commodities pressured (priced in USD, so cost more for foreign buyers)
- US multinational earnings hurt (foreign revenue translates to fewer dollars)
- Gold pressured (opportunity cost of non-yielding asset rises)

Dollar weakening (DXY down):
- EM rally (easier to service debt, capital flows in)
- Commodities rally
- US multinationals benefit
- Gold rallies

Oil vs Inflation vs Rates

Oil price rises
    │
    ├── Input cost for economy → CPI rises (gasoline, jet fuel, plastics, transport)
    │       │
    │       └── Fed may tighten → yields rise → equities struggle
    │
    ├── Energy stocks rally, airlines/transports sell off
    │
    └── If supply-driven (war): stagflationary → worst case for portfolios
        If demand-driven (growth): inflationary but offset by earnings growth

The key distinction: WHY is oil rising?
- Demand pull (strong economy): Oil up, equities up, rates up. Manageable.
- Supply shock (war, OPEC cut): Oil up, equities down, rates complicated (inflation vs growth risk). Stagflation. Much worse for portfolios.

Gold — Three Drivers

Gold doesn't have cash flows, so it can't be valued with DCF. It trades on:

  1. Real interest rates (TIPS yields): Gold and real rates are negatively correlated. When real rates rise, gold has a higher opportunity cost. When real rates fall (or go negative), gold is more attractive.

$$\text{Gold} \approx f\left(-r_{\text{real}}\right)$$

  1. USD: Gold is priced in USD. Dollar down → gold up (mechanical + demand shift).

  2. Fear / tail risk: Gold spikes on geopolitical shocks, banking crises, and "end of the world" scenarios. But this effect is temporary unless the shock leads to sustained real rate decline.

The 2024-2026 anomaly: Gold has been rising DESPITE rising real rates. The driver: central bank buying (China, Russia, Middle East) + de-dollarization narrative. This breaks the traditional model. Risk lesson: relationships can change.

VIX vs S&P 500

  • VIX and SPX are negatively correlated (~-0.75 to -0.85)
  • When stocks fall, vol rises. When stocks rise, vol falls. But NOT symmetrically:
  • A 2% SPX drop → VIX might rise 4-6 points
  • A 2% SPX rally → VIX might drop 1-2 points
  • Vol is convex to the downside — it spikes harder on falls than it drops on rallies
  • VIX above 30: Crisis mode. Expect 2%+ daily moves in equities.
  • VIX below 15: Complacency. Everything is calm... until it isn't.
  • VIX 15-20: Normal. Market is healthy but cautious.

3.2 Correlation Regimes — What Changes When

From... To... What Flips Trigger
Risk-on Risk-off ALL correlations converge. Equities, credit, EM FX, HY all sell together. UST, gold, JPY, CHF rally. Geopolitical shock, credit event, recession signal
Low vol High vol Positions that were stable become violent. VaR models underestimate risk. Liquidity disappears. Surprise event, positioning unwind, leverage cascade
Negative stock-bond correlation Positive Bonds STOP hedging equities. 60/40 breaks. "Nowhere to hide." Inflation shock, rate surprise

PART 4: MARKET INTUITION — The Soft Skills

4.1 Reading Price Action

What Healthy Markets Look Like

  • Orderly moves: up days are calm, corrections are controlled
  • Volume confirms direction: rallies on high volume (conviction), pullbacks on low volume (lack of sellers)
  • Breadth participation: most stocks going up, not just the top 10
  • Volatility is low but not collapsing

What Sick Markets Look Like

  • Violent rallies on short covering, not new buying
  • "Gapping and trapping": opens gap up, immediately sells off (bull trap)
  • Breadth narrowing: only 5-10 mega caps holding the index up while everything else drops
  • VIX elevated but not spiking — persistent anxiety
  • Credit spreads widening while equities hold — credit usually leads

What Panic Looks Like

  • Bid-ask spreads blow out 5-10x
  • Volume 3-5x normal
  • Correlation → 1 (everything falls together)
  • VIX > 40
  • Treasury flash rally (yields collapse as everyone runs to safety)
  • Gold gaps up
  • EM currencies free-fall
  • Counterparty risk becomes real (CDS on banks widen)
  • This is when your stress testing and pre-prepared playbook matter

4.2 Positioning Awareness

Who Is Positioned How

Understanding WHO is in the market and HOW they're positioned tells you what might happen:

  • CTAs (Commodity Trading Advisors / Trend Followers): Follow price trends. When they hit their trigger levels, they buy/sell mechanically. Know where CTA positioning trigger levels are (bank research publishes estimates).
  • Risk parity funds: Target a fixed risk allocation. When vol rises, they sell. When vol falls, they buy. Their rebalancing can amplify moves.
  • 0DTE options traders: Massive daily Gamma exposure. Dealers hedging these options create pronounced intraday moves (acceleration into close, reversals at key gamma levels).
  • Passive/ETF flows: Monthly rebalancing at month-end, quarterly at quarter-end. Creates mechanical buying/selling pressure.
  • Buyback blackout: Before earnings, companies can't buy back stock. Removes a consistent buyer. Post-earnings, buybacks resume (a tailwind).
  • Futures options expiry: On the 3rd Friday, massive Gamma positions expire. "Gamma unclench" — the market maker hedging flows that were suppressing vol disappear, and the market can move freely again. Monday after OPEX is often volatile.

4.3 What to Look At Every Morning

Your daily checklist before the huddle:

  1. Overnight moves: S&P futures, Nasdaq futures, European indices, Asian indices. What happened while you were sleeping?
  2. FX: Dollar index (DXY), EUR/USD, USD/JPY, EM currencies. Any gaps?
  3. Rates: US 2Y, 10Y, 30Y yields. Any curve moves (steepening/flattening)?
  4. Commodities: Oil (Brent, WTI), gold, nat gas. Any supply/demand news?
  5. Credit: CDX IG, iTraxx Main. Widening = stress. Tightening = calm.
  6. Vol: VIX level and term structure. MOVE index (rates vol). Is vol in contango or backwardation?
  7. Flows: Any big block trades, ETF flows, 13F filings?
  8. Calendar: What data is out today? Any central bank speakers? Earnings after close?
  9. Geopolitical: Overnight headlines. Middle East, US-China, any surprises.
  10. YOUR BOOK: How did overnight moves affect each PM's positions? Any limit breaches? Any Greek migrations?
